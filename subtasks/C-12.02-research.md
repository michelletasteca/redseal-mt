# C-12.02 | Cleans Fruit — Enhanced Learning Resource

**Major Work Activity:** C — Produce
**Task:** C-12 | Prepares Fruit
**Enrichment Level:** Red Seal Exam Preparation + Apprentice Field Reference

---

## Overview and Why This Skill Matters

Cleaning fruit seems simple, but it is one of the highest-risk steps in fruit preparation. The surface of fruit — including the peel and rind that is never consumed — can harbour dangerous pathogens. When you cut through unwashed fruit with a knife, the blade carries surface bacteria directly into the edible flesh. This mechanism has caused major foodborne illness outbreaks in Canada and the United States.

Additionally, improper cleaning can strip protective waxes, waterlog delicate fruits, destroy nutrients, cause enzymatic browning, and shorten shelf life. A commercial cook must know not just **how** to clean each fruit type, but **why** each step exists — and what happens when it is skipped.

## Arrival Point (AP) Conditions and Assessment

Before cleaning, assess every batch of fruit received to determine the appropriate tools and method:

| AP Condition | What to Check | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Whole, intact skin** | Firmness, absence of breaks or mold | Wash/brush as appropriate to fruit type |
| **Damaged or bruised** | Soft spots, splits, mold growth | Trim damage before washing; discard if mold has penetrated |
| **Wet or damp from transit** | Surface moisture, condensation | Pat dry before cold storage; moisture accelerates spoilage |
| **Waxed (commercial)** | Shiny, uniform surface — apples, citrus, cucumbers | Wash thoroughly; wax traps pesticides and debris |
| **Organic/unwaxed** | Natural bloom or matte finish | Still requires washing — soil and field contamination |
| **Pre-washed (packaged)** | Label claim | May still require rinsing; inspect before use |
| **Frozen** | Ice crystals, no damage under packaging | Do not wash before use — thaw appropriately |

> **Common Industry Practice:** Even produce labelled "pre-washed" or "ready-to-eat" should be inspected and rinsed in a commercial kitchen setting where volume and cross-contamination risk are higher than at home.

## Cleaning Tools: Selection and Science

### Gloves

**Types used in fruit preparation:**
- **Nitrile gloves (preferred):** Latex-free; puncture-resistant; mandatory in jurisdictions or kitchens with latex allergy policies. Required when handling acidic fruits (lemon, lime, pineapple) that irritate prolonged skin contact. Required when cuts or broken skin are present on hands.
- **Cut-resistant gloves:** Used under or alongside nitrile when paring firm-skinned fruits (pineapple, melons) with large knives. Protects against knife slippage.
- **Latex gloves:** Avoid where possible — latex is a declared allergen in Canada; latex particles can transfer to food and trigger reactions in sensitized individuals.

> **Regulatory Note:** Under provincial OH&S regulations and food premises regulations, food handlers with open wounds or infections must protect food from contamination — gloves are required in those cases. Latex allergy policies vary by employer; nitrile is the safe universal default.

**When gloves are NOT a substitute for handwashing:**
- Gloves must be changed between tasks and after touching non-food surfaces
- Hands must be washed before putting on gloves
- Gloves do not replace handwashing — they supplement it
- A gloved hand that touches a garbage bin is just as contaminated as a bare hand

---

### Produce Brushes

**Why brushes are used:**
Fruit skins, particularly melons and root fruits, have surface texture (netting, crevices, ridges) where soil, bacteria, and pesticide residue accumulate. Rinsing alone does not dislodge material embedded in these surfaces. Mechanical scrubbing with a brush physically removes:
- Soil and field debris
- Surface pathogens embedded in rind texture
- Pesticide residue
- Commercial waxes and coatings

**Critical — Cantaloupe (Muskmelon):**

> **⚠️ FOOD SAFETY WARNING — LISTERIA MONOCYTOGENES**
> Cantaloupe netting is a documented vehicle for *Listeria monocytogenes*. The rough net-like rind traps soil bacteria that persist even after casual rinsing. When a knife cuts through the rind, it carries surface bacteria directly into the flesh. *Listeria* is especially dangerous for pregnant individuals, elderly, immunocompromised, and newborns — it can cause miscarriage, meningitis, and death.
>
> **Always scrub cantaloupe with a produce brush under running potable water before cutting.** This is the only effective intervention because you cannot see *Listeria* contamination and it cannot be detected by smell or appearance.
>
> A 2011 cantaloupe outbreak in the United States caused 33 deaths — the deadliest foodborne illness outbreak in US history at that time. Canadian cantaloupe outbreaks have also been documented. In commercial kitchens, treat all whole melons (cantaloupe, honeydew, casaba) this way.

**Brush hygiene — critical:**
- Produce brushes accumulate bacteria in their bristles rapidly during use
- Must be washed with hot water and detergent and sanitized between produce types
- Must be air-dried bristles-down or bristles-up — never flat, which traps moisture
- Replace brushes when bristles are worn, discoloured, or cannot be sanitized effectively
- Store separately from raw meat/poultry brushes — ideally colour-coded

**Produce brush types by fruit:**

| Fruit Type | Brush Type | Method |
|---|---|---|
| Cantaloupe, honeydew | Firm-bristle produce brush | Scrub all surfaces under running water; rinse thoroughly |
| Watermelon | Firm-bristle produce brush | Scrub entire rind; cut immediately or store whole |
| Potatoes (not technically fruit but same tool) | Vegetable brush | Scrub under running water |
| Apples, pears | Soft produce brush OR fingertips | Light scrub to remove wax film; rinse |
| Citrus (when zesting) | Soft produce brush | Scrub before zesting — wax and surface bacteria on rind |
| Stone fruits (peaches, plums) | Soft cloth or fingertips | Gentle wipe; brush may bruise soft skin |
| Berries | Do NOT use brush | Delicate — rinse in colander only immediately before use |

---

### Knives

**Role in cleaning:**
Knives are used for **paring** — the removal of skin, peel, rind, or damaged areas before or after washing.

**Cross-contamination risk with knives:**
- A knife that cuts through unwashed rind and then contacts flesh carries surface pathogens to the edible portion
- Use one knife for exterior rind removal and a clean knife for portioning the interior — or wash and sanitize between steps
- Never use the same knife for melons as for other ready-to-eat produce without sanitizing

**Knife sharpness is an OH&S and quality issue:**
- A dull knife requires more pressure → increases slip risk → higher injury rate
- A dull knife crushes fruit cells rather than cutting cleanly → bruising → accelerated enzymatic browning
- For citrus: a sharp knife allows controlled peel removal with minimal pith waste
- For pineapple: a sharp chef's knife or serrated knife allows safe crown and base removal

> **Regulatory Note (OH&S):** Maintaining sharp, well-maintained tools is an employer and worker obligation under provincial Occupational Health and Safety legislation. A dull knife is a documented hazard.

## Why Washing Fruit Matters — The Food Safety Science

### Surface Contamination Pathways

Fruit arrives contaminated from multiple sources:

| Contamination Source | Examples | Pathogens Involved |
|---|---|---|
| **Soil and field dust** | All field-grown produce | *E. coli* O157:H7, *Salmonella*, *Listeria* |
| **Irrigation water** | Melons, strawberries, leafy greens | *E. coli*, *Cryptosporidium*, Hepatitis A |
| **Animal activity** | Bird droppings on tree fruits | *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter* |
| **Harvest workers** | Hands, equipment contact | Norovirus, Hepatitis A |
| **Transport and storage** | Crates, truck surfaces | *Listeria* (refrigerated transport) |
| **Pesticide residue** | Conventional produce | Chemical contamination |
| **Commercial waxes** | Apples, citrus, cucumbers | Wax traps surface debris |
| **Processing facility** | Packaged/cut produce | Post-processing contamination |

### The Knife Transfer Mechanism — Why Washing Intact Fruit Matters

Many pathogens cannot penetrate intact, undamaged fruit skin. *Salmonella* and *E. coli* remain on the surface. However:

1. Knife contacts contaminated exterior
2. Knife blade carries organisms into flesh with each cut
3. Every subsequent slice spreads contamination further through the cut surface
4. The flesh is ready-to-eat — no further kill step will follow

**This is why melons and citrus must be washed before cutting, even though the rind is not eaten.**

---

## The Four Cleaning Procedures

### 1. Washing

**Definition:** Submerging, rinsing, or running potable water over fruit to remove surface contamination and debris.

**Science of cold water washing:**
- Cold water (close to 4°C) reduces the rate of microbial growth on wet surfaces during the washing process
- Cold water also limits activation of PPO (polyphenol oxidase) enzymes that cause enzymatic browning
- Water temperature differential: if wash water is much warmer than cold fruit, thermal shock can drive surface bacteria into the fruit through microscopic stomata (pores) — cold water avoids this

> **Regulatory Requirement:** Potable (drinking-quality) water must be used for all produce washing. This is required under provincial food premises regulations across Canada. Non-potable water (e.g., irrigation water, hose water of unknown quality) is not permitted for produce washing in food premises.

**Washing by fruit type:**

| Fruit Type | Method | Key Points |
|---|---|---|
| **Apples, pears** | Rinse under cold running water while rubbing with clean hands or soft brush | Remove wax buildup; rinse stem and calyx end thoroughly |
| **Citrus (oranges, lemons, limes)** | Scrub with soft brush under running water | Critical if zesting or serving wedges with rind intact |
| **Grapes** | Rinse in colander under cold running water; toss gently | Do not soak — waterlogging damages skin and flavour |
| **Strawberries** | Rinse in colander under cold running water | Leave hulls on during washing — hull removal before washing causes waterlogging of the flesh |
| **Blueberries, raspberries, blackberries** | Gently rinse in colander immediately before use | NEVER soak; wash immediately before service only |
| **Cantaloupe, honeydew** | Scrub entire rind with produce brush under running water | Non-negotiable step; see Listeria warning above |
| **Watermelon** | Scrub rind with produce brush | Large surface area — brush all sides |
| **Stone fruits (peaches, plums, cherries, apricots)** | Rinse under cold running water; gently rub | Do not soak; skin is delicate on ripe fruit |
| **Bananas** | Wipe exterior with damp cloth; rinse hands after handling | Peel is not eaten, but knife/hands touching peel then flesh can transfer contamination |
| **Pineapple** | Rinse exterior under water; dry before cutting | Focus on crown area where soil accumulates |
| **Mangoes** | Rinse and rub under running water | Latex-fruit cross-reactivity note: handle with gloves if latex allergies in facility |
| **Kiwi** | Rinse under water | Typically pared; skin edible if desired — scrub if serving unpeeled |
| **Figs** | Very gentle rinse; do not soak | Extremely delicate; damaged by water immersion; pat dry immediately |

---

### 2. Brushing

**Definition:** Using a produce brush to mechanically dislodge surface debris, bacteria, and residue from textured fruit skins.

**Why brushing is more effective than rinsing alone:**
- Rinsing reduces surface bacterial counts; brushing reduces them further
- Studies show that mechanical scrubbing reduces surface microbial loads by 1–2 additional log reductions compared to rinsing alone
- Textured surfaces (cantaloupe netting, apple calyx cavities, citrus oil glands) cannot be reached by water alone

**Brushing procedure:**
1. Wet the fruit surface under cold running water
2. Apply firm, even pressure with the brush across all surfaces
3. Pay attention to crevices, stem ends, blossom ends, and any netting
4. Rinse thoroughly to remove loosened debris
5. Pat dry if using immediately, or allow to drain before storage

**After brushing:** Wash and sanitize the brush before the next use.

---

### 3. Rinsing

**Definition:** The final pass of clean cold running water after washing, brushing, or paring to remove loosened debris, cleaning agents, or cut-surface juices.

**Purpose of a final rinse:**
- Removes loosened pathogens, debris, and any detergent residue (if cleaning agents were used)
- Washes away cut-surface juices that could promote microbial growth during storage
- Lowers surface temperature of fruit before cold storage

**Rinsing is not the same as washing:**
- Rinsing alone (brief pass of water) is insufficient as the primary cleaning step for heavily soiled or textured fruit
- Rinsing is the finish step after mechanical action (rubbing, brushing) has been applied

> **Common Mistake:** Running fruit briefly under water and calling it "washed." Effective washing requires mechanical action — rubbing with hands, a cloth, or a brush — combined with running water.

---

### 4. Paring

**Definition:** The removal of outer skin, rind, peel, or inedible portions of fruit using a knife or peeler.

**Food safety concern — paring sequence:**

The order of operations matters:

| Order | Safety Outcome |
|---|---|
| **Wash → Pare** (correct) | Surface pathogens reduced before knife contacts fruit; knife does not carry contamination to flesh |
| **Pare → Wash** (wrong) | Knife carries surface bacteria to cut surfaces; washing cut surfaces is less effective and may cause waterlogging |

Always wash before paring, not after.

**Paring techniques by fruit:**

| Fruit | Tool | Technique |
|---|---|---|
| **Apple, pear** | Peeler or paring knife | Peeler: top to bottom following contour; Knife: angled strokes; remove calyx and stem |
| **Citrus** | Chef's knife | Cut top/bottom flat; follow curve of fruit from top to bottom; remove all pith |
| **Pineapple** | Chef's knife | Remove crown and base; stand upright; remove rind in strips top to bottom; dig out eyes with paring knife tip |
| **Kiwi** | Paring knife or spoon | Trim ends; insert spoon between skin and flesh; rotate to remove in one piece |
| **Mango** | Chef's knife + paring knife | Slice cheeks off either side of pit; score flesh; invert; or peel first with knife and slice |
| **Peach/nectarine** | Paring knife | Blanch in boiling water 30–60 sec, ice bath → skin slips off (for commercial volume) |
| **Papaya** | Chef's knife | Halve; scoop seeds; peel with knife or peeler |
| **Avocado** | Chef's knife | Halve lengthwise; twist; remove pit with spoon (not knife — knife-in-pit is a documented hand injury) |

**Avocado pit removal — safety note:**

> **⚠️ OH&S Warning:** The "avocado hand" injury — stabbing the palm while trying to remove the pit with a knife — is one of the most common knife injuries reported in kitchens. The correct method is to scoop the pit out with a spoon, or to tap the knife heel firmly into the pit and twist. Never hold the avocado in the palm and stab toward it with the knife tip.

**Enzymatic browning after paring:**

When the flesh of cut fruit is exposed to oxygen, the enzyme polyphenol oxidase (PPO) reacts with phenolic compounds in the fruit to produce brown pigments (melanins). This reaction is rapid in:
- Apples and pears (moderate–fast)
- Avocados (fast)
- Peaches and bananas (moderate)
- Artichokes (fast — though a vegetable, same chemistry)

**Prevention of enzymatic browning:**

| Method | How It Works | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| **Acidulated water** (lemon juice, citric acid, ascorbic acid in cold water) | Acid lowers pH, inhibiting PPO enzyme activity | Apples, pears, avocado, peaches, bananas |
| **Ascorbic acid (vitamin C)** | Antioxidant that is preferentially oxidized instead of phenolic compounds | Commercial anti-browning agents; lemon juice |
| **Cold temperature** | Slows enzyme reaction rate | Refrigerate immediately after cutting |
| **Airtight wrapping** | Excludes oxygen; PPO requires O₂ | Wrap cut avocado tightly with film directly on surface |
| **Blanching** | Heat denatures PPO enzyme completely | Peaches, when peeling for large batches |

> **Red Seal Note:** PPO inhibition via acid is a classical technique. Know the mechanism: acid lowers pH → PPO optimal pH is 5–7 → lower pH slows/stops reaction. Lemon juice does double duty: the ascorbic acid acts as an antioxidant AND the citric acid lowers pH.

## Jurisdictional Regulations

### WHMIS (Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System)

**Relevance to fruit cleaning:**
- Commercial produce washes (if used) are chemicals requiring WHMIS compliance
- All cleaning agents used on surfaces where fruit will rest (cutting boards, sinks) must have a current Safety Data Sheet (SDS) accessible to workers
- Workers must be trained on safe use, storage, and disposal of any cleaning chemicals used near food
- Produce sanitizing solutions (if used commercially — e.g., chlorinated water for produce sanitizing) must be at correct concentration and must be rinsed off before food contact

> **Regulatory Requirement:** WHMIS is federal legislation. SDS for every hazardous product must be accessible within the workplace. Training is mandatory for all workers who use or may be exposed to hazardous products.

---

### HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points)

**Washing fruit as a HACCP control:**
- In HACCP-based food safety plans, produce washing may be identified as a **Prerequisite Program (PRP)** or **Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP)** rather than a Critical Control Point (CCP) — because washing does not eliminate pathogens to a measurable safe level (no validated kill step)
- If a produce wash or sanitizer is part of the HACCP plan, then concentration, contact time, and temperature must be monitored and logged as CCPs
- Temperature logs for refrigerated fruit storage are CCP monitoring records and are legal documents

**What this means in practice:**
- Washing reduces the pathogen load but does not guarantee elimination
- The overall food safety system (supplier controls, temperature control, personal hygiene, cleaning/sanitation of surfaces) must all work together
- Do not rely on washing alone if there are known supply-chain contamination risks (outbreak advisories, recalls)

---

### OH&S (Occupational Health and Safety)

**Fruit cleaning-specific OH&S considerations:**

| Hazard | Risk | Control |
|---|---|---|
| Knife injury during paring | Lacerations to fingers, palm, wrist | Sharp knives; cut-resistant gloves; stable cutting surface; correct grip |
| Wet floors | Slips and falls | Anti-slip mats; non-slip footwear; immediate cleanup of spills |
| Chemical exposure | Skin/eye irritation from produce sanitizers | Gloves; eyewash station access; SDS training |
| Repetitive strain | Wrist/shoulder strain from high-volume paring | Ergonomic tool selection; rotation of tasks; breaks |
| Latex allergy | Anaphylaxis in sensitized workers | Nitrile gloves only; latex-free policy |

---

### Public Health Regulations (Provincial)

**Core requirements under provincial food premises regulations:**
- All water used for washing produce must be potable (drinking water quality)
- Food contact surfaces (sinks, cutting boards, knives) used for produce must be clean and sanitized
- Cleaned fruit stored in the refrigerator must be covered, labelled with content and date, and stored at ≤4°C
- Pre-cut fruit intended for ready-to-eat service must not come into contact with raw meat, poultry, or seafood
- Workers with gastrointestinal illness (vomiting, diarrhea) must not handle ready-to-eat food — fruit that has been pared and cut is ready-to-eat

## Quality Risks During Cleaning and How to Avoid Them

### 1. Waterlogging (Over-Soaking)

**What happens:** Osmosis allows water to enter fruit cells through the skin. This dilutes flavour, softens texture, and creates conditions for mold growth (excess moisture accelerates decay).

**Most vulnerable fruits:** Strawberries, raspberries, blueberries, blackberries, figs, ripe peaches.

**Prevention:**
- Never soak delicate berries — rinse only, immediately before use
- Limit washing time for stone fruits to under 30 seconds
- Do not soak any fruit in standing water for extended periods
- Drain and dry fruit promptly after washing

---

### 2. Mechanical Damage (Bruising)

**What happens:** Physical impact during washing ruptures fruit cells, releasing juice and activating enzymatic browning. Bruised areas become soft, discoloured, and grow mold faster.

**Most vulnerable fruits:** Ripe peaches, plums, figs, raspberries, blueberries.

**Prevention:**
- Handle gently; do not pile fruit in deep containers
- Use appropriate water pressure — do not use high-pressure spray on soft fruit
- Use colanders with wide holes so fruit can drain without being crushed
- Toss gently, not forcefully
- Use smaller batch sizes for delicate fruits

---

### 3. Enzymatic Browning (After Paring/Cutting)

Covered in paring section above. Apply acidulated water immediately after cutting browning-susceptible fruits.

**Standard acidulated water ratio:** 1 litre cold water + 15 mL (1 tbsp) fresh lemon juice or food-grade citric acid.

---

### 4. Flavour Dilution from Washing

**What happens:** Water-soluble flavour compounds (sugars, organic acids, volatile esters) leach from cut surfaces into wash water.

**Prevention:**
- Wash before cutting whenever possible — intact skin acts as a barrier
- Minimize time between washing and service
- Do not wash cut fruit except when absolutely necessary (e.g., rinsing off a soiled surface)

---

### 5. Chilling Injury During Cold Water Washing

**What happens:** Tropical and subtropical fruits (banana, mango, papaya, pineapple, citrus) are sensitive to temperatures below 10–13°C. Exposing them to very cold water (near 4°C) can cause chilling injury — surface pitting, internal discolouration, failure to ripen.

**Prevention:**
- Use cool (not ice-cold) water for tropical fruit washing
- Do not store unripe tropical fruit in refrigerators ≤4°C
- Wash and move to appropriate temperature storage promptly

---

### 6. Mold Transfer During Washing

**What happens:** A single moldy piece of fruit in a wash batch can contaminate others through the water.

**Prevention:**
- Inspect all fruit before washing; remove and discard moldy pieces
- Never wash damaged or moldy fruit alongside sound fruit
- Change wash water if contamination is detected
- Sanitize sink between batches

## Secondary Storage: Principles and Practice

### Why Storage Conditions Change After Cleaning

- Washing removes natural protective waxes (bloom on grapes, epicuticular wax on plums)
- Cut surfaces are exposed to oxygen and bacteria
- Moisture on washed fruit accelerates spoilage if not managed
- Pre-cut fruit is ready-to-eat and must be protected from cross-contamination

---

## Secondary Storage by Fruit Type

| Fruit Type | Storage Temperature | Container | Maximum Shelf Life (Post-Cleaning) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Washed whole apples/pears** | 1–4°C | Covered container or bag | 1–2 weeks | Remove any damaged fruit; check daily |
| **Washed citrus (whole)** | 4–7°C | Open or covered container | 1–2 weeks | Washed citrus stores well; still track |
| **Washed grapes** | 1–4°C | Perforated container on absorbent | 3–5 days | Moisture management critical |
| **Washed berries (strawberries)** | 1–4°C | Single layer on absorbent paper; covered | 1–2 days | Wash ONLY immediately before use; do not pre-wash for storage |
| **Washed berries (blueberries)** | 1–4°C | Covered container, absorbent liner | 2–3 days (washed) | Pre-washed storage is suboptimal; best to wash before service |
| **Cut melon** | 1–4°C | Airtight container | 3–5 days | Mandatory: cover and refrigerate; cut melon is TCS food |
| **Cut stone fruit** | 1–4°C | Airtight container with acidulated water | 1–2 days | Acidulated water prevents browning; drain before serving |
| **Pared kiwi** | 1–4°C | Airtight container | 1–2 days | |
| **Cut pineapple** | 1–4°C | Airtight container | 3–5 days | Juice accumulates; drain and cover |
| **Cut mango** | 1–4°C | Airtight container | 2–3 days | |
| **Pared/cut banana** | 15–18°C (room temp) | Acidulated water or plastic wrap | Hours only | Bananas do not refrigerate well; serve day-of |
| **Cut avocado** | 1–4°C | Pit in, plastic wrap pressed to surface | 1–2 days | Pit slows browning; wrap directly on cut surface |
| **Whole cleaned figs** | 1–4°C | Single layer; covered | 2–3 days | Extremely delicate; do not stack |

## Temperature-Controlled Storage (TCS) and Fruit

**Temperature-Controlled for Safety (TCS) foods** are those capable of supporting rapid bacterial growth if not held at safe temperatures.

Cut fruit is a TCS food because:
- High water activity (aw > 0.85) — sufficient moisture for bacterial growth
- Neutral-to-slightly-acidic pH (most cut fruit is pH 3.5–6.0 — dangerous range for pathogens is 4.6–7.5)
- No intact skin barrier

> **Regulatory Requirement:** Cut melon, cut tomatoes, and cut leafy greens are specifically identified as TCS foods in most Canadian provincial food premises regulations. Cut melon must be held at ≤4°C or ≥60°C. Room-temperature cut melon service must not exceed 2 hours (cumulative time in the Temperature Danger Zone of 4°C–60°C).

**Time Danger Zone (TDZ) rules for cut fruit:**
- TDZ: 4°C–60°C
- Maximum cumulative time in TDZ for TCS foods: **2 hours**
- After 2 hours, discard or assess risk (higher-risk populations = discard; lower-risk = operator judgment based on temperature)
- Cut melon at a buffet: must be kept on ice ≤4°C OR time-stamped and discarded after 2 hours

---

## Labelling and Storage Documentation

All stored cleaned fruit must be labelled with:
- **Contents** (specific fruit type and form — e.g., "cut cantaloupe")
- **Date and time prepared**
- **Use-by date and time** (based on product type and maximum shelf life)
- **Initials of preparer** (common industry practice; required in HACCP-managed kitchens)

> **HACCP Note:** Labelling cleaned and cut ready-to-eat foods is part of Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) and is required in HACCP plans. In the event of a foodborne illness investigation, dated labels allow traceability.

## Common Mistakes — Fruit Cleaning

| Mistake | What Goes Wrong | Correct Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Not washing melon before cutting | *Listeria*/pathogens carried into flesh by knife | Always brush melon under running water before cutting |
| Soaking berries | Waterlogging, flavour loss, mold growth | Rinse in colander immediately before use; never soak |
| Washing berries ahead of service | Pre-wetted berries deteriorate in hours | Wash berries only moments before plating |
| Using non-potable water | Regulatory violation; pathogen contamination | Use potable water only for all produce contact |
| Washing then paring (wrong order) | Knife transfers surface bacteria to flesh | Always wash first, then pare |
| Paring avocado pit with stabbing motion | Serious hand laceration injury | Scoop pit with spoon; or tap knife heel firmly |
| Storing cut melon at room temperature | Rapid bacterial growth; TCS violation | Refrigerate at ≤4°C within 2 hours maximum |
| Not labelling cleaned fruit | Traceability failure; HACCP non-compliance | Label all stored produce: content, date, time, initials |
| Leaving brush bristle-down in water | Bacteria colonize bristles; cross-contamination | Sanitize after each use; air-dry bristles up |
| Removing strawberry hulls before washing | Water enters flesh through hull hole; waterlogging | Wash with hulls intact; hull after washing |
| Using same knife for rind and flesh without sanitizing | Cross-contamination | Sanitize knife between cutting rind and interior |
| Using dull knife for paring | Bruising, injury risk, poor cut quality | Maintain sharp knives; sharpen or hone before service |

## Sink Sanitation Protocol for Fruit Washing

A contaminated sink is a contamination source. Follow this protocol:

1. **Remove food debris** from sink drain and surfaces
2. **Wash** sink with hot water and detergent
3. **Rinse** thoroughly to remove all detergent residue
4. **Sanitize** using approved sanitizer at correct dilution (quaternary ammonium or chlorine-based per local health authority guidelines)
5. **Air dry** or rinse with potable water before produce contact
6. **Verify** sanitizer concentration with test strips (common industry practice; required in HACCP plans)

> **Never wash produce in a sink that has been used for raw meat, poultry, or seafood without completing the full wash–rinse–sanitize cycle.**

## 12 Q&A Pairs — Red Seal Exam Preparation

**Q1: Why must cantaloupe be scrubbed with a brush before cutting, even though the rind is not eaten?**
A: *Listeria monocytogenes* and other pathogens can be embedded in the rough netting of the rind. When a knife cuts through unwashed rind, it carries surface bacteria directly into the flesh. The flesh is a ready-to-eat food — no further kill step occurs before consumption.

**Q2: What is the correct order of operations when paring fruit?**
A: Wash first, then pare. Washing before paring reduces surface pathogens before the knife contacts the fruit. Paring before washing allows the knife to carry surface bacteria to the exposed flesh.

**Q3: Why should berries never be soaked in water before use?**
A: Soaking causes waterlogging — water enters through the delicate skin, diluting flavour and softening texture. Moisture on berries also accelerates mold growth. Berries should be rinsed briefly in a colander immediately before service.

**Q4: What type of gloves should be used when cleaning fruit in a commercial kitchen, and why?**
A: Nitrile gloves are preferred. Latex gloves should be avoided because latex is a documented allergen; latex proteins can transfer to food and trigger reactions in sensitized individuals. Nitrile provides equivalent protection without the allergen risk.

**Q5: What is acidulated water, and what is its purpose in fruit cleaning and preparation?**
A: Acidulated water is cold water with an acid added — typically lemon juice (citric acid + ascorbic acid) or commercial citric acid. It inhibits the PPO (polyphenol oxidase) enzyme responsible for enzymatic browning in cut fruit (apples, pears, avocado, peaches). The acid lowers pH below PPO's optimal range, slowing the browning reaction.

**Q6: What is the maximum time cut melon can remain in the Temperature Danger Zone?**
A: Two hours — the maximum cumulative time any TCS food, including cut melon, can remain between 4°C and 60°C. After this point, the food must be discarded or have been kept under proper temperature control.

**Q7: Why is potable water required for washing fruit? What regulation covers this?**
A: Non-potable water may contain pathogens (*E. coli*, *Cryptosporidium*, Hepatitis A virus) that would be deposited directly on food. Potable water is required under provincial food premises regulations. This is a regulatory requirement, not merely a best practice.

**Q8: Why is it important to sanitize produce brushes after each batch of fruit?**
A: Produce brushes accumulate bacteria, soil, and organic matter in their bristles during use. A contaminated brush used on the next batch of fruit becomes a cross-contamination vector. Washing, sanitizing, and air-drying the brush between batches prevents this.

**Q9: What is the OH&S concern with dull knives during fruit paring?**
A: A dull knife requires more force, which increases the risk of the blade slipping and causing lacerations. Maintaining sharp knives is an OH&S obligation under provincial occupational health and safety legislation. Additionally, dull knives crush fruit cells rather than cutting cleanly, accelerating bruising and browning.

**Q10: How should hulled strawberries be stored after washing?**
A: Strawberries should be hulled after washing (not before), drained, placed in a single layer on absorbent paper in a covered container, labelled with content and date, and refrigerated at ≤4°C. They should be used within 1–2 days. Ideally, strawberries are washed immediately before service rather than pre-washed for storage.

**Q11: What is the difference between a CCP and a PRP/GMP in the context of fruit washing?**
A: A Critical Control Point (CCP) is a step where a specific hazard can be controlled to a measurable safe level (e.g., cooking to 74°C eliminates pathogens). A Prerequisite Program (PRP) or Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) controls general conditions — produce washing is typically a PRP because washing reduces pathogen load but does not guarantee elimination to a quantifiable safe threshold. If a validated produce sanitizer is used, that step might qualify as a CCP with monitoring requirements.

**Q12: What are the four cleaning procedures in the Range of Variables, and when is each applied?**
A: (1) **Washing** — rinsing with cold running potable water, combined with rubbing or agitation; the primary cleaning step for all fruit. (2) **Brushing** — mechanical scrubbing with a produce brush; used for textured-rind fruits (melons, firm citrus, root fruits). (3) **Rinsing** — the final pass of clean running water after washing or paring to remove loosened debris. (4) **Paring** — removal of skin, rind, or peel using a knife or peeler; applied after washing when the exterior is not consumed or is damaged.
