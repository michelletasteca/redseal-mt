## Why "Non-Reactive" Is a Sauce-Maker's First Rule

Sauces concentrate flavour. They also concentrate **chemistry**. A pot that quietly reacts with an acidic ingredient — wine, vinegar, citrus, tomato — can:
- **Discolour** the sauce (a wine reduction in an unlined copper pot turns grey-blue; an egg cream in aluminum goes greenish).
- **Add metallic off-flavours** that no amount of seasoning fixes.
- **Pit and corrode** the cookware itself over time.
- **Release trace metals** (aluminum, iron, copper) into the food. While levels are usually well below toxicity thresholds, **flavour and visual quality suffer first**, and over time this is a food-safety consideration.

For sauce work specifically, the apprentice must understand which materials are **non-reactive** and pick them deliberately. This subtask centres on that choice and on the careful measurement and preparation of every component before the sauce is built.

## Reactive vs Non-Reactive Materials — The Chemistry

| Material | Reactivity | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Stainless steel** | **Non-reactive** | A passive chromium-oxide layer protects the surface from acid and alkaline foods |
| **Glass (borosilicate, tempered)** | **Non-reactive** | Inert silica-based; safe with all foods; brittle when shocked |
| **Glazed ceramic / porcelain** | **Non-reactive** (when intact) | Glaze isolates food from clay; check for cracks/chips |
| **Enameled cast iron** | **Non-reactive** (when intact) | Enamel coating; protects iron beneath |
| **Tin-lined copper** | **Non-reactive** (when lining intact) | Classical sauce-makers' choice; tin is inert; once tin wears, copper reactivity returns |
| **Silver-lined copper** | **Non-reactive** | Specialty; very expensive |
| **Food-grade silicone** | **Non-reactive** | Inert across cooking temperatures; flexible |
| **BPA-free food-grade plastic** | **Non-reactive at cool temperatures** | Some plastics leach with heat, acid, or fat; never use for hot reductions |
| **Aluminum** | **Reactive with acid and alkali** | Dissolves slightly in acidic foods (tomato, wine, citrus); discolours sauces; metallic taste |
| **Unlined copper** | **Reactive** | Copper ions dissolve into acid; turns wine sauces grey-blue; toxicity concern with prolonged use |
| **Cast iron (bare)** | **Reactive with acid** | Iron dissolves into long-cooked acidic sauces; metallic note; can be tolerated briefly |
| **Carbon steel (bare)** | **Reactive** | Similar to cast iron |
| **Reactive non-stick (older Teflon at high heat)** | **Risk of breakdown** | Avoid over-heating; do not use with metal whisks |

> **[Best Practice]** Use **non-reactive cookware for any sauce containing wine, vinegar, citrus, tomato, dairy, egg, or other reactive ingredients.**

## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### E-16.02.01P — Select and Use Non-Reactive Processing Tools and Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** select and use non-reactive processing tools and equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** non-reactive processing tools and equipment are selected and used according to ingredients being used.

#### Tools and equipment (Range of Variables, expanded)

##### Double boilers (bain-marie)

| Purpose | Why |
|---|---|
| **Hollandaise / béarnaise** | Gentle, indirect heat — egg yolks don't scramble |
| **Chocolate melting / chocolate sauce** | Avoid scorching; controlled temperature |
| **Caramel finishing** | After initial caramelization, holding warm |
| **Custards (crème anglaise)** | Gentle thickening of egg-yolk-and-cream mixtures |
| **Holding delicate sauces** | Service-time temperature stability |

**Construction:** outer pot of simmering water + inner bowl (stainless or glass) that fits without touching the water; alternatively, a **purpose-built double-boiler pot set** (the inner pot has a stepped bottom for the outer water).

**Why double boilers work:** the water bath caps the maximum temperature at 100 °C (boiling water) or below — protein-coagulation temperatures (eggs at 65–70 °C; cream at 80+ °C) can be controlled by adjusting the water heat and timing.

> **Operational tip:** the inner bowl should fit snugly without bottom contact. **The water in the lower pan should NEVER touch the upper bowl.** Steam transfer + radiant heat is the cooking mechanism, not direct water contact.

##### Saucepans

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **Stainless-steel sauteuse / sauté pan** (broad, flared sides, low-medium height) | Roux building; pan sauces; reductions in batches |
| **Stainless-steel saucier** (rounded bottom, no corner) | Sauce reduction; whisking corners (no food trapped); classical French choice |
| **Stainless-steel saucepan** (straight-sided, medium-high) | General sauce work; melting; building béchamel and velouté |
| **Heavy-bottomed (sandwich-clad stainless + aluminum core + stainless outer)** | Even heat; prevents scorching at the pan bottom |
| **Wide shallow rondeau** | Large reductions — maximum evaporation surface |
| **Non-stick (lower heat applications only)** | Delicate sauces, fish-pan sauces where sticking is concern; **not for high heat or metal whisks** |

> **[Best Practice]** Choose **heavy-bottomed stainless** as the default workhorse for sauce work. Even, controllable heat; non-reactive; durable; metal-utensil safe.

##### Strainers

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **China cap (chinois coarse perforated)** | First-pass straining of bulk sauces; removing solids |
| **Fine-mesh chinois** | Refining cream sauces; removing fine particles |
| **Cheesecloth-lined chinois** | Ultra-refined sauces (consommé, refined fish sauces, premium hollandaise — though hollandaise rarely strained) |
| **Fine sieve / tamis** | Sieving purées; dusting flour; refining seed-laden coulis (e.g., raspberry) |
| **Coffee filter / paper filter** | Specialty ultra-clear sauces (rare) |

##### Stainless-steel bowls

| Size | Use |
|---|---|
| **Small (≤ 500 mL)** | Slurry mixing; egg-yolk-cream blending for liaison; small mise en place |
| **Medium (1–2 L)** | Liaison tempering; mixing roux + liquid; egg yolk + acid for hollandaise |
| **Large (3–4 L)** | Tempering large batches; whisking hollandaise sabayon-style over a bain-marie |
| **Extra-large (≥ 6 L)** | Large-batch mise en place; production mixing |

**Why stainless:** non-reactive with acid, eggs, and dairy; doesn't retain odours; dishwasher-safe; durable.

##### Plastics (BPA-free)

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **BPA-free food-grade plastic containers (cambros)** | Cold mise en place storage; finished sauce storage in refrigeration |
| **Plastic mixing bowls (cool only)** | Non-thermal mixing tasks |
| **Plastic squeeze bottles** | Sauce service dispensing; plating drizzles |
| **Plastic measuring cups** | Volume measurements (more durable than glass for daily use) |

> ⚠️ **Don't use plastic for hot sauce work.** Heat + acid + plastic can leach plasticizers; flavour is affected. Use plastic for **cool storage and service**, not for cooking.

##### Silicone

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **Silicone spatulas (heat-resistant to ~ 230 °C / 450 °F)** | Stirring and scraping sauces in hot pans; folding cream |
| **Silicone whisks** | Sauce mixing — safe on non-stick surfaces |
| **Silicone mats / parchment alternative** | Cooling caramelized sugar; tempering chocolate; rolling compound butter |
| **Silicone moulds** | Compound-butter shaping (logs, disks) |
| **Silicone basting brushes** | Sauce application |

> **Silicone advantages:** flexible, non-stick, doesn't impart flavour, heat-tolerant, dishwasher-safe.

##### Whisks for sauce work

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **Balloon whisk** | Hollandaise, béarnaise (sabayon-style emulsification); roux + liquid integration; cream finishing |
| **French / narrow whisk** | Sustained whisking in saucier (rounded-bottom pots); pan sauces |
| **Flat / saucier whisk** | Wide-pan sauces; deglazing — reaches pan edges |
| **Coiled / spring whisk** | Small batches and slurry mixing |

##### Additional commonly used items

| Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| **Digital scale (g, kg)** | Weighing fats, flours, herbs, spices |
| **Graduated measuring cups (heat-resistant)** | Liquid measurements |
| **Thermometer (probe)** | Verifying hollandaise yolk temperature; reduction temperatures; service hold |
| **Heat-resistant gloves and pads (kept dry)** | Pot handling — burn prevention |
| **Tasting spoons (sanitized, single-use)** | Quality control; never re-dip |
| **Wooden spoons** | Stirring roux; non-scratching on coated pans |
| **Microplane / fine grater** | Citrus zest, hard cheese (Mornay, Parmesan finishing), spices (nutmeg) |
| **Mortar and pestle** | Spice pastes for curries, mole, romesco |
| **Mandoline** | Fine slicing of shallots, garlic for reductions (cut-resistant glove + food guard) |
| **Tongs, ladles, slotted spoons** | Handling solids in sauces |
| **Heat-resistant rubber spatulas** | Folding cream, scraping bowls |
| **Cheesecloth** | Final straining; bouquet garni for reduction aromatics |
| **Butcher's twine** | Tying bouquet garni; trussing aromatics in reductions |

#### Tool selection logic — match tool to sauce

| Sauce family | Critical tools |
|---|---|
| **Espagnole / demi-glace** | Heavy-bottomed stainless stockpot or wide rondeau; china cap → fine chinois; ladle; skimmer |
| **Velouté / béchamel** | Stainless saucier or sauteuse; balloon whisk; fine chinois; probe thermometer |
| **Sauce tomate** | Heavy-bottomed stainless pot; food mill or food processor; fine sieve |
| **Hollandaise / béarnaise** | Stainless bowl + bain-marie / double boiler; balloon whisk; probe thermometer; clarified-butter dispenser (small saucepan) |
| **Beurre blanc** | Heavy-bottomed saucepan (stainless); balloon whisk; cold-cubed butter in a stainless bowl |
| **Pan gravy / jus** | Roasting pan (deglaze in-pan); strainer; saucepan for final reduction |
| **Curries** | Heavy-bottomed pot; mortar and pestle (for spice paste); food processor |
| **Soy-based sauces** | Stainless saucepan; whisk; measuring cups |
| **Coulis** | Stainless saucepan (for cooking, if needed); stand blender or immersion blender; fine sieve |
| **Chocolate sauce** | Double boiler; silicone spatula; thermometer for tempering work |
| **Caramel sauce** | Heavy-bottomed light-coloured stainless saucepan (visibility of caramel colour); silicone whisk or heat-resistant spatula |
| **Compound butter** | Stand mixer with paddle (or hand mixing) + parchment for rolling logs |

### E-16.02.02P — Measure and Prepare Ingredients According to Recipe Specifications

**Performance Criteria:** measure and prepare ingredients according to recipe specifications
**Evidence of Attainment:** ingredients are measured and prepared according to recipe specifications.

#### Measurement discipline

##### Weight vs volume (recap)

| Ingredient | Best measure |
|---|---|
| **Butter, flour, dry seasoning, mirepoix** | Weight (g) |
| **Stock, milk, cream, wine, vinegar** | Volume (mL, L) — weight equivalent fine |
| **Egg yolks** | Count (3 yolks, etc.); standardize on Grade A medium/large for consistency |
| **Salt** | Volume (tsp) acceptable for small amounts; weight (g) for precision and HACCP |
| **Whole spices** | Volume (tsp) acceptable; weight (g) for batch consistency |
| **Fresh herbs (chiffonade or chopped)** | Volume (tbsp) at minimum; weight (g) better |
| **Wine for deglazing** | Volume (mL) |
| **Cream for liaison** | Volume (mL) — easier to measure than weight at small volumes |

##### Why weight is the professional default

- **Consistency:** 250 g of butter is 250 g of butter, every batch.
- **Scalability:** a 4 × multiplier on weights produces a 4 × yield reliably.
- **HACCP-friendly:** weight measurements feed cleanly into PCP records.

##### Tools for measurement

- **Digital benchtop scale** with tare and at least 1 g precision.
- **Graduated heat-resistant measuring cups** for liquids.
- **Standardized measuring spoons** (calibrated tsp / tbsp / ml sets).
- **Recipe card / printed spec sheet** at the station.

#### Sauce-specific ingredient preparation

##### Bones and stock-based work (espagnole, demi-glace, jus)
- **Confirm stock quality** at retrieval — wobbly gel, clean smell, in-date.
- **Strain stock** through a fine chinois before sauce work if not already done.
- **Weigh out** quantities by the recipe.

##### Roux ingredients (béchamel, velouté, espagnole, sauce tomate)
- **Weigh butter and flour separately** — equal parts by weight.
- **Soften butter slightly** if cold and recipe calls for it.
- **Sift flour** to break up lumps (optional but useful in classical practice).
- **Position pots and whisks** before melting butter.

##### Egg yolks (hollandaise, béarnaise, liaison)
- **Separate eggs carefully** — yolk in one bowl, whites in another (whites are great for consommé clarification, mousse, meringue, etc.).
- **Yolks should be intact** — broken yolk diluted with white is fine for some uses but not ideal for hollandaise.
- **Measure cream / acid** for the recipe; weigh or use volume.
- **Pasteurized yolks**: open container, measure as required; for high-risk populations.

##### Clarified butter (hollandaise, beurre noisette base)
- **Melt unsalted butter** gently in a saucepan.
- **Skim foam** that rises from the top (milk proteins).
- **Decant clear yellow fat** into a separate container, leaving the milky water at the bottom.
- **Hold warm** for hollandaise application (around 50–60 °C — warm enough to flow, not so hot it cooks the yolks).

##### Tomato products (sauce tomate, salsa, curries)
- **Canned tomatoes:** open, drain if specified, crush or chop as recipe requires.
- **Fresh tomatoes for sauce tomate:** **concassée** technique — score X on bottom, blanch 30 sec, ice bath, peel slips off, halve, scoop seeds, dice.
- **Tomato paste:** weigh out; if using a small portion, freeze the rest in plastic-wrap-portioned rounds.

##### Wine (reductions, bordelaise, beurre blanc)
- **Open and measure** by volume.
- **Strain** if there are sediment crystals.
- **Reduce alone first** for sauce-quality work — drives off raw alcohol before integrating with stock or butter.

##### Vinegar (béarnaise reduction, gastrique)
- **Measure by volume** — small quantities.
- **Combine with wine** in reductions for béarnaise base.

##### Aromatics
- **Shallots, garlic:** peel and **mince finely** for reductions and beurre blanc. Avoid bitter green sprouts.
- **Onion:** dice as the recipe specifies; for sauce tomate, sweat is the typical first step.
- **Ginger:** peel with a spoon edge, slice, julienne, or microplane depending on the dish.
- **Lemongrass (for curries):** smash with the back of a knife, slice, or grate the tender base.
- **Fresh herbs (parsley, chervil, tarragon, dill):** wash gently, pat dry, **chop or chiffonade just before use** — oxidation darkens cut herbs fast.
- **Spices (whole vs ground):** whole spices toast and crush fresh for maximum aroma; ground spices weighed out.

##### Cheese (mornay, gratin sauces)
- **Grate fresh from the block** — pre-grated cheese often has anti-caking agents that affect melting.
- **Use Gruyère and Parmesan** for classical mornay; other cheeses for variants.
- **Measure by weight**.

##### Dried mushrooms (curries, demi enrichments)
- **Rehydrate in hot water** for 20–30 min.
- **Save the soaking liquid** — strain to remove grit and use as part of the sauce liquid.

##### Dried chiles (Mexican salsas, mole, harissa)
- **Toast briefly** in a dry pan for aroma.
- **Soak in hot water** for 20–30 min until pliable.
- **Strain and reserve soaking liquid** for flavour.

##### Compound butter ingredients
- **Soften butter** to room temperature (not melted).
- **Prepare flavouring** — chopped herbs, citrus zest (microplaned), anchovy paste, wine reduction (cooled to room temp), garlic confit.
- **Weigh out** by recipe ratio (~ 25–50 g flavouring per 225 g butter).

##### Beurre blanc ingredients
- **Cut cold butter into 1–2 cm cubes** and return to the fridge until use.
- **Reduce wine + vinegar + shallot** ahead of time; rest at room temperature or rewarm gently.
- **Heavy cream (optional in some beurre blanc recipes)** measured separately.

##### Chocolate (chocolate sauce, mole)
- **Chop chocolate finely** for even melting.
- **Measure by weight.**
- **For ganache-style sauce:** heavy cream measured and brought just below simmer separately.

##### Caramel sauce ingredients
- **Granulated sugar** weighed.
- **Heavy cream (≥ 33% MF)** measured; **room temperature, not cold** — cold cream into hot caramel seizes the sugar.
- **Cold butter** cubed (added after caramel + cream is integrated).
- **Salt** for salted caramel.

#### Sanitation during processing (carry-over from A-2.04, A-2.05)

- **Sanitize work surfaces, knives, boards, bowls** between proteins and between raw / RTE.
- **Colour-coded boards** — green for produce, yellow for poultry, blue for seafood, red for raw red meat, brown for cooked, white for dairy, purple for allergen-isolated.
- **Allergen-isolated tools** for celiac, severe-allergen orders.
- **Wash hands** between protein and produce; change gloves.
- **Sanitize whisks and probe thermometers** between uses.
- **Equipment lockout** for blade removal on processors, immersion blenders, etc.
- **Air-dry food-contact surfaces** before reassembly.

## 2. Knowledge Objectives — Deeper Dive

### E-16.02.01L — Sauces and Their Applications (Processing Lens)

#### Factors to consider when processing ingredients for sauces

| Factor | Processing implication |
|---|---|
| **Reactivity of ingredients** | Acidic + reactive cookware = off-flavours; use stainless, glass, ceramic, silicone |
| **Heat sensitivity** | Egg-based emulsions need bain-marie; cream needs gentle heat; chocolate needs double boiler |
| **Allergen-isolated workflow** | Dedicated tools and equipment for severe-allergen orders |
| **Texture target** | Strain through fine chinois for refined; food mill for rustic; food processor for puréed |
| **Cold-chain integrity** | Butter, cream, eggs, dairy held ≤ 4 °C until processing; once out, work quickly |
| **Pre-prep timing** | Mother sauces and demi-glace are pre-shift; pan sauces and hollandaise are à la minute |
| **Yield needs** | Scale ingredient weights linearly; verify pot capacity (⅔ to ¾ full) |
| **Service style** | Buffet service needs sauces that hold; à la carte allows à la minute |
| **Cost efficiency** | Trim management; weighing precisely avoids over-portioning expensive ingredients |

#### Ingredients and ratios used in sauces (carry-over from E-16.01)

| Sauce | Approximate ratio per litre of finished sauce |
|---|---|
| **Espagnole** | Brown stock 1 L; brown roux ~ 100 g (50 g butter + 50 g flour); mirepoix ~ 150 g; tomato 50–100 g |
| **Demi-glace** | Espagnole 1 L + brown stock 1 L, reduced 50% to 1 L finished |
| **Velouté** | White stock 1 L; blond roux ~ 80–100 g |
| **Béchamel** | Milk 1 L; white roux ~ 80–100 g |
| **Hollandaise** | 6 yolks + 250 g clarified butter + 50 mL acid — 1 batch ≈ 350 mL |
| **Béarnaise reduction** | 250 mL wine + 50 mL vinegar + 30 g shallot + 5 g peppercorn + tarragon stems → reduce to ~ 50 mL |
| **Beurre blanc** | 250 mL wine + 50 mL vinegar + 30 g shallot reduce to ~ 100 mL; mount with 250 g cold butter — 1 batch ≈ 350 mL |
| **Compound butter** | 225 g butter + 25–50 g flavouring |
| **Mornay** | Béchamel 1 L + 100 g Gruyère + 50 g Parmesan + 2 yolks |
| **Suprême** | Chicken velouté 1 L + 250 mL cream + reduction; sometimes mushroom + lemon |
| **Pan gravy** | Drippings + 30–50 g flour + 1 L stock |
| **Caramel sauce** | 250 g sugar + 200 mL heavy cream + 50 g butter + salt |
| **Chocolate ganache sauce** | 100 g chocolate + 100 mL cream (varied for thickness) |

#### Dietary requirements (carry-over from E-16.01)

| Need | Implication for processing |
|---|---|
| **Celiac / gluten-free** | Dedicated tools (no wheat-flour cross-contact); cornstarch slurry, rice flour roux, or vegetable-purée body |
| **Dairy-free** | Plant butter and plant cream; no béchamel or hollandaise; reduction-based sauces work; allergen-isolated tools |
| **Egg-free** | No hollandaise, béarnaise, liaison-finished sauces; substitute butter-emulsion or starch-thickened |
| **Vegan** | Vegetable stock; plant cream; plant butter; mushroom and miso umami; vegetable purée body |
| **Halal** | No pork (no salt pork in sauce tomate); no alcohol unless cooked off; certified protein chain |
| **Kosher** | No pork or shellfish; no meat-dairy mixing (classical demi + cream = non-kosher) |
| **Sulphite-sensitive** | Sulphite-free wine or non-alcoholic alternatives |
| **Sodium-restricted** | Unsalted stocks; finishing salt at table |
| **Allergen flags** | Check soy sauce for wheat (use tamari for celiac); check oyster sauce for shellfish; flag mustard, sesame, tree nuts, peanut in international sauces |

### E-16.02.02L — Procedures to Process Ingredients for Sauces

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to process ingredients for sauces.

#### A repeatable processing procedure

1. **Read the recipe end-to-end** before gathering anything.
2. **Identify reactive vs non-reactive needs** — match cookware to ingredient profile.
3. **Stage tools and equipment** in the order they'll be used.
4. **Pre-weigh and measure** all ingredients into clearly labelled containers.
5. **Pre-prepare aromatics** — mince shallots, peel garlic, chop herbs (last for herbs to preserve freshness).
6. **Clarify butter** if making hollandaise.
7. **Reduce wine** if making bordelaise, beurre blanc, béarnaise.
8. **Verify cold-chain integrity** for dairy and eggs.
9. **Sanitize cutting boards and knives** between proteins and between proteins / produce.
10. **Document** mise en place complete, allergen flags noted, dietary requirements verified.

#### Mise en place sequence — example for hollandaise

| Order | Item | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Clarify butter (early, while still cool) | Allow time to skim and settle |
| 2 | Set up double boiler / bain-marie | Heat water to gentle simmer |
| 3 | Separate egg yolks into stainless bowl | Discard whites separately |
| 4 | Measure lemon juice + white wine | For acid component |
| 5 | Position whisk, ladle, tasting spoon, probe thermometer | All within reach |
| 6 | Pre-warm serving bowl or pitcher | Maintain temperature post-cook |
| 7 | Have warm water and salt ready for emergency emulsion rescue | Insurance |
| 8 | Sanitized tasting spoons set out | Single-use per taste |

#### Sanitation during processing

- **Wash hands** between every category of ingredient.
- **Sanitize tools** between proteins, between raw / RTE, between allergens.
- **Use colour-coded boards** rigorously.
- **Verify thermometer calibration** before use.
- **Equipment lockout** for blade removal (immersion blender, food processor — carry-over from A-2.05).

## 3. Food-Safety Considerations During Processing

### Egg handling
- **Crack eggs into a separate bowl first**, never directly into the sauce — a bad egg ruins the batch.
- **Hold separated yolks ≤ 4 °C** if not used immediately.
- **Use pasteurized yolks** for high-risk populations.
- **Sanitize whisks and bowls** between batches.

### Dairy cold-chain
- **Cream and milk ≤ 4 °C** at retrieval; once opened, date and use within shelf life.
- **Bring small amounts to room temperature** just before use to avoid shocking emulsions (cold cream into hot caramel = seized sugar).

### Stock and reduction freshness
- **Verify** stock freshness at retrieval — wobbly gel, clean smell, in-date.
- **Cool finished reductions** per A-2.02 if not for immediate use.

### Cross-contact prevention
- **Allergen-isolated tools** for celiac, peanut, tree nut, sesame, shellfish, soy orders.
- **Separate prep zones** where possible.
- **Sanitize between any allergen-protein transition.**

### Hot-liquid blender safety
- **Hot-liquid blender protocol:** ≤ ⅔ full, vent lid, towel over, pulse.
- For sauce work, the immersion blender is often the better choice — submerge before turning on; lift the cup over the pan to break the vacuum, then off.

## 4. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Setting up to make hollandaise for 12 portions
1. **Tools selected:**
   - Stainless bowl that fits over a saucepan (double boiler).
   - Saucepan with water for the bain-marie.
   - Balloon whisk.
   - Probe thermometer.
   - Small saucepan for clarifying butter.
   - Lemon juicer.
2. **Ingredients staged:**
   - 6 large egg yolks (cracked one at a time into a small bowl first to verify quality, then transferred).
   - 250 g unsalted butter, clarified and held warm.
   - 50 mL fresh lemon juice (strained for seeds).
   - 2 g fine sea salt, pinch cayenne.
3. **Verify** allergen flags (no celiac issue — hollandaise is naturally GF; allergens: egg + dairy).
4. **Sanitize whisk and bowl** before starting.
5. **Begin sabayon-style cook** (the actual cook belongs to E-16.03).

### Scenario 2 — Pre-shift production of 4 L of demi-glace
1. **Tools selected:**
   - Heavy-bottomed wide rondeau (maximum evaporation surface).
   - China cap and fine chinois.
   - Skimmer and ladle.
   - Probe thermometer.
2. **Ingredients staged:**
   - 4 L espagnole (verified wobbly-gel test, clean smell).
   - 4 L additional brown veal stock (same checks).
   - Optional: red wine reduction or Madeira to fortify.
3. **Schedule:** ~ 2–3 h reduction to 50%.
4. **Plan cooling** per A-2.02 if not for immediate service.

### Scenario 3 — Beurre blanc setup for fish service
1. **Tools selected:**
   - Heavy-bottomed stainless saucepan (small to medium).
   - Balloon whisk.
   - Stainless bowl for cubed butter.
2. **Ingredients staged:**
   - 250 mL quality dry white wine (Muscadet, Sancerre, or similar).
   - 50 mL white wine vinegar.
   - 30 g finely-minced shallot.
   - 250 g cold unsalted butter, cubed into 1–2 cm pieces.
   - Salt, white pepper.
   - Optional: 50 mL heavy cream (modern beurre nantais variant).
3. **Pre-reduce** wine + vinegar + shallot to ~ 100 mL syrupy reduction. Hold warm in a separate small saucepan.
4. **Cube butter and refrigerate** until needed.
5. **Hollandaise / beurre blanc cooking is à la minute** in E-16.03.

### Scenario 4 — Vegan velouté processing
1. **Tools selected:**
   - Allergen-isolated tools (dedicated whisk, ladle, pot).
   - Stainless saucepan.
2. **Ingredients:**
   - 1 L vegetable stock.
   - 100 g vegan butter or olive oil.
   - 100 g all-purpose flour (or rice flour for celiac substitution).
   - Plant cream finishing (oat or cashew).
3. **Verify** vegetable stock allergen statement (some commercial bases contain soy or yeast extract with hidden allergens).
4. **Process** with the same procedure as classical velouté but with substitutions.

### Scenario 5 — Caramel sauce setup
1. **Tools selected:**
   - Heavy-bottomed light-coloured stainless saucepan (visibility of caramel colour change).
   - Heat-resistant silicone whisk and spatula.
   - Heat-resistant gloves (sugar burns are severe).
   - Probe thermometer (optional but useful — target ~ 175–180 °C for amber caramel).
2. **Ingredients staged:**
   - 250 g granulated sugar.
   - 200 mL heavy cream **at room temperature** (cold cream causes seizure / steam burns).
   - 50 g cold butter, cubed.
   - 2 g fine sea salt (for salted caramel).
3. **Safety:** caramel reaches ~ 180 °C — far hotter than boiling water. Wear gloves; never lean over the pan; have a sink with cold water nearby for emergency.

### Scenario 6 — Sulphite-sensitive guest, red-wine reduction
1. **Standard wine contains sulphites at ≥ 10 ppm.**
2. **Substitution plan:**
   - **Sulphite-free wine** (organic, biodynamic, "no added sulphites") — verify by label.
   - **Or:** substitute non-alcoholic — stock + apple cider vinegar + concord grape juice for red-wine character.
3. **Use dedicated reduction pan** so previously-reduced wine residue doesn't transfer.
4. **Document** substitution.
5. **Server confirms** at table.

## 5. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Using an aluminum pot for tomato or wine reduction** — discolouration and metallic taste.
- **Using unlined copper for acidic sauces** — copper ions dissolve.
- **Using cast iron for long-cooked tomato sauce** — metallic notes after extended time.
- **Plastic spoons in hot reductions** — heat + acid + fat can leach plasticizers.
- **Cold cream into hot caramel** — seizes the sugar, steam explosion.
- **Cold butter cubes shocked into hot beurre blanc reduction** — emulsion breaks (right technique: reduction warm, butter cubed cold, whisk progressively — see E-16.03).
- **Eggs cracked directly into the sauce bowl** — one bad egg ruins the batch.
- **Pre-grated cheese in mornay** — anti-caking agents affect melting.
- **Forgetting to clarify butter for hollandaise** — milk solids destabilize emulsion.
- **Hot tap water in measurements** — older plumbing can leach metals; flat-tasting.
- **Skipping sanitation between proteins and produce** — cross-contamination.
- **Forgetting cold-chain on yolks and dairy** — quality and food-safety risks.
- **Wet probe thermometer between sauces** — cross-contamination of allergens or proteins.
- **Eyeballing measurements for sauce ratios** — inconsistent body and seasoning shift-to-shift.

## 7. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with E-16.02.

1. **Non-reactive tools (Range of Variables):** **double boilers, saucepans, strainers, stainless steel bowls, plastics (BPA-free), silicone.**
2. **Non-reactive materials:** **stainless steel, glass, ceramic, enameled cast iron, tin-lined copper, silicone, food-grade BPA-free plastic (cool use only).**
3. **Reactive materials to avoid for acid/dairy/egg sauces:** aluminum, unlined copper, bare cast iron, bare carbon steel.
4. **Why non-reactive matters:** acid + reactive metal = discolouration, metallic off-flavours, trace-metal contamination over time.
5. **Heavy-bottomed stainless steel** is the workhorse sauce pot — even heat, non-reactive, durable.
6. **Double boiler / bain-marie** for egg-based sauces (hollandaise, béarnaise, custards) — caps temperature at safe range for delicate proteins.
7. **Wide rondeau / sauteuse** for reductions — maximum evaporation surface.
8. **Strainer hierarchy:** china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth-lined chinois.
9. **Stainless-steel bowls** for tempering, mixing, sabayon work — non-reactive, durable.
10. **Plastic for cool storage only** — heat + acid + plastic can leach plasticizers.
11. **Silicone** (heat-resistant to ~ 230 °C) for stirring, scraping, folding — flavour-neutral.
12. **Whisks:** balloon (sabayon, hollandaise, roux + liquid); French / saucier (sauce work); flat (deglazing); coiled (slurry).
13. **Probe thermometer** for verifying hollandaise temperature (~ 65–70 °C yolk denaturation; ≤ 85 °C max hold).
14. **Weight over volume** for sauce ratios — consistency, scalability, HACCP-friendly.
15. **Crack eggs into a separate bowl first** to verify quality before incorporating.
16. **Pasteurized egg yolks** for hollandaise / béarnaise / liaison in high-risk populations.
17. **Clarified butter** for hollandaise — milk solids destabilize the emulsion.
18. **Room-temperature heavy cream into hot caramel** — cold cream seizes the sugar and risks steam burn.
19. **Cold cubed butter** for beurre blanc — added progressively to the warm reduction.
20. **Fresh-grated cheese** for mornay — pre-grated has anti-caking agents that affect melting.
21. **Mise en place** before commitment — read recipe → identify reactive/non-reactive needs → weigh and measure → pre-prep aromatics → stage tools.
22. **Sanitation between proteins and between raw / RTE** — colour-coded boards, sanitize tools.
23. **Cold-chain ≤ 4 °C** for eggs, dairy, cream until processing.
24. **Allergen-isolated tools** for celiac, peanut, tree nut, sesame, shellfish, soy orders.
25. **Health Canada priority allergens** in sauce processing: egg (hollandaise/liaison), milk (béchamel/cream), wheat (roux), soy (Asian sauces, use tamari for celiac), fish (fumet), shellfish (oyster sauce), mustard, sesame, tree nuts, peanut, sulphites (wine, vinegar).
26. **Equipment lockout** for blade removal on food processors, immersion blenders, robot-coupes.
27. **Hot-liquid blender safety:** ≤ ⅔ full, vent, towel, pulse.
28. **Cooling rule for finished sauce (carry-over from A-2.02):** 60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.
29. **Plan pre-shift production for long-build sauces** (demi, espagnole) and à la minute for emulsions and pan sauces.
30. **Recipe ratios** (recap from E-16.01): hollandaise 6 yolks + 250 g clarified butter + 50 mL acid; beurre blanc 250 g cold butter mounted into ~ 100 mL reduction; mornay 100 g Gruyère + 50 g Parmesan + 2 yolks per L béchamel.
