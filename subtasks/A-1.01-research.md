## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### A-1.01.01P — Identify and Report Potential Hazards

**Performance Criteria:** identify and report potential hazards
**Evidence of Attainment:** potential hazards are identified and reported according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### Why this matters
Kitchens are statistically among the highest-injury workplaces in foodservice. Slip-trip-fall, burns, lacerations, and chemical exposures dominate WSIB/WorkSafe claims. Early hazard identification is a primary **HACCP** principle (Hazard Analysis) and a worker right under provincial **Occupational Health & Safety** Acts: the **right to know, right to participate, right to refuse unsafe work**. **[Regulatory]**

#### What counts as a hazard
From the original Range of Variables, hazards include:
- Water on the floor or mats
- Debris, food trim, broken glass
- Exposed or frayed electrical wires
- Uneven, cracked, or worn flooring
- Unidentified liquids or chemicals (containers without labels)
- Fire risks (grease, open flames, towels near burners)
- Environmental (smoke, heat, fumes, gas leaks)
- Damaged furniture, fixtures, equipment (FF&E)
- Poor lighting (under hoods, in walk-ins)
- Inadequate ventilation (CO buildup, grease aerosols)

#### Reporting flow (typical Canadian kitchen)
1. **Make safe immediately** if possible (signage, isolate area).
2. **Report to direct supervisor / chef on duty** — verbal first for speed.
3. **Log it** in the hazard / maintenance / incident book or digital system.
4. **Escalate** to facilities, OH&S rep, or Joint Health & Safety Committee (JHSC) if unresolved. **[Regulatory — JHSC required in most provinces at ≥20 employees]**

> **Memory aid — "SEE-TELL-LOG-FIX":** *See it, tell someone, log it, fix it.*

### A-1.01.02P — Clear and Clean Walkways

**Performance Criteria:** clear and clean walkways
**Evidence of Attainment:** walkways are cleared and cleaned by removing obstacles, wiping up spills, placing cautionary signage and reporting according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### Why this matters
Slip-trip-fall is the **single most common kitchen injury** category. A wet quarry-tile floor with grease is roughly as slippery as ice. Wet floors near fryers and dish pits are especially dangerous because:
- Grease + water = emulsified film with very low friction
- A fall toward hot equipment can convert a minor slip into a third-degree burn

#### Step-by-step response to a spill
1. **Stay with the spill** or block sight lines until signage is up.
2. **Place wet-floor signs (yellow A-frame, "Caution / Attention")** on both approaches before cleaning. **[Best Practice; bilingual signage required in some jurisdictions]**
3. **Absorb liquids** with a paper towel or absorbent compound (oil-dry / kitty-litter type) for oils.
4. **Sweep solid debris first**, then mop.
5. **Mop with detergent**, rinse if greasy, then **dry-mop or fan dry**.
6. **Leave signage up until dry to the touch.**

#### Common mistakes
- Using a single mop bucket for grease and general spills (spreads contamination).
- Removing the wet-floor sign before the floor is dry.
- Mopping over food debris without sweeping first.
- Leaving milk crates, dunnage racks, or boxes in aisles.

> ⚠️ **Warning:** Hot oil spills are a fire hazard *and* a slip hazard. Never put water on hot oil. Smother with an oil-dry absorbent or a Class K-rated approach.

### A-1.01.03P — Clean Workstation

**Performance Criteria:** clean workstation
**Evidence of Attainment:** workstation is cleaned by washing, rinsing and sanitizing work surfaces using prepared and tested solution according to manufacturers' specifications and jurisdictional regulations.

#### The science: "clean" vs. "sanitized"
These are **not** the same thing.

| Term | What it removes | How |
|---|---|---|
| **Cleaning** | Visible soil, grease, food residue | Detergent + mechanical action + warm water |
| **Sanitizing** | Reduces microorganisms on a clean surface to a safe level (typically a **5-log / 99.999% reduction**) | Heat or chemical sanitizer |
| **Disinfecting** | Higher-level pathogen kill, usually for non-food-contact surfaces (washrooms, etc.) | Stronger chemicals, longer contact |
| **Sterilizing** | Eliminates *all* microbes (rare in kitchens, used in canneries / autoclaves) | Pressure + heat |

You **must clean before sanitizing** — sanitizer is deactivated by organic soil, fats, and proteins. Spraying sanitizer on a greasy surface is essentially wasted chemical. **[Regulatory science basis — public health]**

#### Standard surface sanitation sequence (the food-contact "WRSAD")
1. **W**ash — detergent + warm water (≈ 43 °C / 110 °F) to dissolve fats.
2. **R**inse — potable water to remove detergent (sanitizers can be deactivated by detergent residue).
3. **S**anitize — approved sanitizer at correct concentration and contact time.
4. **A**ir-dry — towel-drying re-contaminates. Surfaces must drain freely.
5. **D**ocument (where required by HACCP plan).

#### Canadian-common sanitizers (food contact)
> Always follow the product label and verify with **test strips** of the matching chemistry.

| Sanitizer | Typical concentration (food contact) | Contact time | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chlorine (sodium hypochlorite / household bleach)** | **100–200 ppm** | ≈ 7–10 seconds minimum | Cheap, fast, corrodes metals, deactivated by organic matter, sensitive to pH (works best ≈ pH 6.5–7.5) |
| **Quaternary ammonium ("quats")** | **200–400 ppm** (per label) | ~30 seconds | Leaves residual film (good for some applications), can be deactivated by hard water and some detergents |
| **Iodine (iodophors)** | **12.5–25 ppm** | ≈ 30 seconds | Stains plastics, temperature-sensitive (don't use > 49 °C / 120 °F — iodine flashes off) |
| **Hot water (thermal sanitizing)** | **≥ 77 °C / 171 °F surface temp** for utensils; **≥ 82 °C / 180 °F final rinse** in mechanical dishwashers (common Canadian spec) | ≥ 30 seconds at temperature | No chemical residue, but uses energy and requires verified thermometers |

**[Regulatory]:** Concentrations and contact times must match the **manufacturer's SDS/label** and provincial public-health code. The numbers above are widely used in Canada but **always verify with test strips**.

#### Why test strips matter
A chlorine bucket made up at 8 a.m. can drop below 50 ppm by lunch from soil load, evaporation, and UV. Below the threshold, the sanitizer is just scented water. Test strips are typically used:
- **At setup** of each sanitizer bucket / sink.
- **At least every 2–4 hours** during service. **[Best Practice]**
- **After any visible soiling**.
- **Whenever a new container is made up**.

> **Operational tip:** Label each sanitizer bucket with **product, concentration, and time made**. A bucket older than ~4 hours during heavy service is usually rebuilt.

#### Common mistakes
- Mixing chlorine with ammonia or acid cleaners → releases **chloramine / chlorine gas** (toxic). **Never mix chemicals.**
- Using "more is better" — over-concentrated sanitizer can leave **chemical residue** that is a food-safety hazard and may corrode equipment.
- Topping up a depleted bucket instead of remaking it.

### A-1.01.04P — Maintain Clutter-Free Workstation

**Performance Criteria:** maintain clutter-free workstation
**Evidence of Attainment:** clutter-free workstation is maintained by removing unnecessary tools, equipment, foods and debris.

#### Why this matters
A clutter-free station is a **food-safety**, **fire-safety**, and **efficiency** issue all at once:
- **Cross-contamination risk:** raw protein near ready-to-eat foods, or knives resting on cutting boards used for produce.
- **Fire risk:** towels, paper, or oily rags near burners or salamanders.
- **Time-in-danger-zone (4 °C – 60 °C):** food left out on a clutter-buried prep table can spend hours in the bacterial growth range without anyone noticing.

> **The Food Danger Zone (Canada):** **4 °C to 60 °C (40 °F to 140 °F).** Within this range, pathogens such as *Salmonella*, *E. coli* O157:H7, *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Listeria monocytogenes*, and *Bacillus cereus* can multiply rapidly. Under optimal conditions, many bacteria can **double roughly every 20 minutes**. **[Best Practice basis: Health Canada / CFIA guidance]**

#### Industry concepts
- **"Mise en place"** — *everything in its place.* A French brigade principle: clean station, organized tools, prepped ingredients before service.
- **5S (Japanese / lean):** *Sort, Set in order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain.* Often used in HACCP-aligned kitchens to drive workstation discipline.
- **"Clean as you go" (CAYG):** the dominant industry mantra — clean during downtime, not after the rush.

#### Common mistakes
- Stacking dirty cambros under the cutting board.
- Leaving knives loose on the line (laceration + cross-contamination risk).
- Storing personal items (phones, drink cups) on prep surfaces — public health violation in most jurisdictions.

### A-1.01.05P — Store and Lock Up Hazardous Products

**Performance Criteria:** store and lock up hazardous products
**Evidence of Attainment:** hazardous products are stored and locked up according to manufacturers' specifications and jurisdictional regulations.

#### The regulatory backbone — WHMIS 2015
**Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System (WHMIS) 2015** is Canada's national hazard-communication standard, aligned with the **Globally Harmonized System (GHS)**. It is enforced via federal **Hazardous Products Act / Hazardous Products Regulations**, and provincial OH&S workplace requirements. **[Regulatory]**

Three pillars of WHMIS:
1. **Labels** — supplier labels and workplace labels (for decanted products).
2. **Safety Data Sheets (SDS)** — a 16-section standardized document for each controlled product. Must be accessible to workers.
3. **Worker training** — generic + workplace-specific. **[Regulatory — employer obligation]**

#### Pictograms an apprentice must recognize
WHMIS/GHS uses red-bordered diamond pictograms. The ones most relevant in kitchens:

| Pictogram | Hazard class | Common kitchen example |
|---|---|---|
| **Flame** | Flammable | Cooking-grade ethanol, sterno, some degreasers |
| **Corrosion** | Skin/eye burns or metal corrosion | Oven cleaner, lime/scale remover, dish-machine detergents |
| **Exclamation mark** | Skin/eye irritant, sensitizer | Some sanitizers, quats |
| **Gas cylinder** | Compressed gas | CO₂ for soda/beer, propane |
| **Health hazard** | Carcinogen / chronic toxicity | Rare in kitchens; some industrial cleaners |
| **Skull & crossbones** | Acute toxicity | Rodenticides (handled by licensed pest control only) |

#### Storage rules
- **Never above food, food-contact surfaces, or single-service items.** Chemicals always **below and separate**. **[Regulatory — public health code in all provinces]**
- **Original labelled containers** preferred. Decanted product **must** have a workplace label (product ID, hazards, safe-handling cues).
- **Lock up** when not in active use. Especially required for high-toxicity items, oven cleaner, and pest-control products. **[Regulatory in most jurisdictions; best practice everywhere]**
- **Segregate incompatibles:** acids away from bleach / chlorinated products; oxidizers away from organics.
- **SDS access:** workers must be able to retrieve the SDS during their shift — paper binder or scannable QR. **[Regulatory]**

> ⚠️ **Critical:** Mixing bleach + acidic descaler (e.g., delimer) releases **chlorine gas**. Mixing bleach + ammonia releases **chloramines**. Both have hospitalized cooks. Read the SDS.

### A-1.01.06P — Store Hazardous Equipment and Utensils

**Performance Criteria:** store hazardous equipment and utensils
**Evidence of Attainment:** hazardous equipment and utensils are stored according to company policy, manufacturers' specifications and jurisdictional regulations.

#### What's "hazardous" in a kitchen?
- Knives, cleavers, mandolines, microplanes
- Slicer blades (Hobart-type), buffalo chopper blades, robot-coupe S-blades
- Meat-grinder worms and plates, sausage stuffers
- Sharpening steels and stones (point hazard)
- Mixer attachments (dough hook, paddle, whip)
- Power tools (immersion blender wand, vacuum sealer cutting bar)

#### Storage best practice
- **Knives** — magnetic strip, knife block, knife guard / edge guard, or dedicated drawer with sheaths. **Never** loose in a drawer (laceration risk) or in a sink under water (invisible blade = severe cuts). **[Best Practice; some jurisdictions explicitly prohibit blades in sinks]**
- **Mandoline / slicer blades** — store with guard engaged, blade retracted, ideally with a "blade out" warning tag during cleaning.
- **Detached slicer blades** — stored on dedicated wall-mount or in a labelled, locked cabinet. Many operations require **two-hand handling** and cut-resistant gloves.
- **Mixer attachments** — hung on a designated rack so they don't fall.
- **Sharpened items together, away from staff thoroughfares.**

> **Memory aid — "BLADE":** *Blade guard on, Locked away, Away from food zones, Dry before storage, Everyone knows where it is.*

### A-1.01.07P — Complete Cleaning Task List (Daily / Weekly / Monthly)

**Performance Criteria:** complete cleaning task list on a daily, weekly and monthly basis
**Evidence of Attainment:** cleaning task list is completed on a daily, weekly and monthly basis according to company policy and jurisdictional regulations.

#### Why the cadence matters
A **Master Sanitation Schedule (MSS)** is a HACCP-supportive document. Public health inspectors and food-safety auditors expect to see one. **[Best Practice / HACCP requirement; not a single national law]**

#### Typical Canadian commercial kitchen MSS

| Frequency | Example tasks |
|---|---|
| **After each task / change of protein** | Sanitize cutting boards, knives, prep surfaces |
| **Daily (end of shift)** | Wipe-down line, empty grease traps for fryers, hood filter swap (if drop-in), floor mop, sanitize sinks, restock dish room, change sanitizer buckets |
| **Weekly** | Deep-clean walk-in cooler shelving, descale dish machine, clean reach-in fan grilles, oven interior, ice machine wipe-down |
| **Monthly** | Hood degrease (interior surfaces — note: hood-system / duct cleaning is a separate certified service), drain treatment, deep-clean storage shelving, calibrate thermometers |
| **Quarterly / Annual** | Pest-control review, kitchen exhaust certified cleaning, refrigeration coil cleaning, fire-suppression inspection |

> **[Regulatory] note:** Commercial kitchen exhaust system cleaning is governed in most Canadian jurisdictions by **NFPA 96** (adopted by fire codes). Frequency depends on cooking volume (solid-fuel = quarterly; high volume = quarterly; moderate volume = semi-annually; low volume = annually). The frequency itself is regulated, not just "nice to do".

#### Common mistakes
- Signing off the MSS without doing the task ("pencil-whipping").
- Cleaning hot equipment surfaces with cold cleaners → thermal shock cracks (ceramic / glass).
- Using steel wool on stainless steel → scratches that harbour bacteria. Use **non-abrasive scrub pads** or **green Scotch-Brite** instead.

### A-1.01.08P — Secure Workstations When Not in Use

**Performance Criteria:** secure workstations when not in use
**Evidence of Attainment:** workstations, when not in use, are secured by minimizing potential hazards according to procedures.

#### "Securing" a station means
From the original Range of Variables — procedures include:
- **Removing knives** to designated storage.
- **Disconnecting electrical appliances** (immersion blenders, slicers, mixers).
- **Shutting down appliances** (burners, salamanders, fryers — note many fryers require cool-down cycles).
- **Wiping up spills**.
- **Placing cautionary signage** for wet floors, hot surfaces, or out-of-service equipment.

#### Why
- **Fire prevention** — unattended burners are a leading kitchen fire cause.
- **Theft / tamper prevention** — open knife storage, open cash, open chemicals.
- **Pest prevention** — uncovered food + crumbs = mice, cockroaches, fruit flies overnight.
- **Energy and water savings** — turning down hoods, water lines.

> **Operational tip — "C-LEAR" before leaving the station:** *Clean, Lock blades, Equipment off, All food covered & stored, Refrigeration verified.*

### A-1.01.09P — Lock Out Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** lock out equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** equipment is locked out according to company policy, jurisdictional regulations and manufacturers' specifications.

#### Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) — what and why
**Lockout/Tagout** is a worker-safety procedure to isolate the energy source of a machine being cleaned or serviced so it cannot be turned on while someone's hand is inside it. It is governed by provincial **Occupational Health & Safety** regulations. **[Regulatory]**

#### When LOTO matters in a kitchen
- **Cleaning a slicer** (Hobart-type) — unplug, remove blade guard.
- **Disassembling a meat grinder, buffalo chopper, food processor** — disconnect first.
- **Servicing the dish machine or combi-oven** — typically the technician's responsibility, but cooks must not power things back on during service.
- **Cleaning under hood or behind hot line** — gas valves off, breakers tagged out if needed.

#### The six classic LOTO steps (industry standard)
1. **Prepare** — identify all energy sources (electric, gas, steam, hydraulic, stored).
2. **Notify** — tell affected workers.
3. **Isolate** — turn off and unplug, or close valves.
4. **Lock/tag** — apply lock and/or tag indicating "do not energize".
5. **Verify** — attempt to start the equipment to confirm it's dead.
6. **Service**, then reverse the steps to re-energize.

> ⚠️ **Common kitchen mistake:** "I just unplugged it" is not full LOTO. If someone else plugs it back in while your hand is on the slicer blade, you can lose fingers. Use a physical lock or at minimum a clear tag, and verify before reaching in.

### A-1.01.10P — Prepare, Test, Label and Store Cleaning and Sanitation Solutions

**Performance Criteria:** prepare, test, label and store cleaning and sanitation solutions
**Evidence of Attainment:** cleaning and sanitation solutions are prepared, tested, labelled and stored according to manufacturers' specifications and jurisdictional regulations.

#### Concentration matters more than apprentices realize
- **Too weak** = ineffective; pathogens survive; false sense of clean.
- **Too strong** = chemical residue (a food hazard), surface corrosion, irritation to staff, regulatory non-compliance.

#### Preparation rules
- **Cold water** for chlorine bleach — hot water gases off chlorine and weakens the solution.
- **Warm water** for detergent (dissolves fats better; ≈ 38–43 °C / 100–110 °F).
- **Follow the dilution chart** on the SDS or auto-dosing dispenser.
- **Add chemical to water**, not water to chemical, when manually mixing acids (prevents splash).

#### Testing
- **Use the matching test strip chemistry** (chlorine strip for chlorine, quat strip for quats, iodine strip for iodine). They are not interchangeable.
- **Re-test** every few hours or after heavy soil load.

#### Labelling
Workplace labels (per WHMIS) for any decanted container must include:
- Product identifier
- Hazard pictogram(s) or statement
- Safe-handling precautions

> **[Regulatory]** Unlabelled containers of chemicals are a frequent WHMIS infraction during workplace inspections.

#### Storage
- **Original or clearly-labelled** containers.
- **Below food**, in a dedicated, ventilated area.
- **Locked** when accessible to the public or in shared spaces.
- **Never** in old food containers — a dishwashing detergent in an old salad-dressing squeeze bottle is how poisoning incidents start.

### A-1.01.11P — Identify Location of Safety Exits and Assembly Areas

**Performance Criteria:** identify location of safety exits and assembly areas
**Evidence of Attainment:** location of safety exits and assembly areas are identified in the event of dangerous situations according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### Why
The National Building Code and provincial fire codes require:
- **Clear, unobstructed exit paths.**
- **Exit signage** that is illuminated and visible.
- **Emergency lighting** on backup power.
- **Assembly point** designated outside the building. **[Regulatory]**

#### Apprentice's first-week checklist
- Where is the **nearest exit from the line**?
- Where is the **secondary exit** if the primary is blocked by fire?
- Where is the **assembly area** (often a parking-lot landmark)?
- Where are **fire extinguishers**, and which class?
- Where is the **gas shut-off valve** for the cookline?
- Where is the **electrical breaker panel**?
- Where is the **first-aid kit**, and who is the certified first-aider on shift?

### A-1.01.12P — Identify Emergency Procedures

**Performance Criteria:** identify emergency procedures
**Evidence of Attainment:** emergency procedures are identified according to company policy.

#### The major emergency categories (from original Range of Variables: fire, explosions, gas malfunctions) plus others apprentices should know:

##### Fire
- **Fire extinguisher classes** are critical in a kitchen:
  - **Class A** — ordinary combustibles (paper, wood)
  - **Class B** — flammable liquids (oil-spill *not yet ignited*, solvents)
  - **Class C** — energized electrical
  - **Class K** — **cooking oils and fats (the kitchen-specific class)**. A Class K extinguisher uses a wet potassium-acetate agent that saponifies hot oil.
- **PASS technique:** **P**ull pin, **A**im at base, **S**queeze handle, **S**weep side-to-side.
- **Never** use water on a grease fire — it instantly flashes to steam and ejects burning oil.
- **Cover small grease fires** with a metal lid; cut the heat; never carry a flaming pan.
- **Hood suppression systems** (Ansul-type) discharge wet chemical onto cooking surfaces and shut off gas. After discharge, the suppression system must be **professionally recharged** before the line is used again. **[Regulatory — NFPA 96 / 17A as adopted by fire codes]**

##### Gas leak / malfunction
- Smell of natural gas (mercaptan additive) or LPG → **do not switch anything electrical on or off** (sparks).
- Shut off the gas at the manual valve if safely accessible.
- Evacuate, then call gas utility from outside.

##### Medical
- Cuts: direct pressure; severe → 911.
- Burns: cool running water 10–20 min for thermal burns; do not apply ice or butter (industry myth).
- Chemical exposure: consult SDS Section 4 (First Aid); eyewash station for splashes ≥ 15 minutes.

##### Foodborne illness suspected
- Stop service of suspected item.
- Retain samples if possible.
- Notify management and public health unit. **[Regulatory in all provinces]**

### A-1.01.01L — Industry Standards and Safe Work Practices

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of industry standards and safe work practices.

#### Terminology associated with health and safety

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **OH&S / OHS** | Occupational Health and Safety — provincial regulation governing worker safety. |
| **WHMIS 2015** | Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System (Canadian, GHS-aligned). |
| **SDS** | Safety Data Sheet — 16-section document on a controlled product. |
| **HACCP** | Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points — preventive food-safety system. |
| **CCP** | Critical Control Point — a step where control is essential to prevent or reduce a hazard. |
| **PPE** | Personal Protective Equipment — non-slip footwear, cut-resistant gloves, aprons, hearing protection in dish pits. |
| **JHSC** | Joint Health & Safety Committee — required at most workplaces ≥ 20 employees. |
| **MSS** | Master Sanitation Schedule. |
| **FIFO** | First In, First Out — stock rotation method. |
| **TCS / PHF** | Time/Temperature Control for Safety food (formerly Potentially Hazardous Food). |
| **Danger Zone** | 4 °C – 60 °C (40 °F – 140 °F). |
| **LOTO** | Lockout/Tagout. |
| **Cross-contamination** | Transfer of harmful microorganisms or allergens from one food/surface/tool to another. |

#### Safe lifting, posture, and movement
From the Range of Variables: *proper lifting, hoisting and transferring procedures, safe posture and movements (sitting, standing and bending).*

- **Lift with the legs**, not the back: feet shoulder-width, squat, keep load close, neutral spine, exhale on lift.
- **Two-person lift** for stockpots > ~15 kg / 30 lb of hot liquid, full sacks of flour (20 kg / 50 lb), full milk crates.
- **Cart, dolly, or pallet jack** whenever possible — back injuries are the most common chronic kitchen injury.
- **No twisting under load** — pivot with the feet.
- **Anti-fatigue mats** at standing stations reduce lower-limb and back strain.

#### Physical and mental impairment risks
- **Fatigue** — long shifts, hot environments, dehydration → reduced reaction time, increased cut/burn risk.
- **Alcohol / cannabis / impairing medication** — prohibited during shifts; an OH&S obligation to report fitness-for-duty issues. **[Regulatory]**
- **Heat stress** — kitchens routinely exceed 35 °C; hydration breaks, electrolyte intake, and ventilation matter.
- **Repetitive strain** — knife work, plating, dishwashing. Rotate tasks, stretch, swap dominant hand for low-skill tasks.
- **Mental load** — service pressure can mask hazards. Slow down at handoffs.

### A-1.01.02L — Kitchen Sanitation Standards

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of kitchen sanitation standards.

#### Sanitizing product families recap

| Family | Pros | Cons | Common use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chlorine** | Cheap, fast, broad-spectrum | Corrosive, deactivated by organic soil, smell | 3-compartment sink, surface sanitizing buckets |
| **Quats** | Stable, residual film, low odour | Deactivated by hard water; not for some allergen programs | Spray bottles, food-contact wipe-down |
| **Iodine** | Visible (amber), broad-spectrum | Stains, temperature-limited, more expensive | Some bar/glassware programs |
| **Hot water** | No chemical residue, no allergen carry-over | Energy cost, requires verification, burn risk | Mechanical dishwashers, utensil sanitizing |
| **Peracetic acid (PAA)** | Effective at low temperatures, biodegradable | Strong odour, requires PPE | Some larger / industrial operations |

#### Why sanitize the *whole* kitchen and equipment

- **Biofilms** — communities of bacteria embedded in a self-produced matrix that adhere to surfaces (drains, gasket seams, ice machines). Biofilms are resistant to sanitizer and must be broken up mechanically first.
- **Allergen control** — a "clean" surface can still have peanut, shellfish, or wheat protein residues that trigger severe reactions. Allergen-specific cleaning is increasingly an industry standard.
- **Pest deterrence** — crumbs and grease residue attract rodents and cockroaches; clean kitchens are inhospitable to pests.
- **Listeria control** — *Listeria monocytogenes* grows at refrigeration temperatures (down to ~0 °C) and is the reason walk-in floor drains, gaskets, and slicers get special sanitation attention.

### A-1.01.03L — Procedures Used to Sanitize Kitchens and Equipment

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to sanitize kitchens and equipment.

#### The 3-compartment sink method (still tested on Red Seal)

| Sink | Contents | Temperature | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Wash** | Detergent + water | ≈ 43 °C / 110 °F minimum (many ops use 49 °C / 120 °F) | Scrape and pre-rinse first |
| **2. Rinse** | Clean potable water | Warm | Removes detergent so it doesn't deactivate sanitizer |
| **3. Sanitize** | Chemical sanitizer at correct ppm **or** hot water ≥ 77 °C / 171 °F | Per chemistry / heat | Verify with test strips or thermometer |
| **Then** | Air dry on rack | — | No towels; gravity drain only |

#### Mechanical dishwashers
Two main types:
- **High-temp** — final rinse at ≥ **82 °C / 180 °F**. Uses heat for sanitation.
- **Low-temp / chemical** — final rinse with chlorine-based sanitizer at ~50 ppm.

Both have **verifying tools**: a thermolabel / max-registering thermometer for high-temp, and test strips for low-temp.

#### Disassembly and reassembly — equipment-specific
- **Slicer (Hobart)** — unplug, lock blade guard, remove ring guard and blade per manufacturer; clean with non-abrasive cloth; never submerge motor housing. Sharpen on a schedule.
- **Buffalo chopper / food processor** — remove S-blade with both hands using a blade-handling tool or thick towel; never reach in with bare fingers, even when "off".
- **Meat grinder** — disassemble feed pan, worm, plates, knife; soak; brush each thread; air-dry; reassemble dry to prevent grit transfer.
- **Combi oven** — run self-clean cycle; never enter while at temperature; descale per manufacturer (water hardness sensitive).
- **Ice machine** — sanitize interior with manufacturer-approved sanitizer; common source of cross-contamination and biofilm in audits.

#### Cleaning chemistry quick reference

| Soil type | Best chemistry |
|---|---|
| **Fats / grease** | Alkaline degreaser (high pH) |
| **Mineral scale / lime / hard water** | Acidic delimer (low pH) |
| **Protein** | Alkaline + chlorinated cleaner |
| **Carbohydrate / starch** | Mild alkaline + warm water |
| **Burned-on carbon** | Strong alkaline + dwell time + non-abrasive scrub |

> ⚠️ Never combine an acid delimer and a chlorinated cleaner in the same step or in the same closed space — releases chlorine gas.

### A-1.01.04L — Regulatory Requirements

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of regulatory requirements pertaining to health and safety.

#### The Canadian regulatory landscape — who does what

| Level | Authority | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Federal** | **CFIA (Canadian Food Inspection Agency)** | Federally-registered processors; *Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR)* — requires HACCP-based **Preventive Control Plans (PCPs)** for many operators. |
| **Federal** | **Health Canada** | Food safety policy, guidance documents (e.g., danger-zone guidance), food additive and contaminant standards. |
| **Provincial** | **Provincial Public Health / Food Safety Regulations** | Restaurants and food-service establishments — typically inspected by Public Health Inspectors. Examples: Ontario *Food Premises Regulation*, BC *Food Premises Regulation*, Alberta *Food Regulation*, Québec *MAPAQ*. |
| **Provincial** | **Provincial OH&S** | Worker safety. Examples: Ontario OHSA, BC WorkSafeBC, Alberta OHS Act, Québec CNESST. |
| **Provincial / Federal** | **WHMIS 2015** | Implemented via federal HPA + provincial OH&S workplace requirements. |
| **Municipal** | **Local fire department, business licensing, building inspection** | Fire code, occupancy, exhaust hood permits. |

#### Mandatory food-handler training
Most provinces require at least one **certified food handler** on duty (e.g., MAPAQ-approved courses in Québec; FoodSafe in BC; *Food Handler Certification* in Ontario through approved providers). Apprentices should know whether their workplace requires it, but the *exact* certification name varies by province. **[Regulatory — varies by jurisdiction]**

#### HACCP — the seven principles (memorize for Red Seal)
1. Conduct a hazard analysis.
2. Determine critical control points (CCPs).
3. Establish critical limits.
4. Establish monitoring procedures.
5. Establish corrective actions.
6. Establish verification procedures.
7. Establish record-keeping and documentation procedures.

#### Procedures used to maintain a sanitary food service operation (from original Range of Variables)
- **Clean** — remove visible soil with detergent.
- **Sanitize** — reduce pathogens with approved chemical or heat.
- **Manage waste and recycle** — separate streams, covered bins, frequent removal; prevents pest attraction and odour.
- **Control pests and rodents** — Integrated Pest Management (IPM): exclusion (seal gaps), sanitation (no food/water access), monitoring (traps, logs), licensed pest-control contractor. **[Regulatory — must use licensed operators for pesticides in commercial premises]**
- **Prevent cross-contamination** — separate raw and ready-to-eat, color-coded cutting boards (common Canadian convention: red = raw red meat; yellow = raw poultry; blue = raw seafood; green = produce; white = dairy/bread; brown = cooked meat), dedicated tools, hand hygiene, glove changes between tasks.

## 3. Bonus Knowledge: Microbiology, Temperatures, and the Danger Zone

Although this subtask is centred on **work environment**, sanitation logic ties directly to food microbiology. Red Seal exams routinely test these numbers.

#### Food Danger Zone — the headline number

**4 °C – 60 °C (40 °F – 140 °F).**

- Below 4 °C: most pathogen growth slows dramatically (*Listeria* is the notable exception — still grows slowly).
- Above 60 °C: most pathogens stop multiplying.
- Pathogens are not necessarily *killed* by either limit — they are *prevented from multiplying*. Killing requires cooking temperatures.

#### Bacterial growth math
Under ideal conditions, many spoilage and pathogenic bacteria divide approximately **every 20 minutes**. One organism becomes:

| Time | Approx. count |
|---|---|
| 0 min | 1 |
| 1 h | 8 |
| 2 h | 64 |
| 4 h | ~4,000 |
| 6 h | ~260,000 |
| 8 h | ~17,000,000 |

This is why the **2-hour / 4-hour rule** is taught:
- Food in the danger zone < 2 hours → safe to refrigerate and reuse. **[Best Practice — Health Canada guidance]**
- 2–4 hours → use immediately, do not return to refrigeration.
- > 4 hours → discard.

#### Cooking temperatures (Canadian Health Canada guidance — apprentice essentials)

| Food | Minimum internal temperature |
|---|---|
| Whole poultry | **82 °C / 180 °F** |
| Poultry pieces, stuffed cuts | **74 °C / 165 °F** |
| Ground beef, pork, ground poultry | **71 °C / 160 °F** (ground poultry → 74 °C) |
| Mechanically tenderized beef (Canada-specific rule) | **63 °C / 145 °F** internal *and* a clearly indicated cook temp; flip at least twice |
| Whole cuts of beef / pork — medium-rare to well | **63 °C / 145 °F** with 3-min rest (medium-rare) up to 71 °C |
| Fish | **70 °C / 158 °F** for general Health Canada guidance |
| Eggs cooked for immediate service | **63 °C / 145 °F** until yolk + white firm |
| Reheating leftovers | **74 °C / 165 °F** within 2 hours |

> **[Regulatory basis]:** Health Canada publishes these as recommended internal temperatures. They are widely adopted into provincial public-health inspection programs.

#### Cooling rule (CFIA / common Canadian best practice)
Cool from **60 °C → 20 °C within 2 hours**, then **20 °C → 4 °C within an additional 4 hours**. Total ≤ 6 hours from hot to cold. **[Best Practice — basis Health Canada / CFIA cooling guidance]**

Tools:
- Ice baths
- Shallow pans (max 5 cm / 2 in deep)
- Blast chillers (commercial gold standard — drops product through the danger zone fast)
- Ice paddles / cooling wands (commonly used for stocks and soups)

#### Reheating
- Reheat to **74 °C / 165 °F** within 2 hours.
- Reheat **once** — repeated thermal cycling damages quality (proteins denature unevenly, starches retrograde, fats oxidize) and stacks risk if the food has been mishandled at any prior point.
- Steam tables hold; they do **not** reheat. A product placed cold in a steam table will sit in the danger zone for hours.

## 4. Practical Kitchen Scenarios (Apprentice-Friendly)

### Scenario 1 — A spilled tray of raw chicken juice on the line
1. Don gloves.
2. Place wet-floor sign.
3. Absorb with disposable paper towels (never a wiping cloth that will go to laundry).
4. Wash with detergent.
5. Rinse.
6. Sanitize at correct ppm.
7. Air dry.
8. Replace cutting boards and tools in the splash zone, sanitize knife handles, change gloves, **wash hands** for 20 seconds with warm water and soap before resuming.

### Scenario 2 — Sanitizer bucket test strip reads pale (under concentration)
- Discard the bucket.
- Rinse bucket.
- Remake at correct dilution per dispenser or SDS.
- Re-test.
- Investigate: is the dispenser failing? Was the cloth dropping too much soil into the bucket?

### Scenario 3 — Mandoline cleanup at end of shift
- Lockout (unplug if powered; for manual, lay flat with blade retracted).
- Cut-resistant glove on dominant hand.
- Brush off debris first.
- Wash, rinse, sanitize the slicing surface.
- Dry.
- Reassemble with guard engaged.
- Store on dedicated shelf, blade-side toward wall.

### Scenario 4 — Small grease fire at the sauté station
- Shout "FIRE" to alert the line.
- Cover with a metal lid or sheet pan.
- Turn off the burner / gas valve.
- If sustained or spreading, evacuate, pull the **Ansul/hood suppression** manual pull station, then evacuate to assembly area, call 911.
- **Never** use water; **never** carry the burning pan.

## 5. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions (Audit-Day Hit List)

- Wiping cloths reused all shift instead of rotated through sanitizer buckets.
- Chemicals in unmarked spray bottles.
- Bleach mixed with detergent or delimer.
- Cutting boards stored stacked while still wet (anaerobic bacteria growth).
- Walk-in fan grills caked with mould.
- "Sanitizing" without cleaning first.
- Personal items on prep surfaces.
- Hand sinks blocked or used to rinse mops (hand sinks are for hands only).
- Thermometers uncalibrated. Calibrate with **ice slurry → 0 °C / 32 °F**; calibrate weekly or after any drop.
- "Cooling" hot stockpots in the walk-in — raises walk-in temperature, heats neighbouring food into the danger zone.
- Reheating in a steam table from cold.
- Reusing single-service items (deli cups, foil pans for raw protein storage).

## 7. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The following points are the highest-yield content for written exam questions and oral/practical assessments aligned to A-1.01.

1. **Food Danger Zone:** **4 °C to 60 °C (40 °F to 140 °F).** Bacteria can double roughly every 20 minutes within this range.
2. **2-Hour Rule:** Food held in the danger zone for more than 2 hours should not be returned to refrigeration; over 4 hours → discard.
3. **Cooling Rule:** 60 °C → 20 °C in ≤ 2 hours; 20 °C → 4 °C in an additional ≤ 4 hours; total ≤ 6 hours.
4. **Reheating Rule:** Reheat once to **74 °C / 165 °F** within 2 hours.
5. **Holding Temperatures:** Hot hold ≥ 60 °C; cold hold ≤ 4 °C.
6. **Cooking Minimums (Canada):** Whole poultry 82 °C; poultry pieces / stuffed 74 °C; ground meats 71 °C (74 °C for ground poultry); reheats 74 °C.
7. **Sanitation Sequence:** Clean → Rinse → Sanitize → Air-dry. Never reverse, never skip.
8. **3-Sink Method:** Wash (detergent, warm) → Rinse (potable) → Sanitize (chemical or heat) → Air-dry.
9. **Chemical Sanitizer Concentrations:** Chlorine 100–200 ppm; Quats 200–400 ppm per label; Iodine 12.5–25 ppm. Test strips required.
10. **High-Temp Dishwasher:** Final rinse ≥ 82 °C / 180 °F.
11. **WHMIS 2015:** Labels + SDS + Training. Recognize pictograms. Workplace labels required for decanted containers.
12. **HACCP 7 Principles:** Hazard analysis → CCPs → Critical limits → Monitoring → Corrective actions → Verification → Records.
13. **Cross-Contamination Controls:** Color-coded boards, separate tools, raw below cooked in storage, hand-washing between tasks.
14. **Fire Classes:** A (ordinary), B (flammable liquids), C (electrical), **K (cooking oils — kitchen-specific)**.
15. **PASS:** Pull, Aim, Squeeze, Sweep.
16. **LOTO Six Steps:** Prepare → Notify → Isolate → Lock/Tag → Verify → Service.
17. **Hand Sinks:** Hands only. Soap + warm water + ≥ 20 sec scrub + single-use towel.
18. **Storage Hierarchy (Top → Bottom in refrigeration):** Ready-to-eat → seafood → whole cuts of beef/pork → ground meats → poultry. (Higher cook-temperature foods go *lower* — drips travel down.)
19. **FIFO** stock rotation.
20. **Pest Control:** IPM — exclusion, sanitation, monitoring, licensed operator for pesticides.
21. **Provincial vs Federal:** CFIA / Health Canada (federal); provincial public-health & OH&S for restaurants; municipal fire & building codes.
22. **Right to Refuse Unsafe Work:** Worker right under provincial OH&S — cannot be disciplined for invoking it in good faith.
23. **Calibration:** Thermometer ice-slurry calibration to 0 °C / 32 °F, weekly or after any impact.
24. **Hood Suppression / NFPA 96:** Commercial kitchen exhaust cleaning frequency depends on cooking volume; suppression systems must be recharged by certified technicians after discharge.
25. **Personal Hygiene:** Clean uniform, hair restrained, no nail polish or jewellery beyond a plain band, cuts covered with blue food-safe bandage + glove.
