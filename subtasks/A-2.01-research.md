## The Big Picture: Why Temperature Is the Backbone of Food Safety

Temperature is the **single most influential variable** in commercial food safety. Pathogens, spoilage bacteria, parasites, and many enzymes all behave predictably with temperature. Control temperature, and you control most foodborne illness risk.

### The Food Danger Zone (Canada)

**4 °C – 60 °C (40 °F – 140 °F).**

- Below 4 °C → most pathogens grow very slowly or stop. (*Listeria monocytogenes* is the notable exception — it grows slowly down to ≈ 0 °C.)
- Above 60 °C → most vegetative pathogens stop multiplying.
- **Neither limit kills pathogens** — they only slow or stop growth. Killing requires cooking temperatures.

### Bacterial growth math
Under optimal conditions, vegetative bacteria can **double approximately every 20 minutes** in the danger zone. Starting from a single cell:

| Time in danger zone | Approximate count |
|---|---|
| 0 min | 1 |
| 1 h | ~8 |
| 2 h | ~64 |
| 4 h | ~4,000 |
| 6 h | ~260,000 |
| 8 h | ~17,000,000 |

This is the **mathematical reason** behind the 2-hour rule and the 6-hour total cooling window.

### TCS / Potentially Hazardous Foods (PHF)
**TCS = Time/Temperature Control for Safety food** (the current term; older Canadian materials say PHF). These are foods that **support rapid pathogen growth** and require strict time-temperature control.

Characteristics that make food TCS:
- **High protein** (meat, poultry, seafood, eggs, dairy, tofu, cooked legumes)
- **High moisture / water activity (a_w > 0.85)**
- **Near-neutral pH (4.6–7.5)**
- **Not preserved** (cured, smoked, dried, fermented, canned)

Examples of TCS foods: raw and cooked meats, poultry, fish, shellfish, eggs, dairy, cooked rice/pasta/potatoes, cooked vegetables, soy products, cut melons, cut leafy greens, cut tomatoes, sprouts, garlic-in-oil mixtures.

Examples of **non-TCS / non-PHF**: whole intact produce, dry goods, hard cheeses, properly cured/smoked products, properly acidified pickles, commercial mayonnaise, jam.

### A-2.01.01P — Select and Use Tools and Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** select and use tools and equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** tools and equipment are selected and used for maintaining safe foods according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### The temperature-control toolkit

##### Thermometers (the most-tested equipment)
| Type | How it works | Best use | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Digital instant-read (thermistor)** | Electronic sensor at probe tip | General-purpose internal temperature checking; receiving | Tip-only sensing — must be inserted to thickest part; needs battery |
| **Bimetallic-coil (analog dial)** | Two metals expand differently, rotating a dial | Older but still common; budget option | **Sensing area is the length of the stem, not the tip** — needs deeper insertion (≥ 5 cm / 2 in) into food; slower response |
| **Thermocouple** | Junction of two dissimilar metals generates voltage | Fastest response (1–3 sec), thin probes for thin foods (burgers, fish) | More expensive; needs occasional calibration |
| **Infrared / laser (non-contact)** | Reads emitted infrared radiation from surface | **Surface temperatures only** — equipment, hot-holding wells, walk-in air temperature, receiving check on cases | **Cannot read internal temperature**; reflective surfaces (foil, stainless) fool it |
| **T-stick / disposable** | Wax indicator melts at threshold | Single-use; quick spot check (e.g., dishwasher rinse) | Not reusable; single-purpose |
| **Time-temperature indicator (TTI)** | Colour-change label | Cold-chain integrity for shipped TCS items | Not a real-time reading |
| **Data loggers / wireless probes** | Continuous logging | HACCP records for cooking/cooling/holding | Higher cost; verify accuracy |

> ⚠️ **Critical clarification:** **Infrared thermometers measure surface only.** Pointing one at a hot-holding pan tells you the pan's surface, not the food's core. Use a probe thermometer for internal verification. **[Best Practice / Regulatory]**

##### Heating / cooling equipment
| Tool | Function | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Bain-marie** | Hot water bath for holding sauces, soups | Must operate so food stays ≥ 60 °C |
| **Steam table / hot well** | Holding line for service | Holds, does not reheat; product must enter ≥ 74 °C |
| **Hot holding cabinet / heated drawer** | Holds plated or batch food | Calibrate for product type; avoid stacking |
| **Refrigerator / reach-in** | Cold storage ≤ 4 °C | Verify air temp daily |
| **Walk-in cooler / freezer** | Bulk cold/frozen storage | Walk-in ≤ 4 °C; freezer ≤ −18 °C |
| **Blast chiller / blast freezer** | Rapid cooling through danger zone | Drops 60 → 4 °C in ~ 90 min for shallow pans; HACCP gold standard |
| **Ice bath** | Submersion cooling; ice + water ≈ 0 °C | More effective than ice alone (water conducts heat better than air) |
| **Ice wand / paddle** | Frozen sealed wand stirred through stock or soup | Cools from the core outward; for tall vessels (stockpots) |
| **Combi-oven** | Steam/convection cooking with internal probe | Cook-and-hold programs leverage probe to hit target internal temp |
| **Sous-vide circulator** | Precise low-temp cooking 50–95 °C | Uses pasteurization tables (time × temperature) |

#### Why the right tool matters
Heat transfer obeys physics:
- **Conduction** — solid contact (pan to food).
- **Convection** — fluid motion (hot air in oven, steam, water in bain-marie).
- **Radiation** — electromagnetic (broiler, salamander, infrared lamp).
- **Phase change** — water → steam absorbs huge energy (steam burns are devastating); water → ice releases energy (ice bath pulls heat out fast).

A **shallow pan in a blast chiller** cools faster than the same volume in a deep cambro in a walk-in because:
1. **Surface-area-to-volume ratio** is higher (more pan surface in contact with cold air).
2. **Forced convection** (blast chiller fans) is dramatically more efficient than the still air of a walk-in.
3. **The food itself does not insulate the deeper core** — heat from the centre of a deep cambro can take 12+ hours to drop to 4 °C, well outside safe windows.

### A-2.01.02P — Verify and Record That Food Is at Required Internal Temperature

**Performance Criteria:** verify and record that food is at required internal temperature
**Evidence of Attainment:** food is checked at specific intervals to ensure it is at required internal temperature by using a thermometer and results are recorded.

#### Why "internal" matters
Surface temperature can be deceiving. A roast pulled from a 200 °C oven might have a crackling 95 °C exterior but a 50 °C core in the danger zone. Only a **probe in the thickest part** confirms the kill step.

#### How to probe properly
1. **Sanitize the probe** with an alcohol wipe or sanitizer between foods. **[Best Practice — prevents cross-contamination]**
2. **Insert into the thickest part** of the food.
3. **Avoid bones** (conduct heat differently, false reading).
4. **Avoid the pan bottom** (radiates pan temperature, not food).
5. **For thin food** (burger, fish fillet, breast): insert horizontally from the side.
6. **Wait for the reading to stabilize** — 5–10 seconds for digital instant-read; 15–30 seconds for bimetallic dial.
7. **Take multiple readings** in different spots for large roasts and bulk batches.
8. **Sanitize again** after use.

#### Critical Control Point (CCP) logging
Under HACCP and HACCP-based preventive control plans, **temperature checks at CCPs must be recorded**. Typical logs:

| Log | Frequency | Records |
|---|---|---|
| **Cooking temperature log** | Each batch | Item, time, temperature, initials |
| **Cooling log** | Each cooling cycle | Start time, midpoint (≤ 2 h to 20 °C), end (≤ 6 h to 4 °C) |
| **Hot holding log** | Every 2–4 hours during service | Item, temperature, initials |
| **Cold holding / unit-temp log** | Twice daily (open and pre-close common) | Walk-in, reach-in, freezer temperatures |
| **Receiving log** | Each delivery | Item, supplier, temperature on receipt |
| **Dishwasher final-rinse log** | Daily (or per cycle in high-volume) | Temperature or chemical ppm |

**[Regulatory]** Federally-registered operators under SFCR must keep a written **Preventive Control Plan (PCP)** that documents these critical limits and monitoring. Provincial public-health programs increasingly require similar records for restaurants.

> **Memory aid — "PROBE":** *Place in thickest part, Read once stable, Off the bone, Between batches sanitize, Enter in log.*

### A-2.01.03P — Read Various Types of Thermometers

**Performance Criteria:** read various types of thermometers
**Evidence of Attainment:** various types of thermometers are read according to manufacturers' specifications.

#### Reading and interpreting

##### Digital instant-read
- Power on, insert, wait for the display to settle.
- Most read to **±1 °C** accuracy.
- **Hold-button** on many models freezes the last reading.
- Replace battery yearly or when readings drift.

##### Analog bimetal dial
- Dial face calibrated in °C and/or °F.
- **Sensing zone is along the stem (typically 4–5 cm)**, not the tip.
- Insert at least **5 cm / 2 in** into food, or angle into thinner cuts.
- Wait 15–30 seconds for the needle to stop moving.
- Adjustable nut under the dial allows recalibration.

##### Infrared / laser
- Aim the dot at a clean surface.
- Distance-to-spot ratio matters (typical 8:1 to 12:1) — at 1 m, you're measuring a 10–12 cm spot.
- **Emissivity** of the surface affects accuracy: shiny stainless steel and aluminum reflect IR and give false-low readings. Most kitchen IR units assume an emissivity of ~0.95 (matte, organic surfaces).
- Use for: walk-in air, reach-in air, holding-well water, receiving-case surface, equipment outer temperatures.
- **Do not** use as the internal-temperature instrument.

#### Calibration — the foundation of trust

A thermometer that reads ±2 °C off is a **false sense of safety**. Calibrate using one or both of these standard methods. **[Best Practice — required under HACCP/PCP]**

##### Ice-point calibration (preferred, daily/weekly)
1. Fill a glass with **crushed ice**, top with cold water, stir 30 seconds.
2. Insert thermometer probe (not touching the glass).
3. Reading should be **0 °C / 32 °F**.
4. Adjust if needed (digital: per manufacturer; bimetal: turn the nut under the dial with a wrench).

##### Boiling-point calibration (alternative, less common at altitude)
1. Bring water to a **rolling boil**.
2. Insert probe (away from sides/bottom).
3. Should read **100 °C / 212 °F at sea level** (water boils at slightly lower temperatures at altitude — Calgary ≈ 96 °C, mountain towns lower).
4. Adjust accordingly.

**Recalibrate:**
- Daily or weekly (per operation HACCP plan).
- After being **dropped**.
- After exposure to **extreme temperature swings** (fryer to walk-in).
- When **readings look suspicious**.

### A-2.01.04P — Monitor Temperature of Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** monitor temperature of equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** temperature of equipment is monitored to ensure safety of products according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### Standard Canadian equipment temperature thresholds

| Equipment | Required temperature | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Refrigerator / walk-in cooler** | **≤ 4 °C / 40 °F** | Internal air temp; many operations target 1–3 °C for buffer |
| **Freezer / walk-in freezer** | **≤ −18 °C / 0 °F** | For long-term frozen storage; some products require lower (e.g., sashimi-grade −20 °C for parasite control) |
| **Hot holding** (bain-marie, steam table, hot well, cabinet) | **≥ 60 °C / 140 °F** | Internal food temperature, not equipment setting |
| **High-temp dishwasher final rinse** | **≥ 82 °C / 180 °F** | Verified with thermolabel or thermocouple |
| **Low-temp / chemical dishwasher final rinse** | Sanitizer at correct ppm (e.g., chlorine ≈ 50 ppm) | Verified with test strip |
| **Sushi rice (acidified)** | < pH 4.6 if held at room temperature | Otherwise treated as TCS and held in danger-zone time limits |

#### Monitoring routine
- **Twice-daily air temperature logs** for all refrigeration units — typical: open of business and pre-service.
- **Hourly to bi-hourly hot-hold checks** during service.
- **Thermometers permanently placed** in walk-ins/reach-ins; many ops use **wireless data loggers** with alarms.
- **External alarms** on walk-ins for after-hours breakdown.

#### Refrigeration physics — why doors matter
- Opening a walk-in door for **15 seconds** can raise interior air temperature **5–10 °C** transiently.
- Hot product placed inside can heat neighbouring TCS items into the danger zone — **always cool before storage** (see corrective measures below).
- **Air circulation**: do not block fans with stacked dunnage; pack with airflow gaps.
- **Frost build-up** on coils reduces efficiency. Coils require periodic defrost cycles and seasonal cleaning. **[Best Practice / Operational]**
- **Refrigerator stocking hierarchy** (top → bottom):
  1. Ready-to-eat foods
  2. Whole/intact seafood
  3. Whole cuts of beef / pork / lamb
  4. Ground meats
  5. Whole poultry, ground poultry, marinating raw protein
  Rationale: any drip travels downward; the food requiring the highest cook temperature is stored lowest, so a drip from above is destined for a high-temperature kill step.

### A-2.01.05P — Hold and Store Finished Product

**Performance Criteria:** hold and store finished product
**Evidence of Attainment:** finished product is held and stored at temperatures and time limits according to company policies and jurisdictional regulations.

#### Hot holding rules
- Hot food must remain **≥ 60 °C / 140 °F** measured internally. **[Regulatory under provincial public-health food premises regulations]**
- Hot-holding equipment is **for holding, not reheating**. Cold or warm food placed in a hot well will sit in the danger zone for hours.
- Cover product, stir periodically, replenish from stock rather than topping up.

#### Cold holding rules
- Cold TCS food must remain **≤ 4 °C / 40 °F**. **[Regulatory]**
- Salad bars, raw bars, and refrigerated displays must hold cold temperature throughout the display; ice baths are common.
- Keep covers on whenever possible.

#### Time as a public-health control (the "4-hour rule")
When TCS food is **deliberately held outside temperature control** (e.g., a butter station at room temperature, or a buffet item without warming), some jurisdictions allow **Time as a Public Health Control**:
- Mark the food with the **time it was removed** from temperature control.
- **Maximum 4 hours**, then discard.
- This option requires a written procedure, training, and approval/authorization in many provinces. **[Regulatory — varies by province]**

#### Service-line storage practice
- **FIFO (First In, First Out)** rotation prevents older product from being forgotten behind newer stock.
- **Day-dot labels** with prep date and use-by date — many operations colour-code by day of the week.
- **Use-by dates** for cooked/RTE refrigerated items: commonly **maximum 7 days from prep**, including prep day. **[Best Practice — varies by province; some specify 4–7 days]**
- **Allergen separation** — peanut, sesame, gluten-free items isolated from general production.
- **Cover everything** in storage — uncovered food is a contamination magnet.

#### Storage hierarchy (recap)
Top → bottom in cold storage:
1. RTE
2. Whole seafood
3. Whole cuts beef/pork/lamb
4. Ground meats
5. Whole poultry, ground poultry

### A-2.01.06P — Take Corrective Measures

**Performance Criteria:** take corrective measures
**Evidence of Attainment:** corrective measures are taken by applying further cooling or heating to ensure required temperature is reached according to company policies and jurisdictional regulations.

This is the **HACCP "corrective action" step** in action. When monitoring shows a critical limit has been missed, the cook must act — and document.

#### Corrective measure decision tree

##### Cooking — internal temperature not reached
- **Continue cooking** until the correct internal temperature is hit.
- Re-probe in multiple spots for large items.
- Document: time, temp, action.

##### Hot holding — temperature below 60 °C
- **If discovered within 2 hours** of dropping below 60 °C: reheat rapidly to **74 °C / 165 °F** (within 2 hours) and return to hot holding. **[Best Practice]**
- **If duration unknown or > 2 hours**: discard.

##### Cooling — too slow
- **If product has not reached 20 °C within 2 hours**: take active corrective action (divide into smaller portions, ice bath, blast chill, ice wand).
- **If product has not reached 4 °C within 6 hours total**: in most jurisdictions, **discard**. **[Regulatory in many provinces; Best Practice everywhere]**

##### Cold holding — above 4 °C
- **If briefly above 4 °C (e.g., during stocking)**: return to cold storage and verify rapid return to ≤ 4 °C.
- **Investigate the cause**: door left open, overstocking blocking airflow, equipment failure.
- **If equipment failure** and product has been > 4 °C for > 2 hours total (and/or > 7 °C for any extended period): treat as compromised and discard TCS items.

#### Cooling — the rule that gets cooks in trouble

**Canadian standard cooling timeline (Health Canada / CFIA-aligned):**

> **60 °C → 20 °C within 2 hours**
> **20 °C → 4 °C within an additional 4 hours**
> **Total: ≤ 6 hours from hot to cold.**

> **[Regulatory / Best Practice — adopted into many provincial food premises regulations and CFIA Preventive Control Plans]**

Why this rule:
- The **20 °C midpoint** ensures product spends minimal time in the upper danger zone (20–60 °C) where many pathogens grow fastest.
- Spore-formers (*Clostridium perfringens*, *Bacillus cereus*) are particular cooling-window threats — they survive cooking as spores and germinate as the food cools through this band.

##### Cooling methods, ranked by speed
| Method | Speed | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Blast chiller** | Fastest | Large batches; shallow pans of soup/sauce; meatloaf, casseroles |
| **Ice bath + stirring** | Fast | Stocks, sauces, soups in stainless containers |
| **Ice wand / paddle in product** | Fast | Tall vessels (stockpots) — cools from core |
| **Divide into shallow pans (≤ 5 cm / 2 in deep)** | Fast | Casseroles, stews; high surface-area-to-volume |
| **Add cold ingredients** | Fast | Hot stock + ice (counts toward total volume); chilled cooked rice mixed with cold dressing |
| **Walk-in cooler (uncovered, shallow)** | Slow | Final cooling stage after rapid drop |
| **Walk-in cooler (deep covered cambro)** | Dangerous | **Not acceptable** — core remains in danger zone for hours |

> ⚠️ **Common failure:** A deep 20 L cambro of stock in the walk-in. The centre can remain > 20 °C for 8+ hours. This is one of the leading causes of *C. perfringens* and *B. cereus* food poisoning in commercial kitchens.

##### Cooling sequence — best practice
1. **Pre-cool** by stirring with ice wand, ice bath, or splitting into shallow pans **on the counter** (rapid heat loss to room air).
2. **Once product is at ~20 °C**, move to walk-in or blast chiller.
3. **Keep uncovered or vented** until cold (covering traps heat).
4. **Cover and label** once at 4 °C.
5. **Log the cooling** with start time, midpoint, and final temperature.

#### Reheating — done right
- Reheat **TCS leftovers to 74 °C / 165 °F** internal, **within 2 hours**. **[Regulatory]**
- Use stovetop, oven, steamer, combi, or microwave — **not** the steam table.
- **Once only**: reheat, serve, discard remainder. Repeated thermal cycling damages texture and microbiologically stacks risk (each cycle gives spore-formers another chance to grow during cooldown).
- After reaching 74 °C, hold at ≥ 60 °C or serve immediately.

#### Impacts of improper re-thermalization on quality and safety

**Safety impacts:**
- **Surviving spores** (*Bacillus cereus*, *Clostridium perfringens*, *Clostridium botulinum*) can germinate during slow reheating in the danger zone.
- **Pre-formed toxins** (*Staph aureus*, *Bacillus cereus* emetic toxin) are **heat-stable** — even correct reheating will not detoxify the food.
- **Pathogen survival** if internal temp never hits 74 °C (cold spots in microwave reheating are a textbook example).
- **Cross-contamination** if reheated food contacts raw-stage utensils or boards.

**Quality impacts:**
- **Protein denaturation cascade** — already-cooked proteins become tough, dry, rubbery with each reheating cycle.
- **Starch retrogradation** — cooked starches (rice, pasta, potato) recrystallize on cooling and don't fully reverse, leaving gritty texture.
- **Moisture loss** — repeated heating drives off water; sauces break, vegetables go limp.
- **Fat oxidation** — repeated heating accelerates rancidity, off-flavours, and loss of nutritional value.
- **Maillard / browning compound degradation** — pleasant roasted flavours get bitter on re-cooking.
- **Cross-contamination risk** from using reheating equipment that wasn't sanitized between cycles.

> **Operational rule of thumb:** plan ahead so you can produce, cool quickly, and reheat *once* to order — avoid the third heating cycle entirely.

### A-2.01.07P — Discard Product

**Performance Criteria:** discard product
**Evidence of Attainment:** product is discarded according to company policies and jurisdictional regulations.

#### When to discard TCS food (mandatory)

- Held in danger zone for **> 4 hours** (total cumulative).
- Cooling failed (did not reach 4 °C within 6 hours total).
- Cooking critical limit not met and cannot be corrected (e.g., RTE item served too cool by mistake).
- Visible spoilage: off-odour, slime, mould, gas in vacuum bag, discoloration.
- **Foreign body contamination** (glass, metal, hair, plastic, pest).
- **Chemical contamination** (cleaner overspray, sanitizer overflow, suspected pesticide).
- **Allergen cross-contact** with an allergen-restricted order — discard and remake.
- **Expired use-by date** even if no visible defect.
- **Suspected illness link** — if a guest reports illness, retain samples sealed and labelled for public health, and discard service stock pending investigation.
- **Power outage / refrigeration failure** with TCS food > 4 °C for > 4 hours.

#### Why discarding "looks-fine" food is sometimes correct
Many spoilage organisms produce visible cues (slime, off-smell). **Most pathogens do not.** *Salmonella*, *E. coli*, *Listeria*, *C. botulinum* (in low-oxygen vacuum packs) can be present in high doses without any visual or olfactory warning. **Time-temperature abuse is invisible.** Logs and discipline are the only defence.

#### How to discard
- **Bag and seal** discarded food to prevent reuse.
- **Label as waste** in some HACCP plans ("Reject" or "Hold for disposal").
- **Document**: item, weight/quantity, reason, date, initials. Critical for food-cost analysis and HACCP review.
- **Sanitize** any equipment that contacted discarded product.
- **Investigate root cause** — don't just discard and repeat the failure.

#### Discarding ≠ rejecting at receiving
- **At receiving**: refuse delivery (do not accept) if the truck temperature is wrong, packaging is damaged, or product is past expiry. The supplier handles disposal.
- **In-house**: discard following internal procedure.

### A-2.01.01L — Safe Conditions and Temperatures

#### Required internal cooking temperatures (Canada — Health Canada guidance)

| Food | Minimum internal temperature | Hold time |
|---|---|---|
| **Whole poultry (chicken, turkey, duck, goose)** | **82 °C / 180 °F** | Instant verification |
| **Poultry pieces, stuffed cuts, stuffing, mixed poultry dishes** | **74 °C / 165 °F** | Instant |
| **Ground meats — beef, pork, lamb, veal** | **71 °C / 160 °F** | Instant |
| **Ground poultry** | **74 °C / 165 °F** | Instant |
| **Mechanically tenderized beef** (Canada-specific labelling rule since 2014) | **63 °C / 145 °F** internal, with at least two flips during cooking | Instant |
| **Whole cuts — beef, pork, lamb (steaks, roasts)** | **63 °C / 145 °F** (medium-rare) up to **71 °C / 160 °F** | 3-minute rest acceptable at lower end |
| **Fish (finfish)** | **70 °C / 158 °F** per Health Canada guidance; many ops cook to 63–65 °C for service | Visual: opaque, flakes easily |
| **Eggs cooked to order** | **63 °C / 145 °F** until yolk and white firm | Immediate |
| **Egg dishes / casseroles / quiche** | **74 °C / 165 °F** | Instant |
| **Reheating leftovers** | **74 °C / 165 °F** within 2 h | Instant |
| **Sous vide** | Pasteurization at the chosen temperature × time (e.g., 55 °C × 80 min for chicken breast equivalent kill) | Per validated pasteurization table |

> **[Regulatory basis]** Health Canada publishes these as recommended minimum internal temperatures; provincial public-health programs and CFIA's PCP guidance adopt them widely. Sous-vide pasteurization tables are operation-specific validated processes.

#### Hot and cold holding (recap)
- **Hot hold:** ≥ 60 °C / 140 °F.
- **Cold hold:** ≤ 4 °C / 40 °F.
- **Frozen:** ≤ −18 °C / 0 °F.

#### Cooling time limits
- 60 °C → 20 °C in ≤ 2 h.
- 20 °C → 4 °C in ≤ 4 h.
- **Total ≤ 6 h.**

#### Storing techniques for hot and cold foods
- **Cover, label, date.**
- **FIFO rotation.**
- **Hierarchy of storage** in cold (RTE on top, raw poultry on bottom).
- **Separation of raw and ready-to-eat.**
- **Adequate spacing** for airflow.
- **No food on the floor** — minimum 15 cm / 6 in clearance off the floor in storage. **[Regulatory in most provinces]**

#### Danger zone (recap)
- **4 °C – 60 °C**.
- **2-hour rule** — TCS food held in danger zone < 2 h can be refrigerated and reused; 2–4 h use immediately; > 4 h discard.

#### Health concerns of improper handling
- **Foodborne illness outbreaks** — vomiting, diarrhea, fever, hospitalization, occasional fatalities (esp. *Listeria* in pregnant women, elderly, immunocompromised; STEC HUS in children).
- **Toxin-mediated illness** with rapid onset and no cooking-based remedy.
- **Allergen-related anaphylaxis** if cross-contact.
- **Spoilage-related illness** — less acute but harmful (histamine in scombroid fish, mycotoxins in moldy grains).
- **Regulatory consequences** — closure orders, fines, brand damage.

#### Foodborne illnesses — Canadian top hits

| Illness | Pathogen / cause | Source | Onset | Key control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Salmonellosis** | *Salmonella* | Raw poultry, eggs, fecal contamination | 6–72 h | Cook to temp; hygiene |
| **Campylobacteriosis** | *Campylobacter jejuni* | Raw/undercooked poultry, unpasteurized milk | 2–5 d | Cook poultry to 74 °C; avoid cross-contamination |
| **STEC / HUS** | *E. coli* O157:H7, other STEC | Raw ground beef, leafy greens, unpasteurized juices | 1–10 d | Cook ground beef ≥ 71 °C; produce wash; hygiene |
| **Listeriosis** | *Listeria monocytogenes* | Deli meats, soft cheese, smoked fish, sprouts | days–weeks | Cold-chain integrity; environmental hygiene; date control |
| **Norovirus gastroenteritis** | Norovirus | Infected handlers, oysters, contact | 12–48 h | Hand hygiene; exclude ill workers |
| ***Staph*** **food poisoning** | *S. aureus* enterotoxin | Cooks with cuts/skin infections; danger-zone holding | 1–6 h | Hygiene; temperature control |
| ***Clostridium perfringens*** | Cooked meats, stews, gravies | Slow cooling and warm holding | 6–24 h | Cooling rule (60→20→4) |
| ***Bacillus cereus*** | Cooked rice, starches | Warm-held cooked starches | 1–16 h | Cool rice rapidly; do not hold cooked rice warm > 2 h |
| ***Clostridium botulinum*** | Improperly canned, vacuum-packed, garlic-in-oil | Anaerobic, low-acid environments | 12–72 h | Avoid home-canning; refrigerate vacuum-packed; acidify garlic-in-oil |
| **Hepatitis A** | Hepatitis A virus | Infected handler, shellfish | 15–50 d | Hygiene; vaccination |
| **Scombroid (histamine)** | Histamine in fish | Tuna, mackerel, mahi, bonito held warm | minutes–hours | Strict cold chain ≤ 4 °C |
| **Anisakiasis / parasites** | *Anisakis*, tapeworms | Raw/undercooked fish | varies | Freeze raw fish (Canada: −20 °C for ≥ 7 days for parasite control) |

#### Potentially Hazardous Foods (PHF / TCS) — recap
- Meats, poultry, seafood, eggs, dairy.
- Cooked rice, pasta, potatoes.
- Cooked vegetables (e.g., cooked beans/peas).
- Tofu and soy products.
- Cut leafy greens, cut tomatoes, cut melons.
- Sprouts.
- Garlic-in-oil mixtures (unless commercially acidified).

### A-2.01.02L — Procedures (Carry-forward from above)

#### Safe handling and storage techniques (Range of Variables)
- **Prevent cross-contamination** — separation, dedicated tools, colour-coded boards, hand and glove changes, sanitize between tasks.
- **Maintain foods at safe temperatures** — danger zone discipline, hot/cold/frozen thresholds, monitoring.
- **Receiving and storing — FIFO** — older stock used first, dated and rotated.

#### Time and temperature control concerning danger zone
- The combined effect of **time × temperature** is what determines microbial load.
- **2 / 4 / 6 framework**:
  - **< 2 h** in danger zone → return to safe storage.
  - **2–4 h** → use now, do not return.
  - **> 4 h** → discard.
  - **Cooling** 6 h total: 2 h to 20 °C, additional 4 h to 4 °C.

Examples of provincial regulations governing temperature control:
- **Ontario** *Food Premises Regulation (O. Reg. 493/17)* under the *Health Protection and Promotion Act*.
- **British Columbia** *Food Premises Regulation* under the *Public Health Act*.

#### HACCP — the 7 principles applied to A-2.01

| Principle | Applied to temperature control |
|---|---|
| **1. Hazard analysis** | Identify pathogens that survive/grow in TCS items at danger-zone temps |

| **4. Monitoring** | Thermometer checks at each CCP at defined intervals |
| **5. Corrective action** | Discard, reheat, continue cooking, repair equipment |
| **6. Verification** | Calibrate thermometers; review logs; audit |

## 3. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Receiving a delivery of chicken
1. Inspect truck: refrigerated, clean, no off-odours.
2. **Probe a box** of chicken: should be **≤ 4 °C**.
3. Inspect packaging: intact, no leaks.
4. Verify expiry dates and lot numbers.
5. Reject (refuse delivery) if temperature is wrong or packaging compromised — **document on the receiving log**.
6. If accepted: move to walk-in within 30 minutes, store on bottom shelf, FIFO rotation, dated and labelled.

### Scenario 2 — Cooling 20 L of chicken stock at end of shift
1. **Wrong way:** lid on, into walk-in. (Centre stays > 20 °C for 8+ hours; *Clostridium perfringens* risk.)
2. **Right way:**
   - Strain into shallow stainless containers (≤ 10 cm / 4 in depth) OR keep in stockpot in an ice bath in the sink.
   - Stir with an ice wand or ladle.
   - Confirm **20 °C by 2 h** with probe; document.
   - Transfer to walk-in, uncovered or vented.
   - Confirm **4 °C by 6 h total** with probe; document.
   - Cover, label, date, store with RTE storage hierarchy.

### Scenario 3 — Steam table item reads 54 °C halfway through service
1. **Probe again** in multiple spots to confirm.
2. **Investigate**: water in the bain-marie low? Lid removed? Item just dropped in cold?
3. **If under 60 °C for < 2 h** and rapidly reheatable: pull, reheat to 74 °C within 2 h, return to steam table at ≥ 60 °C. Document.
4. **If under 60 °C for > 2 h or duration unknown**: discard. Document.
5. **Root cause**: refill water in bain-marie; verify thermostat; service or repair.

### Scenario 4 — Reheating yesterday's beef stew for service
1. Verify the stew was properly cooled yesterday (check log).
2. Bring to **74 °C internal within 2 hours** on the stovetop or in the combi oven.
3. Stir thoroughly to eliminate cold spots.
4. Probe at multiple points; document.
5. Transfer to hot hold ≥ 60 °C OR serve immediately.
6. Do not reheat the same stew twice.

### Scenario 5 — Power outage for 90 minutes overnight
1. Keep walk-in and freezer doors closed.
2. On return of power: **probe internal air temperature** of each unit; then **probe TCS product temperatures**.
3. **If product is still ≤ 4 °C** (or ≤ 7 °C for cooling outage scenarios per some provincial guidance): safe to retain; log and monitor.
4. **If product temperature has risen above 4 °C for > 4 hours** cumulative: discard TCS items. Document.
5. **Frozen items** that have partially thawed and remain at ≤ 4 °C may be cooked through and used **once**, but cannot be refrozen. **[Best Practice]**
6. Notify public health if a major event triggers their thresholds (varies by province).

## 4. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **"It looks fine, smell it"** — pathogens are usually invisible and odourless.
- **Storing hot product in the walk-in with a lid on** to "cool down".
- **Filling the steam table with cold product** and assuming it will heat up.
- **Using an infrared thermometer for internal cook temperature**.
- **Probing through to the pan bottom** for a "fast" reading.
- **Reusing the probe between raw and cooked** without sanitizing.
- **No calibration routine** — thermometers off by 2–3 °C.
- **Cooling deep stockpots** in the walk-in.
- **Topping up** old steam-table product with new instead of fresh-panning.
- **Reheating in the steam table** from refrigerated.
- **Refreezing thawed TCS items** without a cooking kill step in between.
- **Letting raw chicken drips reach RTE items** on the same shelf.
- **Ignoring the cooling log** because "we always make it in time."
- **Holding cooked rice warm at room temperature** — *Bacillus cereus* paradise.

## 6. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with A-2.01.

1. **Food Danger Zone:** **4 °C – 60 °C (40 °F – 140 °F).** Bacteria can double ≈ every 20 minutes.
2. **TCS / PHF** foods: high-protein, high-moisture, neutral-pH foods that support pathogen growth.
3. **Cold hold ≤ 4 °C**, **hot hold ≥ 60 °C**, **frozen ≤ −18 °C**.
4. **Cooling rule:** **60 → 20 °C in 2 h, 20 → 4 °C in 4 h, total ≤ 6 h.**
5. **Reheating rule:** to **74 °C / 165 °F within 2 hours**, once only.
6. **Cooking internal temperatures (Canada):**
   - Whole poultry **82 °C**
   - Poultry pieces / stuffed / mixed-poultry / reheats **74 °C**
   - Ground beef/pork/lamb **71 °C**; ground poultry **74 °C**
   - Whole cuts beef/pork/lamb **63 °C** (medium-rare) up to 71 °C
   - Fish 70 °C (Health Canada guidance)
   - Eggs cooked to order 63 °C; egg dishes 74 °C
7. **2-hour rule** (TCS in danger zone): < 2 h reuse, 2–4 h use now, > 4 h discard.
8. **Thermometer calibration:** ice-point method (0 °C / 32 °F in ice slurry); recalibrate after drops, extreme temperature swings, or per HACCP schedule.
9. **Probe technique:** thickest part, avoid bone, avoid pan bottom, sanitize between uses, wait for stable reading.
10. **Infrared thermometers measure surface only** — never use to verify internal cook temperature.
11. **Dishwasher high-temp final rinse ≥ 82 °C / 180 °F.**
12. **Storage hierarchy (top → bottom in cold storage):** RTE → seafood → whole red meats → ground meats → whole poultry.
13. **FIFO** rotation; date labelling; cooked RTE refrigerated items typically used within ~7 days.
14. **15 cm / 6 in off the floor** for stored food.
15. **HACCP 7 principles** — apply directly to temperature control: hazard analysis → CCPs → critical limits → monitoring → corrective action → verification → records.
16. **CFIA SFCR** requires federally-registered operators to maintain a **Preventive Control Plan (PCP)** documenting temperature CCPs and monitoring.
17. **Provincial public-health food premises regulations** codify the 4 °C / 60 °C / 74 °C thresholds for restaurants.
18. **Foodborne illnesses to know:** *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter*, STEC (incl. *E. coli* O157:H7), *Listeria*, *Staph aureus* (heat-stable toxin), *Clostridium perfringens*, *Bacillus cereus*, *C. botulinum*, norovirus, Hepatitis A, scombroid histamine, anisakiasis.
19. **Spore-formers** (*C. perfringens*, *B. cereus*, *C. botulinum*) survive cooking — control via cooling speed and acid/oxygen environment.
20. **Heat-stable toxins** (*Staph*, emetic *B. cereus*) are **not destroyed by reheating** — prevent formation by temperature and hygiene control.
21. **Reheated food cannot fix temperature-abused food.** Discard.
22. **Time as a Public Health Control** (4-hour rule for deliberately out-of-temp service) requires written procedure and approval; varies by province.
23. **Reject deliveries** at receiving if temperature, packaging, or dating is wrong — easier and cheaper than discarding after acceptance.
24. **Power outage rule of thumb:** TCS in walk-in safe if it never rose above 4 °C; rapidly cook-and-serve once if partially thawed; discard if > 4 °C for > 4 h cumulative.
25. **Parasite control for raw-served fish:** Health Canada guidance — freeze at **−20 °C for ≥ 7 days** (or equivalent time/temperature) prior to raw service.
26. **Cooling failures** are the #1 commercial-kitchen cause of foodborne illness via *C. perfringens* and *B. cereus*.
27. **Reheating impacts on quality:** protein toughening, starch retrogradation, moisture loss, fat oxidation, flavour degradation — plan for single reheat to order.
28. **Discard documentation matters** — log item, reason, quantity for HACCP and cost analysis.
