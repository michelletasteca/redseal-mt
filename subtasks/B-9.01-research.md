## Overview: Communication as a Professional Culinary Skill

In a commercial kitchen operating at full service, a breakdown in communication is not a minor inconvenience — it can mean the wrong dish reaches a guest with a life-threatening allergy, a fire hazard goes unaddressed, a cook gets injured by someone carrying a hot pot around a blind corner, or a table of eight receives their food staggered over 20 minutes.

Communication in a professional kitchen is a **technical skill** developed alongside knife skills and cooking technique. It has its own vocabulary, its own protocols, and its own professional standards. The brigade system that governs most commercial kitchen operations is fundamentally a communication system — a structured hierarchy that routes information from the guest to the line, from the line to the chef, and from the chef back to the plate.

A Red Seal Cook must be able to communicate effectively:
- Upward to supervisors and chefs
- Laterally to colleagues and co-workers
- Downward to apprentices and junior staff
- Outward to guests/consumers, suppliers, and service teams
- In writing, verbally, and non-verbally

### B-9.01.01P — Demonstrate Communication Practices with Individuals or in a Group

**Performance Criteria:** Demonstrate communication practices with individuals or in a group
**Evidence of Attainment:** Instructions and messages are understood by all parties involved

---

#### Communication Modes in the Professional Kitchen

Four modes of communication are used in commercial food service environments. Each has specific applications, strengths, and limitations.

**1. Verbal Communication**

The primary mode in a live kitchen. Must be:
- **Clear** — no ambiguity about what is being requested or communicated
- **Direct** — brief; no lengthy explanations during service
- **Loud enough** — commercial kitchens operate at 80–100 dB; equivalent to a factory floor; communication must cut through noise
- **Acknowledged** — never assume a message was received; require a response ("Heard" / "Yes, Chef")

Best used for: immediate instructions during service, firing orders, safety warnings, real-time coordination.

**2. Non-Verbal Communication**

Non-verbal signals communicate attitude, readiness, urgency, and safety without words. In a noisy kitchen, non-verbal cues are essential supplements to verbal communication.

| Signal | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Raised hand | "Hold" / "Wait" / "Pause" |
| Eye contact + nod | Acknowledgment / understanding |
| Thumbs up | Confirmed / ready |
| Pointing at item | Directing attention to a problem or task |
| Open palm toward someone | "Stop" / "Don't touch" |
| Arm outstretched while walking | "Hot item passing" / "Coming through" |

Non-verbal communication also includes **body language** that reflects attitude:
- Upright posture communicates confidence and engagement
- Slumped shoulders or averted gaze communicates disengagement
- Direct eye contact communicates honesty and attentiveness (within cultural context)
- Crossed arms can signal defensiveness during feedback

> **Cultural Awareness:** Eye contact, personal space, and gesture norms vary across cultures. In some cultures, avoiding direct eye contact with a superior is a sign of respect, not inattention. In a diverse kitchen team, be aware that non-verbal norms are not universal.

**3. Written Communication**

Written communication provides a record, allows reflection, and is not dependent on real-time presence.

| Written Communication Type | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Order ticket (docket) | Records guest order; guides cooking and plating |
| Recipe card | Standard reference for preparation; training document |
| Prep list | Organizes tasks by priority and quantity |
| HACCP log / temperature log | Food safety record; legal documentation |
| Incident report | Documents accidents, near-misses, food safety failures |
| Allergen communication card | Guest-facing or kitchen-facing allergen documentation |
| Daily specials board | Communicates new items to FOH team and guests |
| Shelf labels / storage labels | FIFO; allergen identification; date labelling |
| Staff schedule | Time management communication |
| Maintenance request | Equipment issues |

> **Operational Recommendation:** HACCP logs, temperature records, and incident reports are **legal documents**. They must be accurate, complete, dated, and signed. Falsifying food safety records is a serious professional and legal violation.

**4. Digital Communication**

Increasingly important in modern food service operations:

| Platform/Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| POS system (e.g., Lightspeed, Square, Toast) | Orders flow from FOH to kitchen; kitchen display systems (KDS) replace paper tickets in many operations |
| Messaging apps (WhatsApp, Slack, Line) | Shift scheduling, team updates, allergy alerts, product information |
| Email | Supplier communications, HR, formal notices |
| Scheduling software (7shifts, HotSchedules) | Staff scheduling, availability, shift swaps |
| Kitchen display systems (KDS) | Real-time order management; reduces miscommunication from illegible handwritten tickets |
| Temperature monitoring systems | IoT sensors log refrigeration temperatures automatically; alerts sent digitally when TDZ breaches occur |

---

#### Communication in High-Noise Environments

Commercial kitchens are among the loudest workplaces in any industry. NIOSH (National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health) and WorkSafeBC data suggest kitchen noise levels regularly exceed 85 dB — the threshold above which hearing damage can occur with prolonged exposure.

**Communication strategies for noisy environments:**
- Use short, standardized phrases (kitchen call-outs) rather than sentences
- Position yourself for direct face-to-face communication when delivering critical messages
- Establish visual signal protocols for situations where verbal communication is unreliable
- Use written boards or kitchen display systems to communicate during peak service
- Require verbal acknowledgment of every instruction delivered

> **Regulatory Note (Common Industry Practice):** Prolonged exposure to noise above 85 dB requires hearing protection under provincial OH&S legislation. Kitchen workers may be entitled to hearing protection as PPE. Verify requirements with your provincial OH&S authority.

### B-9.01.02P — Listen Using Active Listening Practices

**Performance Criteria:** Listen using active listening practices
**Evidence of Attainment:** Steps of active listening are utilized

---

#### Active Listening: The Five Steps

Active listening is not passive reception of sound — it is an engaged process that ensures understanding. The five steps from the Range of Variables:

**Step 1 — Hearing**
Physical reception of the message. In a kitchen: position yourself to hear; remove distractions; face the speaker.
- Challenge: background noise, multiple conversations simultaneously
- Technique: step toward the speaker; acknowledge you heard

**Step 2 — Interpreting**
Assigning meaning to what was heard. This is where misunderstandings begin — the listener's interpretation may differ from the speaker's intent.
- Challenge: kitchen terminology unfamiliarity; language barriers in multicultural teams; technical terms assumed to be understood
- Technique: if unsure of meaning, do not guess — ask for clarification immediately

**Step 3 — Reflecting**
Internally processing the message — considering its implications, identifying what it means for your actions.
- Challenge: the fast pace of service leaves little time for reflection; snap interpretations lead to errors
- Technique: pause briefly before responding during non-urgent communication; never act on unclear instructions without confirmation

**Step 4 — Responding**
Communicating a reply or acknowledgment back to the speaker. In a kitchen: "Heard," "Yes, Chef," "Understood."
- Challenge: responding without truly understanding (saying "yes" to avoid looking confused)
- Technique: respond only after genuinely processing the message; if unclear, state it openly: "I want to make sure I understood — you want me to..."

**Step 5 — Paraphrasing**
Restating the message in your own words to confirm understanding. This is the most powerful confirmation technique.
- Challenge: may feel unnecessary during service; may seem slow
- Technique: use brief paraphrasing for complex instructions: "So I fire the fish, then sauce, then garnish — correct?"

**Why Paraphrasing Is Essential for Kitchen Safety:**

A server paraphrasing an allergen alert at the pass ("This is the gluten-free pasta for Table 4, Seat 2, correct?") prevents serving the wrong plate. A cook paraphrasing a new procedure ("So after the pasta is cooked, I transfer to the cast iron to finish in butter — not keep it in the pasta water?") prevents quality failures.

**The Paraphrase Protocol for Dietary Accommodations:**

Before any dietary-modified plate leaves the kitchen:
1. Cook announces: "Dietary plate, Table 4, Seat 2, GF pasta, contains soy"
2. Expediter paraphrases back: "Seat 2 gets GF pasta, soy present, got it?"
3. Server paraphrases to guest: "This is your gluten-free pasta — confirmed with the kitchen"

---

#### Barriers to Active Listening in a Kitchen

| Barrier | Effect | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Noise** | Words are physically missed or misheard | Face-to-face communication; repeat key words; written backup for complex instructions |
| **Distraction** | Mental presence elsewhere (monitoring a pan, counting plates) | Deliberately pause task when receiving critical instruction |
| **Assumption** | Listener assumes they know what will be said and stops listening | Remain engaged through full message; paraphrase to verify |
| **Language barriers** | Different first languages; kitchen jargon | Use simple, clear language; avoid idioms; use demonstration alongside verbal |
| **Emotional state** | Stress, fatigue, frustration during a rush | Awareness of own state; extra confirmation steps under stress |
| **Hierarchy anxiety** | Fear of appearing incompetent prevents asking for clarification | Culture of psychological safety; "there are no stupid questions" norm |

### B-9.01.03P — Receive and Respond to Feedback on Work

**Performance Criteria:** Receive and respond to feedback
**Evidence of Attainment:** Response to feedback indicates understanding and corrective measures are taken

---

#### Receiving Feedback Professionally

Feedback is one of the most valuable learning tools in a trade. In a kitchen hierarchy, feedback often comes from above — from a sous chef, head chef, or executive chef — frequently under time pressure and sometimes with significant bluntness.

**The Professional Response to Feedback:**

| Response | Professional | Unprofessional |
|---|---|---|
| **Verbal** | "Yes, Chef. I'll correct that." | "But I thought..." / "That's not what the recipe said..." |
| **Body language** | Attentive; eye contact; no visible defensiveness | Crossed arms; eye-roll; turning away |
| **Action** | Immediately correct the identified issue | Continue the same way; make changes half-heartedly |
| **Follow-up** | Check back with supervisor to confirm correction is satisfactory | Assume the correction was adequate without verification |

**Why defensive responses to feedback are counterproductive:**
- In a kitchen, defensive responses slow down service
- They signal to the supervisor that the cook is not safe to delegate to (if they argue when corrected, they may not apply corrections in the future)
- Feedback is information — the chef is telling you how to improve; the information has no value if you don't receive it

**Receiving Criticism vs. Harassment:**

Not all negative feedback is harassment. A clear distinction:

| Constructive Feedback | Harassment |
|---|---|
| Targets the work ("This sauce is over-reduced") | Targets the person ("You're incompetent") |
| Specific and actionable ("Add more cream at this point") | Vague and personal ("You always mess this up") |
| Private or within appropriate context | Public humiliation in front of guests or many colleagues |
| One-time or specific to the incident | Ongoing, patterned targeting |
| Professional tone, even if direct | Demeaning, belittling, threatening |

> **Regulatory Note:** Workplace harassment is governed by provincial OH&S legislation and human rights codes across Canada. Employees have the right to a workplace free from harassment and discrimination. If you believe you are experiencing harassment (not just tough feedback), document incidents and report to a supervisor, HR, or through your province's WorkSafe complaint process.

### B-9.01.04P — Explain and Provide Feedback

**Performance Criteria:** Explain and provide feedback
**Evidence of Attainment:** Explanation and timely feedback is provided and task carried out as directed

---

#### Delivering Effective Feedback

When providing feedback to a junior cook, apprentice, or colleague, the goal is behavioural change — not emotional reaction. Effective feedback is:

**1. Timely:** Given as close to the event as possible while the observation is fresh and the behaviour can be corrected.
- During service: feedback should be brief and immediate ("Sauce too thick — more stock, right now")
- After service: more detailed debrief ("When you plated that duck, the sauce was breaking — here's why and how to fix it")

**2. Specific:** Targeted at a specific behaviour or outcome, not the person's character.
- Effective: "The onions need to be brunoise-cut, about 3mm — these are too large"
- Ineffective: "You can't cut onions properly"

**3. Actionable:** The recipient must be able to do something with the feedback.
- Effective: "Re-cut these to 3mm and re-sauté with a higher pan temperature"
- Ineffective: "Do it better next time"

**4. Respectful:** Even urgent correction can be delivered without demeaning the person.
- Effective: "Stop — this is raw; internal temperature is 58°C; it needs to go back in for another 4 minutes"
- Ineffective: "Are you kidding me? This is raw — how do you not know this?"

**5. Confirmed:** Ensure the feedback was understood and the correction applied.
- "Does that make sense? Can you show me how you'll re-check the temperature before it leaves the station?"

---

#### Feedback During Service vs. Debrief

| Context | Feedback Style | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **During service** | Immediate, brief, action-focused | "More sauce — 90 mL per plate. Fix it now." |
| **Post-service debrief** | Reflective, detailed, developmental | "Tonight the sauce broke twice — let's talk through why and how we prevent it tomorrow" |
| **One-on-one coaching** | Exploratory, open-ended, supportive | "What do you think happened with the sauce? What would you do differently?" |

### B-9.01.05P — Ask Open-Ended Questions to Improve Communication

**Performance Criteria:** Ask open-ended questions
**Evidence of Attainment:** Open-ended questions enhance understanding, on-the-job training and goal setting

---

#### Open-Ended vs. Closed Questions

| Type | Structure | Example | Response Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Closed question** | Can be answered yes/no | "Did you check the temperature?" | "Yes" or "No" — confirms or denies, reveals nothing more |
| **Open-ended question** | Requires elaboration | "How did you verify the internal temperature?" | Reveals understanding, process, and potential gaps |

**Why Open-Ended Questions Are Powerful in Kitchen Training:**

A cook who answers "Yes" to "Did you check the temperature?" may mean:
- "Yes, I used the probe thermometer and hit 74°C" (correct)
- "Yes, it looked done" (incorrect — visual check is not temperature verification)
- "Yes, I guessed" (dangerous)

An open-ended question like "Walk me through how you checked the temperature" reveals the actual process and identifies where knowledge gaps or unsafe shortcuts exist.

**Applications of Open-Ended Questions in the Kitchen:**

| Purpose | Closed Question | Open-Ended Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| **Training verification** | "Did you understand how to make the beurre blanc?" | "Describe the process for mounting a beurre blanc." |
| **Safety check** | "Are you being safe?" | "What are the hazards at your station right now and how are you managing them?" |
| **Goal setting** | "Do you want to improve?" | "What skills are you most focused on developing this month?" |
| **Problem-solving** | "Was the food correct?" | "What do you think caused the sauce to break on that ticket?" |
| **Allergen safety** | "Did you check for allergens?" | "Tell me every ingredient in that dish and which ones are potential allergens." |
| **Communication clarity** | "Did you understand the instruction?" | "What's your first step when you get back to your station?" |

> **Operational Recommendation:** Open-ended questions are particularly valuable when briefing a new cook on a complex or safety-critical procedure. Rather than "Did you get that?", ask "Can you walk me through what you're going to do?" The answer reveals actual comprehension.

### B-9.01.06P — Participate in Safety and Information Meetings

**Performance Criteria:** Participate in safety and information meetings
**Evidence of Attainment:** Meetings are attended; information is relayed and applied

---

#### Types of Safety and Information Meetings in Food Service

**1. Pre-Shift Meeting (Line-Up / Roll Call)**

Held before each service period. Typically 5–10 minutes.

Content typically includes:
- Menu specials and modifications for that service
- Dietary accommodations booked (allergies, Halal, vegan, etc.)
- Staffing changes or station assignments
- Any food quality issues (items running low; 86'd items)
- Product recalls or food safety alerts from the previous shift
- Safety reminders (a spill to be cleaned; a piece of equipment tagged out)
- Guest VIP/special requests

> **Operational Recommendation:** The pre-shift meeting is where allergen information should be communicated to every team member — kitchen and FOH. A server who doesn't know about the allergen at Table 7 is as dangerous as a cook who doesn't know. All teams should receive allergy briefings together.

**2. HACCP and Food Safety Meetings**

Scheduled or as-needed meetings focused on food safety systems.

Content includes:
- Review of temperature logs from recent shifts
- Any near-miss or corrective actions required
- HACCP plan updates
- New food safety regulations or CFIA/PHAC advisories
- Staff training on new procedures

**3. OH&S (Occupational Health and Safety) Meetings**

Required under provincial OH&S legislation for many workplaces.

Content includes:
- Workplace hazard identification
- Incident and near-miss reports
- PPE requirements and updates
- WHMIS updates (new products, new SDS)
- Emergency procedures (fire, evacuation, first aid)

> **Regulatory Note:** Most Canadian provinces require Joint Health and Safety Committees (JHSCs) or Health and Safety Representatives for workplaces above a certain employee threshold (typically 5–20 employees, varying by province). Food service operations must comply with their provincial OH&S Act. Verify requirements with your provincial OH&S authority.

**4. Product Information and Training Meetings**

- New product introductions by suppliers
- Menu change briefings
- Equipment training for new kitchen tools
- Allergen information updates (new products; supplier changes)

---

#### Relaying Information from Meetings

When a cook attends a meeting and other team members are not present (different shifts, off-days), the information must be relayed accurately.

**Best practice for information relay:**
- Take brief notes during the meeting
- Post key information in a visible location (prep board, staff bulletin board)
- Brief incoming shift at shift change
- Flag HACCP-relevant updates directly in the HACCP log if they affect documented procedures

### B-9.01.07P — Share Information

**Performance Criteria:** Share information
**Evidence of Attainment:** Information is shared with people in the workplace

---

#### Critical Information-Sharing Scenarios in a Kitchen

**1. Food Safety Information**
- Temperature anomalies (refrigerator reading 8°C; product may have been in TDZ)
- Expired products discovered in storage
- Suspected contamination or cross-contact events
- Product recalls from CFIA/PHAC or supplier

**2. Allergen Information**
- Guest allergen alerts from reservation or FOH
- Ingredient changes in convenience products (supplier changed formulation)
- New menu items with allergen content

**3. Operational Information**
- 86 items (items now out of stock); must be communicated to FOH immediately
- Prep status (mise en place ready for next shift)
- Shift handover notes (what was completed, what still needs to be done)

**4. Safety Information**
- Equipment malfunction or hazard
- Spill hazards
- Temperature or chemical exposure incident

**The Shift Handover: Critical Information-Sharing Moment**

The handover between shifts is one of the highest-risk moments for information loss. Key information to always communicate at handover:

| Category | Information to Share |
|---|---|
| Food safety | Temperature logs reviewed; any anomalies; products approaching use-by date |
| Inventory | What's been used; what needs to be ordered; any shortages |
| Prep | What mise en place was completed; what's still needed for next service |
| Equipment | Any tagged-out equipment; any repair needed |
| Guests | Any VIP or allergy bookings for next service |
| Incidents | Any food safety near-miss or corrective action taken |

### B-9.01.08P — Communicate with Consumers

**Performance Criteria:** Communicate with consumers
**Evidence of Attainment:** Consumers' preferences are determined through communication and interaction

---

#### Kitchen-to-Consumer Communication Channels

Cooks may not always interact directly with guests, but they communicate through:
- The food itself (quality, temperature, presentation)
- Written materials (menus, allergen menus, dietary certification statements)
- The service team (FOH team relays guest information to kitchen and vice versa)
- Direct interaction (open kitchens, chef's tables, cooking classes, food trucks)

#### Determining Consumer Preferences Through Communication

In direct guest interaction (chef's table, special events, cooking school):
- Use open-ended questions to discover preferences: "Are there any flavours or ingredients you love or want to avoid tonight?"
- Do not assume — ask about dietary requirements, portion preferences, past experiences
- Acknowledge and confirm: "So no shellfish, and you'd prefer lighter sauces — noted"

**Handling Guest Complaints:**

The professional response to a food service complaint:

1. **Listen fully** without interrupting — let the guest complete their concern
2. **Acknowledge** without deflecting: "I'm sorry that's not right. Let me fix that."
3. **Take action** immediately — return to kitchen and correct
4. **Follow up** — ensure the corrected dish reaches the guest and they are satisfied
5. **Document** if the complaint has food safety implications (illness, foreign object, allergen)

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not argue with a guest about whether the complaint is valid
- Do not dismiss concerns about allergens or dietary requirements
- Do not blame another team member in front of the guest
- Do not delay action

> **Operational Note:** A guest complaint handled professionally creates loyalty. Research consistently shows that guests whose complaints are resolved quickly and well often return more reliably than guests who never had a problem. The complaint is an opportunity.

### B-9.01.09P — Coordinate Tasks with Co-Workers

**Performance Criteria:** Coordinate tasks with co-workers
**Evidence of Attainment:** Tasks are coordinated with co-workers and completed in a timely manner

---

#### Kitchen Call-Out System

The professional kitchen has a standardized verbal call-out system that coordinates movement, safety, and task execution. Every cook must know and use these calls:

| Call-Out | Meaning | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **"Behind"** | Someone is directly behind you | Any time you pass behind a colleague; prevents spin-turns into each other |
| **"Corner"** | Turning around a blind corner | Before every corner; prevents collisions with people carrying hot/sharp items |
| **"Hot"** / **"Hot behind"** | Carrying a hot item | Warns others not to turn or move into the path |
| **"Sharp"** / **"Knife"** | Carrying an exposed blade | Knives carried to washing area or through the kitchen |
| **"Heard"** / **"Yes, Chef"** | Acknowledgment of an instruction or order | Any time a direct instruction is given; confirms the message was received |
| **"All day"** | Running total of an item currently on ticket | "Three salmon all day" = there are 3 salmon tickets firing; helps stations manage production |
| **"Fire [item]"** | Begin cooking a specified item | Expediter calls the cooking start time for each course |
| **"Pick up [item]"** | Item is ready at the pass | Ready to be served; do not delay |
| **"86 [item]"** | Item is out of stock | Remove from service; inform FOH |
| **"In the weeds"** | Behind, overwhelmed | Signals need for assistance or adjustment |
| **"On the fly"** | Needed immediately; urgent re-fire | A table's dish was dropped, wrong, or delayed |

> **The "Behind" and "Corner" calls are food safety and personal safety calls.** A collision between a cook carrying a pot of boiling stock and a cook turning at a corner can cause severe burns. These are not optional courtesy calls — they are safety protocols. New cooks must internalize these calls immediately.

---

#### Coordinating Mise en Place Across Stations

During prep, coordination prevents duplication and ensures coverage:
- Communicate prep quantities needed for each station
- Share preparation where possible (one person chiffonades herbs for multiple stations)
- Flag any prep that will not be complete before service

#### Coordinating Plate-Up for a Full Table

When plating a large table simultaneously:
- Expediter calls the table: "Table 12 — 2 salmon, 1 beef, 1 vegetarian"
- All stations acknowledge: "Heard — salmon up in 4"
- Expediter stages timing: "Fire beef and salmon simultaneously — vegetarian ready to plate now"
- All plates reach the pass within the same 60-second window

### B-9.01.10P — Maintain a Respectful Attitude

**Performance Criteria:** Maintain a respectful attitude
**Evidence of Attainment:** A respectful attitude is maintained at all times

---

#### Professionalism in the Modern Commercial Kitchen

The commercial kitchen has historically been associated with high-stress environments and aggressive management styles. This culture is increasingly recognized as harmful, legally problematic, and operationally counterproductive.

**Modern professional kitchen culture recognizes:**
- Psychological safety (the ability to speak up without fear of humiliation) produces better outcomes and catches more food safety problems before they happen
- High staff turnover in aggressive kitchen environments has enormous financial cost: recruiting, hiring, and training a line cook costs thousands of dollars; skilled cooks who leave take institutional knowledge with them
- Respectful kitchens have demonstrably better staff retention, fewer accidents, and higher quality consistency

**Respectful attitude includes:**
- Greeting colleagues at the start of a shift
- Acknowledging contributions from junior staff
- Correcting without humiliating
- Owning mistakes without deflection
- Not undermining colleagues in front of others (especially not in front of guests)
- Supporting team members who are struggling during a hard service

---

#### Diversity in the Workplace

Canadian commercial kitchens are among the most diverse workplaces in the country. The value of diversity:
- Diverse teams bring broader culinary knowledge, technique, and flavour vocabulary
- Multiple language skills improve communication with diverse guests
- Different perspectives improve problem-solving and creativity
- A kitchen culture that genuinely values everyone's contribution attracts better talent

**Practical expressions of valuing diversity:**
- Learn key phrases in the languages of your team members
- Create inclusive social environments (not assuming everyone drinks alcohol at team events)
- Be curious about culinary traditions different from your own — your colleagues are a learning resource
- Ensure communication is accessible to everyone (simple language for key safety communications; translated safety postings where required)

### B-9.01.01L — Trade Terminology

**Learning Outcomes:** Demonstrate knowledge of trade terminology

---

#### Kitchen Trade Terminology Reference

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **All day** | Running total of a menu item currently on open tickets ("Three salmon all day" = 3 portions firing) |
| **Behind** | Warning call when passing behind a colleague |
| **Corner** | Warning call before moving around a blind corner |
| **Heard / Yes, Chef** | Acknowledgment of instruction; confirms message received |
| **Fire** | Begin cooking a specified item immediately |
| **Pick up** | Item ready at pass; service to send to guest now |
| **86 / Eighty-six** | Item is out of stock; remove from service |
| **In the weeds** | Behind on orders; overwhelmed; struggling to keep up |
| **Mise en place** | "Everything in its place" — all prep complete and organized before service |
| **On the fly** | Urgently needed; outside normal service sequence (re-fire for a dropped plate) |
| **Push / Rush** | Urgently needed; prioritize |
| **The pass** | The counter between kitchen and FOH where completed dishes are plated and handed to service staff |
| **Expo / Expediter** | Person at the pass who coordinates between kitchen and FOH service |
| **Ticket** | Order slip from FOH; specifies what the table ordered |
| **Check / Chit** | Same as ticket; also refers to the bill for the table |
| **Cover** | One individual diner; one meal served ("We did 200 covers tonight") |
| **Turn** | One table seating (a restaurant with 60 seats doing 2 turns = 120 covers) |
| **VIP** | Very important person/table; requires extra attention |
| **Comp** | Complimentary — food or drink provided without charge |
| **Stage / Stagier** | Culinary intern; someone working in a kitchen for learning and experience (from French "stagiaire") |
| **Dead** | Finished; food that has been killed (off heat and no longer cooking) |
| **Killing it** | Overwhelmed by volume; the kitchen is being "killed" by ticket volume |
| **86 the fire** | Extinguish an open flame immediately (safety call) |
| **Standard** | Short for "standardized recipe" |
| **Mirepoix** | Classic aromatic base: 2 parts onion, 1 part carrot, 1 part celery (by weight) |

### B-9.01.02L — Effective Communication Practices

**Learning Outcomes:** Demonstrate knowledge of effective communication practices

---

#### Importance of Effective Verbal and Non-Verbal Communication

**Verbal communication failures in kitchens directly cause:**
- Wrong dishes served to guests (including allergen accidents)
- Plating errors (two covers of the same item plated differently)
- Timing failures (one course at a table arrives 15 minutes after another)
- Safety incidents (cook unaware someone is behind them; collision with hot item)
- Food waste (wrong quantities prepared because mis-en-place requirement was misunderstood)

**Non-verbal communication failures cause:**
- Supervisors missing that a cook is struggling (non-verbal distress signals not noticed)
- Apprentices afraid to ask for clarification (reading the chef's body language as hostile)
- Guests feeling dismissed (cook/server avoiding eye contact)

#### Personal Responsibilities and Attitudes for On-Job Success

| Responsibility | Application |
|---|---|
| **Asking questions** | Never guess when uncertain about a food safety, quality, or safety procedure. A question takes 10 seconds; an error costs minutes of re-work or hours of illness |
| **Working safely** | Following OH&S, HACCP, and kitchen safety protocols consistently; not taking shortcuts |
| **Accepting constructive feedback** | Treating feedback as information; applying corrections immediately; thanking specific feedback |
| **Time management and punctuality** | Arriving before scheduled start time (pre-shift setup); meeting prep deadlines; completing tasks without causing bottlenecks for other stations |
| **Respect for people** | Treating everyone in the workplace with dignity; no harassment; including diverse team members |
| **Good stewardship** | Not wasting product; returning tools to their place; caring for shared equipment; respecting property |
| **Efficient work practice** | Avoiding unnecessary movement; combining tasks where possible; mise en place mentality applied to all tasks |

#### Harassment and Discrimination: Legal and Professional Framework

**Definition (from Range of Variables):**
> Harassment: objectionable conduct, comment or display made either on a one-time or continuous basis that demeans, belittles, or causes personal humiliation or embarrassment to the recipient.

**Discrimination is prohibited based on (Canadian Human Rights Act and provincial equivalents):**
- Race
- National or ethnic origin
- Colour
- Religion
- Age
- Sex
- Sexual orientation
- Gender identity or expression
- Marital status
- Family status
- Disability
- Genetic characteristics
- Pardoned conviction

> **Regulatory Note:** The *Canadian Human Rights Act* (CHRA) applies to federally regulated workplaces. Provincial human rights codes (e.g., Ontario Human Rights Code, BC Human Rights Code) apply to most food service employers. These codes prohibit discrimination in employment and may include additional protected grounds (e.g., source of income, political beliefs). Verify with your provincial human rights tribunal or commission.

**Forms of Workplace Harassment Relevant to Kitchens:**
- Verbal abuse, demeaning language, public humiliation
- Sexual harassment (unwanted comments, touching, or advances)
- Bullying (persistent negative targeting of an individual)
- Racial or cultural harassment (slurs, mockery, exclusion)

**Responding to Harassment:**
1. If safe to do so, clearly state to the person that their behaviour is unacceptable
2. Document the incident (date, time, what was said/done, witnesses)
3. Report to a supervisor, HR, or the Joint Health and Safety Committee
4. If retaliation occurs for reporting, this itself is a violation — document and report
5. Provincial human rights commissions and WorkSafe boards accept complaints and provide protection

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Impact | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Not using kitchen call-outs ("Behind," "Corner") | Complacency; unfamiliarity | Collision; spill; burn; serious injury | Internalize call-outs as automatic; non-negotiable habit |
| Saying "Yes" without genuinely understanding an instruction | Fear of looking incompetent | Task done incorrectly; possible food safety failure | Ask for clarification immediately; "I want to make sure I understood — can you repeat that?" |
| Delivering feedback publicly in a harsh manner | Frustration; replicating observed behaviour | Demeaning junior staff; morale damage; potential harassment | Urgent corrections brief and direct; debrief in appropriate setting |
| Not relaying safety meeting information to absent team members | Assuming it doesn't matter or someone else will do it | Critical safety or allergen information not reaching team; risk of incident | Post key information; brief incoming shift explicitly |
| Using closed questions to verify understanding | Habit; faster-seeming | Creates illusion of understanding without confirming actual knowledge | Use open-ended questions for training verification |
| Ignoring non-verbal distress signals from a struggling team member | Busy; not attentive | Team member makes preventable error; safety risk | Develop awareness of team body language; check in when someone seems overwhelmed |
| Dismissing a guest complaint | Frustration; defensiveness | Guest dissatisfied; potential allergen or food safety issue uninvestigated | Acknowledge fully; take action; follow up |
| Not using kitchen terminology correctly | Unfamiliarity; rushing | Confusion on the line; timing errors; potential miscommunication about allergens | Learn kitchen terminology as a core trade skill; ask if unsure about a call |
| Information not shared at shift handover | Rushing; assumption other team knows | Next shift unaware of food safety issues, 86'd items, allergy bookings | Complete written handover note; verbal brief to incoming lead cook |
| Tolerating harassment because "that's kitchen culture" | Normalization of historical abuse | Mental health harm; staff turnover; potential legal liability | Report harassment; understand the line between direct feedback and harassment |

## Memory Aids

**Active Listening Steps — "HIRRP":**
- **H**earing
- **I**nterpreting
- **R**eflecting
- **R**esponding
- **P**araphrasing

**Kitchen Safety Call-Outs — "BCH":**
- **B**ehind (I'm behind you)
- **C**orner (I'm coming around a corner)
- **H**ot (I'm carrying something hot)
→ These three calls prevent the majority of kitchen collision injuries

**Open-Ended Question Starters:**
- "Describe how you..."
- "Walk me through..."
- "What would you do if..."
- "How did you..."
- "Tell me about..."
→ None of these can be answered with "Yes" or "No"

**Feedback Quality Check — "STAR":**
- **S**pecific (what exactly)
- **T**imely (right after the event)
- **A**ctionable (can be corrected)
- **R**espectful (targets work, not person)

**Trade Terminology Quick Reference — "FAPH86":**
- **F**ire = start cooking
- **A**ll day = running total on tickets
- **P**ick up = ready to serve
- **H**eard/Yes Chef = acknowledged
- **86** = out of stock

**Learning Styles Triad — "S.H.T." (See, Hear, Try):**
- Show the technique (visual)
- Explain while showing (auditory)
- Have them attempt it with your guidance (kinesthetic)
→ All three together produces the fastest, deepest learning

### Exam Q&A

**Q1: What are the five steps of active listening in order?**
A: (1) Hearing — physically receiving the message; (2) Interpreting — assigning meaning; (3) Reflecting — internally processing the message; (4) Responding — replying to acknowledge receipt; (5) Paraphrasing — restating in own words to confirm understanding.

**Q2: What is the difference between a closed question and an open-ended question? Give a kitchen example of each.**
A: A **closed question** can be answered with "yes" or "no": "Did you check the temperature?" A **open-ended question** requires elaboration: "How did you verify the internal temperature?" Open-ended questions reveal actual knowledge, process, and potential gaps that yes/no answers conceal.

**Q3: Why are the kitchen call-outs "Behind" and "Corner" considered safety protocols rather than mere courtesy?**
A: Commercial kitchens have confined spaces, poor sightlines, and workers constantly carrying hot, sharp, or heavy items. A cook who spins around at a corner without warning can collide directly with someone carrying a pot of boiling stock, causing severe burns. These call-outs prevent physical injury by creating audio warning of movement in blind spots.

**Q4: Define workplace harassment as it applies to a commercial kitchen.**
A: Harassment is objectionable conduct, comment, or display — made either on a one-time or continuous basis — that demeans, belittles, or causes personal humiliation or embarrassment to the recipient. This is distinct from constructive feedback, which targets specific behaviours in a professional, actionable way. Harassment is directed at the person rather than the work, and can be verbal, physical, or behavioral.

**Q5: What are the three learning styles identified in the Range of Variables, and how should a chef use all three when teaching a technique to an apprentice?**
A: **Seeing it** (visual), **Hearing it** (auditory), and **Trying it** (kinesthetic). Effective training uses all three: the chef **demonstrates** the technique while **verbally explaining** what they are doing and why, then has the apprentice **attempt it** with guided feedback. Confirmation of understanding uses open-ended questions ("Walk me through what you just did").

**Q6: What does "86" mean in kitchen terminology, and what must happen when an item is 86'd?**
A: "86" means the item is out of stock — completely unavailable. When an item is 86'd, the kitchen must immediately communicate this to all FOH staff so servers stop offering it to guests. Failure to 86 an item promptly leads to disappointed guests and wasted preparation time.

**Q7: What is "all day" as used in a kitchen, and why is it useful?**
A: "All day" is the running total of a specific item currently on all open tickets in the kitchen. For example, "four salmon all day" means there are four salmon orders across all current tickets that need to be produced. It helps cooks manage their production quantities in real time without re-reading every individual ticket.

**Q8: Name four grounds on which discrimination in the Canadian workplace is prohibited.**
A: Any four of: race, national or ethnic origin, colour, religion, age, sex, sexual orientation, gender identity or expression, marital status, family status, disability, genetic characteristics, pardoned conviction. (Under the Canadian Human Rights Act; provincial codes may add additional grounds.)

**Q9: A chef observes a junior cook use a visual check ("it looks done") rather than a probe thermometer to verify internal temperature of chicken. Instead of asking "Did you check the temperature?", what open-ended question should the chef ask?**
A: "Walk me through how you verified the internal temperature of that chicken" — or "How did you confirm it reached 74°C?" These questions require the cook to describe their actual process, revealing that no thermometer was used, which the yes/no question would likely not surface.

**Q10: What are the four components of effective (STAR) feedback?**
A: **S**pecific — targets a specific behaviour or outcome, not the person's character; **T**imely — delivered as close to the event as possible; **A**ctionable — the recipient can do something with it; **R**espectful — professional tone; targets work, not person.

**Q11: What information must be shared at a shift handover from the departing cook to the incoming cook?**
A: Key categories: (1) Food safety — temperature log review; any anomalies; products approaching use-by date; (2) Inventory — what's been used; what's short; ordering needs; (3) Prep status — what mise en place is complete; what's still needed; (4) Equipment — any tagged-out or malfunctioning equipment; (5) Upcoming bookings — VIP or allergy guests for next service; (6) Incidents — any food safety near-miss or corrective action taken during the previous shift.

**Q12: Why does a culture of psychological safety in a kitchen produce better food safety outcomes?**
A: When kitchen staff feel safe to speak up without fear of humiliation or retaliation, they are more likely to: report temperature anomalies; flag suspected contamination or allergen cross-contact; ask for clarification on food safety procedures; admit mistakes early (before a guest is harmed); and raise concerns about equipment failures. In a culture where speaking up is punished, food safety failures go unreported until a guest is harmed. Psychological safety directly supports HACCP effectiveness by ensuring that corrective actions are identified and communicated when needed.
