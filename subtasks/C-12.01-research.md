# C-12.01 | Selects Fruit — Enriched Research Document

**Major Work Activity:** C — Produce
**Task:** C-12 | Prepares Fruit
**Competency:** Selects fruit

---

## Overview and Why This Matters

Fruit selection is a daily professional decision that determines the quality ceiling of every dish, dessert, and plated item that uses fruit. A skilled cook understands that fruit is a living, dynamic biological product — it continues to ripen, respire, and change after harvest. Selection decisions must account not only for current quality but for the timeline from purchase to service.

This competency requires knowledge of:
- How fruits are classified and why each classification behaves differently in storage and cooking
- The science of ripening — and why some fruits can be purchased underripe while others cannot
- How to assess freshness accurately by sensory assessment
- Which market form is appropriate for which application
- Food safety risks specific to fruit — including several that have caused serious Canadian foodborne illness outbreaks

## Fruit Classifications

### Classification 1: Tree Fruits (Pome Fruits)

**Definition:** Fruits that grow on trees and have a core structure with seeds surrounded by thick, fleshy tissue. The botanical term is **pome** (from Latin *pomum* = fruit).

**Examples:** Apples (*Malus domestica*), pears, quince

**Structural science:**
The flesh of a pome fruit is not the true botanical fruit — it is the enlarged **floral receptacle** (hypanthium) that surrounds the true fruit (the core). The seeds are enclosed in a papery core surrounded by this fleshy tissue. This understanding matters because the seeds of apple and pear contain trace amounts of **amygdalin**, a cyanogenic glycoside. In normal culinary use (seeds are not consumed in quantity), this poses no concern.

**Key properties:**
- **High pectin content** — especially apples and quince; pectin is concentrated in the skin and just below; this makes pome fruits ideal for jams, jellies, and set preserves without added pectin
- **Primary acid: malic acid** — responsible for the characteristic tartness; also present in cherries and other tree fruits
- **Long post-harvest life** — commercial apples are stored in Controlled Atmosphere (CA) storage at low oxygen and low temperature for up to 12 months; the majority of apples available in Canadian winter are CA-stored domestic product
- **Most are climacteric** — continue to ripen after harvest (see Ethylene section below)

**Canadian commercial significance:**
- Okanagan Valley (B.C.): Gala, Fuji, Ambrosia, Spartan, Honeycrisp — major national supply
- Ontario: McIntosh, Empire, Cortland, Honeycrisp — September–November peak season
- Nova Scotia: heritage varieties; McIntosh

**Culinary applications:**

| Variety | Eating Quality | Cooking Behaviour | Best Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Granny Smith | Tart, crisp | Holds shape well; does not disintegrate | Tarte Tatin, sautéed apples, garnish, cheese boards |
| McIntosh | Sweet-tart; fragrant | Breaks down quickly when cooked | Applesauce, apple butter |
| Gala/Fuji | Sweet, crisp | Moderate — softens but holds some shape | Baking, fresh eating, caramelizing |
| Honeycrisp | Very crisp, sweet-tart | Holds shape; juicy | Fresh eating, salads, light cooking |
| Bartlett pear | Sweet, aromatic; soft when ripe | Breaks down easily | Poaching; sauce; fresh eating |
| Bosc pear | Firm, less sweet | Holds shape when cooked | Poaching; baking; charcuterie boards |

### Classification 2: Citrus Fruits

**Definition:** Fruits from the genus *Citrus*, characterized by aromatic peel containing essential oils and acidic, segmented flesh divided into juice vesicles.

**Examples:** Oranges, lemons, limes, grapefruits, tangerines, mandarins (clementines), blood oranges, pomelo, Meyer lemon, yuzu

**Anatomy of a citrus fruit:**

| Layer | Term | Characteristics | Culinary Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Outer coloured layer | **Flavedo** (zest) | Contains essential oil sacs — limonene, linalool, and other terpenes; intense concentrated flavour | Zesting, infusions, candied peel, marinades |
| White pithy layer | **Albedo** (pith) | Bitter flavour; contains flavonoids; no culinary value | Avoid including in zest |
| Segmented flesh | **Endocarp** | Juice vesicles; contains citric acid and sugars; primary source of juice | Segments for salads; juice for cooking and sauces |

**Primary acid:** Citric acid — gives citrus its characteristic sharp, bright tartness

**Key characteristics:**
- Very high **Vitamin C** content (ascorbic acid)
- **Non-climacteric** — do not ripen after harvest; must be selected at peak ripeness
- Aromatic compounds in zest: monoterpene limonene (characteristic citrus smell); varies significantly by variety — lime zest has more herbaceous notes; bergamot (used for Earl Grey tea) is intensely perfumed
- **Citrus juice as a cooking acid**: lower pH than other fruit juices; 2.0–3.3 depending on variety; essential for balance in sauces, dressings, marinades, desserts
- Grapefruit contains **furanocoumarins** (especially bergamottin) that inhibit cytochrome P450 enzymes responsible for metabolizing many medications — this is a genuine drug-food interaction issue relevant to healthcare food service

> **Operational Tip:** Always zest citrus before juicing — once the fruit is juiced, the skin is difficult to hold and zest. Store leftover zested citrus halves cut-side down on a small plate at room temperature or in a damp cloth wrap to prevent drying.

---

### Classification 3: Stone Fruits (Drupes)

**Definition:** Fruits with a fleshy outer layer (mesocarp) surrounding a hard inner stone or pit (endocarp) that contains a seed. Botanically called **drupes**.

**Examples:** Peaches, nectarines, plums, cherries, apricots, mangoes (also a drupe)

**Key biological characteristic — the amygdalin consideration:**
Stone fruit pits contain seeds that have **amygdalin** (a cyanogenic glycoside). When metabolized, amygdalin releases hydrogen cyanide. In normal culinary practice, whole pits are not consumed and seeds are not cracked and eaten — this is not a practical concern. The traditional inclusion of whole cherry stones in clafoûtis (French fruit tart) during baking is safe because the trace amounts from skin contact are negligible. However, intentionally consuming crushed pits or bitter apricot seeds in quantity should be avoided.

**Varieties and cooking applications:**

| Fruit | Freestone/Clingstone | Ripening | Best Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Peach (freestone) | Flesh separates from pit easily | Climacteric | Fresh eating; poaching; grilling; preserves |
| Peach (clingstone) | Flesh adheres to pit | Climacteric | Commercial canning; preserves |
| Nectarine | Smooth-skin peach; same fruit | Climacteric | Fresh eating; grilling; fruit salads |
| Plum | Most freestone | Climacteric | Fresh; prunes (dried); plum sauce; charcuterie |
| Sour cherry | Small; very tart | Climacteric | Pies; preserves; clafoûtis; sauces |
| Sweet cherry | Bing, Rainier | Climacteric | Fresh eating; desserts; garnish |
| Apricot | Freestone | Climacteric | Fresh; dried; jam; Middle Eastern cooking |
| Mango | One large flat stone | Climacteric | Tropical desserts; chutneys; sauces; fresh eating |

> **⚠ Mango Urushiol Warning:** Mango peel contains **urushiol** — the same compound found in poison ivy and poison oak. People with latex allergy may experience a reaction. Handlers with sensitive skin should wear gloves when peeling mangoes and avoid direct contact with the sap near the stem. The flesh itself does not contain urushiol.

**Browning after cutting:** Stone fruits contain polyphenol oxidase (PPO) and brown rapidly when cut. Apply citric acid (lemon juice) or ascorbic acid solution immediately after cutting to prevent browning.

---

### Classification 4: Vine Fruits

**Definition:** Fruits that grow on climbing or trailing vines.

**Examples:** Grapes, kiwi, passion fruit

**Grapes — commercial significance:**
- Largest commercially grown fruit category by volume worldwide
- Table grapes vs. wine grapes (very different sugar, acid, and tannin profiles)
- **Bloom** on grapes: the natural white powdery coating on grape surfaces is wild yeast — a natural protective layer; not a quality defect; do not wash until use
- Primary acid: **tartaric acid** — unique to grapes; not common in other fruits; remains stable during fermentation (critical for wine)
- Dried forms: raisins (large dark), sultanas (Thompson seedless), currants (small dark/Zante)
- Common Canadian applications: fruit platters, cheese boards, wine reduction sauces, garnish

**Kiwi — key food science properties:**
- High vitamin C content (higher per gram than oranges)
- Contains **actinidin** — a protease enzyme that breaks down protein
- **Cannot be used with gelatin** — actinidin prevents gelatin from setting (same issue as pineapple bromelain and papaya papain)
- To use kiwi with gelatin: cook the kiwi first (heat deactivates actinidin); or use agar-agar instead of gelatin
- Store at room temperature to ripen; refrigerate once ripe

---

### Classification 5: Tropical Fruits

**Definition:** Fruits native to tropical and subtropical climates; require warm growing conditions year-round; imported to Canada.

**Examples:** Mango, pineapple, papaya, banana, coconut, guava, lychee, dragon fruit, star fruit (carambola), passion fruit, jackfruit

**Key fruit enzyme hazards (critical for pastry and gel applications):**

| Fruit | Enzyme | Effect | Solution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pineapple** | **Bromelain** (protease) | Prevents gelatin setting; tenderizes proteins | Heat pineapple first (deactivates enzyme); or use agar-agar |
| **Papaya** | **Papain** (protease) | Prevents gelatin setting; meat tenderizer | Cook first; same solution as pineapple |
| **Kiwi** | **Actinidin** (protease) | Prevents gelatin setting | Cook first; same solution |
| **Figs** | **Ficin** (protease) | Prevents gelatin setting | Cook first; same solution |
| **Mango** (marginally) | Lower protease activity | Minimal effect on gelatin in small quantities | Generally not a concern at culinary amounts |

> **Memory Aid — PPKF:** **P**ineapple, **P**apaya, **K**iwi, **F**igs. The "Fresh Four" that prevent gelatin from setting. Cook them first, or switch to agar-agar.

**Banana — ripening science:**
- **Climacteric** — among the most dramatic examples; ripening is ethylene-triggered starch-to-sugar conversion
- Green banana: mostly starch (resistant starch); low sweetness; firm; used in savoury applications in some cuisines
- Yellow banana: starch converted to sugars (glucose, fructose, sucrose); sweet; soft; ideal for fresh eating
- Spotted/black banana: maximum sweetness; very soft; fermentation beginning; ideal for banana bread and baking (higher sugar = better browning and flavour in baked goods)
- Bananas are **major ethylene producers** — storing bananas with other ripening-sensitive fruit dramatically accelerates their ripening

**Tropical fruit storage — never refrigerate:**
> Tropical fruits stored at standard refrigerator temperature (4°C) suffer **chilling injury** — cell membranes rupture, causing internal black discolouration, off-flavours, and texture breakdown. Store at 10–15°C (room temperature in most Canadian kitchens). Once fully ripe and cut, tropical fruits are refrigerated.

### Classification 6: Berries

**Note on botanical vs. culinary definition:** In botanical terms, a "true" berry is a simple fleshy fruit from a single ovary — by this definition, grapes, tomatoes, cucumbers, bananas, kiwi, and avocados are true berries. Strawberries, raspberries, and blackberries are NOT botanical berries. In culinary use, "berry" refers to any small, fleshy, seed-containing fruit — the culinary definition is used throughout this document.

**Examples (culinary):** Strawberries, raspberries, blackberries, blueberries, cranberries, gooseberries, currants, elderberries

**Key characteristics:**
- Extremely perishable — most berries deteriorate within 2–4 days even under refrigeration
- Fragile cell structure — physical handling causes immediate cell rupture and juice loss
- High antioxidant content — anthocyanins (blue, red, purple pigments) are among the most studied antioxidant compounds in the food supply

**Berry-specific handling notes:**

| Berry | Special Note | Storage | Wash Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Blueberries** | Bloom (white powder) is natural wild yeast protective coating — not a defect | 4°C; 5–10 days | Wash immediately before use, not before storage |
| **Strawberries** | Not a true botanical berry (accessory fruit); hull before use | 4°C; 2–3 days; single layer | Wash immediately before use; never wash before storing |
| **Raspberries** | Aggregate fruit; extremely fragile; single layer only | 4°C; 1–2 days | Do not wash; use as-is |
| **Blackberries** | Aggregate fruit; slightly firmer than raspberries | 4°C; 2–3 days | Wash gently immediately before use |
| **Cranberries** | Extremely tart; high proanthocyanidins; will not soften sufficiently raw — always cooked | 4°C; 3–4 weeks (firmer, more durable) | Wash before use |

> **Berry Food Safety:** Berries — particularly strawberries, raspberries, and blueberries — have been implicated in multiple foodborne illness outbreaks involving *Cyclospora cayetanensis*, *Hepatitis A virus*, and *Norovirus*. Fresh berries used for raw consumption should be rinsed gently before service. Frozen berries imported from regions with known contamination risks have caused outbreaks — follow public health advisories.

## Fruit Properties

### Property 1: Acid and Sugar Content

**Sugar content — Brix measurement:**
- **°Brix** (degrees Brix) measures the percentage of dissolved solids (primarily sugar) in fruit juice
- Measured with a refractometer: a drop of juice is placed on the prism; light bends according to sugar concentration; reading in degrees Brix
- Typical Brix ranges: table grapes 16–20°Brix; ripe peach 12–15°Brix; orange juice 10–14°Brix; lemon juice 7–9°Brix

**Primary acids by fruit:**

| Fruit | Primary Acid | Approximate pH |
|---|---|---|
| Lemons, limes | Citric acid | 2.0–2.5 |
| Oranges, grapefruit | Citric acid | 3.0–4.0 |
| Apples, pears, cherries | Malic acid | 3.0–4.0 |
| Grapes | Tartaric acid | 2.8–3.8 |
| Strawberries | Citric acid + malic acid | 3.0–3.5 |
| Bananas (ripe) | Malic acid + citric acid | 4.5–5.2 |
| Cranberries | Citric acid + quinic acid | 2.3–2.5 |

**Sugar:acid ratio** is the primary measure of eating quality and ripeness — a high-sugar, lower-acid fruit tastes sweet; a high-acid, lower-sugar fruit tastes tart; optimal balance varies by variety and intended application (cooking vs. fresh eating).

**Starch-to-sugar conversion in ripening:**
- Unripe fruit: complex starch stored; firm texture; low sweetness
- As ethylene triggers ripening: amylase enzyme converts starch → glucose, fructose, sucrose
- Ripe fruit: maximum sugar; reduced starch; characteristic soft texture
- This conversion is irreversible once complete — you cannot convert an overripe fruit back to underripe

---

### Property 2: Size

- **Grade and size** are related but not identical — size is a physical measurement while grade includes multiple quality factors
- Larger does not mean higher quality — flavour density is often higher in smaller fruits (cherry tomatoes vs. beefsteak tomatoes; French Charentais melon vs. large Cantaloupe)
- Uniform size matters for: portioning consistency (half a peach per serving), even cooking, and presentation uniformity

---

### Property 3: Colour

**Fruit colour as a ripeness signal:**

| Fruit | Colour Signal | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Banana | Green → yellow → spotted black | Green = unripe; yellow = ready; spotted = very ripe (baking); black = overripe |
| Mango | Varies by variety — do NOT rely on colour alone | Ataulfo: green→yellow when ripe; Tommy Atkins: mostly red/green regardless of ripeness; use touch test |
| Avocado | Varieties vary: Hass turns black-purple when ripe | Black-purple + slight give = ripe; bright green + hard = unripe |
| Strawberry | Fully red throughout to stem end | White or pale shoulders = underripe; deep red = ripe |
| Blueberries | Deep blue-purple; no red tinges | Red tinges = underripe; uniform deep colour = ripe |
| Pineapple | Yellow-gold base/bottom | More yellow = riper; green but slightly soft = acceptable |

> **Important:** Colour is a useful but incomplete ripeness indicator. Some varieties of fruit (particularly mangoes and avocados) are not reliable to judge by colour. Always combine colour assessment with **touch (firmness)** and **aroma** for the most accurate ripeness evaluation.

---

### Property 4: Grade

**Canada Grade Standards (CFIA):**

| Grade | Criteria | Culinary Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Canada No. 1** | Top quality; free of defects; consistent size and colour | Fresh service; fruit platters; fine dining |
| **Canada No. 2** | Minor blemishes, slight colour inconsistency | Cooking applications; baking; preserves; sauces |
| **Canada No. 3** | More significant defects; may include bruising | Processing; purees; large-batch jam; commercial use |

> **Operational Note:** Canada No. 2 fruit is often significantly less expensive than Canada No. 1 while being essentially identical in flavour and nutrition. For applications where the fruit will be cooked, puréed, or not displayed whole (compotes, soups, reductions, tarts), No. 2 grade provides better food cost management without quality compromise. For fruit platters, garnish, or presentations where the whole fruit is visible, No. 1 grade is appropriate.

## The Science of Ripening — Ethylene and Climacteric vs. Non-Climacteric Fruit

This is one of the most practically important pieces of science in fruit selection and storage management.

### Ethylene Gas

**Ethylene (C₂H₄)** is a natural plant hormone produced by ripening fruit. At trace concentrations (parts per million), it triggers:
- Starch-to-sugar conversion
- Cell wall softening (pectin depolymerization)
- Chlorophyll breakdown (colour change)
- Aroma volatile production
- Increased respiration rate

### Climacteric vs. Non-Climacteric Fruits

| Type | Definition | Ripening Behaviour | Practical Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Climacteric** | Produce a large burst of ethylene during ripening; continue to ripen after harvest | Can be purchased underripe; ripen at room temperature | Ripeness management is possible; buy ahead of peak |
| **Non-climacteric** | Produce minimal ethylene; respiration doesn't peak during ripening; do not ripen further after harvest | Must be harvested at peak ripeness; will not improve after picking | Must be received and used at optimal ripeness |

**Climacteric fruits (continue ripening after harvest):**
Apples, pears, bananas, mangoes, peaches, plums, apricots, kiwi, avocados, tomatoes, figs, papayas

**Non-climacteric fruits (must harvest ripe):**
Citrus (oranges, lemons, limes, grapefruit), grapes, strawberries, raspberries, blueberries, blackberries, pineapple, cherries, watermelon, cucumbers

### Ethylene Management in the Kitchen

**Accelerating ripening:**
- Store climacteric fruits at room temperature (18–22°C) to ripen
- Place fruit with a banana in a closed paper bag — the banana's ethylene is trapped and accelerates ripening of other climacteric fruit
- A paper bag is preferred over plastic (allows some gas exchange; plastic traps moisture)

**Slowing ripening:**
- Refrigerate at 4°C (cold significantly slows ethylene production and enzyme activity)
- Separate fruit from other fruit in cold storage (reduce ethylene concentration)
- Ethylene absorbers (commercial sachets) in cold storage rooms

**Preventing ethylene damage to non-fruit produce:**
- Apples are among the strongest ethylene emitters
- **Never store apples near:** leafy greens (accelerates yellowing); broccoli, cauliflower (accelerates yellowing, loss of colour); carrots (causes bitterness — ethylene triggers iscoumarin production in carrots); berries (accelerates softening and spoilage)
- Separate produce cold storage areas for high-ethylene and ethylene-sensitive items

> **Memory Aid — Apple Warning:** "An apple a day keeps the doctor away — but keeps all your other produce away too." Apples produce large amounts of ethylene and should be stored in isolation or in their own designated area of the walk-in refrigerator.

## C-12.01.02P — Identify Indicators of Freshness and Quality

### Performance Criteria
Indicators of freshness and quality of fruit are identified according to sensory assessment.

---

### Freshness Indicator 1: Firmness

**What it reveals:** The turgor pressure of fruit cells and the state of pectin in the cell walls.

| Fruit | Fresh/Ripe Firmness | Interpretation of Deviation |
|---|---|---|
| **Apple, pear (ripe)** | Crisp and firm; snaps cleanly | Soft = overripe or CO₂-depleted CA storage |
| **Peach, nectarine (ripe)** | Slight give under gentle pressure; not hard | Rock hard = underripe; very soft = overripe/bruised |
| **Avocado** | Slightly yielding near stem end | Hard = 3–5 days from ripe; completely soft/hollow = overripe |
| **Berries** | Firm, plump, not leaking juice | Soft, wet, or leaking = overripe or mechanically damaged |
| **Citrus** | Firm and heavy for its size | Light for size = dried out inside; soft spots = rot beginning |
| **Mango** | Slight give overall; not hard | Soft to hard varies by variety; use colour + aroma as additional indicators |
| **Banana** | Firmness indicates ripeness stage | See colour-ripeness correlation above |
| **Pineapple** | Crown leaves pull out with slight resistance; give at base | Leaves pull out easily = ripe; hard with no give = underripe |

> **The "Heavy for Size" Test for Citrus:** A citrus fruit that feels light for its visual size has dried out internally — the juice content is low. Always select citrus that feels notably heavy for its size; these will yield more juice and be more recently harvested.

---

### Freshness Indicator 2: Colour

**Pigment changes during ripening:**

| Pigment | Fruit | Change During Ripening |
|---|---|---|
| **Chlorophyll** | All green fruit | Breaks down as fruit ripens → underlying pigments (carotenoids, anthocyanins) become visible → colour shifts from green |
| **Carotenoids** | Peaches, mangoes, apricots, citrus (orange/yellow) | Already present; become visible as chlorophyll degrades; some increase during ripening |
| **Anthocyanins** | Strawberries, cherries, blueberries, grapes, plums | Produced/increase as ripening progresses; red/blue/purple deepens |
| **Lycopene** | Watermelon, guava, pink grapefruit | Produced during ripening; heat also increases lycopene in tomatoes |

**Colour defects:**
- **Brown spots or patches** → enzymatic browning (PPO reaction) from physical damage (bruising); or fungal/bacterial decay at surface — inspect closely; may extend deep
- **Mold** (white, grey, or black fuzzy growth) → fungal contamination; discard product
- **Dull, faded colour** → aging; prolonged cold storage; post-peak ripeness
- **Shrivelling** → moisture loss; dehydration; age

---

### Freshness Indicator 3: Texture

**Surface texture and structural integrity:**

| Texture Observation | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Firm, turgid, smooth skin** | Fresh; cells hydrated and intact | Accept |
| **Soft spots** | Bruising or early rot; cell walls collapsed from impact or fungal activity | Inspect closely; for cooking applications, cut out bruised area with margin; for fresh service, reject |
| **Sliminess or wet surface** | Advanced bacterial or fungal decomposition | Reject |
| **Shrivelled or wrinkled skin** | Significant moisture loss through evaporation | Quality reduced; flavour may be concentrated (acceptable in some dried/semi-dried applications); reject for fresh service |
| **Watery or weeping** | Freeze damage; extreme overripeness; cell rupture | Reject for fresh service |
| **Sticky surface** | Overripe; sugars concentrating on surface from cell leakage | Inspect; may be salvageable for cooking/processing only |

> **Aroma as an Additional Freshness Tool:** Although not listed in the Range of Variables, aroma is a powerful freshness and ripeness indicator: ripe fruit should smell intensely of its characteristic aroma; no scent may indicate underripeness or cold-storage damage; fermented, alcoholic, or vinegar notes indicate overripeness or microbial activity; musty or mouldy smell indicates fungal growth.

## Market Forms of Fruit

### Comparative Guide

| Market Form | Shelf Life | Best Culinary Application | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Whole** | Longest; varies by fruit | All applications; maximum flexibility | Requires full prep labour; maximum nutritional value |
| **Peeled** | Shorter; browning risk | Convenience prep (mango, pineapple, citrus segments) | Check for browning; acid treatment often applied commercially |
| **Cut** | Shortest (12–24 hours) | Pre-portioned service; fruit salads; buffet; convenience | Highest browning risk; citric acid often added; inspect carefully |
| **Dehydrated** | Months to years | Baking; cheese/charcuterie boards; granola; muesli; trail mix; stuffings | **Sulfites often used** — priority allergen in Canada; concentrated sweetness; reduced volume |
| **IQF (Individual Quick Frozen)** | 12–18 months frozen | Smoothies; sauces; baking; compotes; plated fruit | Shake bag to test; should be loose; do not use for fresh-eating applications |
| **Bulk frozen** | 12–18 months frozen | Purees; smoothies; large-volume sauces; compotes | May be clumped; partially thaw and drain before use in dry-heat applications |
| **Canned** | Years at room temperature | Compotes; garnish; sauces; baking | In syrup (high sugar) or in juice; already soft; heat only; BSRD rejection rule |

### Sulfites in Dried Fruit — Priority Allergen

> **Regulatory Requirement (Canada — SFCA/CFIA):** Sulfites (sulfur dioxide, potassium metabisulfite, sodium bisulfite) used as preservatives in dried fruit must be declared on the label as a priority allergen when present above 10 ppm. **Sulfites are one of Canada's 14 priority allergens.** Dried apricots, raisins, dried cranberries, and many mixed dried fruit products commonly contain sulfites — check every product label.
>
> An unsulfited dried apricot is dark brown or amber; a sulfited dried apricot is bright orange. The colour difference visually identifies the presence of sulfite treatment.

### Fruit-Specific Market Form Notes

| Fruit | Best Market Form | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Berries (strawberries, raspberries) | Fresh (primary); IQF for cooking | Avoid canned — texture completely lost; fresh far superior |
| Citrus (lemons, limes) | Whole (primary); fresh juice | Pre-bottled juice lacks volatile aromatic freshness; fresh is standard |
| Stone fruits (peaches) | Fresh (primary); IQF; canned | Canned peaches in juice acceptable for compotes and pies |
| Pineapple | Fresh (primary); canned; IQF | Canned is acceptable for cooked applications; fresh essential for raw |
| Mango | Fresh (primary); IQF; frozen pulp | Frozen Ataulfo mango pulp is high quality for sauces and desserts |
| Bananas | Fresh only | No substitute; use ripe, naturally sweetened for baking |

## Factors to Consider When Selecting Fruits

| Factor | Practical Consideration |
|---|---|
| **Local availability** | B.C. cherries, Okanagan apples/pears, Ontario strawberries/peaches, Nova Scotia blueberries/apples, Quebec cranberries — local means shorter supply chain, higher freshness potential, less transport damage |
| **Seasonal availability** | Canadian seasonality determines peak quality and pricing; out-of-season imports are typically more expensive and of lower quality |
| **Freshness** | Days from harvest; visible indicators at receiving; ask supplier for harvest/ship date |
| **Storage life** | Berries: 1–3 days; stone fruits: 3–7 days; apples (CA): months — order quantities matched to shelf life |
| **Applications** | Underripe pear for poaching; very ripe banana for baking; fully ripe mango for fresh service |
| **Processed vs. fresh** | Cost and labour trade-off; nutritional and flavour comparison |
| **Sustainability** | Organic certification; local/seasonal choice; fair-trade status; air-freight carbon footprint |
| **Ripeness** | Must match the service timeline and application: buy 3–4 days underripe for a Saturday dinner if purchasing Tuesday |

### Canadian Seasonal Availability Guide

| Season | Peak Fruits (Canadian or Regional) |
|---|---|
| **Spring (May–June)** | Quebec greenhouse strawberries beginning; Ontario rhubarb (technically a vegetable used as a fruit) |
| **Summer (July–August)** | Ontario/BC strawberries; Ontario/BC raspberries, blueberries; BC Rainier and Bing cherries; Ontario peaches, nectarines |
| **Fall (Sept–Nov)** | Okanagan/Ontario apples; Okanagan pears; BC/Nova Scotia apples; Ontario grapes; cranberries |
| **Winter (Dec–March)** | CA-stored domestic apples; imported citrus (Florida, California, Mexico); imported tropical |

## Dietary Requirements Specific to Fruits

| Category | Fruit-Specific Consideration |
|---|---|
| **Allergies** | **Latex-fruit syndrome:** mango, avocado, banana, kiwi, papaya cross-react with latex protein — relevant for guests or staff with latex allergy; **Oral Allergy Syndrome (OAS):** apple, peach, cherry, pear cross-react with birch pollen (raw only — cooking denatures the protein); **Sulfites in dried fruit** — priority allergen; **Kiwi allergy** — increasing prevalence |
| **Ethical (vegan)** | All fresh fruit is vegan; check processing aids in commercial fruit products (some wines used in fruit sauces are clarified with animal-derived fining agents) |
| **Religious (Islamic)** | Wine-based fruit sauces and alcohol-containing fruit preparations prohibited; fresh fruit has no restrictions |
| **Religious (Kosher)** | Insects are non-Kosher; Kosher fruit inspection requires checking figs, strawberries, raspberries for insects — berries with visible insect contamination require specific washing/inspection protocols under Kosher law |
| **Health-related: Diabetic** | Whole fruit preferred over juice (fibre slows glucose absorption); lower GI choices: berries, citrus, apples; higher GI: watermelon, pineapple, ripe banana, dates |
| **Health-related: Renal diet** | High-potassium restriction: limits on bananas, oranges, kiwi, mangoes, melons — lower potassium options: apples, berries, grapes, pineapple |
| **Health-related: Warfarin patients** | Grapefruit and grapefruit juice contain furanocoumarins that significantly inhibit medication metabolism — **grapefruit must be flagged** for patients on warfarin, statins, and many other medications; communicate to healthcare food service team |
| **Health-related: Kidney stones** | High-oxalate fruits (strawberries, rhubarb, star fruit) may be restricted for some kidney stone patients; star fruit also contains nephrotoxic compounds dangerous for kidney disease patients |
| **Environmental** | Seasonal local fruit vs. air-freighted; organic certification (CFIA Canada Organic); fair-trade labelling |

## Food Safety Considerations in Fruit Selection

### Biological Hazards Specific to Fruit

| Pathogen / Hazard | Fruit Vehicle | Canadian Significance |
|---|---|---|
| ***Listeria monocytogenes*** | **Cantaloupe (rockmelon)** | Multiple Canadian outbreaks; the rough netted skin traps *Listeria*; when the melon is cut, the knife transfers surface bacteria to flesh |
| *E. coli* O157:H7 | Strawberries; unpasteurized apple cider | Documented outbreaks; washing required |
| *Cyclospora cayetanensis* | Raspberries; strawberries; fresh berries | Canadian outbreaks from imported berries |
| *Hepatitis A virus* | Berries; frozen berries; fresh produce | Fecal-oral contamination route; handwashing critical |
| *Norovirus* | Berries and soft fruits | Handler contamination during picking and processing |

> **⚠ Cantaloupe *Listeria* Warning:** This is one of the most important food safety points in fruit handling. *Listeria monocytogenes* is trapped in the netted surface of cantaloupe (and honeydew) and cannot be removed by a standard rinse. The knife used to cut the melon transfers surface bacteria directly into the flesh. Every cantaloupe must be scrubbed thoroughly with a produce brush under cold running water before cutting — regardless of whether the rind will be consumed. This is **non-negotiable** and is an explicit recommendation from Health Canada and provincial public health authorities.

### Common Mistakes When Selecting Fruits

| Mistake | Consequence | Correct Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Refrigerating tropical fruits (mango, banana, pineapple) when unripe | Chilling injury: black internal discolouration; off-flavour; texture breakdown | Store at 10–15°C until ripe; refrigerate only after ripe and cut |
| Not scrubbing cantaloupe before cutting | Transfer of surface *Listeria* to the flesh via the knife | Scrub with produce brush under cold running water before every cut |
| Judging mango ripeness by colour alone | Tommy Atkins mango stays red-green regardless of ripeness; purchasing unripe mango | Supplement colour assessment with firmness and aroma |
| Washing berries before storage | Moisture accelerates mould growth and cell damage; reduces shelf life dramatically | Wash immediately before use only; never before storage |
| Storing apples near other produce | Ethylene production accelerates ripening and spoilage in everything nearby | Isolate apples in own storage area or section of walk-in |
| Using fresh pineapple, kiwi, or papaya in gelatin-set desserts | Protease enzymes prevent gelatin from setting — the dessert never firms | Cook the fruit first to deactivate enzymes, or use agar-agar |
| Not checking dried fruit labels for sulfites | Undisclosed allergen (sulfites) reaches a guest with sulfite sensitivity | Check every dried fruit product label; declare sulfites on any menu item |
| Accepting IQF bags that are solid blocks | Product has experienced temperature abuse; quality significantly compromised | Shake bag on receiving; reject solid blocks |
| Not considering ripeness timeline when purchasing | Fruit is perfect the day it arrives but overripe by service day | Purchase at appropriate pre-ripe stage for the service day |
| Cutting fruit on boards used for raw protein | Cross-contamination with pathogens | Dedicated fruit cutting boards; clean and sanitize before use |

### Exam Q&A — 12 Practice Questions

**Q1: What are the six fruit classifications in the Red Seal curriculum, and give one example of each?**
A: (1) Tree (pome) — apple; (2) Citrus — lemon; (3) Stone (drupe) — peach; (4) Vine — grape; (5) Tropical — mango; (6) Berry — blueberry.

**Q2: What is the difference between a climacteric and a non-climacteric fruit, and give two examples of each?**
A: Climacteric fruits produce a burst of ethylene gas and continue to ripen after harvest — examples: bananas, apples, mangoes, peaches. Non-climacteric fruits do not produce this ethylene burst and must be harvested at peak ripeness because they will not ripen further — examples: strawberries, grapes, citrus fruits, cherries.

**Q3: Why can fresh pineapple, kiwi, papaya, and figs not be used in gelatin-set desserts, and what is the solution?**
A: These fruits contain protease enzymes (bromelain, actinidin, papain, and ficin respectively) that break down protein — including the protein structure of gelatin — preventing the gelatin from setting. The solution is to cook or heat the fruit first, which deactivates the enzyme, before using it with gelatin. Alternatively, agar-agar (which is not affected by proteases) can be used as the setting agent.

**Q4: What is the critical food safety procedure required before cutting any cantaloupe, and why?**
A: Cantaloupe must always be scrubbed thoroughly with a produce brush under cold running water before cutting. The rough, netted skin of cantaloupe is a known environment for *Listeria monocytogenes* — the pathogen lodges in the netting and cannot be removed by a simple rinse. When the melon is cut, the knife blade transfers surface bacteria directly into the flesh, which is then consumed raw. This scrubbing requirement applies even though the rind is not eaten.

**Q5: What are the Canada grade standards for fruit, and how does this affect purchasing decisions for a commercial kitchen?**
A: Canada No. 1: top quality, free of defects — for fresh service, platters, and visible presentations. Canada No. 2: minor blemishes — acceptable for cooking, baking, sauces, and preserves. Canada No. 3: more significant defects — for processing and purees. For dishes where the whole fruit is displayed, No. 1 is required; for cooked or puréed applications, No. 2 provides equivalent flavour and nutrition at lower cost.

**Q6: Why should berries never be washed before storage, and when should they be washed?**
A: Washing berries before storage introduces moisture onto the surface and into the cell structure. This moisture activates mould growth, softens cell walls, and dramatically reduces shelf life (from 3–5 days to less than 24 hours). Berries should be washed gently under cold running water immediately before use — not in advance.

**Q7: Why should apples be stored separately from other produce in a walk-in refrigerator?**
A: Apples are one of the strongest ethylene gas producers among commercially used fruits. Ethylene triggers ripening and deterioration in surrounding produce — causing leafy greens to yellow, broccoli to lose colour, carrots to develop bitterness (from isocoumarin production), and berries to soften and spoil prematurely.

**Q8: What are sulfites, why are they used in dried fruit, and what is their regulatory status in Canada?**
A: Sulfites (sulfur dioxide, potassium metabisulfite, sodium bisulfite) are preservatives that prevent enzymatic browning and mould growth in dried fruits — they are why sulfited dried apricots are bright orange while unsulfited ones are dark brown. Under Canada's Safe Food for Canadians Act (SFCA/CFIA), sulfites are a priority allergen and must be declared on food labels when present above 10 ppm. Any dish containing dried fruit with sulfites must be considered for allergen declaration.

**Q9: What is the white powdery coating on fresh blueberries, and is it a quality defect?**
A: The white powder (called "bloom") on blueberries is a natural coating of wild yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae* and related species) that forms on the skin and acts as a protective barrier against pathogens and moisture loss. It is not a quality defect; in fact, its presence indicates fresh, recently harvested, undamaged blueberries. It should not be washed off before storage — wash only immediately before use.

**Q10: What is the "heavy for size" test for citrus, and what does a light citrus fruit indicate?**
A: A citrus fruit that feels notably heavy for its visual size has a high juice content — this indicates a recently harvested, well-hydrated fruit. A citrus fruit that feels light for its size has dried out internally — the juice vesicles have dehydrated, the Brix may be concentrated but the volume of juice is low. Always select the heaviest specimens of any citrus fruit.

**Q11: What is oral allergy syndrome (OAS) in relation to fruit, and which fruits are most commonly affected in Canada?**
A: Oral allergy syndrome is a reaction where proteins in raw fruit cross-react with similar proteins in pollen, causing tingling or mild swelling of the lips and mouth. In Canada, the most common trigger is birch pollen, which cross-reacts with raw apples, pears, peaches, cherries, and plums. Cooking denatures the cross-reactive proteins — the same person may eat cooked apple pie without a reaction but react to raw apple. This is an allergy consideration for fresh fruit service.

**Q12: What ripeness stage of banana is ideal for (a) fresh eating, (b) baking, and why?**
A: (a) Fresh eating: fully yellow with no green shoulders — starch has converted to sugars; flavour is sweet; texture is firm enough to hold structure when sliced. (b) Baking (banana bread, muffins): heavily spotted or beginning to turn black — maximum sugar content from complete starch conversion; soft texture blends easily into batter; higher sugar content = better Maillard browning in the baked product and more intense banana flavour. A green or barely yellow banana in banana bread produces a bland, insufficiently sweet result.
