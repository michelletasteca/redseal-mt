# B-9.02 | Uses Mentoring Techniques — Deep Learning Resource

**Major Work Activity:** B — Common Occupational Skills
**Task:** B-9 | Uses Communication and Mentoring Techniques
**Enrichment Level:** Red Seal Exam Preparation + Apprentice Learning Guide

---

## Overview: Mentoring as a Core Professional Responsibility

Achieving a Red Seal designation does more than certify an individual cook's competency — it creates a professional obligation to pass that knowledge forward. The apprenticeship system that produces Red Seal Cooks depends on journeypersons and experienced cooks who can teach, demonstrate, coach, assess, and support learners in a live kitchen environment.

Mentoring in a commercial kitchen is not an informal activity that happens when convenient. It is a **structured professional responsibility** governed by apprenticeship legislation, OH&S obligations, and human rights law. An effective kitchen mentor must:
- Set clear learning objectives and explain why each skill matters
- Demonstrate skills using a systematic approach that covers the full context (who/what/where/when/why/how)
- Create safe conditions for practice and progressively release responsibility to the learner
- Provide feedback that is timely, specific, and respectful
- Document training for apprenticeship compliance
- Actively maintain a harassment-free learning environment

This subtask also applies when any experienced cook coaches a less experienced colleague — not only in the formal apprenticeship context.

### B-9.02.01P — Identify and Communicate Learning Objective and Point of Lesson

**Performance Criteria:** Identify and communicate learning objective and point of lesson
**Evidence of Attainment:** Apprentice or learner can explain the objective and point of lesson

---

#### What Is a Learning Objective?

A **learning objective** is a specific statement of what the learner will be able to **do** after the lesson that they could not do before. It is always framed in terms of observable performance, not vague intent.

**Format:** "By the end of this lesson, you will be able to _____ [action verb] _____ [specific outcome]."

| Weak Objective | Strong Learning Objective |
|---|---|
| "Learn about knife safety" | "Demonstrate the correct grip, stance, and claw technique when julienne-cutting a carrot" |
| "Understand HACCP" | "Record an accurate temperature log entry with date, time, product, location, and corrective action" |
| "Know about allergens" | "Identify the 14 Canadian priority allergens in a standardized recipe and propose a safe substitute for each" |
| "Practice the sauce" | "Mount a beurre blanc using cold butter and maintain an emulsified consistency for 5 minutes" |

---

#### The Point of the Lesson

The **point of the lesson** answers the question the learner has but may not ask: **"Why does this matter?"**

A learner who understands why a skill matters is more motivated, retains the learning longer, and applies it more reliably. A learner who is told "do it because that's the rule" without understanding the reason may comply during training but revert to shortcuts when not supervised.

**Examples of communicating the point:**

| Skill Being Taught | The Point (Why It Matters) |
|---|---|
| Probe thermometer use for chicken | "Chicken carries *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter*. At 74°C, those organisms are killed. Below that temperature, a guest can get seriously ill. The thermometer is what protects both the guest and the operation." |
| FIFO rotation in the walk-in | "FIFO prevents older product from being forgotten at the back of the shelf. *Listeria monocytogenes* can grow even at refrigeration temperatures — a product that is technically in date but old is still a risk. First in, first out is your first line of defence." |
| Allergen cross-contact prevention | "A guest with anaphylaxis can go into cardiac arrest from a trace amount of peanut. When I show you how to set up a dedicated allergen-free station, I'm showing you how to protect someone's life." |
| Knife sharpening | "A dull knife requires more force, which increases the chance of slipping. More cuts happen with dull knives than sharp ones. Sharpening is safety practice, not just quality practice." |

---

#### Confirming the Learner Can Articulate the Objective

The Evidence of Attainment requires that the learner **can explain** the objective and point — not just listen to them. This requires a check:

- After stating the objective, ask: "Can you tell me in your own words what you'll be able to do by the end of this lesson?"
- After explaining the point, ask: "Why do you think this matters in the kitchen?"
- Correct or reinforce the response before moving forward

### B-9.02.02P — Link Lesson to Other Lessons and Job Activities

**Performance Criteria:** Link lesson to other lessons and job activities
**Evidence of Attainment:** Lesson order and unplanned learning opportunities are defined

---

#### Why Linking Lessons Matters

Skills in a commercial kitchen are not learned in isolation — they form an interconnected network where mastery of one skill enables progression to the next. A mentor who helps the learner understand these connections accelerates skill acquisition and creates deeper understanding.

**Building on prior learning:**
- Before teaching sauce emulsification, confirm the learner understands fat and water chemistry basics
- Before teaching HACCP documentation, confirm the learner can use a probe thermometer accurately
- Before teaching complex plating, confirm the learner can portion correctly and plate under time pressure

**Connecting to the job:**
- "You're learning this knife cut today because you'll use it at the garde manger station during Friday's service — here's where it fits"
- "This HACCP log you're completing right now is the document that protects you if a guest has a food safety complaint — it's not paperwork, it's your professional record"

---

#### Unplanned Learning Opportunities

Not all learning in a kitchen is planned. Service creates **unplanned learning opportunities** that a good mentor recognizes and uses:

| Unplanned Situation | Learning Opportunity |
|---|---|
| Sauce breaks during service | "Let's talk about why this broke and how to rescue it — right now is a perfect time" |
| Cook notices a temperature anomaly in the walk-in | "Walk me through what you should do now — this is exactly why we have the corrective action protocol" |
| Guest sends back a dish | "Let's debrief on what happened with that plate — what do you think went wrong and what would you do differently?" |
| A new allergy request comes in during service | "This is a real accommodation scenario — let's handle it together and I'll talk you through the decision-making" |

> **Operational Recommendation:** Maintain a brief mental or written note of unplanned learning moments during a shift. The post-service debrief is the right time to revisit them as teaching moments, when the learner can reflect without the pressure of live service.

### B-9.02.03P — Demonstrate Performance of a Skill to an Apprentice or Learner

**Performance Criteria:** Demonstrate performance of a skill
**Evidence of Attainment:** Steps required to demonstrate a skill are performed

---

#### The Six Questions: Context for Every Skill Demonstration

From the Range of Variables, every skill demonstration should address: **Who, What, Where, When, Why, and How.**

| Question | Purpose | Example (Teaching Cooling Protocol) |
|---|---|---|
| **Who** | Identifies responsibility | "This is every cook's responsibility — not just the one who made the soup" |
| **What** | Defines the skill | "We're cooling this large batch of chicken stock from 60°C to 4°C in two stages" |
| **Where** | Locates the task | "This happens in the blast chiller or in the walk-in with an ice bath — never at room temperature" |
| **When** | Establishes timing | "The moment any hot food drops to 60°C, the 6-hour cooling clock starts" |
| **Why** | Explains the principle | "*Clostridium perfringens* and other spore-formers thrive in slowly cooling large batches — rapid cooling kills their opportunity to multiply" |
| **How** | Shows the technique | "Divide into shallow pans no deeper than 10 cm, label, place in ice bath, stir every 30 minutes, document temperature every 2 hours" |

---

#### The Four-Step Job Instruction Method

The most effective structure for demonstrating a trade skill follows these four steps:

**Step 1 — Prepare the Learner**
- Explain what you are about to teach (the learning objective)
- Explain why it matters (the point of the lesson)
- Connect to what the learner already knows (prior knowledge link)
- Create psychological safety: "There are no wrong questions at this stage"

**Step 2 — Present the Task (Demonstrate)**
- Perform the skill at normal speed first so the learner sees the complete process
- Then perform slowly, explaining **each step** as you do it: narrate the technique aloud
- Emphasize critical points (food safety, safety hazards, quality indicators): "Notice I'm reading the thermometer at eye level — parallax error is how incorrect readings happen"
- Check for understanding periodically: "What did you notice about how I held the knife there?"

**Step 3 — Try Out (Guided Practice)**
- Have the learner attempt the skill while you observe
- Do not perform the skill for them — guide with words, not by taking over their hands
- Provide real-time coaching without interrupting continuously: wait for natural pause points
- Correct safety issues immediately; correct quality issues at the end of the attempt

**Step 4 — Follow Up**
- After the attempt, provide specific feedback (STAR: Specific, Timely, Actionable, Respectful)
- Ask the learner to self-assess: "What do you think you did well? What would you change?"
- Determine whether more guided practice, limited independence, or full independence is appropriate
- Schedule the next practice opportunity

---

#### Demonstrating While Narrating: The Dual Channel

When demonstrating a skill, narrating simultaneously activates both the **visual** and **auditory** learning channels. This dual-channel approach has been consistently shown in educational research to produce better retention than demonstration alone or verbal explanation alone.

**Narration technique:**
- "Now I'm placing the thermometer in the thickest part — away from bone, which conducts heat differently than muscle"
- "I'm waiting for the reading to stabilize — that takes 10–15 seconds; a reading taken before it stabilizes will be inaccurate"
- "74°C — that's our critical limit for poultry. The moment I see that number and it holds, I document it"

### B-9.02.04P — Set Up Conditions for Skill Practice

**Performance Criteria:** Set up conditions required for an apprentice or learner to practice a skill
**Evidence of Attainment:** Practice conditions are set up so that skill can be practiced safely

---

#### The Three Practice Conditions

From the Range of Variables: practice conditions progress from **guided** → **limited independence** → **full independence**.

This is the **gradual release model** of skill instruction — progressively transferring ownership from mentor to learner.

**1. Guided Practice**
- Mentor and learner work simultaneously; mentor may demonstrate each sub-step, then have learner repeat it
- Immediate correction of errors
- Safety and quality standards maintained by mentor oversight
- Used for: brand-new skills; safety-critical procedures; complex multi-step techniques

**Example:** A cook learning to use a Japanese mandoline for the first time. The mentor demonstrates, then guides the apprentice's first 10 passes, adjusting hand position and cut-resistant glove use in real time.

**2. Limited Independence**
- Learner performs the skill independently; mentor observes without intervening unless safety issue arises
- Feedback is provided after completion, not during
- Learner is encouraged to problem-solve within reasonable parameters
- Used for: skills that have been demonstrated and practiced once or twice under guidance

**Example:** The apprentice sets up and portions a fish order for a full table. Mentor watches from a distance. After the table is plated, mentor gives feedback on consistency and portioning accuracy.

**3. Full Independence**
- Learner performs without supervisor observation; output is checked, not process
- Mentor is available for questions but does not monitor
- Learner identifies their own issues and self-corrects
- Used for: skills that have been mastered to a standard that allows unsupervised performance

**Example:** An apprentice who has mastered the cooling protocol completes and documents all post-service HACCP cooling logs independently. The mentor reviews the logs at shift end.

---

#### Setting Up a Safe Practice Environment

Skill practice in a live kitchen carries real risk. The mentor's responsibility:

| Safety Consideration | Mentor's Action |
|---|---|
| **Sharp tools** | Confirm learner has correct PPE (cut-resistant gloves for mandoline/vegetable peeler use); demonstrate correct tool placement; ensure non-slip cutting surface |
| **Hot surfaces and liquids** | Ensure learner uses appropriate heat-resistant tools; confirm pot handles turned away from aisle; never practice in a congested area during service |
| **Allergen cross-contact during practice** | If practicing with allergen-containing ingredients, confirm dedicated practice equipment is available; do not practice allergen-free protocol with shared tools |
| **Chemical handling (WHMIS)** | If practicing cleaning and sanitization, confirm learner has reviewed relevant SDS; confirm dilution ratios before practice begins |
| **Food safety during practice** | Time-temperature protocol applies during skill practice — an apprentice practicing sauce-making is producing food that may be served; HACCP standards are not suspended for training |

> **Regulatory Note:** Under provincial OH&S legislation, employers are responsible for the safety of all workers, including apprentices. Training activities that expose learners to hazards must be conducted under appropriate supervision. Verify specific responsibilities with your provincial OH&S authority.

### B-9.02.05P — Perform Tasks with Increasing Independence

**Performance Criteria:** Assess apprentice or learner's ability to perform tasks with increasing independence
**Evidence of Attainment:** Performance improves with practice to a point where skill can be done with little supervision

---

#### Recognizing Readiness for Increased Independence

A mentor must actively assess when a learner is ready to move from guided practice to limited independence, and from limited independence to full independence. Readiness indicators:

| Practice Stage | Readiness Indicators for Next Stage |
|---|---|
| **Guided → Limited independence** | Learner completes each step correctly without prompting; demonstrates understanding of why each step matters; handles unexpected variations without requesting constant guidance |
| **Limited → Full independence** | Learner's output consistently meets quality and safety standards; learner identifies and self-corrects errors before they escalate; learner can explain the skill to another person |
| **Not ready to advance** | Recurring same errors after repeated feedback; inability to explain the rationale for a step; errors in safety-critical steps (temperature, allergen) without self-correction |

---

#### Measuring Improvement

Improvement must be **observable and measurable**, not just a general impression. In a kitchen context:

| Skill | How to Measure Improvement |
|---|---|
| Knife cuts | Time to complete a standardized cut (e.g., 200 g brunoise carrot); consistency of cut size (measure with ruler; count proportion of correct-size pieces) |
| Temperature logging | Accuracy of readings (compare with calibrated reference thermometer); completeness of log entries; appropriate corrective action entries |
| Sauce-making | Consistency of finish (appearance, viscosity, flavour balance) over multiple attempts; reduction in time taken as technique improves |
| Portioning | Weight deviation from standard across 10 consecutive portions (target: ±5% of standard portion) |
| Allergen protocol | Checklist completion rate; zero cross-contact events |

### B-9.02.06P — Give Support and Feedback for Improvement

**Performance Criteria:** Give support and feedback for improvement
**Evidence of Attainment:** Apprentice adopts best practice after having been given timely support and feedback for improvement

---

#### Feedback as a Teaching Tool

See B-9.01 for the STAR feedback framework. In the mentoring context, feedback is an ongoing, structured activity rather than just a corrective response to an error.

**Types of Feedback in Mentoring:**

| Type | When to Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Immediate correction** | Safety issue; critical error in progress | "Stop — hold the knife higher; your fingers are exposed. Let me show you again." |
| **In-the-moment coaching** | Minor technique issue during practice | "Try slowing down right before the cut — rushing is where the inconsistency comes from" |
| **Post-task debrief** | After completing a full task or service period | "Overall, the portioning was very consistent tonight. The one area to work on — the sauce was 30 mL over on three plates." |
| **Developmental feedback** | Scheduled one-on-one for growth conversations | "You've mastered the basic sauce technique. Next month, we'll work on your ability to rescue a broken sauce under pressure." |
| **Positive reinforcement** | When learner achieves a milestone or best practice | "That temperature log is exactly right — correct readings, corrective action documented, initialled. That's the professional standard." |

---

#### The Feedback Cycle

Effective feedback does not end with the mentor speaking. The cycle requires:

1. **Mentor observes** specific behaviour or outcome
2. **Mentor gives specific, actionable feedback**
3. **Learner acknowledges** and repeats back understanding
4. **Learner applies** the correction on next attempt
5. **Mentor observes again** and confirms improvement
6. **Mentor gives positive reinforcement** for successful correction

If the learner does not apply the feedback after two or three attempts, the mentor must consider whether:
- The feedback was not clear enough (repeat using different language; demonstrate again)
- The learner has a learning need that requires a different approach
- There is a foundational skill gap that must be addressed first

### B-9.02.07P — Document Technical Training Opportunities

**Performance Criteria:** Document technical training opportunities while pursuing technical training opportunities
**Evidence of Attainment:** Training opportunities are documented to use as a planning tool for future training

---

#### Why Documentation Matters in Apprenticeship

Apprenticeship in Canada is a **regulated activity**. Provincial apprenticeship authorities require documented records of:
- On-the-job training (OJT) hours
- Competency sign-offs (specific skills demonstrated to standard)
- Technical training (in-school periods; credits earned)
- Progression milestones

**Without documentation:**
- Hours worked may not be credited toward apprenticeship completion
- Competencies may not be recognized when the apprentice applies for certification
- Disputes about training progress have no verifiable record
- The mentor (journeyperson) cannot be held to account for their training obligations

---

#### Training Documentation Tools

| Document | Purpose | Who Maintains |
|---|---|---|
| **Apprenticeship training record / log** | Official record of OJT hours; signed by journeyperson | Apprentice + journeyperson; submitted to apprenticeship authority |
| **Competency checklist** | Documents which skills have been demonstrated to standard | Journeyperson completes; apprentice retains copy |
| **Training plan** | Outlines which skills will be covered during the apprenticeship period; timeline | Developed jointly by apprentice and mentor |
| **Performance review notes** | Informal record of progress, feedback given, areas for improvement | Mentor; used for developmental feedback conversations |
| **In-school training transcript** | Credits earned in technical school periods | Issued by the school; submitted to apprenticeship authority |
| **Incident reports** | Documents safety incidents or near-misses involving apprentice | Completed by mentor or supervisor; relevant to OH&S compliance |

---

#### Using Documentation as a Planning Tool

The training record is not just a compliance document — it is a **planning roadmap**. By reviewing what has been documented:
- Identify which competencies have been signed off and which remain outstanding
- Identify patterns in performance (consistently strong in one area; recurring difficulty in another)
- Plan upcoming training to address gaps before the in-school period or certification exam
- Track whether the apprentice is on pace to complete within the apprenticeship program timeline

### B-9.02.08P — Support Apprentices in Pursuing Technical Training

**Performance Criteria:** Support apprentices or learners in pursuing technical training opportunities
**Evidence of Attainment:** Technical training is completed within timeframe prescribed by apprenticeship authority

---

#### The Apprenticeship System in Canada

Canadian apprenticeship programs for Cooks are administered at the **provincial and territorial level**, with the **Red Seal (Interprovincial Standard)** program providing national recognition.

**Typical Cook Apprenticeship Structure (general; verify with your provincial authority):**

| Level | On-the-Job Hours | In-School Period | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Level 1** | ~1,500–1,800 hours | 8 weeks (approximately 240 hours) | Level 1 certificate |
| **Level 2** | ~1,500–1,800 hours | 8 weeks | Level 2 certificate |
| **Level 3** | ~1,500–1,800 hours | 8 weeks | Journeyperson Certificate of Qualification |
| **Red Seal Exam** | After Journeyperson cert | Written examination | Interprovincial Red Seal Endorsement |

> **Regulatory Note:** Apprenticeship program requirements vary by province and territory. Hours, levels, and in-school periods differ. Verify current requirements with your provincial apprenticeship authority (e.g., SkilledTradesOntario, ITA BC, Alberta AIT, Skills and Training New Brunswick, etc.). Program structures are periodically updated.

**The Journeyperson-to-Apprentice Ratio:**

Most provinces require a minimum ratio of journeypersons or qualified cooks to apprentices in a registered workplace. This ratio ensures adequate supervision and mentoring capacity. Ratios vary — verify with your provincial authority.

---

#### How Mentors Support Technical Training

| Mentor Action | Impact on Apprentice |
|---|---|
| Scheduling work assignments that cover all competency areas before in-school period | Apprentice arrives at technical school with practical experience that reinforces classroom learning |
| Explaining the apprenticeship timeline and milestones | Apprentice understands what is expected and when |
| Signing off completed competencies promptly and accurately | Apprentice has documented record required for progression |
| Encouraging attendance and preparation for in-school periods | Apprentice completes technical training on time |
| Discussing Red Seal exam content and connecting it to on-the-job practice | Apprentice develops confidence and relevance for certification exam |
| Providing time for study during less busy periods | Apprentice is not forced to choose between work and training requirements |

### B-9.02.09P — Support a Harassment-Free Workplace

**Performance Criteria:** Support a harassment-free workplace
**Evidence of Attainment:** Workplace is harassment and discrimination-free; employees are encouraged to speak out against harassment and discrimination

---

#### The Mentor's Specific Responsibility

In a mentoring relationship, the mentor holds **positional power** over the apprentice. This power imbalance makes harassment in the mentor-apprentice relationship particularly harmful and particularly important to prevent.

The mentor must:
- Never use their position of authority to coerce, demean, or intimidate an apprentice
- Create an environment where the apprentice can ask questions, make mistakes, and raise concerns without fear
- Not allow the traditional kitchen culture of hazing or harsh treatment to be directed at apprentices in their charge
- Intervene when they witness harassment directed at the apprentice (or anyone else) by others in the kitchen
- Encourage the apprentice to report harassment through appropriate channels

**Prohibited Grounds for Discrimination (from Range of Variables):**
Race, national or ethnic origin, colour, religion, age, sex, sexual orientation, gender identity or expression, marital status, family status, disability, genetic characteristics, pardoned conviction.

> **Regulatory Note:** The *Canadian Human Rights Act* applies to federally regulated employers. Provincial human rights codes apply to most food service workplaces and may include additional protected grounds. OH&S legislation in all provinces also prohibits workplace harassment and violence. Mentors who engage in or enable harassment may face personal legal liability under these frameworks. Verify specifics with your provincial human rights commission or OH&S authority.

---

#### Building a Culture Where Speaking Up Is Safe

Psychological safety — the ability to speak up about concerns without fear of negative consequences — is both a workplace safety requirement and an operational necessity.

In a kitchen, a culture where speaking up is encouraged produces:
- Earlier identification of food safety failures (temperature anomaly, suspected contamination)
- Fewer preventable injuries (someone afraid to say they don't know how to use a piece of equipment gets hurt)
- Better allergen management (cook flags an uncertainty about an ingredient rather than guessing)
- Higher quality outcomes (cook admits they're struggling with a technique rather than guessing repeatedly)
- Better staff retention (people stay in environments where they feel respected)

**Mentor behaviours that build psychological safety:**
- Thank staff for raising concerns, even if the concern turns out to be unfounded
- Correct errors privately, not publicly
- Share your own past mistakes as learning examples ("I made this exact error in my second year...")
- Apply rules consistently — not more harshly to some team members than others
- Follow up after someone raises a concern to show it was taken seriously

### B-9.02.10P — Assess Apprentice or Learner Suitability for the Trade

**Performance Criteria:** Assess apprentice or learner suitability to trade
**Evidence of Attainment:** Apprentice or learner is given timely feedback that helps them identify their own strengths and weaknesses and suitability for trade

---

#### What Suitability Assessment Means

Assessing suitability for the trade is not a judgment of a person's worth — it is a professional evaluation of whether an individual has developed (or has the capacity to develop) the knowledge, skills, attitudes, and physical capabilities required to work safely and effectively as a professional cook.

**Areas assessed for trade suitability:**

| Domain | What Is Evaluated |
|---|---|
| **Technical skills** | Knife skills, cooking methods, temperature knowledge, recipe execution, presentation |
| **Food safety practice** | Consistent application of HACCP principles, temperature monitoring, allergen management, personal hygiene |
| **Professional attitude** | Punctuality, reliability, acceptance of feedback, initiative, respect for colleagues |
| **Communication** | Ability to receive and relay kitchen instructions; kitchen terminology; team coordination |
| **Essential skills** | Numeracy (scaling, portioning), reading (recipes, HACCP), document use (logs, tickets), digital technology |
| **Physical demands** | Ability to stand for extended periods, manage heat and noise, handle time pressure |
| **Continuous learning** | Engagement with technical training; genuine interest in improvement |

---

#### How to Conduct a Fair Suitability Assessment

**Evidence-based assessment:**
- Base feedback on specific, documented observations — not general impressions
- Use the apprenticeship competency checklist as a framework
- Document examples: "On three occasions last week, internal temperature was not verified before plating; on two of those, product was below 74°C"

**Not influenced by protected grounds:**
- Suitability assessment must be completely free of any consideration of the apprentice's race, gender, age, disability, or any other protected ground
- Different performance must be tracked to different observed behaviours and outcomes, not assumptions about what someone can do

**Delivered as growth feedback:**
- Frame the assessment as information the apprentice can use, not as a verdict
- Include both strengths (specific) and development areas (specific)
- Offer a path forward: "Here is what I see; here is what I recommend you focus on; here is what success looks like"

**Including the apprentice's self-assessment:**
- Ask the apprentice to assess their own strengths and development areas before sharing your assessment
- Compare the two: a large gap between self-assessment and mentor assessment is itself important information
- "Where do you think you are strongest right now? Where do you feel you have the most to develop?"

### B-9.02.01L — Strategies for Learning Skills in the Workplace

**Learning Outcomes:** Demonstrate knowledge of strategies for learning skills in the workplace

---

#### The Importance of Individual Experience

Every learner brings a different prior experience to kitchen training. A new apprentice who grew up cooking at home has different foundational skills than one who has never held a kitchen knife. Neither is automatically better prepared — different starting points require different entry-level instruction.

The mentor's role: **meet the learner where they are**, not where they should be.

**Assessing prior experience at the start of mentoring:**
- Ask open-ended questions about the learner's background: "What cooking have you done before this?"
- Observe early performance without judgment
- Use first demonstrations partly as diagnostic tools: "Show me how you would break down this chicken" reveals level without formal testing

---

#### Shared Responsibilities for Workplace Learning

Workplace learning is not a one-way transmission from mentor to learner. It is a **shared responsibility**:

| Party | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Employer** | Providing qualified mentors; scheduling time for training; covering in-school technical training; maintaining training records |
| **Journeyperson / Mentor** | Teaching, demonstrating, coaching, documenting, assessing, and supporting the apprentice |
| **Apprentice** | Attending work and school consistently; engaging actively with training; asking questions; applying feedback; maintaining training records |
| **Apprenticeship Authority** | Setting standards; approving workplaces; processing applications; certifying completion |

---

#### Essential Skills in the Food Service Context

The nine essential skills are foundational to all other workplace performance. They are not alternatives to technical skills — they are the **platform on which technical skills are built**.

| Essential Skill | Food Service Application |
|---|---|
| **Reading** | Recipe cards, HACCP procedures, SDS sheets, health inspection reports, allergen menus |
| **Writing** | Temperature logs, HACCP records, incident reports, prep lists, order communication |
| **Document use** | Order tickets, inventory forms, competency checklists, apprenticeship logbooks |
| **Oral communication** | Kitchen call-outs, allergen communication, service coordination, feedback delivery |
| **Numeracy** | Recipe scaling (conversion factor), food cost (AP/EP yield), temperature conversion (°C/°F), portion weights |
| **Thinking** | Problem-solving during service disruption; allergen substitution decisions; HACCP corrective actions |
| **Working with others** | Brigade coordination, training relationships, FOH/BOH communication, team scheduling |
| **Digital technology** | POS systems, KDS, digital temperature loggers, scheduling apps, CFIA recalls via Health Canada alerts |
| **Continuous learning** | Pursuing in-school training; industry certifications; new cuisines; food safety updates |

---

#### Learning Styles and Their Application

| Style | Characteristics | Mentor Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Seeing it (Visual)** | Learns best through observation; benefits from diagrams, plate photos, demonstrations, written recipes with photos | Demonstrate before explaining; use visual guides; provide plate maps |
| **Hearing it (Auditory)** | Learns through verbal explanation; benefits from discussion, narrated demonstrations, verbal feedback | Explain rationale out loud while demonstrating; debrief verbally; discuss theory |
| **Trying it (Kinesthetic)** | Learns through hands-on practice; benefits from doing, repetition, physical guidance | Prioritize early hands-on attempts; allow repetition; use physical correction (guide hand position) |

> **Best practice:** Most people learn through a combination of styles. Use all three — demonstrate (visual), narrate (auditory), and provide early practice opportunity (kinesthetic) — as the default approach for any skill. Do not assume a learner's style before observing them.

---

#### Different Learning Needs

| Learning Need | How It May Present | Mentor Adaptation |
|---|---|---|
| **Learning disability (e.g., dyslexia)** | Difficulty reading recipe cards, HACCP forms; may read slowly; may struggle with written exams | Provide verbal instructions; use photos/diagrams alongside written recipes; allow oral responses for assessment where possible |
| **Learning preference (e.g., hands-on learner in a lecture-heavy school)** | Disengages in in-school classroom; excels on the line | Emphasize on-the-job context for school content; connect classroom theory to live practical examples |
| **Language proficiency** | May understand spoken instruction better than written; technical vocabulary unfamiliar | Use demonstration extensively; build vocabulary gradually; use visual supports; avoid idioms and jargon when first introducing concepts |
| **Physical learning curve** | Skill-related: some people develop knife skills slowly; some have larger hands that make fine work harder | Patience; alternative techniques where available; focus on safety first, speed second |
| **Prior negative kitchen experience** | Fear of making mistakes due to past harsh treatment | Explicitly build psychological safety; reward questions; correct privately |

### B-9.02.02L — Strategies for Teaching Skills in the Workplace

**Learning Outcomes:** Demonstrate knowledge of strategies for teaching skills in the workplace

---

#### The Role of the Workplace Mentor

A workplace mentor in a commercial kitchen serves simultaneously as:

| Role | Function |
|---|---|
| **Instructor** | Teaches specific technical skills through demonstration and guided practice |
| **Coach** | Provides ongoing feedback and adjustment to improve performance |
| **Assessor** | Evaluates competency against standard; documents progress |
| **Advocate** | Supports the apprentice's access to training opportunities and fair treatment |
| **Model** | Demonstrates professional standards in every action — not just during designated teaching moments |
| **Role model** | Embodies the professional values of the trade: food safety, quality, respect, continuous improvement |

> **The most powerful teaching happens when the mentor doesn't know they're teaching.** An apprentice watches how a journeyperson responds to a mistake, handles a complaint, and conducts themselves under pressure. The mentor's everyday behaviour teaches as much as formal instruction.

---

#### Choosing the Right Time and Place for a Lesson

| When to Teach | When NOT to Teach |
|---|---|
| During quiet prep periods | During peak service rush |
| Before service begins (pre-shift setup) | When either party is emotionally elevated (frustrated, stressed) |
| After service — during debrief | When the kitchen is undermanned and each cook is critical to service |
| When a natural teaching moment arises and can be paused safely | When the physical environment is unsafe for the learner to practice |
| During scheduled training time | When the lesson requires equipment that is currently in use |

---

#### Components of a Skill (The Context Framework)

From the Range of Variables, each skill has components that must be understood before it can be safely and competently performed:

| Component | Example: Safe Thawing of Frozen Protein |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Prevent bacterial growth during temperature transition |
| **Food safety principle** | Thawing at room temperature puts food surface in TDZ while interior remains frozen |
| **Procedure** | Refrigerator thaw (preferred); cold running water (≤21°C); microwave (cook immediately after) |
| **Tools/equipment** | Container, drainage shelf, thermometer |
| **Timing** | 24 hours per 2.5 kg for refrigerator thaw |
| **Documentation** | HACCP thawing log if required by operation's plan |
| **Common errors** | Room temperature thawing; warm water; re-freezing |
| **Consequences of error** | Surface bacteria multiply; re-freezing locks in higher bacterial load |

---

#### Assessing Skills and Progress: Methods

| Assessment Method | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Observation checklist** | Mentor observes task performance against a defined checklist of steps | Procedural skills (HACCP documentation, thawing protocol) |
| **Output evaluation** | Mentor evaluates the product of the skill (plated dish, portioning accuracy, temperature log) | Technical skills with measurable outcomes |
| **Verbal questioning** | Open-ended questions reveal understanding of principles, not just procedure | Confirming conceptual knowledge behind a skill |
| **Self-assessment** | Learner rates their own performance; mentor compares to own observation | Building self-awareness; identifying blind spots |
| **Peer review** | One apprentice evaluates another's work against the standard | Collaborative learning environments; builds critical eye |
| **Timed performance** | Measure how long a task takes as competence develops | Skills where speed is a workplace requirement (portioning, plating under service pressure) |

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Impact | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Skipping the "why" when teaching | Rushing; assuming the learner will just do it | Learner follows procedure without understanding; first shortcut occurs when mentor is not watching | Always explain the principle and the consequence before demonstrating |
| Teaching during service peak | Convenience; the moment has arisen | Learner overwhelmed; learning not retained; safety risk if learner is distracted | Delay teaching to a low-pressure moment; use the incident as a flag for a post-service lesson |
| Performing the task for the learner instead of coaching | Impatience; faster to just do it | Learner watches again but does not develop skill; dependency created | Resist taking over; guide with words and small corrections; allow longer time for the attempt |
| Advancing to full independence before limited independence stage is completed | Optimism about learner's readiness | Learner without support makes recurring errors; safety risks in unsupervised performance | Use the three-stage readiness criteria before advancing each practice stage |
| Not documenting training because "it's obvious they've done it" | Paperwork fatigue | Apprentice's hours and competencies not credited; progression delayed; disputes with apprenticeship authority | Document contemporaneously; sign competency as observed; do not backfill |
| Using public feedback that humiliates | Replicating experienced kitchen culture; frustration | Learner becomes afraid to attempt; psychological harm; potential harassment | Immediate corrections brief and private wherever possible; public correction only for safety-critical issues |
| Assessing suitability based on personality rather than performance | Subjective preference | Unfair assessment; potential discrimination | Base all suitability feedback on specific, observable, documented behaviours |
| Ignoring learning disabilities or language barriers | Unawareness; "they just need to try harder" | Learner fails to progress despite effort; mentor relationship breaks down | Recognize signs of different learning needs; adapt delivery; involve employer support resources |
| Failing to link lessons to prior knowledge | Treating each lesson as standalone | Learner lacks a mental framework; skills feel disconnected; retention poor | Always ask: "What do you already know that connects to this?" at the start of each lesson |
| Tolerating harassment from other team members toward the apprentice | "That's just how it is" | Harassment harms the apprentice; legal liability for mentor and employer; apprentice leaves the trade | Intervene immediately; support reporting; model respectful behaviour consistently |

## Memory Aids

**Four-Step Job Instruction Method — "PPTF":**
- **P**repare the learner (objective, point, prior knowledge)
- **P**resent the task (demonstrate at speed, then slowly with narration)
- **T**ry out (guided practice with coaching)
- **F**ollow up (observe independently; feedback; advance practice stage)

**Six Context Questions — "4W + H + W":**
- **W**ho is responsible
- **W**hat is being done
- **W**here it occurs
- **W**hen it must be done
- **H**ow to do it
- **W**hy it matters (the most important one)

**Three Practice Stages — "G.L.F.":**
- **G**uided (hands-on with mentor; real-time correction)
- **L**imited independence (learner attempts; mentor observes; feedback after)
- **F**ull independence (output checked; learner self-corrects)

**Mentor Roles — "ICAAML":**
- **I**nstructor
- **C**oach
- **A**ssessor
- **A**dvocate
- **M**odel (of technique)
- **L**eader (of professional culture)

**Essential Skills in Food Service — "RWD OCN TDC":**
- **R**eading, **W**riting, **D**ocument use
- **O**ral communication, **C**ontinuous learning, **N**umeracy
- **T**hinking, **D**igital technology, working with others (**C**olleagues)

**Learning Styles — "SHT":**
- **S**ee it (demonstration, diagrams, photos)
- **H**ear it (narration, verbal explanation, discussion)
- **T**ry it (hands-on practice, repetition)
→ Use all three simultaneously for every skill

### Exam Q&A

**Q1: What are the four steps of the Job Instruction Training method used when teaching a skill to an apprentice?**
A: (1) **Prepare the learner** — state the learning objective, explain why it matters, connect to prior knowledge; (2) **Present the task** — demonstrate at normal speed first, then slowly with verbal narration of each step; (3) **Try out** — have the learner attempt the skill with coaching; (4) **Follow up** — observe independently, provide feedback, determine readiness to advance.

**Q2: What are the six context questions a mentor should address when demonstrating any skill?**
A: **Who** (who is responsible for performing this), **What** (what skill is being performed), **Where** (where in the kitchen/process it occurs), **When** (when it should be done), **Why** (why it matters — the principle and consequence), **How** (the technique step by step). The most important is "Why" — it is the reason the learner will apply the skill consistently when not supervised.

**Q3: What are the three practice conditions in order from most supported to most independent?**
A: (1) **Guided** — mentor works alongside the learner; errors corrected in real time; used for new, complex, or safety-critical skills; (2) **Limited independence** — learner attempts independently while mentor observes; feedback given after task completion; (3) **Full independence** — learner performs without monitoring; output checked; learner self-corrects.

**Q4: What are the nine essential skills identified in the Range of Variables?**
A: Reading, writing, document use, oral communication, numeracy, thinking, working with others, digital technology, continuous learning.

**Q5: How does the mentor's positional authority affect their responsibility regarding harassment?**
A: The mentor holds power over the apprentice (can influence their training progress, assessments, and career path). This power imbalance makes harassment in the mentoring relationship especially harmful — the apprentice may feel unable to report or resist. The mentor has a heightened obligation to create psychological safety, model respectful behaviour, intervene against harassment from others, and ensure the apprentice knows how to report concerns safely.

**Q6: A mentor notices that an apprentice consistently struggles to read recipe cards and HACCP forms accurately, but performs well when given verbal instructions and demonstrations. What learning need might this indicate, and how should the mentor adapt?**
A: This pattern is consistent with a reading-related learning need (such as dyslexia). The mentor should: prioritize verbal instructions alongside written ones; use visual guides and photos with recipe cards; allow oral responses for assessment where appropriate; avoid treating the difficulty as lack of effort; consider discussing with the employer whether additional support resources are available. The apprentice's fundamental capability is not in question — only the delivery format needs adjustment.

**Q7: Why should a mentor always explain the "Why" before or during demonstrating a skill, rather than simply showing the technique?**
A: A learner who understands why a skill matters will apply it consistently — including when not supervised, under time pressure, or when tempted to take shortcuts. A learner who only knows the procedure (without the principle) will often skip or modify steps when challenged. In food safety specifically, the "Why" (bacterial growth in the TDZ; allergen cross-contact risk; botulinum toxin in anaerobic conditions) is what makes a cook consistently safe rather than compliant only when watched.

**Q8: What is the difference between a learning objective and the point of a lesson? Give an example.**
A: The **learning objective** states what the learner will be **able to do** after the lesson: "You will be able to complete a two-stage cooling entry in the HACCP temperature log correctly." The **point of the lesson** explains **why this matters**: "Because food cooling too slowly through the temperature danger zone is one of the leading causes of foodborne illness in commercial kitchens, and this log is your evidence that you managed the risk." Both are needed — the objective defines the outcome; the point provides the motivation to achieve it.

**Q9: What documentation must a mentor maintain during an apprenticeship, and why does this matter?**
A: Key documents: apprenticeship training record/log (OJT hours; signed by journeyperson), competency checklist (skills demonstrated to standard), training plan, performance review notes. Documentation matters because: (1) OJT hours must be verified for apprenticeship progression; (2) competency sign-offs are required for certification; (3) disputes about training progress require written evidence; (4) documentation supports planning for what training is still needed before in-school or exam periods.

**Q10: A mentor observes that an apprentice self-assesses their knife skills as "excellent" but consistently produces uneven cuts that don't meet the brunoise standard. What does this gap indicate and what should the mentor do?**
A: The gap between self-assessment and mentor observation indicates a **lack of self-awareness** about the standard — the apprentice may not know what "excellent brunoise" looks like, or may not be able to critically evaluate their own work. The mentor should: show side-by-side comparison of the apprentice's cuts vs. the standard; use a ruler or measurement to make the gap objective (not a matter of opinion); have the apprentice assess their work against the defined standard ("A standard brunoise is 3mm × 3mm × 3mm — how many of yours fall within ±0.5mm?"); connect the feedback to the practical consequence (uneven cuts have uneven cooking times).

**Q11: What is the Red Seal (Interprovincial Standard) program and how does it relate to the mentor's role?**
A: The Red Seal program is a nationally recognized certification system that allows qualified tradespersons to work across Canadian provinces and territories without additional examination. In the Cook trade, it is the standard of excellence that apprentices work toward. The mentor's role in supporting Red Seal includes: ensuring the apprentice develops competencies aligned with the national occupational standard; documenting OJT hours as required by the provincial apprenticeship authority; supporting in-school attendance and exam preparation; providing feedback that directly relates to the skills assessed in the Red Seal examination.

**Q12: When assessing an apprentice's suitability for the trade, what two criteria must a mentor ensure are met to make the assessment fair and professional?**
A: (1) **Evidence-based** — the assessment must be based on specific, documented, observable behaviours and outcomes — not impressions, personality judgments, or assumptions; (2) **Protected grounds-neutral** — the assessment must be completely free of any consideration of the apprentice's race, gender, age, disability, or any other prohibited ground under the Canadian Human Rights Act or applicable provincial human rights code. Different performance levels must be traceable to different documented behaviours, not assumptions about what someone is capable of based on personal characteristics.
