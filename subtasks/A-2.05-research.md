## Why Cleaning Is the Foundation Under Everything Else

In food safety:
- **Cleaning** removes soil → so sanitizer can work.
- **Sanitizing** kills microbes → so food doesn't pick them up.
- **Cooking** kills surviving microbes in food → final defense.
- **Cold storage** stops microbial growth → preserves what's safe.

Skip cleaning, and every downstream control fails:
- Sanitizer applied to greasy surfaces is **inactivated** before it can kill anything.
- Biofilms grow on uncleaned surfaces — communities of bacteria embedded in self-produced slime that resist chemical sanitizers and require **mechanical disruption** to remove.
- Cooked-on residue harbours pathogens in micro-pockets unreachable by routine wiping.
- Cross-contamination travels easily across grimy surfaces.

> **Cleaning is the precondition for safety. Sanitizing on dirt is theatre.**

## The Science of Soil and Cleaning Chemistry

Different soils require different chemistry. Choosing the wrong cleaner wastes time and can damage equipment.

| Soil type | Examples | Best chemistry | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fats & grease** | Fryer oil, butter, meat fat, sauté splatter | **Alkaline degreaser** (pH 9–12) | Saponifies fat — converts it to water-soluble soap |
| **Protein** | Egg, dairy, meat residue, blood | **Alkaline + chlorinated cleaner** | Alkali denatures protein; chlorine bleaches and breaks bonds |
| **Carbohydrate / starch** | Sauces, gravy thickener, pasta water residue | **Mild alkaline + warm water** | Starch dissolves with heat + mild base |
| **Mineral scale / lime / hard water** | White deposits in dish machines, steam kettles, ice machines | **Acidic delimer** (pH 2–4) | Acid dissolves carbonate minerals |
| **Carbon / burned-on** | Sheet pan carbon, oven build-up, fryer baskets | **Heavy alkaline + dwell time** | Strong caustic breaks down polymerized residues |
| **Rust** | Iron surfaces, neglected equipment | **Acid + mechanical** | Acid + scrubbing |
| **Coffee/tea stain** | Brewing equipment | **Oxidizing cleaner** (chlorinated or oxygen-based) | Bleaches tannins |
| **Sugar/syrup** | Pastry, beverage | **Hot water + mild alkali** | Dissolves with heat |

> ⚠️ **Never combine acid (delimer) with chlorinated cleaner or chlorine bleach** — releases **chlorine gas**. Never combine bleach with ammonia (some glass cleaners and quats contain ammonia compounds) — releases **chloramines**. Both have hospitalized cooks. **[Regulatory — WHMIS / OH&S safe handling]**

### What "soap" actually is

The original subtask says "hot soap solutions." In commercial kitchens, this almost always means a **detergent** — a synthetic surfactant designed to reduce surface tension, lift soil, and emulsify fats. True bar/soap is not used in commercial dishwashing because soap reacts with hard-water minerals to form scum.

A surfactant molecule has:
- A **hydrophilic head** that loves water.
- A **hydrophobic tail** that grabs grease.

Mechanical agitation + warm water + surfactant + dwell time = soil lifted into solution and rinsed away.

### Why warm water (≈ 43 °C / 110 °F)

- Below ~ 32 °C, fats stay solid; detergent struggles to emulsify them.
- Above ~ 49 °C, proteins start to denature and bond to surfaces ("baked-on" effect) — and operator scald risk rises.
- The sweet spot for wash water is **38–49 °C / 100–120 °F** — hot enough to liquefy fats, cool enough to keep proteins mobile.

### A-2.05.01P — Identify Utensils, Tools, Equipment and Appliances to Be Cleaned

**Performance Criteria:** identify utensils, tools, equipment and appliances to be cleaned
**Evidence of Attainment:** utensils, tools, equipment and appliances to be cleaned are identified.

#### Categories of cleanable items in a commercial kitchen

| Category | Examples | Cleaning approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Hand tools** | Knives, peelers, zesters, microplanes, tongs, spatulas, whisks, ladles | 3-sink or dish machine; manual sanitize after each protein |
| **Cutting boards & containers** | Plastic boards, cambros, sixth-pans, half-pans, full hotel pans | Dish machine or 3-sink |
| **Smallwares** | Mixing bowls, sheet pans, sieves, china caps, chinois, cheesecloth | Dish machine; specialty: chinois often re-cleaned manually |
| **Disassemble-required equipment** | Slicer, buffalo chopper, robot-coupe, meat grinder, mandoline, immersion blender, stand mixer attachments | Lockout, disassemble, soak, scrub, sanitize, dry, reassemble |
| **Heavy stationary equipment** | Combi-oven, range, fryer, flat-top grill, char-broiler, salamander, plancha, tilt skillet, steam kettle | Brand-specific procedures; mostly in-place cleaning with degreaser |
| **Refrigeration** | Walk-ins, reach-ins, blast chillers, prep-table refrigerators, ice machines | Scheduled deep clean; daily wipe-down of gaskets/drip trays |
| **Sinks & dish area** | 3-compartment sink, hand sink, dish machine, pre-rinse spray, dishtable | Daily and per-shift cleaning |
| **Hood and ventilation** | Hood filters, grease trays, exhaust ducts | Filter swap daily–weekly; duct cleaning by certified contractor under NFPA 96 |
| **Floors and drains** | Quarry tile, drains, mats | Daily mopping, drain treatment, mat washing |
| **Storage shelving** | Walk-in shelves, dry storage, dunnage racks | Weekly to monthly deep-clean per MSS |
| **Service ware** | Plates, cutlery, glassware, banquet pieces | Dish machine; polished separately |
| **Cleaning tools themselves** | Mop heads, buckets, cloths, brushes | Cleaned and dried daily (a dirty mop spreads contamination) |

#### How to identify what needs cleaning right now

| Trigger | What to clean |
|---|---|
| **Task complete** | The tools and surface you just used |
| **Protein change** | All tools and surfaces between proteins |
| **Allergen task** | Allergen-isolated tools fully decontaminated |
| **Visible soiling** | Anything with visible debris, grease, or residue |
| **End of shift** | Station break-down per closing checklist |
| **Master Sanitation Schedule (MSS) cadence** | Items scheduled for daily/weekly/monthly deep clean |
| **Audit / inspection trigger** | Pre-inspection deep clean of all surfaces |
| **Pest indicator** | Affected and surrounding equipment |
| **Equipment failure** | Inspect, drain, clean before service or repair |

> **Memory aid — "CLEAN AS YOU GO" (CAYG)** is the underlying discipline. Cleaning is continuous, not a single end-of-shift event.

### A-2.05.02P — Disassemble and Assemble Kitchen Tools and Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** disassemble and assemble kitchen tools and equipment for cleaning purposes
**Evidence of Attainment:** kitchen tools and equipment are disassembled and assembled for cleaning purposes according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### The safety preamble — Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)

Before disassembling any **powered equipment**, isolate energy sources:
1. **Turn off** the equipment.
2. **Unplug** OR lock the breaker.
3. **Tag** if shared with others ("DO NOT ENERGIZE").
4. **Verify** by attempting to start — should be dead.
5. Only then begin disassembly.

**[Regulatory under provincial OH&S]** — see [A-1.01-research.md](A-1.01-research.md) for full LOTO procedure.

#### Equipment-specific disassembly

##### Slicer (Hobart-type)
1. LOTO — unplug.
2. Set blade to **"0"** position (zero gauge).
3. Engage blade guard.
4. Remove sharpening assembly (per manufacturer).
5. Remove the **ring guard** and **blade** (manufacturer-specific — often requires a key or knob). **Two-hand handling** + **cut-resistant glove** required.
6. Wash/rinse/sanitize each component manually — **do not submerge motor housing**.
7. Sanitize, air-dry, reassemble in reverse order.
8. Wipe motor housing with sanitizer-dampened cloth (never spray onto controls).
9. Test run before next use.

> **Cut-resistant glove + extreme care** — a slicer blade is razor sharp even when cool and stationary. **[Regulatory under OH&S; Best Practice apprenticeship rule]**

##### Buffalo chopper / food processor
1. LOTO.
2. Remove the S-blade with a blade handler or thick folded towel — **never bare fingers** even when off.
3. Disassemble bowl, lid, pusher, attachments.
4. 3-sink wash, rinse, sanitize, air-dry.
5. Wipe base with sanitizer-dampened cloth (do not submerge).
6. Reassemble dry.

##### Meat grinder
1. LOTO.
2. Disassemble: feed pan, hopper, worm/auger, knife, plate(s), ring/locking nut.
3. **Brush every thread** on the worm with a stiff brush — meat residue trapped in threads is a major contamination source.
4. 3-sink: hot detergent wash, rinse, sanitize, air-dry.
5. Reassemble dry — never assemble wet (grit transfer; rust).
6. Some operations lightly food-safe-oil the plates between uses.

##### Mixer (planetary, e.g., Hobart)
1. LOTO.
2. Remove the attachment (paddle, whip, dough hook).
3. Remove the bowl from the yoke.
4. 3-sink wash for attachments and bowl.
5. Wipe head/yoke with sanitizer cloth.
6. Wipe the **bowl-lift mechanism** — often missed; flour and dough buildup.
7. Reassemble.

##### Mandoline
1. LOTO (manual mandolines — no power, but treat the blade as live).
2. Engage blade lock if available.
3. Cut-resistant glove on dominant hand.
4. Brush off debris first (don't push residue into the cutting slot).
5. 3-sink wash, sanitize, air-dry.
6. Reassemble with guard engaged.

##### Robot-Coupe / vertical cutter mixer
1. LOTO.
2. Pull cover, blade assembly, pusher.
3. **Two-hand** blade removal with handler.
4. 3-sink for parts; sanitize-wipe base.

##### Immersion blender (stick blender)
1. LOTO.
2. Detach motor unit from blending shaft (usually a twist-lock).
3. Wash shaft and bell guard — water cannot enter the motor housing.
4. Wipe motor with sanitizer cloth.

##### Combi-oven
1. Empty cavity; remove racks and probe.
2. Run **manufacturer's self-clean cycle** (most modern combis use built-in chemicals and steam).
3. Manual: wipe cavity, glass door, gaskets with combi-safe degreaser.
4. **Descale** per water-hardness schedule — scale degrades steam efficiency and damages the boiler.
5. Reassemble; sanitize probe between uses.

##### Fryer
1. **Allow oil to cool to safe handling temperature** — never drain hot oil. Industry practice: cool below ~ 50 °C before manual handling; some boil-out procedures specify higher operating temperature with care.
2. Drain into a labelled grease cart (cooking oil recycling).
3. **Boil-out**: fill with water + fryer boil-out chemical, heat per chemical instructions, then drain.
4. Scrub interior with non-abrasive pad.
5. Rinse thoroughly — boil-out residue is alkaline and will turn oil rancid if not fully removed.
6. Refill with fresh or filtered oil.

> ⚠️ **Hot oil + water = explosive steam flash.** Boil-out chemicals are alkaline (lye-based) and require goggles, rubber gloves, apron. **[Regulatory — PPE per SDS]**

##### Ice machine
1. Disconnect or follow manufacturer service mode.
2. Remove ice; empty bin.
3. Sanitize interior with **manufacturer-approved sanitizer** (ice-machine-specific products).
4. Clean condenser coils per schedule.
5. Replace water filter on schedule.
6. **Common audit finding:** pink/black biofilm and mould on internal surfaces — strict schedule required.

##### Refrigeration (walk-in / reach-in)
1. Move product to backup or shift schedule to allow access.
2. Clean shelving (out of unit if possible) in 3-sink.
3. Wipe interior walls, ceiling, floor, gaskets with food-safe detergent then sanitizer.
4. Clean fan blades and grilles (off, with PPE; some require service technician).
5. Clean drip pan and condensate drain.
6. Verify temperature post-cleaning.

#### Reassembly principles
- **Reassemble dry** — moisture trapped in seams encourages microbial growth and rust.
- **Verify** all parts present and seated correctly.
- **Inspect** for damaged seals/gaskets; replace if needed.
- **Test-run** before returning to service.
- **Document** disassembly cleaning on the MSS log.

### A-2.05.03P — Remove Residual Debris

**Performance Criteria:** remove residual debris
**Evidence of Attainment:** residual debris is removed by using hot soap solutions according to manufacturers' specifications prior to sanitizing processes, while ensuring soap concentration according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### Why "remove debris first" is not optional

- **Sanitizer + organic matter = inactivated sanitizer.** Chlorine in particular is consumed by organic soil — proteins, blood, fats. By the time the sanitizer "should" be killing pathogens, it's been chemically neutralized.
- **Biofilms** form anchored to soil. Mechanical removal of soil is what breaks the biofilm matrix; sanitizer alone barely penetrates it.
- **Cooked-on debris** physically shields underlying microbes from chemical and heat sanitation.

#### Standard pre-clean sequence

1. **Scrape** — pull off solid debris with a bench scraper, putty knife, or grill brick.
2. **Pre-rinse** — warm water rinse or pre-rinse spray to remove loose soil.
3. **Apply detergent** — at correct dilution from a metered dispenser or via SDS-specified mix.
4. **Allow dwell time** — chemical contact time matters; check label (typically 30 seconds to several minutes).
5. **Mechanical action** — scrub, brush, or use mechanical wash.
6. **Rinse** with potable water.
7. **Now ready** for sanitizing.

#### Detergent concentration discipline

- Use a **metered dispenser** where available — consistent and verified.
- Follow **SDS / manufacturer label** when mixing manually.
- **Too weak** = ineffective cleaning, residual soil.
- **Too strong** = chemical residue (taste, irritation, possible deactivation of sanitizer), waste, possible surface damage.
- **Verify dilution** per operation HACCP plan; some ops test detergent concentration with quaternary or alkaline test strips depending on product.

#### Common ineffective shortcuts
- "Wiping" with a damp cloth in place of washing.
- Spraying sanitizer onto soil ("spray and walk").
- Reusing a soiled cleaning cloth across multiple surfaces.
- Using cold water on greasy equipment.

### A-2.05.04P — Apply Cleaning Procedures

**Performance Criteria:** apply cleaning procedures
**Evidence of Attainment:** cleaning procedures are applied according to company policies and jurisdictional regulations.

#### The 3-compartment sink (manual warewashing) — still a Red Seal staple

| Sink | Contents | Temperature | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Wash** | Detergent + water | ≈ 43–49 °C / 110–120 °F | Scrape and pre-rinse first |
| **2. Rinse** | Clean potable water | Warm | Removes detergent (residual deactivates sanitizer) |
| **3. Sanitize** | Chemical sanitizer at correct ppm **or** hot water ≥ 77 °C / 171 °F | Per chemistry / heat | Verify with strips or thermometer |
| **Then** | Air-dry on rack | — | No towels; gravity drain only |

> **[Regulatory]** Most provincial public-health food premises regulations require a 3-compartment sink (or equivalent dishwasher) in foodservice operations, with the wash-rinse-sanitize-airdry sequence specified.

##### Loading the 3-sink properly
- Pre-rinse heavily soiled items in the pre-rinse spray or scrape area first.
- Don't overfill the wash sink — soil load reduces effectiveness.
- **Change wash water** when visibly soiled or every 2 hours during heavy use.
- **Re-test sanitizer** after each major load.
- Items go from **dirty side to clean side** — never reverse.

#### Mechanical dishwashers

Two types:

##### High-temperature dishwasher
- Final rinse **≥ 82 °C / 180 °F**. **[Regulatory under public-health code]**
- No chemical sanitizer needed — heat does the work.
- Internal booster heater required for many models to hit this temp.
- **Verify** with a thermolabel (max-registering label that turns colour at the threshold), max-registering thermometer, or thermocouple plate.

##### Low-temperature / chemical dishwasher
- Final rinse with chlorine-based (or other) sanitizer at typically **~ 50 ppm** chlorine.
- Lower energy cost, but slower kill and ongoing chemical cost.
- **Verify** with chlorine test strip in each cycle (or per shift).

##### Dish machine maintenance
- **Daily**: empty, clean wash arms (unclog spray nozzles), strain tank, wipe gaskets.
- **Weekly**: descale (delime) per water hardness.
- **Monthly**: check chemical lines, verify temperatures, replace worn curtains.
- **Periodically**: verify booster heater function; check rinse-aid level.

##### Glasswashers (separate)
- Lower wash temperature for delicate glassware.
- Chemical sanitization; often have separate detergent for glass clarity.

#### Heat-based cleaning techniques

- **Steam cleaning** for ovens and hoods — degrease + sanitize in one step.
- **Boil-out** for fryers (above).
- **Self-clean** cycles in combi-ovens and some commercial ovens.
- **Hot-water sanitizing** in 3-sink by immersion at ≥ 77 °C for ≥ 30 sec.

#### Drying techniques

| Method | When | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Air-dry** | Always preferred for food-contact items | Required after sanitizing — towel-drying re-contaminates |
| **Drip-dry on racks** | After 3-sink | Items stored on edge for airflow |
| **Drying cabinet** | Some commercial dishwashers / large ops | Hot air circulation |
| **Compressed air** | Specialty applications (electronics) | Not typical for food contact |
| **Towel-dry** | **Only non-food-contact** items like exteriors of machines | Use single-use or laundered microfibres |

> ⚠️ **Never towel-dry food-contact surfaces.** Towels re-contaminate. **[Regulatory under public-health codes]**

#### Cleaning chemicals — use of chemicals (Range of Variables)

Per WHMIS 2015 (recap):
- All chemicals must have **SDS** accessible. **[Regulatory]**
- **Supplier labels** on original containers.
- **Workplace labels** on decanted containers (product ID, hazards, safe handling).
- **PPE per SDS Section 8** — typically rubber gloves, goggles, apron for concentrated cleaners.
- **Never mix** chemicals (bleach + acid, bleach + ammonia).
- **Storage** below food, ventilated, often locked.

### A-2.05.05P — Remove Tough Cooked-on, Carbonized, Dehydrated or Stubborn Residual Debris

**Performance Criteria:** remove tough cooked-on, carbonized, dehydrated or stubborn residual debris or stains
**Evidence of Attainment:** tough cooked-on, carbonized, dehydrated or stubborn residual debris or stains are removed using cleaning tools and equipment.

#### Tools for tough soil (Range of Variables) — and when to use them

| Tool | Use | Caution |
|---|---|---|
| **Bench scraper / putty knife** | Removing crusts from grills, flat-tops, sheet pans | Don't scratch coated surfaces |
| **Grill brick (pumice block)** | Carbonized residue on flat-tops; minor stainless soil | Crumbles fine — not for delicate surfaces |
| **Stiff nylon brush** | Equipment interiors, sheet pans, grinder threads | OK on most surfaces |
| **Stainless wool / pads** | Heavy carbon on stainless steel | **Never on coated cookware** (non-stick, anodized) |
| **Green Scotch-Brite / scouring pad** | General heavy cleaning | Avoid on shiny polished stainless (scratching) |
| **White or blue scouring pad** | Stainless steel without scratching | OK for most kitchen surfaces |
| **Steel wool** | Heavy cast-iron, occasionally aluminum | **Never on stainless** — leaves rust-forming residue |
| **Hood and oven degreaser** | Built-up grease and carbon | **PPE required** — these are caustic; goggles + chemical gloves + apron |
| **Carbon/oven cleaner spray** | Heavy carbonized residue in ovens | High-pH; rinse thoroughly |
| **Soak overnight in hot detergent** | Stubborn baked-on residue on sheet pans | Saves scrubbing time |
| **Power washer** | Floor mats, walk-in floors, exterior gear | **Never near electrical** equipment |

#### The "soak first, scrub second" principle

The single most-overlooked time-saver in commercial cleaning:

- **Cover the soil with hot detergent solution** and let it dwell.
- **30 minutes to overnight** of soaking can do 80% of the work that hours of scrubbing otherwise require.
- Dwell time **converts** insoluble cooked-on residue into soluble or liftable form.

#### Surface compatibility — pick the right pad

| Surface | Safe |
|---|---|
| **Stainless steel** | White/blue scouring pad, soft brush, baking soda; AVOID steel wool (rust), green Scotch-Brite (scratches polish on visible surfaces) |
| **Non-stick / Teflon** | Soft cloth or sponge only; NO abrasive |
| **Anodized aluminum** | Soft cloth or mild abrasive; NO heavy abrasive |
| **Cast iron** | Hot water + bristle brush; salt scrub; minimal soap (preserves seasoning); re-season after deep clean |
| **Copper** | Mild abrasive or specialty copper cleaner; lemon + salt traditional |
| **Glass / ceramic** | Non-abrasive pad; specialty cooktop cleaner |
| **Plastic cutting boards** | Hot detergent + soft brush; replace deeply scored boards |
| **Wood cutting surfaces** | Hot soapy water + scrub; avoid soaking; sanitize and dry quickly |

#### Special situations

##### Carbonized sheet pans
- Soak overnight in hot detergent.
- Sprinkle with baking soda; add hydrogen peroxide if needed.
- Use a green pad or nylon brush.
- For severe carbon: commercial pan cleaner (alkaline degreaser, PPE required).
- Heavy-duty: send to deep-clean cycle in combi or have stripped professionally.

##### Burnt-on residue in pots
- **Deglaze with water** while still warm (carefully — steam burns).
- Add water + baking soda or detergent; simmer.
- Scrape with wooden or silicone spatula.
- Never use steel wool on aluminum (pits metal) or non-stick.

##### Stained plastic containers (cambros) — turmeric, tomato, oil
- Soak in oxygen bleach solution.
- Sun exposure (UV) helps bleach turmeric stains.
- Once permanently stained but clean, often replaced for visual standards.

### A-2.05.06P — Rinse and Dry

**Performance Criteria:** rinse and dry utensils, tools, equipment and appliances
**Evidence of Attainment:** utensils, tools, equipment and appliances are rinsed and dried thoroughly.

#### Why rinse thoroughly

- **Detergent residue** can deactivate chemical sanitizer.
- **Sanitizer residue** at the wrong concentration is itself a contamination — chemical hazard.
- **Soap residue** on cookware can taint food flavour.
- **Caustic residue** from boil-outs and oven cleaners can damage skin and contaminate food.

#### How to rinse

- **Potable water only** — never sink-runoff or recycled.
- **Adequate flow** — submerge or spray with running water.
- **Inspect** for visible residue; re-rinse if any.
- **Especially thorough** after caustic cleaners (oven cleaner, boil-out, dish machine descaler).

#### Why air-dry over towel-dry

- **Towel-drying re-contaminates** — every towel carries some microbial load.
- **Air-drying** allows the surface to dry while sanitizer continues its kill action briefly.
- Items must be **stored dry** — wet stacking traps moisture and grows bacteria (especially gram-negatives like *Pseudomonas*).

#### Air-dry technique

- **Wire racks** with good airflow.
- **On edge / inverted** for plates and bowls.
- **Hung** for utensils where possible.
- **Wood cutting boards** — dry quickly to avoid warping.
- **Cast iron** — towel-dry briefly to prevent rust, then dry over low heat and re-season.
- **Inside of containers** — facing down to drain.

#### Storage of clean tools and equipment

- **Stored in a clean, dry location** — not in dirty areas, not on the floor.
- **Knives** stored on magnetic strips, in blocks, or in sheaths — not loose in drawers.
- **Container nesting** allowed only when fully dry.
- **Smallwares** stored upside-down or covered.

### A-2.05.02L — Procedures for Disassembling/Assembling and Cleaning

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to clean tools and equipment.

#### Disassembly principles (recap)

1. **LOTO** powered equipment.
2. **Manufacturer instructions** — every machine differs.
3. **Use proper PPE** — cut gloves for blades, chemical gloves for caustic cleaning.
4. **Reassemble dry**.
5. **Test** before next use.
6. **Document** in MSS log.

#### Cleaning procedures (Range of Variables)

##### Use of chemicals
- Right chemical for the soil.
- Correct dilution, verified.
- PPE per SDS.
- Dwell time before scrubbing.
- Rinse fully.

##### Three-sink method
- Wash → Rinse → Sanitize → Air-dry.
- Change water as it soils.
- Test sanitizer concentration regularly.

##### Proper heat and drying techniques
- Heat sanitizing ≥ 77 °C / 171 °F surface or ≥ 82 °C / 180 °F dish-machine final rinse.
- Air-dry only for food contact.
- Inspect dryness before storing.

### A-2.05.03L — Regulatory Requirements

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of regulatory requirements pertaining to cleaning tools and equipment.

#### Canadian regulatory layers for cleaning

| Authority | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Health Canada** | Food safety policy and sanitation guidance |
| **CFIA — SFCR** | Federally-registered: PCP with sanitation SSOPs |
| **Provincial Public Health Food Premises Regulations** | Restaurants: 3-sink or dishwasher with prescribed temperatures and sanitizer concentrations |
| **WHMIS 2015** | Chemical hazard communication (labels, SDS, training) |
| **Provincial OH&S** | Worker safety; PPE for chemical handling; LOTO for powered equipment |
| **Municipal fire codes / NFPA 96** | Hood and exhaust cleaning frequency |
| **Municipal public health units** | On-site inspection |

#### What inspectors look for on cleaning

- Functional 3-sink (or properly running dishwasher) with prescribed temperatures.
- Sanitizer at correct concentration with test strips on-site.
- WHMIS labels on all chemical containers.
- SDS binder/QR accessible.
- Master Sanitation Schedule (MSS) documented and signed.
- Clean equipment in service — interior, gaskets, blade-area of slicer/grinder.
- Dish machine final rinse verified.
- Drains and hood filters cleaned.
- Cleaning tools (mops, cloths) in good condition and stored properly.

#### HACCP perspective on cleaning

Cleaning is typically a **prerequisite program** (SSOP — Sanitation Standard Operating Procedure) rather than a CCP. But it is **essential to the success of every CCP** — without it:
- Cooking CCPs are compromised (cross-contamination after the kill step).
- Cooling CCPs are compromised (dirty cooling pans).
- Cold-holding CCPs are compromised (uncleaned walk-ins).

Typical SSOP elements:
1. **Purpose** — what is being cleaned and why.
2. **Frequency** — daily, weekly, monthly.
3. **Procedure** — step by step.
4. **Chemicals and tools** — names, concentrations.
5. **PPE** — required equipment.
6. **Responsibility** — who performs it.
7. **Verification** — how compliance is checked.
8. **Record** — sign-off form.

## 3. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Mid-service slicer cleaning between proteins
1. Notify line: "Slicer down for cleaning."
2. **LOTO** — unplug.
3. Set blade to "0", engage guard.
4. Remove ring guard and blade per manual (cut-resistant glove on).
5. Wipe gross debris.
6. 3-sink: wash, rinse, sanitize, air-dry blade and components.
7. Wipe motor housing with sanitizer cloth.
8. Reassemble dry; test.
9. Plug in; resume service.

### Scenario 2 — End-of-shift hood filter cleaning
1. Allow filters to cool.
2. Remove and place in soak tank with hot alkaline degreaser (PPE: goggles, gloves, apron).
3. Soak overnight or per manufacturer.
4. Rinse with hot water until clear.
5. Air-dry on rack.
6. Reinstall.
7. **Note:** Hood system / duct cleaning is performed by certified contractor under NFPA 96 frequency requirements — this is filter cleaning only. **[Regulatory]**

### Scenario 3 — Burnt-on tomato sauce in a 20 L stockpot
1. Fill with water above the residue.
2. Add detergent.
3. Bring to a low simmer on low heat (do not boil dry).
4. Turn off; let dwell for 30–60 minutes.
5. Scrape with wood/silicone scraper while still warm.
6. Soak overnight if needed.
7. Run through 3-sink.

### Scenario 4 — Carbonized sheet pan from over-roasted vegetables
1. Soak overnight in a hot tub with detergent.
2. In the morning: green pad + baking soda or commercial pan cleaner with PPE.
3. Rinse thoroughly.
4. If still stained but clean, OK to use; if scored/pitted, replace.

### Scenario 5 — Dish machine final rinse reads 76 °C instead of 82 °C
1. **Stop service** of mechanical dishwashing — pull a dishwasher card if available.
2. Switch to 3-sink for warewashing.
3. Inspect dish machine: rinse aid level? Booster heater function?
4. Service call.
5. Verify after repair with thermolabel before resuming.

### Scenario 6 — Spilled cleaning chemical concentrate during decanting
1. **Evacuate area**; activate ventilation.
2. Check SDS Section 6 (Spill response) for the specific product.
3. PPE: chemical gloves, goggles, apron, respirator if SDS specifies.
4. Contain with spill kit absorbent.
5. Bag and dispose per SDS Section 13.
6. Wash affected surfaces.
7. Document incident in OH&S log.
8. **If splashed on skin/eyes**: 15-minute eyewash + medical attention.

## 4. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **"Spray sanitizer; it'll clean it"** — Sanitizer does not clean. Soil deactivates sanitizer.
- **Skipping the rinse** between wash and sanitize — detergent residue weakens sanitizer.
- **Towel-drying food-contact surfaces** — re-contaminates instantly.
- **Steel wool on stainless steel** — leaves rust-forming iron particles; scratches harbour bacteria.
- **Green pad on polished stainless** — visible scratches and bacterial harbourage.
- **Stacking wet** — promotes biofilm and gram-negative growth.
- **Reusing cleaning water all shift** — soil load reduces effectiveness to near zero.
- **Pouring water into hot fryer** — explosive steam ejection; serious burns.
- **Mixing chemicals** — chlorine gas, chloramines, surprise reactions.
- **No PPE for heavy degreaser / oven cleaner** — caustic burns to eyes and skin.
- **Assembling equipment wet** — rust, microbial growth, contamination.
- **Cleaning slicer/grinder/chopper while plugged in** — life-threatening.
- **Not testing sanitizer concentration** — invisible failure.
- **Ignoring scale buildup in dish machines** — efficiency drop, lower rinse temperatures.
- **Cleaning combi-oven interior with abrasives** — damages catalyst and surfaces.
- **Pencil-whipping the MSS log** — common audit citation.

## 6. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with A-2.05.

1. **Cleaning removes soil; sanitizing reduces microbes.** Sanitizer does not work on dirty surfaces.
2. **3-sink method:** Wash (detergent, ≈ 43–49 °C) → Rinse (potable) → Sanitize (chemical or heat ≥ 77 °C) → Air-dry.
3. **High-temp dishwasher final rinse: ≥ 82 °C / 180 °F.**
4. **Low-temp / chemical dishwasher:** chlorine sanitizer typically ~ 50 ppm; verify with strip per cycle.
5. **Standard food-contact sanitizer concentrations:** chlorine 100–200 ppm; quats 200–400 ppm; iodine 12.5–25 ppm.
6. **Detergent vs sanitizer:** never substitute one for the other; rinse between them.
7. **WHMIS 2015** governs chemical labels and SDS access; workplace labels required on decanted containers.
8. **Lockout/Tagout** required before disassembling powered equipment (slicers, choppers, grinders, mixers).
9. **Cut-resistant glove + two-hand technique** for blade removal on slicers, choppers, robot-coupes.
10. **Reassemble dry** — moisture promotes rust and microbial growth.
11. **Air-dry only** for food-contact surfaces — never towel-dry.
12. **Soil-specific chemistry:** alkaline degreaser for fats, alkaline + chlorinated for protein, acid for scale/lime.
13. **Never mix:** bleach + acid = chlorine gas; bleach + ammonia = chloramine.
14. **Wash temperature ≈ 43–49 °C / 110–120 °F** — hot enough to melt fats, cool enough to keep proteins mobile.
15. **Soak first** — dwell time saves scrubbing on tough residues.
16. **Pad selection:** white/blue for general stainless; green Scotch-Brite for tough soil (not on polished surfaces); steel wool only on cast iron; never abrasive on non-stick.
17. **Cooled fryer + boil-out + thorough rinse** — never pour water into hot oil.
18. **Combi-oven self-clean** + periodic descaling.
19. **Ice machine** requires scheduled deep clean — biofilm and mould are common audit findings.
20. **Dish machine maintenance:** daily strain, weekly descale, monthly chemical-line check.
21. **Hood filters** cleaned daily/weekly; **hood ducts cleaned by certified contractor per NFPA 96** at frequencies tied to cooking volume.
22. **Master Sanitation Schedule (MSS)** — documented daily/weekly/monthly cadence; signed off.
23. **SSOP (Sanitation Standard Operating Procedure)** — HACCP prerequisite program; describes who/what/how/verification for each task.
24. **Test strips** on-hand for each sanitizer chemistry used.
25. **Calibrated thermometer** — verifies wash and sanitize temperatures; ice-point calibration to 0 °C.
26. **PPE** for chemical handling — rubber gloves, goggles, apron per SDS Section 8.
27. **Stored clean** — dry, off floor, covered or inverted, knives in dedicated storage.
28. **Pencil-whipping logs** — top audit citation; documentation must match reality.
29. **Sanitation is the foundation under temperature control, hygiene, and allergen control** — fails silently, undermines everything else.
30. **Inspector hot zones:** slicer interior, grinder threads, ice machine, walk-in gaskets, dish-machine rinse temp, drain biofilm.
