## Why Processing Is Where Stocks Are Won or Lost

By the time a stock simmers, the cook has very little control over outcome. Every flaw introduced during processing — wrong tools, sloppy measurements, missed blanching, oversized vegetable cuts, dirty cheesecloth — is **baked into the final product**.

**Processing covers six critical zones:**
1. **Tool & equipment readiness** — the right pot, the right strainer.
2. **Yield math** — the right amount for the day's service.
3. **Mise en place** — everything in place, in sequence.
4. **Measuring** — by weight whenever possible.
5. **Ingredient preparation** — blanching, roasting, sweating, cutting, bundling.
6. **Food-safety discipline** — temperature control, cross-contamination prevention, sanitation.

The principle: **a stock simmer cannot fix processing failures.**

## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### D-13.02.01P — Select and Use Tools and Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** select and use tools and equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** tools and equipment are selected and used according to recipe specifications.

#### The stock-making toolkit (Range of Variables, expanded)

##### Stockpots

| Feature | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Tall and narrow** profile | Minimizes evaporation surface relative to volume; preserves liquid during long simmers; vertical convection keeps gentle simmer stable |
| **Heavy-gauge bottom** | Even heat distribution; prevents scorching on gas burners |
| **Stainless steel** (preferred) | Non-reactive; safe with acidic deglazes and tomato; durable |
| **Aluminum** (older / value tier) | Reactive with acid → can affect colour and flavour; lighter weight |
| **Volume range** | 8 L (small batch), 20 L, 40 L, 60 L+ (banquet) — match to recipe yield |
| **Capacity rule** | Fill to ~ ⅔ to ¾ full — leaves room for boil and skimming without overflow |
| **Side handles + lifting handles** | Two-person lift required when full and hot |

##### Spigot pots (drain-valve stockpots)

- A **spigot pot** (also called a "valve pot," "soup-and-stock pot with drain") has a **valve near the base** for **draining liquid without disturbing solids**.
- Allows the cook to draw off finished stock from the bottom while leaving the bones, vegetables, and risen scum settled above.
- **Reduces strain on the back** — no lifting and pouring 40 L of hot liquid.
- **Improves clarity** — less agitation of the solid bed means cleaner final stock.
- **[Operational / Best Practice]** Standard in higher-volume Canadian kitchens.

##### Roasting pans (for brown stocks)

- **Heavy-gauge, low-sided** so vegetables and bones brown rather than steam.
- **Large enough** to spread bones in a **single layer** — overlapped bones steam instead of roast → no Maillard reaction → no colour, no depth.
- **Deglazable** — the **sucs** (caramelized fond) on the pan bottom are the reason brown stock is brown. Discard those and you have a pale beef-bone water.

##### Strainers

| Strainer | Mesh | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **China cap (chinois)** | Conical perforated metal; coarse | First-pass straining of stock — removes bones, vegetables, large solids |
| **Fine-mesh chinois** | Conical fine wire mesh | Second-pass for clarity — removes scum particles, small bone shards |
| **Cheesecloth-lined chinois** | Above + cheesecloth | Removes the finest particles for clear stocks and consommé |
| **Fine sieve / tamis** | Flat-bottom fine wire | Particle removal for sauces and finishing |

**Strainers are stacked when possible** — coarse first, then fine, then cheesecloth-lined — for progressively cleaner output.

##### Skimmers and ladles

| Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| **Mesh skimmer (spider)** | Lifting bones, vegetables, large solids out of the pot |
| **Wide flat skimmer** | Removing scum and foam from the surface |
| **Ladle** | Transferring stock; portioning into cooling vessels |
| **Fat skimmer / gravy separator** | Removing surface fat after cooling (or use a chilled stock — fat solidifies on top for easy removal) |

##### Cutting boards and knives

| Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| **Heavy chef's knife or butcher's knife** | General cutting; trimming vegetables |
| **Cleaver** | Splitting smaller bones; cracking joints |
| **Bone saw** | Cutting larger bones (typically a butcher's task before bones reach the cook) |
| **Cutting boards (colour-coded)** | Red for raw red meat, yellow for poultry, blue for seafood, green for produce — see [A-2.04-research.md](A-2.04-research.md) |
| **Sanitize between proteins and between raw/RTE** | Critical |

##### Scales

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **Digital platform scale (large capacity, e.g., 15–30 kg)** | Bones, large vegetables, water by weight |
| **Digital benchtop scale (≤ 5 kg)** | Mirepoix, aromatics |
| **Tare function** | Essential — zero out container weight |
| **g / kg dual mode** | For both small and large batches |

> **Why weight over volume:** A "cup of chopped onion" varies wildly depending on cut size and packing. **400 g of onion is 400 g of onion, every time.** Professional recipes are written in weight for this reason. **[Best Practice]**

##### Cheesecloth

| Use | How |
|---|---|
| **Sachet d'épices** | Bundle peppercorns, bay, thyme, parsley stems, optional clove — tied with twine |
| **Lining a strainer** | Final clarity pass — captures the finest scum particles |
| **Wrapping vegetables / aromatics** for easy retrieval | Less common but useful for short-cook stocks |
| **Clarifying consommé** | Strain the cleared liquid through cheesecloth-lined chinois after the raft has formed |

**Use food-grade cheesecloth only.** Rinse in clean water before first use to remove sizing/starch. **[Best Practice]**

##### Butcher's twine

| Use | How |
|---|---|
| **Tying bouquet garni** | Around the stems; long tail to tie to pot handle for easy retrieval |
| **Closing a sachet d'épices** | Cinched around the gathered cheesecloth |
| **Trussing larger items** for poaching in court-bouillon | Holds shape during cooking |

**Use undyed, food-grade cotton twine.** Synthetic or coloured twines may shed dye or melt.

##### Other commonly-used items
- **Pot stand / trivet** — for placing stockpot on counter without scorching.
- **Heat-resistant gloves / pads** — handling hot pots; remember to keep **dry**.
- **Hotel pans (shallow)** — for cooling per the 2/4/6 rule.
- **Ice wand / ice paddle** — for cooling deep stockpots.
- **Storage containers** — labelled, dated; stainless or food-grade plastic.
- **Probe thermometer** — for verifying simmer temperature and cooling milestones.

#### Equipment selection logic — matching tool to task

| Task | Tools |
|---|---|
| **Splitting/cracking bones** | Cleaver or bone saw, butcher's board |
| **Blanching veal bones** | Stockpot, cold water, china cap for draining |
| **Roasting bones for brown stock** | Roasting pan, oven (~ 200–230 °C), tongs |
| **Sweating fumet vegetables and bones** | Stockpot, butter, low heat |
| **Long simmer** | Stockpot (preferably spigot), heat source, skimmer |
| **Skimming** | Wide skimmer, ladle, parchment cap (cartouche) for retaining heat without lid |
| **Straining** | China cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth-lined chinois |
| **Cooling** | Ice bath, ice wand, shallow hotel pans, blast chiller |
| **Storage** | Stainless containers, labels, dating |

### D-13.02.02P — Calculate Quantity of Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** calculate quantity of ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** quantity of ingredients for yield are calculated according to recipe specifications.

#### Yield math — the cook's daily arithmetic

##### Step 1 — Define the target yield
- **Working yield**: the amount of finished stock needed for service (e.g., 12 L for tomorrow's sauces and soups).
- **Buffer**: 10–20% extra for unexpected service demand and reduction losses.

##### Step 2 — Account for evaporation and reduction loss
- **Long-simmered stocks lose ~ 20–30% of starting liquid** to evaporation over 6–12 hours.
- **Fumet** loses ~ 10–15% over 30–45 min.
- **Brown stocks** typically lose more than white because of higher temperatures during initial roasting and longer simmer.

##### Step 3 — Reverse-calculate starting volume

> **Rule of thumb:** to finish with **L₁ litres** of stock, start with **L₀ litres** where **L₀ ≈ L₁ ÷ (1 − evaporation rate)**.

Example: target 12 L of brown veal stock with ~ 25% evaporation expected:
- L₀ = 12 ÷ (1 − 0.25) = 12 ÷ 0.75 = **16 L starting water**.

##### Step 4 — Calculate bones and mirepoix from water volume

Using a 1 : 1.5–2 bones-to-water ratio and 8–10% mirepoix:

For **16 L starting water** → **8–10 kg bones** → **800 g – 1 kg mirepoix**.

| Ingredient | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Water | Target × (1 + buffer) ÷ (1 − evap) | 16 L |
| Bones | Water (kg) ÷ 1.5 to 2 | 8–10 kg |
| Mirepoix total | Bones × 0.08 to 0.10 | 0.8–1.0 kg |
| → Onion (50%) | Mirepoix × 0.50 | 400–500 g |
| → Carrot (25%) | Mirepoix × 0.25 | 200–250 g |
| → Celery (25%) | Mirepoix × 0.25 | 200–250 g |
| Tomato paste (brown stock) | ~ 1–2% of bones | 80–200 g |
| Sachet d'épices | Standard small bundle | 1 unit |

##### Scaling a recipe
Common approach in commercial kitchens:

1. Establish the **base recipe** with measured weight outputs.
2. **Multiplier** = desired yield ÷ recipe yield.
3. Apply multiplier to every ingredient line.
4. Round to **practical** quantities (e.g., not "0.327 kg" — round to 325 g or 330 g).

##### Unit conversions (apprentice toolkit)
- **1 L of water ≈ 1 kg** (at room temperature).
- **1 kg = 1000 g**.
- **1 fl oz (US) ≈ 30 mL**; **1 oz (US, dry weight) ≈ 28.35 g**.
- **1 lb = 453.6 g ≈ 16 oz**.
- **1 US gallon ≈ 3.79 L**; **1 imperial gallon ≈ 4.55 L**.
- **°C = (°F − 32) × 5/9**; **°F = °C × 9/5 + 32**.

##### Bakers' percentages (for those moving between brigades)
A common system in baking that some chefs apply to stocks:
- Base ingredient (often water or flour) = 100%.
- All other ingredients expressed as % of the base.
- Useful for scaling and consistency.

For stock: water = 100%, bones = ~50–67%, mirepoix = ~5–7% of water weight, etc.

### D-13.02.03P — Gather Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** gather ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** individual ingredients are gathered and accessible in a sequential manner according to recipe specifications.

#### Mise en place — the brigade principle

**Mise en place** (Fr. "to put in place") is the foundation of every working kitchen. For stocks specifically:

1. **Read the recipe end-to-end** before gathering anything.
2. **Lay out all ingredients in the order they will be used** — bones first (for blanching/roasting), then mirepoix (for adding to the pot or roasting alongside), then aromatics (sachet built and ready), then liquids.
3. **Pre-portion** by weight into clearly labelled containers if working with multiple items.
4. **Set out tools** in the order they'll be used: scale → cutting board/knife → pot → strainer/cheesecloth.
5. **Verify nothing is missing** — once the pot is on the fire, you can't pause.

#### Sequential gathering — example for a brown veal stock

| Order | Item | Why this order |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Bones | First to roast / blanch — kick off the timeline |
| 2 | Roasting pan + oven preheat to 220 °C | Pan and oven must be hot when bones arrive |
| 3 | Cold water, stockpot, burner | While bones roast, set up the simmer station |
| 4 | Mirepoix (cut and weighed) | Roasted in the same oven or separately, timed to finish with bones |
| 5 | Tomato paste | Painted on bones late in roast |
| 6 | Deglazing liquid (water or wine) | For lifting *sucs* from the roasting pan |
| 7 | Sachet d'épices (pre-built) | Added with cold water in stockpot |
| 8 | Strainer setup (china cap → chinois → cheesecloth) | Ready for hours later |
| 9 | Cooling vessels (shallow pans, ice bath, blast chiller) | Ready before the simmer is done |

#### Why this matters
- **No mid-process scrambling** → less risk of food-safety errors (forgetting to sanitize, leaving items in danger zone, etc.).
- **Better quality** — every step happens at its optimal moment.
- **Faster overall** — sequential parallelism beats serial chaos.
- **Reduces fatigue** — one of the leading sources of kitchen injury.

> **Memory aid — "MEP" (Mise En Place):** *Mental prep, Equipment ready, Pre-measured.*

### D-13.02.04P — Measure Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** measure ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** ingredients are measured according to recipe specifications.

#### Weight vs volume — which to use

| Ingredient | Best measure |
|---|---|
| **Bones** | Weight (kg, lb) — always |
| **Vegetables (mirepoix)** | Weight (g, kg) — preferred |
| **Water** | Volume (L) is standard; weight is fine (1 L water ≈ 1 kg) |
| **Tomato paste** | Weight (g) |
| **Dry spices in sachet** | Weight (g) for precision; tsp/tbsp acceptable for small amounts |
| **Salt** | Stocks are unseasoned; reserve salting for finished dishes |
| **Wine for deglazing** | Volume (mL) |

#### Why weight is the professional default

- **Consistency batch-to-batch.** A "cup of carrot" varies dramatically by cut size, density, and packing. 250 g is 250 g.
- **Scalability.** Doubling a weight is a clean multiplication; doubling volumes can introduce packing errors.
- **Translatability.** Metric weight measures translate across kitchens, countries, and languages.
- **Documentation.** HACCP plans and recipe systems expect weight measures.

#### Tools for accurate measurement

- **Digital scale** with tare function and at least 1 g precision for benchtop work.
- **Large platform scale** (15–30 kg) for bones and bulk vegetables.
- **Graduated measuring vessels** for volume (L marks visible).
- **Thermometer** for water temperature when relevant.

#### Common measurement errors

- **Eye-balling** instead of weighing.
- **Loosely packed** vs **tightly packed** volume measures.
- **Forgetting to tare** the container.
- **Mixing units** (cups of bones + grams of mirepoix) — convert to consistent units.
- **Substituting volume for weight** with dense vs light ingredients.

### D-13.02.05P — Prepare Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** prepare ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** ingredients are prepared according to recipe specifications.

This is the heart of the subtask: **the prep work that happens before the simmer.**

#### Bone preparation

##### Cutting / cracking / splitting
- **Smaller surface area = faster extraction.** Whole 30-cm marrow bones release flavour slowly; cut into 5–8 cm sections, they release dramatically faster.
- **Cracked knuckles** expose cartilage, accelerating gelatin extraction.
- **Most operations receive bones pre-cut** from the butcher; if not, use a bone saw or cleaver with care. **[Operational]**

##### Blanching (for white veal/beef stock) — the classical step
1. Place **cold bones in a pot of cold water**, fully covered.
2. Bring to a **gentle boil**.
3. Boil for **5–10 minutes** — scum (coagulated blood proteins and impurities) rises to the surface.
4. Drain bones in a colander.
5. **Rinse bones thoroughly** under cold running water — remove every trace of scum.
6. Bones are now ready for the actual stock cook in fresh cold water.

##### Why blanch?
- **Cleaner final stock** — most of the impurities that would scum the stock for hours are removed upfront.
- **Cleaner colour** — pale, neutral stock.
- **Less skimming required** during the actual simmer.
- **Not used for brown stocks** — those are roasted instead, which similarly drives out blood and surface protein while creating Maillard compounds. **[Classical]**

##### Roasting (for brown stock)
1. **Preheat oven to ~ 200–230 °C / 400–450 °F.**
2. **Single-layer the bones** in a heavy roasting pan — overlap = steam, not roast.
3. **Roast 30–60 minutes**, turning once or twice, until **deep mahogany brown** (not blackened — burnt bones make bitter stock).
4. **Add mirepoix** to the same pan (or a second pan) in the last 20–30 minutes to caramelize.
5. **Brush tomato paste onto bones** in the last 15–20 minutes — caramelizes without burning, develops *pinçage* (the toasted-tomato note).
6. **Transfer to stockpot.**
7. **Deglaze the roasting pan** with water or wine — scrape every bit of *sucs* into the pot. **The fond on the pan is the entire reason brown stock is brown.**
8. Cover with cold water and proceed.

##### Sweating (for fumet)
1. **Melt butter** in the stockpot over low heat.
2. **Add white mirepoix** (onion, celery, leek, parsnip) and **sweat 5–10 minutes** — soft but no colour.
3. **Add fish bones** and continue to sweat until bones turn opaque, ~ 5 more minutes.
4. **Add white wine** (acidity helps protein extraction; aromatic complexity).
5. **Add cold water** to cover.
6. Add sachet; simmer gently 30–45 minutes.

#### Vegetable preparation

##### Washing
- Wash all vegetables under cold running potable water to remove soil and pesticide residue.
- For leeks: split lengthwise and rinse between layers — they trap soil.
- For mushrooms: brush off debris; rinse quickly if needed (they absorb water rapidly).

##### Cutting size — match to cook time

| Cook duration | Cut size | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **8–12 h** (veal, beef stock) | **Large rough cut (2.5–5 cm pieces)** | Smaller pieces disintegrate, muddy the stock, are hard to strain out |
| **3–4 h** (chicken stock) | **Medium rough cut (~ 2 cm)** | Balance of extraction and structural integrity |
| **30–45 min** (fumet) | **Small dice or fine slice** | Fast extraction in short cook |
| **45–60 min** (vegetable stock) | **Medium dice** | Fast extraction |

##### Trim management (carry-over from D-13.01)
- **Use clean trim** — onion ends, carrot peels (washed), celery tops, leek greens, mushroom stems.
- **Avoid** — brassicas, beet, potato peels, anything spoiling.

#### Aromatic preparation

##### Building a bouquet garni
1. Gather **parsley stems (5–10), thyme sprigs (3–5), bay leaf (1)**.
2. **Stack inside two leek-leaf halves** (split lengthwise) for the classic French method.
3. **Tie tightly with butcher's twine.**
4. **Leave a long tail of twine** — tie to the pot handle for easy retrieval at the end.

##### Building a sachet d'épices
1. Cut a square of **food-grade cheesecloth** (~ 15 × 15 cm).
2. Add:
   - 5–10 **parsley stems**
   - 3–5 **thyme sprigs**
   - 1 **bay leaf**
   - 1 tsp **black peppercorns** (whole)
   - Optional: 1 **whole clove**, 1 **crushed garlic clove**
3. **Gather the corners** and tie with butcher's twine into a pouch.
4. Long tail to pot handle.

##### Oignon piqué / brûlé
- **Oignon piqué:** peel a small onion; pin a **bay leaf** to it with a **whole clove**; add to white stocks or béchamel.
- **Oignon brûlé:** halve a peeled onion; cook **cut-side down on a dry hot pan** until **deeply blackened**. Adds amber colour and depth to brown stocks and consommé. **[Classical]**

#### Water preparation
- **Cold, potable water.**
- **Filter if necessary** for heavily chlorinated or hard-water municipalities.
- **Measure by volume or weight** before adding to the pot.
- **Cover bones by ~ 5 cm / 2 in** in the pot — too much water dilutes; too little exposes bones and scorches.

#### Protein preparation (for broths)
- **Trim excess fat** but leave some for flavour.
- **Sear or brown** for some bone broths and pho-style broths to add depth.
- **For meaty broths (chicken bouillon):** older boiling fowl or stewing hens have more connective tissue and richer flavour than young birds.

## 2. Knowledge Objectives — Deeper Dive

### D-13.02.01L — Stocks, Broths, and Their Applications (Processing Lens)

> Definitions, types, ratios, and dietary requirements are covered in detail in [D-13.01-research.md](D-13.01-research.md). The processing-specific extensions are below.

#### Factors to consider when processing ingredients (Range of Variables)

| Factor | Processing-stage implication |
|---|---|
| **Freshness** | Sensory check at retrieval; process within 24–48 hours of receipt for bones |
| **Cost** | Manage trim, minimize over-processing waste, scale batch size to avoid surplus |
| **Fat content** | Trim excess fat caps before roasting; skim aggressively in the early simmer; degrease finished stock when chilled |
| **Intended application** | Different processing methods yield different stocks — blanch for white, roast for brown, sweat for fumet |
| **Type of bone** | Knuckles need cracking for gelatin release; marrow bones can be roasted whole; fish frames need head/tail trimming |
| **Cartilage content** | Higher cartilage = more cracking surface area pays off |
| **Managing trim** | Pre-collect daily trim in a freezer bag; ready for next stock production |
| **Availability of ingredients** | Substitute white mirepoix components when leek or parsnip unavailable; substitute fish species when supplier rotates |

#### Processing-stage dietary requirements

| Requirement | Processing-stage care |
|---|---|
| **Halal / kosher** | Dedicated processing area (no cross-contact with pork or non-certified meat); dedicated cutting boards and knives; certified twine and cheesecloth |
| **Vegan** | Dedicated tools that have never touched animal products; mushroom and kombu processing in clean station |
| **Fish allergy** | Fumet processing completely isolated from non-fish station; tools fully sanitized; cross-contact precautions |
| **Gluten** | Avoid wheat-flour dredging of bones for browning; check tomato paste / commercial pastes for wheat additives |
| **Sulphites** | Choose non-sulphited wine for deglazing, or substitute non-alcoholic |

### D-13.02.02L — Procedures Used to Process Ingredients

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to process ingredients for stocks and broths.

#### Process flow — white stock (chicken or white veal)

1. **Inspect bones** — temperature, sensory, dating.
2. **Cut/crack bones** if needed.
3. **Blanch** (veal/beef white stock): cold water start, bring to boil, drain, rinse.
4. **Set up clean stockpot** with cold potable water.
5. **Add bones** to stockpot.
6. **Cut mirepoix** in rough 2–5 cm pieces (white mirepoix for veal; standard for chicken).
7. **Build sachet d'épices** with peppercorns, bay, thyme, parsley stems.
8. **Add mirepoix and sachet** to pot.
9. (Sticking with processing — simmer phase is the next subtask.)

#### Process flow — brown stock

1. **Inspect bones.**
2. **Cut/crack** if needed.
3. **Preheat oven.**
4. **Roast bones** in single layer until mahogany.
5. **Roast mirepoix** alongside or separately.
6. **Brush tomato paste** on bones in last 15–20 min.
7. **Transfer to stockpot.**
8. **Deglaze roasting pan** — scrape all *sucs* into stockpot.
9. **Add cold water, sachet.**

#### Process flow — fumet

1. **Inspect fish frames** — sensory critical (no ammonia, clear gills).
2. **Trim** dark blood lines, gills (gills are bitter), guts if present.
3. **Sweat white mirepoix** in butter.
4. **Add bones, sweat** until opaque.
5. **Add white wine, reduce briefly.**
6. **Add cold water and sachet.**

#### Process flow — court-bouillon

1. **No bones.**
2. **Slice mirepoix** thinly for fast extraction.
3. **Combine** with water + acid (wine, vinegar, or lemon juice).
4. **Add bay, peppercorns, salt.**
5. **Bring to simmer 30–45 min.**
6. Strain; ready to use as a poaching liquid.

#### Process flow — dashi (ichiban)

1. **Inspect kombu** — should be flexible, deep dark green, no powdery mould (the white "kombu dust" is umami crystals, not mould — leave it on).
2. **Wipe kombu** lightly with a damp cloth (don't wash — removes flavour).
3. **Cold water + kombu** in a pot; steep 30 minutes off heat.
4. **Bring slowly to just below the boil** (~ 60–65 °C) over 10–15 minutes; **remove kombu before boiling** (boiling kombu releases bitter compounds).
5. **Bring water to a brief boil**, **turn off heat**.
6. **Add katsuobushi** (bonito flakes).
7. **Steep 3–5 minutes off heat** — flakes settle.
8. **Strain** through cheesecloth-lined chinois without pressing (pressing extracts bitter compounds).

#### Process flow — emulsified / bone broth

1. **Inspect bones.**
2. **Roast and/or blanch** depending on style (tonkotsu emulsified pork: blanch heavily; bone broth: roast for depth).
3. **Process to small pieces** for max surface area.
4. **High-volume water; long simmer.**
5. For **emulsified style:** rolling boil instead of simmer — the agitation emulsifies fat and collagen into the liquid for the opaque-white tonkotsu result.

#### Sanitation during processing (carry-over)

- **Wash hands** between protein and produce.
- **Change gloves** between raw protein tasks and tasks involving the finished product.
- **Sanitize boards and knives** between proteins.
- **Use colour-coded boards** (red for raw red meat, yellow for poultry, blue for seafood, green for produce).
- **Wash all produce** thoroughly.
- **Sanitize the stockpot** before use — even "clean" pots from the dish room benefit from a sanitizer rinse for stock work.
- **Avoid cross-contact** with allergens for allergen-specific orders.

## 3. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Scaling up for a banquet: 25 L of brown chicken stock for tomorrow's wedding
1. **Yield target:** 25 L finished, with ~ 20% buffer = ~ 30 L target.
2. **Evaporation buffer (~ 20% for chicken):** start with 30 ÷ 0.80 = **~ 38 L water**.
3. **Bones:** 38 L water at 1 : 2 ratio = **~ 19 kg chicken bones** (backs, necks, wings — high-collagen).
4. **Mirepoix (~ 10%):** 1.9 kg total — 950 g onion, 475 g carrot, 475 g celery.
5. **Tomato paste:** ~ 1.5% of bones = ~ 285 g.
6. **Sachet x 2** for the pot volume.
7. **Pot size:** ~ 60 L for headroom.
8. **Roast bones** in batches (multiple roasting pans), single layer.
9. **Mise en place** all pre-portioned, weighed, labelled.
10. **Sequential workflow:** roast bones → roast mirepoix → tomato paste → assemble in stockpot → deglaze roasting pans → simmer → cool per 2/4/6 → store.

### Scenario 2 — Single-pot mise en place for a fumet
1. **Tools out:** stockpot, cheesecloth + twine for sachet, sharp boning knife, blue cutting board, scale, ladle, china cap + chinois + cheesecloth, shallow pans for cooling.
2. **Ingredients pre-measured:** 2 kg fish frames trimmed; 200 g white mirepoix sliced thin; 50 g butter; 250 mL white wine; 3 L cold water; sachet built.
3. **Sequence:**
   - Sweat butter + mirepoix 5 min.
   - Add bones, sweat 5 min.
   - Add wine, reduce 2 min.
   - Add cold water + sachet.
   - Simmer 30–45 min.
   - Strain through stacked chinois.
   - Cool rapidly (ice bath).

### Scenario 3 — Apprentice forgets to blanch the veal bones
1. **Observation during simmer:** continuous heavy scum, cloudy stock, strong meaty odour.
2. **Mitigation:** skim aggressively in the first 30–60 minutes; can recover acceptable stock but quality is compromised.
3. **Lesson:** blanching is not optional for white veal stock if classical quality is the target.
4. **Document for next time:** add blanching step to the SOP card; reinforce mise en place training.

### Scenario 4 — Yield miscalculation: pot is too full
1. **Observation:** liquid sloshes during simmer; risk of boil-over.
2. **Action:**
   - Reduce heat to a barely-trembling simmer.
   - Skim off some water (and surface fat) if necessary.
   - Acknowledge the dilution; plan to reduce more aggressively at the end.
3. **Lesson:** stockpot capacity should be ⅔ to ¾ full at the start.

### Scenario 5 — Sanitation lapse during processing
1. **Observation:** chef notices the apprentice cut mirepoix on the same yellow board used for chicken bones, without sanitizing.
2. **Action:**
   - Discard the mirepoix (cross-contamination risk).
   - Sanitize the board and knife.
   - Recut fresh mirepoix on the green produce board.
3. **Lesson:** colour-coded boards exist for a reason; never short-cut sanitation. (See [A-2.04-research.md](A-2.04-research.md).)

## 4. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Eyeballing weights** — leads to inconsistent stocks shift to shift.
- **Overpacking the roasting pan** — bones steam, no Maillard, pale stock.
- **Burning the bones** — bitter, acrid stock that cannot be saved.
- **Discarding the deglazed roasting pan** — wastes the *sucs* that make brown stock brown.
- **Cutting mirepoix too small for long simmers** — disintegration muddies the stock.
- **Cutting mirepoix too large for short simmers** — under-extraction in fumet / court-bouillon.
- **Skipping the blanching step** for white veal stock — cloudy stock all day.
- **Using dirty cheesecloth** — particulates and dust in the stock.
- **Using coloured / synthetic twine** — dye or melted fibres contaminate.
- **Tap water at high chlorine** — chemical off-flavours.
- **Stockpot too small for the batch** — boil-overs, evaporation imbalance.
- **Stockpot too large for the batch** — too much air space, faster evaporation, harder to maintain temperature.
- **Hot tap water** — can leach lead from older pipes; lower oxygen content; flat-tasting.
- **Forgetting the sachet** — sachet must go in at the start of the simmer to extract flavours over the full cook.
- **Adding salt** — stocks are unseasoned; salt during reduction concentrates and ruins downstream applications.
- **Not labelling stocks in storage** — easy to confuse chicken with veal.
- **Processing all ingredients in one bowl** — cross-contamination between raw bones and produce.
- **Not having cooling vessels staged before the simmer ends** — cooling failure when the stock comes off the fire.

## 6. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with D-13.02.

1. **Tools (Range of Variables):** strainers (china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth), spigot pots, roasting pans, skimmers, stockpots, cutting boards, knives, scales, cheesecloth, butcher's twine.
2. **Stockpot rule:** **tall and narrow**, fill to ⅔–¾ full, heavy-gauge stainless preferred.
3. **Spigot pot** allows clean draining from the base — improves clarity and reduces back strain.
4. **Roasting pan rule:** **single-layer bones** for true browning — overlap means steam, no Maillard.
5. **Strainer hierarchy:** china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth-lined chinois for progressively cleaner stock.
6. **Scales — weigh, don't guess.** Weight is the professional default; volume only for liquids.
7. **Yield math:** L₀ = L₁ ÷ (1 − evaporation rate); 20–30% evaporation typical for long stocks.
8. **Standard ratios (carry-over):** bones-to-water 1 : 1.5–2; mirepoix 8–10% of bone weight; mirepoix 50/25/25 onion/carrot/celery.
9. **Mise en place (MEP)** — everything in place, in sequence, before the pot goes on.
10. **Bones — bone preparation differs by stock:**
    - **White veal/beef:** blanch (cold start, boil 5–10 min, drain, rinse).
    - **Brown:** roast at ~ 200–230 °C until mahogany.
    - **Fumet:** sweat with white mirepoix in butter; never roast.
11. **Why blanch?** Removes blood and impurities for cleaner pale stock.
12. **Why roast?** Maillard reaction develops brown colour and umami depth.
13. **Why sweat?** Extracts flavour without colour for delicate fumet.
14. **Cut size matches cook time** — large for long simmers, small for short.
15. **Vegetables roasted alongside bones** for brown stocks; sweated in butter for fumet; added cold for white stocks.
16. **Tomato paste** added late in roast to develop *pinçage* without burning.
17. **Deglaze every *suc*** — the entire reason brown stock is brown.
18. **Bouquet garni**: fresh herbs in leek-leaf bundle, tied. **Sachet d'épices**: parsley/thyme/bay/peppercorns/clove in cheesecloth.
19. **Oignon piqué** (clove + bay pinned to onion) and **oignon brûlé** (charred onion cut-side) — classical aromatics.
20. **Cold potable water start** — always.
21. **Cover bones by ~ 5 cm / 2 in.**
22. **Food-grade cheesecloth + undyed cotton butcher's twine** only.
23. **Stocks are unseasoned** — salt belongs in the final dish, not the stock.
24. **Sanitation during processing:** colour-coded boards, hand-wash and sanitize between proteins/produce, sanitize stockpot before use.
25. **Court-bouillon processing:** thin-sliced mirepoix, water + acid (wine/vinegar/lemon), aromatics, 30–45 min simmer.
26. **Dashi processing:** kombu in cold water, steep 30 min, remove kombu before boiling, brief boil, off heat add katsuobushi, steep 3–5 min, strain without pressing.
27. **Tonkotsu / emulsified broth** is the exception that uses rolling boil for fat/collagen emulsification.
28. **Receive bones at ≤ 4 °C / frozen ≤ −18 °C**; process within 1–2 days fresh.
29. **Plan cooling before simmer** — shallow pans, ice bath, blast chiller ready (carry-over from A-2.02).
30. **Cooling rule (carry-over):** **60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h** — stocks are *C. perfringens* high-risk.
