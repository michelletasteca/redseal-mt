## Why Processing Is Where Thickeners Succeed or Fail

The agent itself is rarely complicated. The **technique** is everything.

- A roux is just fat and flour — but cook it too quickly and you scorch the flour into bitter pyrazines.
- A liaison is just yolks and cream — but skip tempering and you have scrambled eggs floating in your sauce.
- A cornstarch slurry is just starch and water — but stir the slurry into a non-simmering soup and you get a starchy aftertaste.
- A reduction is just heat and patience — but boil too aggressively and you scorch the bottom of the pan.

This subtask is where the apprentice learns the **practical chemistry**: how to combine, build heat, judge consistency, and recover from mistakes — the difference between a thickener that does its job invisibly and a sauce that betrays its construction.

### E-15.02.02P — Calculate Ratio of Thickening and Binding Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** calculate ratio of thickening and binding ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** ratio of thickening and binding ingredients is calculated according to desired results.

#### Roux ratios

**Standard:** equal parts (by weight) fat and flour.

| Target sauce body | Roux weight per litre of liquid | Approximate sauce thickness |
|---|---|---|
| **Light / nappant** (just coats the back of a spoon) | ~ 30–60 g/L | Velouté, light cream soups |
| **Medium / sauce body** | ~ 80–120 g/L | Béchamel, classic velouté, sauce suprême |
| **Heavy / gravy body** | ~ 150–200 g/L | Country gravy, casseroles |
| **Binding (for soufflé base, croquettes)** | ~ 250 g/L | Heavy paste, bound mixtures |

> **[Best Practice]** Ratios vary by reference (CIA, Pauli, Larousse) and operation. Apprentices should learn the house standard and understand the relative scale.

#### Roux colour vs thickening power

| Stage | Cook time at medium-low heat | Thickening power |
|---|---|---|
| **White** | 3–5 min | 100% (reference) |
| **Blond** | 5–10 min | ~ 90% |
| **Brown** | 15–30 min | ~ 50–60% |
| **Dark / chocolate** (Cajun) | 45+ min | ~ 30–40% |

**Darker roux ≠ stronger thickener.** Caramelization breaks down starch granules, weakening their gelatinizing ability. **For gumbo, the dark roux is for flavour and colour; you compensate with more roux per litre.**

#### Starch slurry ratios

| Starch | Standard ratio | Thickening power vs flour |
|---|---|---|
| **Cornstarch** | ~ 5–10 g starch per 250 mL liquid for medium body | ~ 2 × |
| **Arrowroot** | ~ 5–10 g per 250 mL | ~ 2 × |
| **Potato starch** | ~ 5–8 g per 250 mL | ~ 2–3 × |
| **Tapioca starch** | ~ 5–10 g per 250 mL | ~ 1.5–2 × |
| **Rice flour** | ~ 10–15 g per 250 mL | ~ 1–1.5 × |

**Slurry-mixing ratio:** typically **1:1 to 1:2** starch:cold water by weight, mixed into a smooth thin paste before adding to the hot liquid.

#### Liaison ratio

**Classical:** ~ 3 egg yolks + 250 mL heavy cream (≥ 33% MF) per litre of sauce or soup.

- **For thicker, richer body:** up to 4 yolks + 300 mL cream per litre.
- **For lighter finish:** 2 yolks + 200 mL cream per litre.

**Yolks contribute body** via lecithin emulsification and coagulation; **cream contributes richness** and prevents the yolks from coagulating too tightly.

#### Beurre manié ratio

**Standard:** equal parts (by weight) cold butter and flour, kneaded into a smooth paste.

- **Pinch size for adjustment:** marble-sized (~ 10–15 g per pinch); add to a simmering sauce and whisk; cook 1–2 min between additions until the desired body is reached.

#### Vegetable purée ratios

- **No fixed ratio** — the purée IS the body.
- **Typical:** 200–400 g cooked purée per litre of liquid for medium body in a purée soup.
- **Adjustment:** add more stock to thin; add more purée or reduce to thicken.

#### Reduction targets

| Target | Volume remaining | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Half reduction** | 50% | Stronger stock; sauce base |
| **Demi-glace** | ~ 25–30% (or 50% of a 1:1 espagnole-stock mix) | Spoon-coating body |
| **Glace** | 5–10% | Firm syrup; gels firmly cold |

#### Sauce-thickness terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Nappant** | "Coats" — sauce coats the back of a spoon, with a clean line drawn by a finger |
| **Coats a spoon** | Same as nappant — the standard sauce body |
| **Sauce consistency** | Thick enough to cling without pooling; thin enough to flow when plated |
| **Glace consistency** | Spoonable, syrupy, firm cold |
| **Demi-glace consistency** | Slightly thinner than glace; classical |
| **Velouté consistency** | Light pourable cream-sauce body |
| **Soup consistency** | Thinner than sauce; meant to fill a bowl |

### E-15.02.03P — Incorporate Thickening and Binding Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** incorporate thickening and binding ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** thickening and binding ingredients are incorporated according to recipe specifications and desired results.

This is the core technical skill of the subtask — **how each agent is built and combined with the cooking liquid.**

#### Roux construction and incorporation

##### Procedure

1. **Heat fat** in a heavy-bottomed pan over medium-low heat.
2. **Add flour all at once**; whisk or stir into a smooth paste.
3. **Cook to desired colour**:
   - **White:** 3–5 min, no colour change.
   - **Blond:** 5–10 min, pale gold.
   - **Brown:** 15–30 min, mahogany.
   - **Dark / chocolate:** 45+ min, deep brown to almost black (Cajun gumbo).
4. **Stir constantly** during darker stages — burning happens in seconds and is unrecoverable.
5. **Cool slightly** if working with cold liquid; or keep hot if working with cold liquid (see incorporation rule below).

##### The hot-cold incorporation rule

> **Hot roux + cold liquid = smooth.**
> **Cold roux + hot liquid = smooth.**
> **Hot roux + hot liquid = lumps.**

Why: the **temperature differential** allows starch granules to gelatinize gradually and evenly. Same-temperature combinations gelatinize the surface of clumps before the centre is dispersed.

##### Whisking-in technique

1. **Add liquid in stages**, whisking constantly between additions.
2. The first addition produces a thick paste — keep whisking; subsequent additions thin it out.
3. **Bring to a gentle simmer** for **15–20 minutes** to fully gelatinize and cook out raw-flour notes.
4. **Strain** through fine chinois if the apprentice needs ultra-smoothness or has any lumps.

##### Common roux problems

| Problem | Cause | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| **Lumps** | Hot + hot combination; or whisking too slowly | Strain through chinois; whisk hard; if severe, blend with immersion blender |
| **Bitter / burnt** | Roux scorched | Discard and restart |
| **Pale / raw-flour taste** | Roux not cooked long enough | Continue simmering; raw notes cook out at simmer |
| **Soup or sauce too thin** | Not enough roux for liquid | Knead more roux into beurre manié pinches and add |
| **Soup or sauce too thick** | Too much roux | Add hot stock or milk to thin |

#### Starch slurry incorporation

##### Procedure

1. **Whisk starch with cold water** in a small bowl to a smooth thin paste (typically 1:1 to 1:2 starch:water).
2. **Bring the cooking liquid to a gentle simmer.**
3. **Gradually whisk the slurry into the simmering liquid** — small additions, stirring constantly.
4. **Cook 1–2 minutes** at gentle simmer until the sauce thickens and clears (cornstarch turns from cloudy to translucent).
5. **Don't aggressively boil** after thickening — high heat can break down the starch and thin the sauce back out.

##### Why cold water?

- Dispersing starch in cold water keeps granules separate and prevents premature gelatinization (which would lump).
- Adding dry starch directly to hot liquid almost always produces lumps.

##### When to use slurry vs roux

- **Slurry**: last-minute thickening; clear/glossy finish; gluten-free; quick.
- **Roux**: opaque sauces, classical mother sauces, longer-built sauces where flour flavour is desired.

#### Liaison incorporation (tempering)

##### Procedure

1. **Combine egg yolks and heavy cream** in a stainless or ceramic bowl. Whisk to blend.
2. **Bring the sauce to a gentle simmer** (~ 85 °C); remove from direct heat or move to a lower-heat zone.
3. **Temper:** ladle a small amount (1 ladle, ~ 60 mL) of hot sauce into the yolk-cream mixture, whisking constantly. **Repeat with 3–5 more ladles**, gradually bringing the yolk mixture up to ~ 60–65 °C.
4. **Pour the tempered liaison back into the sauce**, whisking continuously.
5. **Return to gentle heat** — **hold ≤ 85 °C**. **Never boil after adding liaison** — eggs scramble at ~ 70 °C without the cream's protective effect, and a hard boil curdles even cream-protected eggs.
6. **Serve immediately** or hold at ~ 70 °C if essential, but quality degrades over time.

##### The science of liaison

- **Egg yolks** contain **lecithin** (emulsifier) and **proteins** that coagulate at ~ 65–70 °C.
- **Cream's fat** dilutes the yolk's protein concentration, raising the effective coagulation temperature and producing a smooth, gloss-rich finish instead of scrambled clumps.
- **Tempering** brings the yolk mixture up gradually to prevent shocking the proteins into rapid coagulation.

##### Common liaison problems

| Problem | Cause | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| **Curdled / scrambled** | Boiled after liaison addition; not tempered | Difficult to fully recover — sometimes saved by blending with an immersion blender and straining; often unsalvageable |
| **Thin sauce** | Not enough yolks; sauce not cooked enough after liaison addition | Add another tempered yolk; gentle reheat to ~ 80 °C |
| **Floury / starchy aftertaste** | Liaison applied to under-cooked roux-based sauce | Cook the velouté longer before applying liaison |

##### Food-safety note for liaison

- Egg yolks tempered to ~ 65 °C are within the *Salmonella*-pasteurization range with sufficient holding time.
- Liaison-finished sauces should be **served immediately** or held **≥ 60 °C** for safety.
- For high-risk populations (immunocompromised, elderly, young children, pregnant guests), use **pasteurized egg yolks** in the liaison. **[Best Practice]**

#### Beurre manié incorporation

##### Procedure

1. **Knead equal parts (by weight) cold butter and flour** until smooth (use fingers, a fork, or a food processor for batch production).
2. **Hold cold** until needed — beurre manié keeps in the fridge for a few days or freezer for longer.
3. **Bring the sauce to a gentle simmer.**
4. **Pinch off marble-sized pieces** and whisk into the simmering sauce **one at a time.**
5. **Allow each piece to fully melt and incorporate** (~ 30 sec) before the next.
6. **Cook 1–2 minutes** after the last addition to cook out raw flour.

##### When to use beurre manié

- **Last-minute thickening** when a sauce is too thin.
- **Adjustment** during service when there's no time to build a roux.
- **Texture preservation** when adding bulk roux would change the sauce character.

##### Difference from roux

- **Roux** is cooked fat-and-flour; the flour is partially gelatinized in fat.
- **Beurre manié** is **uncooked**; thickening happens in the sauce.
- **Beurre manié sauces** must be cooked briefly to remove raw-flour notes.

#### Vegetable purée incorporation

##### Procedure

1. **Cook the vegetable** tender in stock, water, or its own moisture.
2. **Blend** in stand blender or with immersion blender (hot-blender safety rules apply).
3. **Strain through fine chinois** for refined finish; skip for rustic.
4. **Combine with the cooking liquid** in the desired ratio.
5. **Simmer** briefly to integrate.
6. **Adjust consistency** with stock (thinner) or additional purée (thicker).

##### Why purée works as a body builder

- **Plant cell walls** contribute pectin, hemicellulose, and starches that disperse in hot liquid.
- **Particles** (microscopic plant fibre fragments) provide colloidal body without traditional starch thickening.
- **Natural vegetable colour** integrates into the sauce.

#### Reduction technique

##### Procedure

1. **Strain the liquid** to be reduced (so solids don't burn at the pan edges).
2. **Transfer to a wide shallow pan** — surface area drives evaporation rate.
3. **Bring to a steady simmer**, not a hard boil — boiling can scorch starches/sugars and break cream emulsions.
4. **Skim** any fat or scum that surfaces.
5. **Watch the volume** — visual cue marks or periodic measurement.
6. **Stir occasionally** — especially as the reduction concentrates and the bottom is at scorching risk.
7. **At target volume**, **remove from heat** — reductions continue concentrating from residual heat.
8. **Don't season until late** — salt concentrates aggressively.

##### Reduction physics

- **Atmospheric boiling: 100 °C.**
- **As water evaporates**, dissolved solids (gelatin, sugars, salt, flavour compounds) concentrate.
- **Gelatin** provides the structural body — a properly extracted stock with high gelatin produces silky reductions naturally.
- **Sugars caramelize** at the pan edge first — risk of scorched flavour transfer if not stirred.

##### Common reduction problems

| Problem | Cause | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| **Scorched bottom** | Heat too high; not enough stirring | Transfer immediately to a clean pan; **don't scrape the burnt fond** — leave it behind |
| **Over-reduced (too thick / paste)** | Reduced too far | Re-thin with fresh stock or water; rebalance flavour and salt |
| **Broken cream reduction** | Boiled cream | Whisk in a small amount of cold cream off heat; re-warm gently |
| **Salty** | Started with salted stock or seasoned mid-cook | Dilute with unsalted stock or water; rebalance |

### E-15.02.02L — Procedures Used to Prepare Thickening and Binding Agents

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to prepare thickening and binding agents.

#### Summary of procedures

##### Roux
1. Heat fat in heavy pan.
2. Add flour, whisk to paste.
3. Cook to colour (white / blond / brown / dark).
4. Cool slightly OR keep hot per temperature rule.
5. Whisk into liquid using the hot-cold rule.
6. Simmer 15–20 min to fully gelatinize and cook out flour.
7. Strain if needed.

##### Starch slurry
1. Mix starch + cold water to a smooth paste.
2. Bring liquid to a gentle simmer.
3. Whisk slurry in gradually.
4. Simmer 1–2 min until thickened and clear.
5. Don't boil aggressively after.

##### Liaison
1. Whisk yolks and cream in a non-reactive bowl.
2. Bring sauce to ~ 85 °C; remove from direct high heat.
3. Temper with hot sauce ladle by ladle.
4. Pour tempered mixture back into sauce.
5. Hold ≤ 85 °C — never boil.
6. Serve immediately.

##### Beurre manié
1. Knead equal-weight cold butter and flour.
2. Hold cold until use.
3. Whisk pinches into simmering sauce one at a time.
4. Cook 1–2 min after last addition.

##### Vegetable purée
1. Cook vegetable tender.
2. Blend (hot-blender safety).
3. Strain for refined finish; combine with liquid.
4. Adjust consistency.

##### Reduction
1. Strain liquid.
2. Transfer to wide shallow pan.
3. Steady simmer; skim.
4. Watch volume; stir as it concentrates.
5. Pull at target; rest concentrates from residual heat.
6. Season late.

#### Sanitation during processing (carry-over from A-2.04, A-2.05)

- **Allergen-isolated tools** for gluten-free vs wheat-based production.
- **Sanitize whisks, bowls, and pots** between productions and between proteins.
- **Lockout immersion / stand blenders** for cleaning; never bare-hand the blade.
- **Air-dry food-contact surfaces** before reassembly.
- **Sanitize probe thermometers** between uses.

## 3. Food-Safety Considerations During Processing

### Egg-based liaison food safety
- **Tempering brings yolks to ~ 60–65 °C** — sufficient for *Salmonella* pasteurization if held for several seconds. **[Best Practice]**
- **Hot-hold liaison-finished sauces at ≥ 60 °C** for service.
- **For high-risk populations**, use **pasteurized egg yolks**. **[Best Practice]**
- **Cold liaison-finished sauces** (e.g., classical-style mayonnaises) carry raw-yolk *Salmonella* risk — pasteurized yolks recommended for commercial production. **[Best Practice]**

### Roux storage
- **Cooked roux can be stored cold** for several days; bring to room temperature before adding cold liquid (or use hot from a hot batch).
- **Dark Cajun roux** is often made in large batches and stored — verify cold-chain ≤ 4 °C.

### Reduction food safety
- **Reductions sit in the upper danger zone (60–100 °C)** during cooking — fine while hot, but extended hot-holding without re-evaporation can support spore germination.
- **Cool finished reductions per A-2.02** if not for immediate use.

### Cream-based sauces and *Listeria*
- **Cream and dairy** can carry *Listeria monocytogenes* — strict cold-chain discipline before use.
- **Pasteurization in commercial dairy** kills *Listeria*, but post-pasteurization contamination is possible during handling.
- **Use within shelf life and verify temperature ≤ 4 °C at retrieval.**

### Allergen cross-contact during processing
- **Whisks, bowls, and pots** carry residue between productions.
- **Wheat-flour whisks** in gluten-free production = cross-contact.
- **Dedicated allergen-isolated tools** for celiac and severe allergen orders.
- See [A-2.04-research.md](A-2.04-research.md).

## 4. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Building a blond roux for 4 L of velouté
1. **Ratio:** ~ 100 g roux per litre × 4 L = **400 g total roux** = 200 g butter + 200 g flour.
2. **Mise en place:** stainless saucepan, balloon whisk, scale, butter and AP flour weighed.
3. **Heat 200 g butter** in saucepan over medium-low until foamy.
4. **Whisk in 200 g flour**; cook 5–10 min to pale blond.
5. **Cool slightly** (or keep hot — see below).
6. **In a separate pot, bring 4 L white chicken stock to a hot temperature.**
7. **Decision:** roux is hot, stock is hot — too risky for hot-hot lumps. **Cool roux slightly OR start with cold stock.**
8. **Recommended:** whisk **hot roux into a portion of cold stock** in a separate bowl to disperse, then add to the simmering main stock; OR cool the roux briefly before adding the hot stock.
9. **Simmer 15–20 min** for full gelatinization.
10. **Strain** through fine chinois.

### Scenario 2 — Tempering a liaison into a salmon velouté
1. **Velouté simmering at ~ 85 °C; remove from direct heat.**
2. **In a stainless bowl, whisk 3 egg yolks + 250 mL heavy cream.**
3. **Ladle 1 ladle of hot velouté into the yolk-cream**, whisking — temper rise.
4. **Repeat 3–5 more times** to bring liaison up to ~ 60–65 °C.
5. **Pour the tempered liaison into the velouté**, whisking.
6. **Return to gentle heat (≤ 85 °C)** for 1–2 min.
7. **Serve immediately.**

### Scenario 3 — Cornstarch slurry adjustment in a Cantonese-style sauce
1. **Sauce simmering**; tastes great but is too thin.
2. **In a small bowl, whisk 1 tbsp cornstarch + 2 tbsp cold water** to a smooth paste.
3. **Drizzle slurry into the simmering sauce while stirring.**
4. **Cook 1–2 minutes** until thickened and translucent.
5. **Don't boil hard after** — high heat breaks the gelatinized starch.

### Scenario 4 — Last-minute beurre manié rescue
1. **Stew gravy** at the pass too thin.
2. **Pinch off 3 marble-sized beurre manié pieces** (pre-made in fridge).
3. **Whisk into the simmering gravy one at a time.**
4. **Cook 1–2 minutes** after the last piece.
5. **Verify thickness** and serve.

### Scenario 5 — Vegan cauliflower purée for a sauce
1. **Cauliflower (500 g) boiled or steamed tender** in vegetable stock.
2. **Drain;** transfer to stand blender with a portion of cooking liquid.
3. **Blend smooth** (hot-blender safety: ≤ ⅔ full, vent, towel, pulse).
4. **Strain through fine chinois.**
5. **Adjust** with more stock to desired consistency.
6. **Season** with salt, white pepper, lemon, finishing olive oil.

### Scenario 6 — Reducing 4 L of stock for demi-glace
1. **Strain stock** to a wide shallow rondeau.
2. **Set on medium-low burner;** steady gentle simmer.
3. **Skim** any fat surfacing.
4. **Target volume: ~ 2 L** (50% reduction for demi-glace stage).
5. **Stir occasionally** as concentration increases.
6. **After ~ 60–90 min**, target volume reached.
7. **Remove from heat;** rest 5 min.
8. **Cool per A-2.02** if not used immediately.

## 5. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Hot roux + hot liquid → lumps.** Standard rookie error.
- **Cold roux + cold liquid → poor gelatinization.** Won't thicken well.
- **Aggressive boiling after slurry addition** → starch breaks down; sauce thins again.
- **Skipping the cook-out** on a roux-thickened sauce → raw-flour taste.
- **Not tempering liaison** → scrambled eggs.
- **Boiling after liaison addition** → curdled sauce.
- **Cold (not pasteurized) cream into hot reduction** → curdles.
- **Using table cream (10–18% MF) in liaison** → curdles; needs ≥ 33% MF.
- **Reducing seasoned stock** → concentrated salt; rebalance is hard.
- **Scorched reduction** → bitter, unrecoverable; lower heat, stir.
- **Slurry added when sauce isn't simmering** → starch doesn't fully gelatinize; gritty / starchy aftertaste.
- **Modified food starch source not verified** → hidden gluten if wheat-derived.
- **Aluminum bowl for egg + acid** → discolours and reacts; use stainless or ceramic.
- **Whisk left in hot sauce** → can fall in and contaminate; use heat-resistant tools.
- **Wet roux storage** → moisture invites microbial growth; cool and refrigerate properly.
- **Sanitation lapse on flour bin or whisks** → cross-contact in gluten-free production.
- **Salt added at the start of a long reduction** → ten-fold concentration as water evaporates.

## 7. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with E-15.02.

1. **Tools (Range of Variables):** bowls, whisks, ladles, pots, strainer, blender — plus probe thermometer, scale, heat-resistant spatula, wooden spoon.
2. **Use non-reactive bowls** (stainless, ceramic, glass) for egg- and acid-containing work.
3. **Heavy-bottomed pots** for roux and reductions — even heat, prevents scorching.
4. **Roux standard ratio:** 50:50 fat:flour by weight; ~ 80–120 g/L for medium-bodied sauce.
5. **Roux colour vs thickening:** white (100%) → blond (90%) → brown (50–60%) → dark (30–40%). Darker = more flavour, less thickening.
6. **Hot-cold rule:** **hot roux + cold liquid OR cold roux + hot liquid → smooth; hot + hot → lumps.**
7. **Cook-out time:** roux-based sauces simmer 15–20 min after liquid is incorporated to fully gelatinize and remove raw-flour notes.
8. **Starch slurry:** mix with cold water 1:1 to 1:2; whisk into simmering liquid; cook 1–2 min; don't boil hard after.
9. **Cornstarch vs flour:** ~ 2 × thickening power; glossy translucent finish.
10. **Liaison ratio:** ~ 3 yolks + 250 mL heavy cream (≥ 33% MF) per litre of sauce.
11. **Liaison procedure:** temper hot sauce into yolk-cream mixture gradually; pour back; hold ≤ 85 °C; never boil.
12. **Liaison food safety:** tempering reaches *Salmonella*-pasteurization range; pasteurized yolks for high-risk populations.
13. **Beurre manié:** equal parts cold butter + flour kneaded; marble-sized pinches into simmering sauce; cook 1–2 min.
14. **Vegetable purée:** cook tender → blend (hot-blender safety) → strain → integrate. 200–400 g cooked purée per L for medium body.
15. **Reduction:** wide shallow pan; steady gentle simmer; skim; stir as concentration rises; pull at target volume; season late.
16. **Reduction stages:** half (50%) → demi-glace (~25–30%) → glace (5–10%).
17. **Sauce body terminology:** nappant / coats a spoon / sauce consistency.
18. **Hot-liquid blender safety:** ≤ ⅔ full, vent lid, towel, pulse.
19. **Salt management:** never season at the start of a long reduction — concentrates aggressively.
20. **Acidic reductions** reduce alone first to drive off raw alcohol / sharp acid before combining with cream or stock.
21. **Reductions of cream:** never boil; use ≥ 33% MF heavy cream for stability.
22. **Slurry vs roux:** slurry for clear-glossy / last-minute / gluten-free; roux for classical opaque mother sauces.
23. **Strain through fine chinois** for refined sauce work; cheesecloth-lined for ultra-fine.
24. **Health Canada priority allergens** affecting thickener processing: **wheat** (roux, beurre manié, some modified starches), **dairy** (butter, cream), **egg** (liaison), **sulphites** (wine reductions).
25. **Allergen-isolated tools** for gluten-free and other allergen-restricted production.
26. **Sanitize whisks, bowls, pots, probes** between productions and proteins.
27. **Probe thermometer sanitized** between uses; verify liaison temperature ≤ 85 °C.
28. **Cool finished thickener-containing sauces per A-2.02:** 60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.
29. **Cooked roux can be stored cold** for several days for batch operations; verify cold-chain.
30. **Plan timing:** slurry and beurre manié for on-line service speed; roux and reduction for pre-service preparation.
