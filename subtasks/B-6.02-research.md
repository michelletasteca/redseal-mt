# B-6.02 | Receives Products — Enhanced Learning Resource

**Major Work Activity:** B — Common Occupational Skills
**Task:** B-6 | Manages Products

> **What this subtask is about:** Receiving is the most critical boundary in the food supply chain. Everything upstream — farming, processing, transport, distribution — is outside the kitchen's control. The moment product crosses the receiving dock, that control transfers to the operation. A receiving process that fails to verify temperatures, inspect quality, confirm specifications, and immediately prioritize storage allows unsafe or substandard product to enter the kitchen invisibly, where it contaminates other food, fails in service, or causes foodborne illness. This document enriches the Red Seal competency with the food safety science, product inspection knowledge, regulatory framework, and operational precision an apprentice needs to receive products as a professional.

---

## The Receiving Dock as a Critical Control Point

Under HACCP, **receiving is a Critical Control Point (CCP)** in most commercial food service food safety plans. This is because:

1. It is the last moment at which a food safety decision can prevent contaminated or substandard product from entering the kitchen
2. Once product is accepted and stored, it blends with other inventory — tracing and removing it becomes difficult
3. The inspection at receiving is the only time the kitchen can verify the cold chain was maintained throughout transport

**The Cold Chain and Why Breaks Are Irreversible**

The **cold chain** is the continuous, unbroken maintenance of perishable product at safe temperatures from production through distribution, delivery, and storage until use. A cold chain break — any period when a temperature-sensitive product warms above its safe threshold — starts the clock on bacterial growth.

Critical fact: **even if a product that experienced a cold chain break is re-cooled, the time spent in the temperature danger zone is not erased.** The bacteria that multiplied during that warm period remain. If the product has accumulated sufficient time in the danger zone (4°C–60°C), it may be unsafe even if it looks, smells, and feels normal when received.

**What a cold chain break looks like at receiving:**

| Product | Signs of Cold Chain Break |
|---|---|
| Fresh protein (meat, poultry) | Temperature above 4°C; surface browning/graying; off odour; excessive liquid in vacuum pack |
| Frozen product | Soft or partially thawed; large ice crystals inside the package; meat juice pooled at bottom of frozen package; frost patterns suggesting condensation and re-freezing |
| Fresh fish | Temperature above 4°C; cloudy eyes; brown gills; flesh that doesn't spring back; "fishy" or ammonia odour |
| Live shellfish | Gaping shells that do not close when tapped; off odour; temperature above 4°C |
| Fresh dairy | Temperature above 4°C; off odour; evidence of separation in cream |
| Fresh produce | Wilting, sliminess, bruising, mold — signs of accelerated decomposition from temperature abuse |

**Performance Criteria:** Verify invoices

#### Deep Dive: Invoice Verification

An **invoice** is the supplier's billing document — a legal claim for payment listing what was delivered, in what quantities, at what prices. Signing an invoice without verifying it is equivalent to agreeing to pay for something without checking what you received.

**The Three-Way Match**

Best practice in commercial receiving is a **three-way match** — reconciling three documents before approving an invoice:

```
PURCHASE ORDER (what was ordered, at what price)
         +
DELIVERY INVOICE (what the supplier says was delivered)
         +
RECEIVING RECORD (what was actually counted and inspected)
         │
         ▼
All three must agree before the invoice is signed and payment approved.
```

**What to Verify on Every Invoice**

| Invoice Field | What to Check | Common Discrepancies |
|---|---|---|
| **Supplier name and account number** | Correct supplier; not a mislabelled invoice | Invoice from wrong supplier accidentally included in delivery |
| **Delivery date** | Matches today; not a future or past date | Pre-dated invoices used to manipulate payment timing |
| **Product descriptions** | Every item matches what was ordered and what was delivered | Substitutions not communicated at ordering; different market form delivered |
| **Quantities** | Each line item quantity matches both what was ordered and what was actually received (counted/weighed) | Short delivery; extra items not ordered |
| **Unit of measure** | Consistent with the order (kg, L, each, case) | Price listed per kg vs. invoice charged per piece; unit confusion |
| **Unit price** | Matches the quoted/agreed price for each item | Price increases not communicated; wrong pricing tier applied; market-price items not confirmed at ordering |
| **Extensions** | Unit price × quantity = line item total | Arithmetic errors on paper invoices |
| **Invoice total** | Sum of all line items + applicable taxes = stated total | Calculation errors |
| **Credits applied** | Any outstanding credit notes from previous returns are deducted | Credits not applied as promised |

**Resolving Invoice Discrepancies**

| Discrepancy Type | Immediate Action | Documentation |
|---|---|---|
| **Short delivery** (fewer units than invoiced) | Note shortage on invoice; driver signs acknowledgement; do not pay for undelivered items | Credit note from supplier; record on receiving log |
| **Wrong product delivered** | Refuse product; note on invoice; driver acknowledges; request correct product | Credit note; communication to kitchen about potential shortage |
| **Price discrepancy** | Do not sign invoice until resolved; contact supplier's accounts receivable | Document quoted price; note on invoice; obtain written price confirmation |
| **Damaged product** | Refuse damaged items; note on invoice with driver's acknowledgement | Separate credit note for each refused item with reason |
| **Substituted product** (supplier replaced an ordered item without prior approval) | Evaluate whether substitute meets specification; accept only if it does; refuse if it doesn't | Note substitution on receiving record; verify price; advise kitchen of any spec change |

> **Operational Recommendation:** Never sign an invoice before physically counting and inspecting the delivery. A delivery driver who pressures you to sign quickly so they can move on is creating a situation where discrepancies will be discovered after they have left and cannot be documented with the driver's acknowledgement.

### B-6.02.02P
**Performance Criteria:** Verify products received

**Evidence of Attainment:** Products received are verified according to product specifications and quantities ordered

---

#### Deep Dive: Product Inspection at Receiving

**The Receiving Inspection Sequence**

A consistent receiving sequence ensures nothing is missed, even during a busy delivery:

```
Step 1: COUNT — verify case counts against invoice line items before opening
Step 2: WEIGH — weigh items ordered by weight (proteins, produce by the kg)
Step 3: INSPECT — open cases; inspect for quality, condition, specification compliance
Step 4: TEMPERATURE — probe temperature of perishables at the delivery
Step 5: DATE CHECK — verify best-before/use-by dates; confirm minimum shelf life remaining
Step 6: DOCUMENT — record findings on receiving log; note any discrepancies
Step 7: SIGN or REFUSE — sign invoice if all satisfactory; annotate and refuse items that fail
Step 8: STORE — immediately prioritize and move product to appropriate storage
```

---

#### Inspection by Product Category

**Meat and Poultry**

*Visual inspection:*

| Product | Acceptable | Reject |
|---|---|---|
| **Fresh beef** | Bright cherry-red colour; firm texture; slight moist surface; no off odour | Brown/grey discolouration; slimy surface; sour or off odour; excessive drip loss |
| **Fresh pork** | Light pink to pinkish-grey; firm; slight sheen | Green or grey tones; sour or ammonia odour; slimy texture |
| **Fresh lamb** | Pink to light red; firm | Brown discolouration; off odour |
| **Fresh chicken** | Pinkish-white; firm; no visible contaminants | Grey or yellow tinge; strong "chicken" or ammonia odour; slimy surface |
| **Fresh turkey** | Similar to chicken | Same rejection criteria |
| **Vacuum-packed meat** | Vacuum seal intact; no gas puffing; dark purplish-red colour normal (metmyoglobin — brightens on exposure to air) | Seal broken; package gas-inflated (putrefaction); excessive liquid; dark brown throughout |

*Temperature:* ≤4°C — probe into the product through the packaging or in the folds of skin for poultry; do not accept if above 4°C

*Traceability:* Packaged meat from federally inspected facilities will carry a CFIA-registered establishment number on the label. Verify this is present.

*Note on vacuum-packed meat colour:* Purple-brown or dark colour in vacuum-packed beef is **normal** (due to lack of oxygen — myoglobin is in deoxy state). It is not a sign of spoilage. The colour brightens to cherry red within 15–30 minutes of opening as oxygen contacts the meat surface. This is called **blooming**.

---

**Fresh Fish and Seafood**

Fresh fish inspection is a multi-sense assessment requiring practice:

| Indicator | Fresh (Accept) | Deteriorating (Reject) |
|---|---|---|
| **Eyes** | Clear, bright, bulging slightly | Cloudy, sunken, shrunken |
| **Gills** | Bright red; moist; no slime | Brown, grey, or black; slimy |
| **Flesh** | Firm; springs back when pressed; translucent | Soft; indent remains when pressed; opaque |
| **Scales** | Adherent to skin; shiny | Falling off easily; dull |
| **Odour** | Fresh, clean, sea/ocean smell | "Fishy" (trimethylamine = bacterial decomposition); ammonia; sour; sulfurous |
| **Skin/surface** | Moist; natural sheen | Dry; excessive slime |
| **Temperature** | ≤4°C; stored on ice | Above 4°C |

**Why fish smells "fishy":**
The characteristic "fishy" odour is produced when bacteria break down trimethylamine oxide (TMAO) — a compound naturally present in fish tissues — into **trimethylamine (TMA)**. TMA is the primary compound responsible for the strong fish smell. Fresh fish has very little TMA; as bacterial decomposition progresses, TMA accumulates. This is why fresh fish smells of the ocean, not of "fish."

*Live shellfish inspection:*
- **Shells must be closed**, or close tightly when gently tapped (the muscle response indicates the animal is alive)
- **Gaping shells** that remain open and do not respond = dead shellfish; **must be discarded**
- **Odour** must be clean and oceanic; any off odour = discard
- **Temperature:** ≤4°C
- **Tag verification:** Shellfish must arrive with a **CSSP tag** attached to the container. The tag identifies the harvest area, harvester, species, quantity, and date. The harvest area must be classified as **Approved or Conditionally Approved** under the Canadian Shellfish Sanitation Program. **Never accept shellfish without a valid tag — this is both a regulatory violation and a serious food safety risk.**

*Frozen seafood inspection:*
- Product should be **solidly frozen throughout** — no soft or pliable areas
- No evidence of **thaw-refreeze cycle**: large irregular ice crystals inside vacuum pack; meat juice pooled at bottom of bag; frost patterns on outside of packaging
- **Freezer burn**: white opaque patches on the flesh surface indicate dehydration and oxidation; affects quality but not safety; acceptable only if the specification allows
- Check **glaze percentage** notation matches purchase spec (relevant for IQF products)

---

**Fresh Produce**

Produce inspection is primarily sensory — visual, tactile, and olfactory:

| Sign | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Yellowing or browning of leafy greens** | Chlorophyll breakdown; age or ethylene exposure | Reject if severe; accept if within spec and usable immediately |
| **Wilting or limpness** | Water loss from improper temperature or humidity | Reject; indicates age or temperature abuse |
| **Mold growth** | Fungal contamination; may spread to adjacent product | Reject affected units; inspect adjacent items carefully |
| **Soft spots or bruising** | Physical damage or advanced ripening | Reject for fresh application; may be acceptable for cooked applications if spec allows |
| **Insect presence** | Pest contamination; may indicate improper storage in transit | Reject; notify supplier; document |
| **Off odour** | Fermentation, decay, or rot | Reject immediately |
| **Water damage on packaging** | Packaging failure; potential contamination and accelerated decay | Inspect product carefully; reject if product quality is compromised |

*Temperature for produce:*
Most produce is received and stored at ≤4°C. Notable exceptions that are damaged by cold:
- **Bananas:** Damaged below 13°C; receive and store at ambient (13–15°C)
- **Tomatoes:** Quality degrades below 10°C; store at 10–13°C
- **Avocados, mangoes, papayas:** Cold sensitive; store at 10–13°C while ripening
- **Potatoes:** Store at 7–10°C; cold converts starch to sugar, affecting fry colour and flavour
- **Winter squash, pumpkins:** Store at 10–13°C

---

**Dairy**

| Product | Accept | Reject |
|---|---|---|
| **Milk, cream** | ≤4°C; best-before date adequate; packaging sealed | Above 4°C; past or imminent best-before; open or damaged packaging |
| **Butter** | Firm; pale yellow; no off odour; ≤4°C | Rancid smell (short-chain fatty acid oxidation); yellow-orange discolouration at surface; above 4°C |
| **Hard cheese** | Appropriate moisture level; rind intact (for natural-rind types); no unwanted mold | Excessive surface mold beyond spec for type; ammonia odour; soft/wet packaging failure |
| **Soft/fresh cheese** | ≤4°C; packaging sealed; within use-by date | Above 4°C; bulging or gas-inflated packaging (bacterial contamination); off odour |
| **Eggs** | Grade A Canada; clean shells; no cracks; ≤7°C | Cracked shells (contamination risk); dirty shells; foul odour |

*Note on eggs and temperature:* In Canada, commercial eggs should be received and stored at ≤7°C. Once refrigerated, eggs must remain refrigerated — temperature cycling (cold → warm → cold) promotes condensation on shells that can drive bacteria through the pores.

---

**Dry Goods**

| Check | What to Look For | Action on Failure |
|---|---|---|
| **Packaging integrity** | No tears, holes, broken seals, moisture staining | Reject; request replacement |
| **Pest evidence** | Insect damage, gnawing, frass (insect excrement), live insects | **Reject entire case**; notify supplier and pest control; inspect adjacent items in delivery |
| **Can integrity** | No swelling, no denting at seams, no leaking | Reject any swollen or seam-dented can (*C. botulinum* risk) |
| **Best-before dates** | Adequate shelf life remaining at delivery | Reject items with imminent or past best-before dates; specify minimum shelf life in purchase spec |
| **Label legibility** | All ingredient, allergen, and date information readable | Reject if critical information is missing or obscured |

> **Food Safety Warning — Swollen Cans:** A swollen or bulging can indicates gas production inside, which is a sign of *Clostridium botulinum* activity. *C. botulinum* produces botulinum toxin — the most acutely toxic biological substance known. A can that is swollen, leaking, or dented along the seam must **never** be opened, used, or tasted. It must be handled carefully, clearly labelled as a hazard, and disposed of safely.

### B-6.02.03P
**Performance Criteria:** Operate receiving equipment

**Evidence of Attainment:** Receiving equipment is operated according to manufacturers' specifications

#### Deep Dive: Receiving Equipment

**Scales**

Scales are the primary tool for verifying that products ordered by weight were actually delivered at the invoiced weight. Every commercial receiving area should have a calibrated platform scale.

*Types:*
- **Platform/floor scale:** Heavy-duty; weighs full cases and large items (whole primals, case produce); typically 50–300 kg capacity
- **Portion/counter scale:** Weighs individual items or small amounts (±1–10 kg capacity); used for high-value proteins
- **Hanging/spring scale:** Suspended scale for produce bags and similar; less common in modern operations

*Calibration:* Scales must be calibrated regularly (zeroed before each use; certified calibration per manufacturer's schedule or provincial Measurement Canada requirements). An inaccurate scale produces inaccurate receiving records and inaccurate food cost calculations.

*How to use for receiving:*
1. Zero/tare the scale before each use (or with the empty container)
2. Weigh the item; compare to invoice weight
3. A tolerance of ±2–3% is typically acceptable for natural products (weight variation in natural product); more than this warrants a query to the supplier
4. Record actual received weight on the receiving log — this is the food cost figure, not the invoiced weight if it differs

**Hand Trucks and Dollies**

*Hand truck (two-wheel truck):* Used to move single stacked cases; tilted on two wheels, load against the upright bar, tipped back and rolled.

*Four-wheel dolly (flat dolly):* Used to move full pallets or multiple cases; slide under stacked product; roll to storage location.

*Safe operation (OH&S):*
- Bend at the knees, not the waist, when loading heavy items onto hand trucks
- Keep the load stable and at a manageable height (over-stacking creates tip risk and blocked visibility)
- Secure the load if moving over uneven surfaces or up ramps
- Keep pathways clear; announce presence when moving through blind corners ("Corner!")
- Never exceed the rated capacity of the equipment

**Conveyors**

Belt conveyors in larger receiving areas move product from the loading dock into the building. Common in high-volume hotel, hospital, or institutional kitchens.

*Safe operation:*
- Keep hands, loose clothing, apron strings, and hair away from moving belt and rollers
- Do not attempt to stop a moving conveyor by hand
- Do not overload (weight per linear foot should not exceed rated capacity)
- Inspect belt for damage before each use; report damaged conveyors immediately (do not use a damaged conveyor)
- Observe lockout/tagout procedures when cleaning or maintaining

**Lifts (Manual and Powered)**

*Manual pallet jack:* A hydraulic device that slides forks under a pallet, pumps to raise the load off the floor, and rolls it to position. Requires physical pumping and maneuvering skill.

*Powered pallet jack / reach truck:* Electrically powered; forks are raised/lowered by controls; moves under its own power.

> **OH&S Regulatory Note:** In most Canadian provinces, operation of powered pallet jacks, reach trucks, and forklifts requires specific training and certification by a qualified instructor. Workers must demonstrate competency before being authorized to operate powered equipment. Operating powered lift equipment without proper training is an OH&S violation.

*Safe operation of manual pallet jack:*
- Inspect forks and hydraulic pump before use
- Ensure forks are fully engaged under the pallet before raising
- Keep feet clear of pallet and load during lifting and transport
- Do not exceed rated capacity (typically 2,000–4,500 kg for manual jacks)
- Lower load before moving over ramps or uneven surfaces
- Never place any part of the body under a raised load

### B-6.02.04P
**Performance Criteria:** Determine priority of storage

**Evidence of Attainment:** Products are identified and prioritized for storage according to form, temperature and jurisdictional regulations

---

#### Deep Dive: Storage Priority and Organization

**Why Immediate Storage Prioritization Is a Food Safety Requirement**

Product that sits on the receiving dock after delivery begins warming. Every minute of delay is additional time in the temperature danger zone for perishable products. Receiving priority = temperature urgency.

**The Storage Priority Sequence**

```
PRIORITY 1 — FROZEN PRODUCTS
   → Freezer immediately (≤-18°C)
   → Most urgent: any temperature abuse during storage is
     more visible (thawing) and less reversible

PRIORITY 2 — FRESH PROTEIN: MEAT, POULTRY, SEAFOOD
   → Refrigerator immediately (≤4°C)
   → Highest microbiological risk; shortest safe window at ambient

PRIORITY 3 — DAIRY AND EGGS
   → Refrigerator (≤4°C; eggs ≤7°C)
   → Shorter window than dry goods but longer than protein

PRIORITY 4 — FRESH PRODUCE
   → Refrigerator (≤4°C) for most; ambient for cold-sensitive items
   → Generally lower microbiological risk than proteins but
     quality degrades rapidly if warm

PRIORITY 5 — DRY GOODS
   → Dry storage (cool, dry, dark, ≥15 cm off floor)
   → Shelf-stable; can wait briefly while perishables are stored
   → Still must be stored promptly to clear receiving area
```

**Storage Organization and Cross-Contamination Prevention**

The way products are stored in a refrigerator is not arbitrary — it follows a specific vertical arrangement based on the internal temperature required to cook each item safely. This prevents **drip contamination**: raw protein juices dripping onto product below.

**Correct Refrigerator Storage — Top to Bottom:**

```
SHELF 1 (TOP)    Ready-to-eat (RTE) foods
                 Cooked foods, salads, desserts, dairy,
                 sliced deli meats, open produce
                 ─────────────────────────────────────
SHELF 2          Whole fish (cook temp: ≥70°C)
                 ─────────────────────────────────────
SHELF 3          Whole cuts of beef and pork
                 (cook temp: ≥63°C for whole muscle)
                 ─────────────────────────────────────
SHELF 4          Ground meat and ground fish
                 (cook temp: ≥71°C for ground)
                 ─────────────────────────────────────
SHELF 5 (BOTTOM) Poultry — chicken, turkey, duck
                 (cook temp: ≥74°C)
                 Highest cooking temperature = must be
                 at the bottom to prevent drip onto
                 lower-cook-temperature products
```

> **Why this order matters:** If a package of raw chicken leaks and drips onto whole cuts of beef below it, the beef can be contaminated with *Salmonella* or *Campylobacter* from the chicken. When the beef is later cooked to its internal target (63°C for whole muscle), those pathogens — which require 74°C to be reliably destroyed — may survive. Storing poultry at the bottom eliminates this risk.

**Additional Storage Organization Rules**

- **FIFO (First In, First Out):** All newly received product goes behind or below existing stock of the same item; oldest stock is used first
  - Practical method: label every item with a **received date** (rotation label) at receiving; staff can then ensure oldest-dated product is used first without guessing
- **Separate raw from RTE:** Raw proteins and RTE (ready-to-eat) foods must never be stored in direct contact or in positions where drip contamination can occur
- **Allergen segregation:** Major allergens (nut products, shellfish, dairy, gluten-containing ingredients) should be stored in clearly labelled, covered containers; minimize risk of cross-contact with allergen-free preparations
- **No floor storage:** In most provincial food premises regulations, food must be stored **at least 15 cm (6 inches) off the floor** to allow cleaning and prevent pest access
- **No cardboard in food storage:** Corrugated cardboard is a known harborage for cockroaches, their eggs, and other pests; decant product into food-grade storage containers before bringing into food storage areas
- **Chemical separation:** Cleaning products, sanitizers, and chemicals must **never** be stored with food or above food preparation areas; a separate locked cabinet is the standard

**Temperature Requirements for Each Storage Zone**

| Storage Zone | Required Temperature | Products |
|---|---|---|
| **Freezer** | ≤-18°C | All frozen products |
| **Walk-in cooler / refrigerator** | ≤4°C | Fresh proteins, dairy, most produce, cooked food, RTE items |
| **Produce cooler** (separate unit where available) | 4°C–7°C | Produce benefiting from slightly warmer than standard refrigerator |
| **Cold-sensitive produce ambient area** | 10–15°C | Bananas, tomatoes, avocados, potatoes, winter squash |
| **Dry storage** | 10–21°C (cool and dry) | Dry goods, canned goods, dried spices, oils, vinegars |
| **Chemical storage** | Separate from food storage; temperature as per SDS/label | Cleaning chemicals, sanitizers |

#### Deep Dive: Receiving Terminology and Forms

**Key Receiving Terminology**

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Invoice** | Supplier's billing document listing delivered products, quantities, and prices; legal request for payment |
| **Purchase order (PO)** | Kitchen's formal order document; used to match against invoice at receiving |
| **Receiving record / receiving log** | Kitchen's internal document recording what was actually received, inspected, and the results |
| **Credit note** | Document issued by supplier acknowledging a return, refusal, or short delivery; reduces amount owed |
| **Backorder** | Item that could not be delivered because the supplier was out of stock; will be delivered on a future date |
| **Requisition** | Internal request for product from a central store or inventory area; not an external order |
| **Product tracking slip** | Internal document following a product as it moves within a facility |
| **FIFO** | First In, First Out — oldest product used before newer product |
| **Cold chain** | Continuous temperature management of perishable product from production through receipt and storage |
| **Cross-contamination** | Transfer of harmful substances (bacteria, allergens, chemicals) from one surface or food to another |
| **Drip contamination** | Specific form of cross-contamination where raw protein juices drip onto food stored below |
| **Market form** | How a product is processed and packaged (e.g., whole, fillet, boneless/skinless) |
| **Best-before date** | Date after which a product's quality may decline; not a safety date for most products |
| **Use-by date** | Date after which a product should not be consumed; a safety-based date applied to certain high-risk products |
| **Lot code** | Alphanumeric code identifying the specific production batch of a product; enables traceability in a recall |
| **Tare weight** | Weight of an empty container; subtracted from gross weight to obtain net product weight |
| **Net weight** | Weight of product only, excluding packaging |
| **Metmyoglobin / bloom** | The dark purple-brown colour of vacuum-packed beef (normal; brightens to red on oxygen exposure = blooming) |
| **TMA (trimethylamine)** | Bacterial decomposition product responsible for "fishy" odour in deteriorating fish |

**Forms and Reports in Receiving**

| Form / Report | Purpose | Who Uses It |
|---|---|---|
| **Invoice** | Billing document from supplier; verified against PO and receiving record at delivery | Receiver, accounts payable |
| **Purchase order (PO)** | Internal document pre-authorizing the purchase; used to verify invoice | Receiver, purchaser, accounts payable |
| **Receiving log / receiving report** | Records every delivery: items, quantities, temperatures, quality findings, discrepancies | Receiver; HACCP records |
| **Credit note / refusal form** | Records refused or short items; supplier issues credit against next invoice | Receiver, accounts payable, supplier |
| **Inventory form** | Updated with newly received items; used to calculate current on-hand stock | Receiver, stores manager |
| **Product tracking slip** | Tracks product movement between departments or storage areas within facility | Used in large institutional operations |
| **Requisition** | Internal request for product from central stores | Station cooks, chef de partie |
| **Shellfish tag log** | Records shellfish tag information (harvest area, date, species) for traceability | Receiver; HACCP records |

#### Deep Dive: Complete Receiving Procedures

**Receiving Log — Complete Entry**

A receiving log entry should capture:

- Date and time of delivery
- Supplier name and delivery driver
- Invoice number
- For each product received:
  - Product name and specification
  - Quantity received (actual count/weight)
  - Quantity invoiced
  - Discrepancy (if any)
  - Temperature (for perishables)
  - Quality check result (pass / fail / conditional)
  - Shellfish tag number (for live shellfish)
  - Best-before / use-by date
  - Disposition of rejected items
- Receiver's name and signature
- Supervisor review signature

**Confirming Information on Invoices**

The procedure for confirming invoice accuracy:

1. **Before the delivery arrives:** Have the purchase order available; review what was ordered so you know what to expect
2. **When the driver arrives:** Obtain the invoice before unloading begins; do not allow the driver to rush the process
3. **During unloading:** Count cases as they come off the truck; note any that arrive open, damaged, or at unexpected temperatures
4. **After unloading:** Weigh and inspect items; record actual quantities received
5. **Match against invoice:** For each line item: actual count/weight = invoiced count/weight? Product matches description and spec?
6. **Check prices:** Compare invoice prices to quoted/purchase order prices for each item
7. **Note discrepancies in writing:** On the invoice itself (both receiver and driver should sign noting discrepancies)
8. **Sign only what you agree to:** Never sign an unverified invoice; if items need to be removed from the invoice, do so in writing before signing

**Procedures for Damaged and Returned Products**

When product fails inspection or is damaged:

| Step | Action | Documentation |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Identify and isolate** | Immediately separate the problematic item from accepted product; keep it on the truck if possible | Mark clearly — "DO NOT USE"; "RETURN TO SUPPLIER" |
| **2. Do not bring into the kitchen** | Refused product must not enter food storage areas | Keep at loading dock until driver acknowledgement |
| **3. Note on invoice** | Circle or annotate the line item; write reason for refusal (temperature, damaged packaging, wrong spec, quality failure, etc.) | Both receiver and driver sign the annotated invoice |
| **4. Obtain credit confirmation** | Driver should note the return on their delivery record; request credit note number or reference | Credit note number recorded in receiving log |
| **5. Communicate to supplier** | Contact the supplier's customer service or sales rep same day to report the refusal and confirm the credit | Email or phone record |
| **6. Schedule overage returns** | If an item was delivered in excess of the order and needs to be returned, coordinate a scheduled pickup with the supplier | Return authorization number from supplier |
| **7. Reschedule delivery if needed** | If a critical item was refused and there is no substitute, contact the supplier to arrange emergency redelivery or advise kitchen immediately | Kitchen must adjust production schedule |
| **8. Communicate with kitchen** | Advise chef / sous chef of any refused items that may affect the production schedule; allow time for substitution or menu adjustment | Verbal + production board update |

#### Deep Dive: Regulatory Framework at Receiving

**HACCP at Receiving**

Receiving is a **Critical Control Point (CCP)** in most commercial kitchen HACCP plans. The HACCP documentation requirements at receiving include:

| HACCP Element | Receiving Application |
|---|---|
| **Critical limit** | Refrigerated products ≤4°C; frozen products ≤-18°C; product from approved sources; packaging intact |
| **Monitoring procedure** | Temperature check with calibrated thermometer; visual inspection of every delivery; shellfish tag verification |
| **Corrective action** | Reject product that does not meet critical limits; document refusal; advise kitchen of impact |
| **Records** | Receiving log with temperature, quality check result, discrepancies, and disposition |
| **Verification** | Supervisor review of receiving logs; periodic calibration check of receiving thermometers |

**Public Health Regulations — Key Requirements at Receiving**

Provincial food premises regulations generally require that:
- Food is received only from **approved sources** (licensed, inspected suppliers)
- Refrigerated food arrives at ≤4°C
- Frozen food arrives solidly frozen
- Food is in **wholesome condition** (not adulterated, not decomposed, not otherwise unsafe)
- Shellfish arrives with CSSP traceability tags

Violations at receiving can result in:
- Health inspection deficiency citations
- Orders to discard non-compliant product
- Fines under provincial food premises legislation
- Licence suspension for chronic non-compliance

**WHMIS at Receiving**

When a delivery includes cleaning chemicals, sanitizers, or other hazardous products:
- Verify an **SDS is available** for each hazardous product being received for the first time; obtain from driver or supplier if not already on file
- Check **WHMIS supplier labels** are intact and legible on all containers
- Store chemicals immediately in designated **chemical storage area**, separated from food products
- Never accept a hazardous product without proper labelling or an available SDS

**OH&S at Receiving**

Receiving involves physical hazards that are governed by provincial OH&S legislation:
- **Manual handling:** Proper lifting technique required (bend knees, not waist; keep load close to body); back injuries are the most common injury in receiving operations
- **Slip and fall:** Receiving docks and floors can be wet from delivery vehicles; proper non-slip footwear is required
- **Cold environment exposure:** Workers receiving into freezers or cold external environments require appropriate PPE (insulated gloves, jackets); OH&S regulations may specify exposure limits and warm-up break requirements for prolonged cold work
- **Powered equipment:** Operators of powered pallet jacks and forklifts require training and authorization
- **Sharp packaging:** Case staples, broken cases, and metal banding require cut-resistant gloves

## Why "Fishy" Smells and Purple Meat Are Not What They Seem

Two of the most common points of confusion for new kitchen workers involve normal product appearances that seem like problems:

**Purple/brown beef in vacuum packaging → Normal; accept**
Fresh beef contains oxymyoglobin (bright red) when exposed to oxygen. In a vacuum pack, oxygen is removed. Without oxygen, myoglobin converts to deoxymyoglobin — which is **purple-brown**. This is the expected colour of vacuum-packed beef and is not a sign of spoilage. When the package is opened and meat is exposed to air, it **blooms** back to bright red within 15–30 minutes. Only reject if there is an off odour, slimy texture, or excessive liquid — not on the basis of colour alone.

**"Fishy" smell → Sign of bacterial decomposition; reject**
Fresh, high-quality fish smells clean, briny, and oceanic — not "fishy." The characteristic fishy smell is **trimethylamine (TMA)**, a bacterial breakdown product of trimethylamine oxide (TMAO) naturally present in fish muscle. As bacteria on the fish's surface decompose TMAO, TMA accumulates. The stronger the "fishy" smell, the more advanced the bacterial decomposition. High-quality fish should have virtually no TMA odour.

## Common Mistakes in Receiving

| Mistake | Risk | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Signing invoice without verifying contents | Paying for product not received; accepting substandard product without recourse | Never sign until physical count, weight check, and quality inspection are complete |
| Not taking temperatures of perishables | Cold chain breaks accepted without detection; dangerous product enters the kitchen | Temperature every perishable delivery; probe internal, not just surface |
| Accepting shellfish without a valid CSSP tag | Regulatory violation; loss of traceability for recall response; food safety risk | Every container of live bivalve shellfish must have a tag; refuse without exception |
| Refusing to accept damaged product because the driver says it's fine | Product enters kitchen in violation of specs; food safety risk | Specifications are the standard; if product does not meet spec, refuse it regardless of what the driver says |
| Assuming vacuum-packed purple beef is spoiled | Rejection of perfectly good product; unnecessary food cost loss | Understand the myoglobin blooming process; evaluate by odour and texture, not colour in vacuum pack |
| Rejecting fish based on "fishy smell" threshold misunderstanding | Accepting fish that is already beginning to spoil | Fresh fish should smell like the ocean; any TMA fishy odour = deterioration beginning; reject |
| Storing products in order of delivery rather than temperature priority | Perishables at ambient temperature while dry goods are being stored | Always store frozen first → fresh protein → dairy → produce → dry goods |
| Storing raw poultry above whole cuts of beef | Drip contamination; salmonella or campylobacter from chicken can contaminate beef | Poultry always at the bottom; RTE foods always at the top |
| Leaving cardboard boxes in food storage | Pest harborage; cockroach eggs transported into the kitchen in corrugated cardboard | Decant product into food-grade storage containers before entering food storage areas |
| Not applying rotation labels at receiving | FIFO impossible without date information; old product used last; spoilage | Label every item with received date at receiving; FIFO every time |

## Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge

This section summarizes the most commonly tested concepts from this subtask for Red Seal examination preparation.

### Critical Temperature Reference

| Product | Maximum Acceptable Receiving Temperature | Reject If |
|---|---|---|
| All refrigerated products | **≤4°C** | Above 4°C |
| All frozen products | **≤-18°C** (solidly frozen) | Above -18°C or any evidence of partial thaw |
| Live shellfish | **≤4°C** | Above 4°C; gaping shells; off odour; no CSSP tag |
| Hot catered/delivered food | **≥60°C** | Below 60°C |
| Cold-sensitive produce (bananas, tomatoes) | 10–15°C | Below 10°C (chilling injury) |

### Correct Refrigerator Storage Order (Top to Bottom)

1. Ready-to-eat (RTE) foods — **top**
2. Whole fish
3. Whole cuts of beef and pork
4. Ground meat and ground fish
5. Poultry (chicken, turkey, duck) — **bottom**

### Likely Exam Questions and Concepts

1. **What is a three-way match in receiving and why is it used?**
   → Reconciliation of three documents: Purchase Order (what was ordered), Invoice (what the supplier says was delivered), and Receiving Record (what was actually received and inspected). All three must agree before an invoice is signed and payment authorized. Prevents paying for undelivered product and ensures specification compliance.

2. **Why is receiving considered a Critical Control Point (CCP) under HACCP?**
   → It is the last opportunity to prevent contaminated, temperature-abused, or substandard product from entering the kitchen. Once accepted and stored, problematic product becomes difficult to identify and remove.

3. **What are the critical limits for temperature at receiving, and what is the corrective action if a product exceeds them?**
   → Refrigerated products: ≤4°C; Frozen products: ≤-18°C. Corrective action: refuse the product; document the refusal on the invoice; notify the supplier; advise kitchen staff of any production impact.

4. **What are the signs of a cold chain break in frozen seafood?**
   → Large irregular ice crystals inside vacuum packaging; meat juice pooled at bottom of frozen bag; frost patterns (condensation and re-freezing on packaging exterior); soft or partially thawed texture. Any of these indicate the product was thawed and re-frozen.

5. **Why must raw poultry be stored at the bottom of a refrigerator?**
   → Poultry requires the highest internal cooking temperature (74°C) to safely destroy Salmonella and Campylobacter. Storing it at the bottom ensures that if juices drip or leak, they fall onto food that requires an equally high or higher cooking temperature — not onto products that are cooked to lower temperatures or eaten raw.

6. **What does "blooming" mean for vacuum-packed beef, and why is it important to understand at receiving?**
   → Blooming is the colour change of beef from purple-brown (deoxymyoglobin, the normal colour of vacuum-packed beef without oxygen) to bright cherry-red (oxymyoglobin, formed on exposure to air). A receiver who does not understand this may incorrectly reject good beef based on colour. Evaluation should be based on odour, texture, temperature, and packaging integrity — not colour.

7. **What is TMA and why does it matter when inspecting fresh fish?**
   → Trimethylamine (TMA) is a bacterial decomposition product formed when bacteria convert trimethylamine oxide (TMAO), naturally present in fish muscle, into TMA. TMA is responsible for the characteristic "fishy" odour. Fresh fish has virtually no TMA; any significant fishy smell indicates bacterial activity and deterioration has begun. Fresh fish should smell clean and oceanic.

8. **What are the minimum requirements for accepting live shellfish at receiving?**
   → Product must arrive at ≤4°C; shells must be closed or close when tapped; odour must be clean; a valid CSSP (Canadian Shellfish Sanitation Program) tag must be attached to the container identifying the harvest area, species, date, and harvester. Shellfish without a CSSP tag must be refused.

9. **What is the correct procedure when product arrives damaged or out of specification?**
   → Do not bring the product into the kitchen; keep at the loading dock; note the refusal on the invoice with the reason; have the driver sign acknowledging the return; contact the supplier same day to request a credit note; communicate the shortage to kitchen staff; adjust the production schedule if needed.

10. **Name the five pieces of receiving equipment and describe the OH&S consideration for each.**
    → Hand truck (proper lifting technique; do not overstack); dolly/four-wheel cart (keep load stable; clear pathway); scales (calibrate regularly; tare before use); conveyor (keep hands and clothing clear of moving parts); lifts/pallet jacks (manual: weight limits; powered: training and certification required).

11. **Name the five storage zones and their required temperatures.**
    → Freezer (≤-18°C); Walk-in cooler/refrigerator (≤4°C); Cold-sensitive produce ambient (10–15°C); Dry storage (10–21°C, cool and dry); Chemical storage (separate from food, as per SDS).

12. **Why must corrugated cardboard not be brought into food storage areas?**
    → Corrugated cardboard is a known harborage for cockroaches, cockroach eggs, and other pests. Deliveries in cardboard boxes are a primary vector for introducing pest infestations into a food storage area. Product should be decanted into food-grade storage containers before storage.
