## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### A-1.02.01P — Dress in Industry-Specific Clothing and Approved Footwear

**Performance Criteria:** dress in industry-specific clothing and approved footwear
**Evidence of Attainment:** industry-specific clothing and approved footwear are worn to prevent injuries and product contamination.

#### Why the chef uniform is what it is
The classic chef uniform is engineered. Every element has a safety and/or sanitation purpose — it is not merely cultural.

| Item | Purpose | Why this design |
|---|---|---|
| **Double-breasted chef jacket** | Burn protection, presentable on either side | The double front layer creates an insulating air gap that delays radiant heat from grills/salamanders. The flap can be reversed mid-shift to hide stains for a quick service transition. |
| **Long sleeves** (in many ops) | Forearm burn protection from oven racks, steam, oil splash | Some operations allow rolled sleeves for cooler stations; pastry and grill cooks often keep them down. |
| **Knotted cloth buttons** (traditional) | Tear-away in case of fire or chemical splash | Plastic buttons can melt onto skin; knotted cloth releases under stress. |
| **Neckerchief / kerchief** | Wicks sweat from neck before it reaches food | Increasingly replaced by under-collar towels but still found in classical brigades. |
| **Apron (waist or bib)** | Protects uniform, catches drips, can be removed quickly if contaminated | Should be removed before going to washrooms, taking out garbage, or leaving the kitchen. **[Best Practice / public-health-aligned]** |
| **Side towels (clean & dry)** | Heat-protective grip for hot pans and lids | **Wet towels conduct heat instantly and have caused serious burns** — keep at least two: dry for hot, damp for wiping. |
| **Chef pants (loose, often houndstooth)** | Loose fit allows hot liquids to "blow off" rather than cling; pattern hides stains visually | Avoid wide cuffs that catch on equipment. No shorts, no leggings. |
| **Hat / skull cap / hairnet / beard net** | Hair restraint — prevents physical contamination | Required by all provincial public-health food premises regulations. **[Regulatory]** |
| **Non-slip closed-toe footwear** | Slip-resistant; protects from dropped knives, hot spills, broken glass | See dedicated section below. **[Regulatory in all jurisdictions for foodservice]** |

#### Footwear — the most-tested PPE element
Canadian commercial kitchens require **closed-toe, slip-resistant footwear**. Many operations additionally require **CSA-rated safety footwear** (steel- or composite-toe) — particularly receiving, butchery, and warehouse-adjacent roles.

- **CSA Standard Z195** governs protective footwear in Canada. Symbols you'll see:
  - **Green triangle** — Grade 1 toe protection + puncture-resistant sole (heavy duty; common in butchery, receiving).
  - **Yellow triangle** — Grade 2 toe protection + puncture-resistant sole.
  - **White rectangle with Ω (omega)** — Electric shock resistance.
  - **Red rectangle with SD** — Static-dissipative.
- **Slip resistance** — look for soles tested to **ASTM F2913** or **SATRA TM144** with a coefficient of friction ≥ 0.5 on wet/oily surfaces. **[Best Practice — specific rating varies by employer]**
- **Why this matters:** Slip-trip-fall is the #1 kitchen injury category, and dropped knives / hot pans send people to ER every year.

> ⚠️ **Common mistakes:**
> - Running shoes or canvas sneakers (no slip rating, no oil resistance, fabric soaks up grease and water).
> - Crocs without the heel strap — heel strap **must** be in the back position in foodservice. Many provincial health units treat unstrapped Crocs as non-compliant.
> - Wet, worn, or grease-saturated soles — replace when the tread is smooth.

#### Personal hygiene rules attached to "dress"
- **No jewellery** beyond a plain wedding band (cross-contamination + foreign-object risk). **[Regulatory in most jurisdictions]**
- **No nail polish, no artificial nails, nails trimmed short** — chips end up in food. **[Best Practice; regulatory in many provinces]**
- **No watches or fitness trackers on prep hands.**
- **Hair fully restrained**, facial hair covered if long. **[Regulatory]**
- **No perfume or cologne** — taints aroma-sensitive product (cheese, chocolate, wine). **[Operational]**
- **Cuts and wounds covered** with a **blue waterproof food-service bandage** (blue is metal-detectable and visible if it falls off), then a glove over top. **[Best Practice]**
- **Personal clothing storage** in lockers — not in the kitchen.

> **Memory aid — "CLEAN":** *Closed-toe footwear, Long sleeves down for heat, Even hair restraint, Apron off when leaving zone, Nails short and unpolished.*

### A-1.02.02P — Locate PPE and Safety Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** locate PPE and safety equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** PPE and safety equipment are located according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### Where everything lives — apprentice's first-week walkthrough
Within the first shift, the apprentice should be able to point to each of the following without hesitation. This is both an OH&S expectation and a survival skill.

| Item | Typical location | Visual cue |
|---|---|---|
| **Fire extinguishers** | Near every exit, near cookline (ABC and K), max travel distance per fire code | Red cylinder, mounted on wall, signage above |
| **Fire blanket** | Cookline wall, near grill / fryer | Red pouch with pull tabs |
| **Hood suppression manual pull station** | Near cookline exit | Red handle, "PULL IN FIRE" |
| **Gas shut-off valve** | Near cookline or in mechanical room | Yellow handle on gas line |
| **Electrical panel** | Back-of-house / mechanical area | Grey metal box |
| **First aid kit** | Posted location (chef office, dish area, or both) | White box with red cross |
| **AED (if present)** | Common area, signed | Green-and-white pictogram |
| **Eyewash station** | Within 10 seconds / 50 ft of chemical hazards | Green sign |
| **WHMIS / SDS station** | Near dish pit or chef office | Binder labelled "SDS" or scan-station |
| **Cut-resistant gloves** | Near slicer / mandoline / butchery | Hung or in a labelled bin |
| **Heat-resistant gloves / pads** | Near oven, fryer, grill | On hook adjacent to the equipment |
| **Spill kit (biological / chemical)** | Back-of-house or near washrooms | Labelled bucket |
| **Assembly point** | Outside, designated landmark | Green sign |
| **Exit routes (primary + secondary)** | Marked exit signs | Illuminated green/red sign |

> **[Regulatory]** Provincial OH&S Acts and Codes (e.g., Ontario *Reg. 851*, BC *Occupational Health and Safety Regulation*, Alberta *OHS Code*) require that emergency equipment be **accessible**, **unobstructed**, and **clearly identified**. Blocking a fire extinguisher with stacked dunnage is a common, serious citation.

#### Layout standards worth knowing
- **Fire extinguisher travel distance** — per **NFPA 10** (adopted into provincial fire codes): Class K extinguishers no more than ~9 m / 30 ft from cooking hazard; Class ABC extinguishers max ~23 m / 75 ft travel distance for ordinary hazards. Exact numbers vary by jurisdiction; the principle is **short, unobstructed reach**. **[Regulatory]**
- **Eyewash stations** — per the widely-referenced **ANSI/ISEA Z358.1** standard (a US standard, but commonly adopted by Canadian employers and referenced in CSA/OH&S guidance): reachable within **10 seconds** (≈ 17 m / 55 ft) of the hazard, on the same level, free of obstructions, with **tepid water (16–38 °C)** and a **15-minute continuous flush** capability. **[Best Practice — Z358.1 is a US standard; some Canadian OH&S codes reference equivalent requirements]**

### A-1.02.03P — Select PPE and Safety Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** select PPE and safety equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** PPE and safety equipment are selected according to jurisdictional regulations, task to be performed and company policy.

#### PPE selection — task-by-task

##### Gloves — pick the right chemistry and shape

| Glove type | Material | Best for | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Disposable food-handling** | **Nitrile** (most common), vinyl, latex | Ready-to-eat food, allergen tasks, dressing wounds | **Latex is allergenic** — most Canadian kitchens are nitrile-only. Vinyl is cheap but tears easily. |
| **Heavy-duty utility** | **Rubber / neoprene** | Dishwashing, chemical handling, cold storage cleaning | Don't reuse a glove that has had chemicals inside it for food tasks. |
| **Cut-resistant** | UHMWPE (Dyneema/Spectra), Kevlar, steel mesh | Mandoline, oyster shucking, deboning, hand slicing | **Cut-resistant is not cut-proof.** Rated by ANSI A1–A9 or EN 388. |
| **Heat-resistant** | Aramid / silicone / leather composite | Oven, fryer baskets, sheet trays | Most kitchen gloves rated to about **250–260 °C / 480–500 °F** for short durations. **Never use wet** — moisture conducts heat. |
| **Cryo / freezer gloves** | Insulated, often impermeable | Dry ice, liquid nitrogen, deep-freeze work | Required for any liquid-nitrogen contact. |

##### Cut-resistance ratings (apprentice cheat sheet)

| Standard | Scale | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| **ANSI/ISEA 105** | **A1 (lowest) → A9 (highest)** | Grams of cutting load before blade penetrates |
| **EN 388** | **A → F (cut)**, plus abrasion / tear / puncture digits | ISO 13997 cut performance |

For mandoline / oyster work, **ANSI A5 or higher** is a typical industry expectation. **[Best Practice]**

##### Eye and face protection
- **Safety goggles** — splash protection for chemical decanting, descaling, dish-machine maintenance, oven-cleaner use.
- **Face shield** — high-splash tasks (deep-cleaning chemicals, butchery of certain large carcasses).
- **Why:** chemical splashes to the eye can cause permanent corneal damage in seconds. Sodium hydroxide (lye, common in oven cleaners) and quaternary ammonium splashes are particular risks.

##### Respiratory protection
- **Dust masks (N95/KN95)** — flour dust on bake stations, dry ingredient handling, illness control.
- **Half-face respirator with cartridge** — only in rare cases like enclosed deep cleaning with strong solvents. Cartridge selection (organic vapour vs acid gas) must match the SDS Section 8 recommendation. **[Regulatory]** if required by SDS; respirator users in Canada generally require **fit testing** (per **CSA Z94.4**) and medical clearance.

##### Aprons
- **Rubber apron** — dish pit, butchery, fish prep, deep cleaning.
- **Cloth apron** — line cooking, prep.
- **Replace** when torn, oil-soaked, or contaminated.

##### Hearing protection
- **Dish-pit zones, large mixers, garburators** can exceed 85 dBA — at which point provincial OH&S typically requires hearing-protection programs. **[Regulatory at 85 dB threshold in most jurisdictions]**

#### Safety-equipment selection at the moment of need

| Emergency | Right equipment | Wrong equipment (do not use) |
|---|---|---|
| **Grease fire on stove** | Class **K** extinguisher; lid; fire blanket | Water; Class A; carrying the pan |
| **Electrical fire (toaster, panel)** | Class **C** (or ABC) | Water; Class K |
| **Burning paper / cardboard** | Class **A** or **ABC** | — |
| **Burning solvent / alcohol** | Class **B** (or ABC) | Water |
| **Person on fire** | **Fire blanket** — drop, wrap, roll; or "Stop-Drop-Roll" | Run; spray with extinguisher at face |
| **Chemical eye splash** | **Eyewash station** — 15 min continuous, eyelid held open | Rub eyes; small cup of tap water |
| **Cardiac arrest** | **AED** + CPR; call 911 | Wait passively |
| **Severe bleed** | First aid kit; direct pressure; tourniquet if available; 911 | Tourniquet without training on small bleeds |
| **Chemical inhalation** | Move to fresh air; check SDS Section 4 | Stay and "tough it out" |

> **Memory aid — fire classes "ABCK":**
> **A**shes (ordinary combustibles), **B**oils (flammable liquids), **C**urrents (electrical), **K**itchen (cooking oils/fats).

### A-1.02.04P — Identify Worn, Defective and Expired PPE/Safety Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** identify worn, defective and expired PPE and safety equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** worn, defective and expired PPE and safety equipment are identified according to jurisdictional regulations and sensory assessment.

#### Inspection cues — what to look for

##### Fire extinguishers
- **Pressure gauge** in the green band (not red).
- **Tamper seal intact** on the pin.
- **Inspection tag** signed and current.
  - **Monthly visual inspection** by trained on-site staff. **[Regulatory under NFPA 10 as adopted]**
  - **Annual service** by a certified technician. **[Regulatory]**
  - **Internal maintenance / hydrostatic testing** at intervals defined by extinguisher type (typically 5–12 years).
- **No corrosion, dents, or hose damage.**
- **Nozzle free of obstructions** (insects, grease).

##### Fire blankets
- **Sealed package**, no holes or scorching.
- **Pull tabs accessible.**
- **Replace after any use** — they are typically not re-certifiable.

##### Eyewash stations
- **Weekly activation test** (run for ~3 minutes) to flush stagnant water and confirm flow. **[Best Practice per ANSI Z358.1]**
- **Annual full inspection.**
- **Caps and covers present** to prevent airborne contamination of nozzles.
- **Plumbed stations** — water clear, tepid; **portable** — fluid within manufacturer-stated dating (typically 1–2 years).

##### First-aid kit
- **Inventory checklist** signed monthly.
- **No expired** sterile dressings, gauze, eyewash bottles, or burn gels.
- **Replenished** after any use.
- **CSA Z1220 / provincial OH&S** dictate kit contents based on workplace size and hazards. **[Regulatory — provincially defined; CSA Z1220 is a referenced standard]**

##### AED
- **Status indicator green** (or equivalent).
- **Pads not expired** (typical 2-year life from manufacture).
- **Battery within service life.**
- **Recorded weekly check** per program policy.

##### Disposable gloves
- **No tears, no powder residue on food-contact tasks** (powdered gloves are largely phased out for food).
- **Change** between tasks, after contamination, every ~4 hours during continuous use, and any time the glove tears. **[Best Practice / public-health-aligned]**

##### Cut-resistant gloves
- **No cuts through the weave** (check by stretching against light).
- **Wash regularly** — typically machine-washable per manufacturer.
- **Replace** when fibres show or rating may be compromised.

##### Heat-resistant gloves and pads
- **No dampness** before use (moisture conducts heat → severe burns).
- **No scorching or charring.**
- **No oil soak-through** — saturated insulation transmits heat fast.

##### Footwear
- **Tread depth visible** — smooth soles fail slip tests.
- **No oil soak**, no separated soles, intact heel strap (Crocs).
- **CSA tag intact** if a safety boot.

##### Aprons & uniforms
- **Replace** when torn, oil-saturated, or chemically stained beyond cleaning.
- **Launder daily** at minimum, separately from personal laundry. **[Best Practice; many ops use commercial laundry]**

#### Sensory assessment cues
The evidence statement specifies **sensory assessment** — meaning apprentices should use sight, smell, touch, and operational feel:

| Sense | Cue |
|---|---|
| **Sight** | Tears, fading, corrosion, missing tags, low pressure gauges, discoloured first-aid items |
| **Smell** | Rubber degradation odour from old gloves, mildew on aprons, stale dishpit gloves |
| **Touch** | Crumbly elastic on goggles, brittle straps, sticky residue, moisture in gloves |
| **Operational feel** | A heat glove that "feels thinner" than it used to; an extinguisher that suddenly feels light |

> **Memory aid — "PATCH" for inspection:**
> **P**ressure (gauge / battery), **A**ttachments intact (pins, tags, straps), **T**ears or damage, **C**ondition of label/date, **H**azard environment matched to PPE class.

## 2. Knowledge Objectives — Deeper Dive

### A-1.02.01L — Types, Applications, Maintenance, and Procedures

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of PPE and safety equipment, their applications, maintenance and procedures for use.

#### Procedures for use — the high-yield items

##### How to use a fire extinguisher — PASS
- **P**ull the pin.
- **A**im at the **base** of the fire (the fuel, not the flames).
- **S**queeze the handle.
- **S**weep side to side until the fire is out, then watch for re-ignition.

Stand **2–3 m / 6–10 ft back** to start; close in as the fire reduces. Most extinguishers discharge in **8–20 seconds total**. You get one shot — don't aim wrong.

##### How to use a fire blanket
- Pull both tabs straight down.
- Hold blanket up like a shield, edges rolled around your hands to protect them.
- Place (do not throw) over the fire to smother.
- Turn off heat source.
- Leave the blanket in place until cool.

##### How to use an eyewash station — Z358.1 protocol
- Activate within **10 seconds** of exposure.
- Hold eyelids open with fingers.
- Flush for a **minimum of 15 minutes** continuously.
- Roll eyeballs to flush full surface.
- Seek medical attention — do not skip even if you "feel okay" after.
- Check SDS Section 4 (First Aid) for any chemical-specific extension (some require 60 minutes).

##### How to use an AED
- Turn it on (some open the lid to activate).
- Follow voice prompts.
- Bare and dry the chest; shave heavy hair if pads stick to fur.
- Attach pads as diagrammed.
- Clear the patient ("clear!") before shock.
- Resume CPR immediately after shock or "no shock advised".
- Do not remove pads until paramedics take over.
- **Apprentices should consider basic First Aid + CPR-C + AED certification** — required by some employers, encouraged by all. **[Best Practice]**

##### Glove change protocol
Change gloves whenever:
- Switching between **raw and ready-to-eat**.
- Switching between **different allergens** (especially gluten, peanut, shellfish).
- After **touching face, hair, phone, garbage, dirty cloths**.
- After **handling money** (no money handling at all if possible on prep stations).
- **Every ~4 hours** of continuous wear or whenever damaged.
- **Wash hands between glove changes.** Gloves do not replace handwashing — the inside of a sweaty glove is a microbial environment.

#### Maintenance overview

| Item | Routine maintenance |
|---|---|
| **Fire extinguishers** | Monthly visual; annual certified service; hydro-test per type |
| **Fire blanket** | Monthly visual; replace after any use |
| **Hood suppression** | Semi-annual certified inspection (NFPA 17A); recharge after discharge |
| **Eyewash** | Weekly activation; annual inspection |
| **AED** | Weekly readiness check; pad/battery replacement per dates |
| **First aid kit** | Monthly inventory; replenish after use |
| **Cut-resistant gloves** | Wash per manufacturer; replace when degraded |
| **Heat gloves / pads** | Dry storage; replace if scorched or wet-saturated |
| **Footwear** | Daily clean; replace when tread fails |
| **Knives / sharpening steels** (PPE-adjacent) | Sharpen and hone regularly — dull knives cause more cuts than sharp ones, because force/pressure increases on slip |

### A-1.02.02L — Regulatory Requirements for PPE and Safety Equipment

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of regulatory requirements pertaining to PPE and safety equipment.

#### Canadian regulatory layers — who governs PPE

| Level | Body | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Federal** | **Hazardous Products Act / Hazardous Products Regulations** | WHMIS 2015 supplier requirements (labels, SDS). |
| **Federal** | **Health Canada / CFIA** | Food safety guidance (e.g., glove use, handler hygiene). |
| **Provincial** | **OH&S Acts and Regulations** (Ontario OHSA + Reg. 851; BC WorkSafeBC; Alberta OHS Code; Québec CNESST / LSST; etc.) | Employer obligation to provide and maintain PPE; worker obligation to use it; training. |
| **Provincial / National** | **CSA Standards** (e.g., **Z195** footwear, **Z94.3** eye/face, **Z94.4** respirator fit testing, **Z180.1** compressed breathing air, **Z1220** first aid kits) | Referenced into provincial OH&S as the de facto specifications. |
| **National (referenced)** | **NFPA 10** fire extinguishers, **NFPA 17A** wet chemical (hood) suppression, **NFPA 96** kitchen exhaust | Referenced by provincial fire codes. |
| **National (referenced)** | **ANSI/ISEA Z358.1** eyewash stations | Widely adopted but a US standard — verify provincial reference. |
| **Provincial** | **Public Health Food Premises Regulations** | Personal hygiene, hair restraint, illness exclusion. |
| **Municipal** | **Fire department, business licensing** | On-site inspection of fire and safety equipment. |

#### The hierarchy of controls — *why* PPE is the last line
Canadian OH&S follows the standard **Hierarchy of Controls**:

1. **Elimination** — remove the hazard entirely.
2. **Substitution** — replace with a less hazardous alternative (e.g., quat instead of bleach + ammonia situation).
3. **Engineering controls** — physical changes (guards on slicers, drains in floors, ventilation hoods).
4. **Administrative controls** — procedures, training, signage, schedules.
5. **PPE** — last line of defense.

**PPE is not a substitute for guards, training, or design.** A slicer guard is engineering; a cut-resistant glove is PPE. Both should be used; never just the glove. **[Regulatory principle]**

#### Worker rights and duties under OH&S
- **Right to know** about hazards (WHMIS).
- **Right to participate** in workplace safety (e.g., JHSC member, safety meetings).
- **Right to refuse** unsafe work without retaliation.
- **Duty to wear** required PPE and follow training.
- **Duty to report** defective or missing PPE.

> A worker cannot be disciplined for refusing to use equipment that lacks required PPE or guards. Employers are legally obligated to provide PPE that is appropriate, in good condition, and trained-on. **[Regulatory — provincial OH&S]**

#### Public health / food safety angle
- **Hair restraint** required when working with exposed food. **[Regulatory — provincial public health]**
- **Illness exclusion** — workers with vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice, sore throat with fever, or infected wounds **must not handle food**. Reportable in many provinces. **[Regulatory]**
- **Bandages** — blue, waterproof, glove over top. **[Best Practice]**
- **Glove use** — gloves are not legally required for all tasks in Canada (unlike some US jurisdictions); hand hygiene is the primary control. Gloves **must not** substitute for handwashing.

## 3. The Allergen / Cross-Contact Lens on PPE

Allergen control is rising fast as a regulated topic in Canada, particularly under the **Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR)** for federally-registered operators and increasingly mirrored in provincial food premises rules.

#### Priority allergens in Canada (Health Canada list)
Eggs, milk, mustard, peanuts, crustaceans/molluscs, fish, sesame seeds, soy, sulphites, tree nuts, wheat & triticale.

#### PPE-related controls
- **Dedicated colour-coded utensils** and gloves for allergen-isolated production (some operations use purple for allergen-free prep).
- **Glove change** between allergen tasks — not just raw/cooked.
- **Apron change** if working a celiac/gluten-free order out of an allergen-heavy line.
- **Hair restraint** to prevent stray hairs carrying flour or peanut residue.

## 4. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Setting up to use the mandoline
1. Verify the mandoline blade is sharp and intact.
2. Don a **cut-resistant glove (ANSI A5+)** on the **product-handling hand**.
3. Engage the **food guard / hand guard** — never freehand.
4. Position the unit on a non-slip mat.
5. Cut down to the guard; switch hands or change technique before the guard runs out.
6. After: lock blade, clean with brush + sanitize, dry, store with blade retracted.

### Scenario 2 — Decanting oven cleaner from drum to spray bottle
1. Consult SDS — confirm PPE requirements.
2. Put on **safety goggles**, **chemical-resistant rubber gloves**, and **rubber apron**.
3. Decant in a well-ventilated area away from food.
4. **Workplace-label** the new bottle (product, hazard, date).
5. If splashed in the eye → eyewash 15 minutes, then medical.
6. Store original drum locked and below food storage.

### Scenario 3 — Pulling a hotel pan from a 175 °C oven
1. **Dry** heat-resistant gloves or folded dry side towel.
2. Both hands underneath the pan with stable grip.
3. Verbal call: "Hot behind!" / "Hot pan coming!"
4. Place on heat-stable surface, not on a wet rag (steam burn).

### Scenario 4 — Co-worker collapses unresponsive in the kitchen
1. Call out, assess scene safety (no live electrical, no spill).
2. Tap and shout — no response → call 911 / send someone to call.
3. Send a second person to fetch the **AED** and first-aid kit.
4. Begin CPR (30 compressions : 2 breaths) if trained.
5. Apply AED as soon as available; follow voice prompts.
6. Continue until paramedics take over.
7. Post-event: document, debrief, review what worked.

## 5. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- Wearing **wet** heat gloves or using a **wet side towel** to grab a hot pan → second-degree burn.
- Wearing **cloth gloves** near open flame (catches fire).
- Treating **cut-resistant gloves as cut-proof** — they reduce, do not eliminate, laceration risk.
- Using a **Class A water extinguisher on a grease fire** — explosive flash and burn ejection.
- Storing **fire extinguisher pins** with the seal broken because "we tested it once" → it's now considered discharged.
- **Eyewash station** left without weekly flushing → biofilm/Legionella risk in the very fluid meant to save your eye.
- **Latex glove** snapped on a guest with shellfish allergy and unknown latex allergy. (Most Canadian kitchens are nitrile-only.)
- **Apron worn into the washroom** and back → fecal-route contamination risk.
- **Personal phone on the prep table**, then handling food without handwashing.
- **Earphones / earbuds** during service — blocks situational cues ("Hot behind!", "Sharp!", "Corner!").

## 7. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with A-1.02.

1. **Chef uniform purpose** — double-breasted jacket for burn protection and reversibility; loose pants for hot-liquid blow-off; side towels **must be dry** to handle hot pans.
2. **Footwear** — closed-toe, slip-resistant; **CSA Z195** safety footwear (green triangle = Grade 1 toe + puncture sole) common in receiving and butchery.
3. **Hierarchy of Controls:** Elimination → Substitution → Engineering → Administrative → **PPE (last)**.
4. **Fire extinguisher classes:** **A** (ordinary combustibles), **B** (flammable liquids), **C** (electrical), **K** (cooking oils/fats).
5. **PASS technique** — Pull, Aim (at base), Squeeze, Sweep.
6. **NFPA 10 / 17A / 96** — extinguisher placement and inspection, wet-chemical suppression, kitchen exhaust standards, adopted by Canadian fire codes.
7. **Eyewash protocol** — within 10 seconds of hazard, flush ≥ 15 minutes continuously, tepid water (16–38 °C) per ANSI Z358.1.
8. **AED** — turn on, follow prompts, clear before shock, resume CPR immediately after.
9. **Glove selection** — disposable nitrile for food contact (latex avoided due to allergens); cut-resistant ANSI A5+ for mandoline/oyster; heat-resistant kept **dry**; rubber for chemical handling.
10. **Glove change triggers** — task change, raw → ready-to-eat, allergen change, contamination, every ~4 hours; **hand-wash between changes**.
11. **Personal hygiene** — no jewellery beyond plain wedding band; no polish/false nails; hair restrained; blue waterproof bandage + glove on cuts; no perfume.
12. **Illness exclusion** — vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice, fever + sore throat → not handling food.
13. **WHMIS 2015** — labels + SDS + training; workplace labels required on decanted containers; SDS accessible during shift.
14. **CSA standards to recognize:** Z195 footwear, Z94.3 eye/face, Z94.4 respirator fit testing, Z1220 first aid kits, Z180.1 breathing air.
15. **OH&S worker rights:** right to know, right to participate, right to refuse unsafe work.
16. **OH&S worker duties:** wear required PPE, follow training, report defects.
17. **Employer duties:** provide appropriate PPE, train workers, maintain equipment, ensure JHSC where required.
18. **Eye splash response:** eyewash 15 min, then medical — even if symptoms seem mild.
19. **Grease fire response:** cover with metal lid OR Class K extinguisher OR fire blanket; **never water**; never carry the pan.
20. **Hood suppression** — recharge by certified technician after any discharge; semi-annual inspections under NFPA 17A.
21. **Footwear with smooth tread = slip-test failure.** Replace.
22. **Wet heat protection = no heat protection.** Moisture transmits heat instantly.
23. **Cut-resistant ≠ cut-proof.** Always keep guards engaged on slicers/mandolines.
24. **Hand sinks are for hands only** (carry-over from A-1.01).
25. **Latex glove caution** — many Canadian kitchens are nitrile-only due to allergen risk to staff and guests.
26. **Hearing protection** required where noise exceeds **85 dBA** (typical Canadian OH&S trigger).
27. **First aid + CPR + AED certification** — strongly recommended (often required) for line cooks and supervisors.
28. **Hot-zone vocabulary** ("Hot behind!", "Sharp!", "Corner!") — communication is itself a safety control.
