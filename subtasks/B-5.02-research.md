# B-5.02 | Uses Documentation — Enhanced Learning Resource

**Major Work Activity:** B — Common Occupational Skills
**Task:** B-5 | Manages Information

> **What this subtask is about:** Documentation is the paper (and digital) trail that proves a commercial kitchen is operating safely, legally, and professionally. Every log filled in, tag retained, SDS filed, and form completed is part of a system that protects guests from foodborne illness, workers from injury, and operators from legal liability. When something goes wrong — a foodborne illness outbreak, a workplace injury, a regulatory inspection — accurate documentation is the difference between a manageable incident and a serious legal and reputational crisis. This document enriches the Red Seal competency with the regulatory knowledge, practical reasoning, and document-specific detail an apprentice needs to handle kitchen documentation with confidence.

---

## Why Documentation Matters in a Commercial Kitchen

Documentation serves four distinct purposes simultaneously:

**1. Food Safety Evidence**
Temperature logs, sanitizer strength records, and HACCP monitoring forms prove that critical control points were monitored and managed. In a foodborne illness investigation, these records are the primary evidence that the operation followed safe food handling procedures.

**2. Legal Compliance**
Many documents are required by law — shellfish tags under federal food safety regulations, SDS under WHMIS legislation, accident reports under provincial OH&S acts, apprenticeship records under provincial apprenticeship acts. Non-compliance exposes the operator to fines, licence suspension, or prosecution.

**3. Traceability and Recall Response**
When a food safety recall is issued (e.g., a specific harvest area is closed due to paralytic shellfish poisoning), the operation needs records showing which specific lots of product were received, when they were used, and which guests were served. Without documentation, a recall response is impossible.

**4. Operational Intelligence**
Waste logs, inventory forms, and performance evaluations generate data that drives better purchasing decisions, reduces food cost, identifies staff development needs, and improves overall kitchen performance.

> **The documentation principle:** If it wasn't written down, it didn't happen. A food handler's verbal assurance that temperatures were checked is worthless in a regulatory inspection or legal proceeding. The written or digital record is the only form of documentation that counts.

#### Deep Dive: Organizing and Filing Kitchen Documents

Effective filing is not just tidiness — it is about **retrievability**. During a health inspection, an auditor may ask to see the last 30 days of refrigerator temperature logs, the SDS for every cleaning chemical on the premises, or the shellfish tags for a specific delivery. If these documents cannot be produced quickly, the operation may fail the inspection.

**Filing System Principles**

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Chronological order** | Temperature logs, waste logs, and sanitizer strength records filed by date — most recent on top or most accessible |
| **Category separation** | HACCP records, WHMIS/SDS binders, OH&S records, shellfish tags, and recipe files kept in distinct, labelled locations |
| **Access control** | SDS must be accessible to all workers at all times — not locked away; operational records should be accessible to supervisors and inspectors on demand |
| **Retention compliance** | Documents are kept for the minimum required period defined by regulation or company policy; not discarded earlier |
| **Protection from damage** | Food safety logs kept in a clean, dry location; not stored near food prep surfaces where they can become contaminated or moisture-damaged |

**Common Filing Locations in a Commercial Kitchen**

- **SDS binder:** Typically mounted near the chemical storage area or in a designated location known to all employees; must be accessible at all times
- **Temperature logs:** Posted at or near each refrigeration unit; centrally filed in a food safety binder at end of each shift or day
- **Shellfish tags:** Attached to the container until the last product is used, then filed chronologically in a dedicated tag file
- **Banquet event orders (BEOs):** Filed by event date; active BEOs posted in a visible production area; completed BEOs archived
- **HACCP records:** Centralized food safety binder; organized by monitoring form type (receiving, cooking, cooling, hot-holding, reheating)

**Retention Periods**

> **Regulatory note:** Retention requirements are set by jurisdictional regulations and vary by document type and province. The following are common industry references — verify current requirements with your provincial food safety authority, OH&S regulator, and apprenticeship authority.

| Document Type | Common Retention Reference | Regulatory Context |
|---|---|---|
| Temperature logs / HACCP records | Minimum 1–2 years (common practice) | Required under HACCP-based food safety plans; verify with provincial authority |
| Shellfish tags | 90 days after last product from that lot is sold or served (common Canadian practice) | Canadian Shellfish Sanitation Program; verify with provincial public health |
| SDS | Current versions must be on file; retain superseded versions for defined period | WHMIS 2015 / Hazardous Products Regulations |
| Workplace injury / accident records | Typically 2–3 years minimum | Provincial OH&S and Workers' Compensation legislation |
| Apprenticeship records | Duration of apprenticeship + post-completion | Provincial Apprenticeship Act requirements |

### B-5.02.02P
**Performance Criteria:** Process documents

**Evidence of Attainment:** Documents are processed according to company policies and jurisdictional regulations

---

#### Deep Dive: Processing Documents

"Processing" a document means acting on its information — not just receiving and filing it but reading, interpreting, verifying, and responding to what it contains.

**Document Processing by Type**

**Shellfish Tags**
1. At receiving: match the tag to the delivery — verify species, quantity, and that the harvest area is approved (not closed)
2. Attach the tag to the container of live shellfish
3. Keep the tag with the container throughout storage and use
4. When the last product from that lot is consumed: remove the tag, record the date of last use, and file
5. Retain for the required period in case of a regulatory inquiry or illness investigation

**SDS (Safety Data Sheets)**
1. At product receiving: verify an SDS is on file for the product; obtain one if not
2. Review Section 2 (Hazard Identification), Section 4 (First Aid Measures), and Section 7 (Handling and Storage) for any new product before it is used in the workplace
3. Ensure the SDS version is current (suppliers must provide an updated SDS when they learn of significant new hazard information — check the revision date on the SDS)
4. File in the SDS binder in the designated accessible location

**Temperature Logs**
1. At each monitoring interval: take temperature reading with calibrated thermometer
2. Record: date, time, temperature, unit or food item, employee initials
3. Compare reading to critical limit (e.g., refrigerator must be ≤4°C)
4. If within critical limit: initial and move on
5. If deviation found (e.g., refrigerator reading 8°C): take corrective action immediately (move product, investigate equipment); document the corrective action on the log
6. File completed log at end of shift or day

**Banquet Event Orders (BEOs)**
1. Receive BEO from event coordinator or manager
2. Review for completeness: date, time, covers, menu, dietary requirements, service timeline, special setup
3. Identify any ambiguities or conflicts with production capacity and resolve before production begins
4. Use BEO to build the production schedule for that event
5. After event: file the BEO with a notation of actual covers served and any deviations from the plan

### B-5.02.03P
**Performance Criteria:** Recognize documentation errors

**Evidence of Attainment:** Documentation errors are recognized and corrected or reported

---

#### Deep Dive: Recognizing and Correcting Documentation Errors

Documentation errors in a food safety context are not just administrative inconveniences — they can indicate that a critical control point was not actually monitored, or they can undermine an operation's defence in a foodborne illness investigation.

**Types of Documentation Errors**

| Error Type | Example | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Missing entry** | A temperature log has a blank for the 2:00 PM refrigerator check | Suggests the check was never done; creates gap in food safety monitoring record |
| **Implausible value** | Refrigerator temperature logged as 2°C every single time across 30 days | Suggests records are being fabricated rather than measured; a regulatory red flag |
| **Incorrect units** | Temperature recorded in °F on a °C log form | Misread as in-range when it may actually be dangerously high (37°F = 2.8°C is fine; 37°C = 98.6°F is dangerously warm) |
| **Unsigned entry** | Temperature log entry with no employee initials | Cannot assign accountability; cannot verify the check was actually performed |
| **Wrong date or time** | Log entry dated yesterday but describing today's shift | Breaks chronological integrity; undermines traceability |
| **No corrective action recorded** | A log shows a refrigerator at 9°C but no corrective action is noted | HACCP non-compliance; what happened to the food in that unit? |
| **Illegible handwriting** | Temperature written as ambiguous number | Creates interpretive errors; temperature of 6 vs. 8°C is a material difference |
| **SDS missing or outdated** | SDS for a new cleaning chemical not obtained | WHMIS violation; worker cannot access required safety information |
| **Shellfish tag separated from product** | Tag left at receiving desk; product moved to storage without it | Loss of traceability; regulatory violation |

**Correcting Documentation Errors**

When a documentation error is discovered:
- **Minor errors (wrong date, transposed numbers, illegible entry):** Draw a single line through the error, write the correction beside it, initial and date the correction. **Never use correction fluid (white-out) on legal records** — it creates the appearance of deliberate falsification.
- **Missing entries:** If the check was actually performed but not recorded: record the information immediately with a note explaining the delay, and initial. Do not fabricate a reading that was not taken.
- **Significant errors (missing corrective action, data suggesting a product safety issue):** Report to supervisor immediately. The supervisor determines whether product disposition decisions need to be revisited.
- **Systematic errors (a pattern of missing or implausible records):** Escalate to management for investigation. This may indicate a training gap, a process problem, or a falsification concern.

> **Important:** Correcting your own error the moment you notice it is professional conduct. Discovering and correcting someone else's error, or reporting a pattern of errors you cannot resolve, is equally important — food safety documentation exists to protect guests, not to create paperwork.

#### Deep Dive: Completing Forms — Document by Document

**Food Safety Forms (HACCP Monitoring Records)**

HACCP monitoring forms are the primary documentation tool for proving Critical Control Points are being managed. A complete entry includes:

- Date and time
- Product or equipment being monitored
- Temperature or other measurement value
- Critical limit being monitored (stated on the form header)
- Employee name or initials
- Corrective action (if critical limit was not met)
- Verification signature (supervisor sign-off, typically daily or weekly)

*Why every field matters:* In a food safety investigation, inspectors look for patterns. A form missing times, or with all entries by the same person who never varies their readings, triggers scrutiny. Authentic, consistent, complete records reflect a functioning food safety system.

**Inventory Forms**

- Conducted on a regular schedule (typically weekly for perishables, monthly for dry goods)
- Records: item name, unit of measure, quantity on hand, unit cost, total value
- Used to calculate food cost: Opening inventory + Purchases − Closing inventory = Cost of goods used
- Errors in inventory forms directly distort food cost calculations
- FIFO rotation should be visually confirmed during inventory — product with earlier dates must be in front

**Work Schedules**

- Allocate labour based on forecasted covers, menu complexity, and station requirements
- Must comply with provincial Employment Standards Act provisions: maximum hours, minimum rest periods, overtime thresholds
- Apprenticeship hours tracking: apprentices (and their employers) must log all hours worked against their apprenticeship training plan

**Jurisdictional Accident Documentation**

In Canada, workplace accidents must be documented under provincial OH&S legislation and Workers' Compensation Board (WCB) requirements. While forms vary by province, the standard information captured includes:

- Name and contact information of injured worker
- Date, time, and location of accident
- Nature and description of the injury or illness
- Part of body affected
- Task being performed when injury occurred
- Equipment or materials involved
- Contributing factors (wet floor, inadequate lighting, lack of PPE, etc.)
- Names and contact information of witnesses
- First aid provided
- Whether the worker required medical attention or lost time from work

> **Regulatory note:** The reporting obligations for workplace injuries vary by province and injury severity. In most Canadian jurisdictions, serious injuries must be reported to the provincial OH&S authority within a defined timeframe (often 24 hours for critical injuries). Consult your provincial OH&S legislation for specific requirements.

**First Aid Logs**

Separate from formal accident reports, first aid logs record every first aid treatment given in the workplace — even minor ones. This log creates a record of all incidents that may escalate, and demonstrates that first aid was provided. Typical entries: date, time, worker name, nature of complaint, treatment given, name of first aid provider.

**Apprenticeship Documentation**

- **Apprenticeship agreement:** Signed by the apprentice, employer, and provincial apprenticeship authority. Establishes the legal training relationship.
- **Training plan:** Outlines the competencies to be achieved and the sequence of training.
- **Hours log (work record book):** The apprentice records all hours worked, signed off by the employer at regular intervals. This is the primary record for accumulating the hours required for Red Seal certification.
- **Technical training records:** Completion records from in-school periods at a college or training institution.
- **Exam application:** Submitted to the provincial apprenticeship authority when the apprentice has accumulated sufficient hours and technical training to challenge the Red Seal examination.

> **Red Seal Program Context:** The Red Seal Program (also called the Interprovincial Standards Program) is a national certification recognized across Canadian provinces and territories. It is administered by the Canadian Council of Directors of Apprenticeship (CCDA). A Red Seal endorsement means a tradesperson's skills have been assessed against a national standard, allowing them to work in their trade anywhere in Canada without re-examination. Hours requirements for Cook apprenticeships vary by province (typically 3–4 years of on-the-job training combined with technical training).

**Performance Evaluations**

- Assess skills, knowledge, professional conduct, and safety compliance
- In apprenticeship contexts, performance evaluations document the apprentice's progress against the training plan
- Must be accurate, specific, and objective — vague or falsely positive evaluations do not serve the apprentice's development or protect the employer
- Evaluations should be signed by both the evaluator and the employee

#### Deep Dive: Document Types, Applications, and Procedures

**Complete Reference: Kitchen Documents and Their Purpose**

**Shellfish Tags**

*What they are:* Traceability tags that must accompany every container of live shellfish (oysters, clams, mussels, scallops, and other bivalves) from the point of harvest through to sale or service.

*Why they exist:* Shellfish are **filter feeders** — they draw water through their bodies to extract nutrients, and in doing so they can concentrate pathogens and toxins present in their growing water. The bacteria and toxins that are most dangerous in shellfish include:

- ***Vibrio parahaemolyticus* and *Vibrio vulnificus*** — naturally occurring marine bacteria that thrive in warm coastal waters; *V. vulnificus* can cause life-threatening illness in immunocompromised individuals
- **Norovirus** — the most common cause of foodborne illness outbreaks associated with shellfish; highly contagious; very low infectious dose
- **Paralytic Shellfish Poisoning (PSP)**, **Amnesic Shellfish Poisoning (ASP)**, **Diarrhetic Shellfish Poisoning (DSP)** — caused by biotoxins produced by harmful algal blooms; cannot be destroyed by cooking; require monitoring of harvest areas

*Canadian regulatory context:* The **Canadian Shellfish Sanitation Program (CSSP)** is administered jointly by the **Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA)**, **Environment and Climate Change Canada (ECCC)**, and **Fisheries and Oceans Canada (DFO)**. The CSSP classifies shellfish growing areas and monitors for biotoxins, microbial contamination, and chemical contaminants. Only shellfish from **approved** growing areas may be harvested for commercial sale.

*What a shellfish tag contains:*
- Harvester/dealer identification number
- Harvest area identification
- Harvest date
- Species
- Quantity
- Any applicable classification of the growing area

*What the operator must do:*
- Keep the tag attached to the container until the **last shellfish from that container is used**
- File the tag after use and retain for the required period (commonly 90 days — verify with your provincial authority)
- Never accept shellfish without a tag — this is a serious food safety and legal violation
- If a shellfish growing area is subsequently closed (e.g., due to an algal bloom), health authorities can use the tag records to identify which restaurants received product from the affected area

> **Regulatory Requirement:** Shellfish tagging requirements are federal under the Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR) and the CSSP. Commercial food service operators must retain tags to enable traceability. This is not a recommendation — it is a legal obligation.

---

**SDS (Safety Data Sheets)**

*What they are:* Standardized documents that provide comprehensive hazard information about a chemical product, required under WHMIS 2015.

*Regulatory context:* WHMIS (**Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System**) is Canada's national standard for hazard communication. It was updated in 2015 (WHMIS 2015) to align with the **Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals (GHS)**. WHMIS 2015 is implemented through the **Hazardous Products Act** and **Hazardous Products Regulations** (federal), and corresponding provincial OH&S legislation.

*The 16 Sections of a GHS-Compliant SDS:*

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| 1 | Identification — product name, manufacturer, intended use, emergency phone |
| 2 | Hazard Identification — classification, hazard symbols, signal word, hazard and precautionary statements |
| 3 | Composition / Information on Ingredients — chemical names, CAS numbers, concentrations |
| 4 | First-Aid Measures — what to do in case of exposure (skin, eyes, inhalation, ingestion) |
| 5 | Fire-Fighting Measures — suitable extinguishing media, special hazards |
| 6 | Accidental Release Measures — spill clean-up procedures |
| 7 | Handling and Storage — safe use instructions, incompatible materials |
| 8 | Exposure Controls / PPE — occupational exposure limits, required protective equipment |
| 9 | Physical and Chemical Properties — appearance, odour, pH, flammability, etc. |
| 10 | Stability and Reactivity — chemical stability, conditions to avoid, incompatibilities |
| 11 | Toxicological Information — health effects, routes of exposure, carcinogenicity |
| 12 | Ecological Information — environmental impact |
| 13 | Disposal Considerations — waste disposal methods |
| 14 | Transport Information — shipping classification |
| 15 | Regulatory Information — applicable regulations |
| 16 | Other Information — date of preparation, revision date |

*Kitchen chemicals requiring an SDS:*
- Chlorine-based sanitizers (bleach solutions)
- Quaternary ammonium (quat) sanitizers
- Degreasers and oven cleaners
- Floor cleaning chemicals
- CO₂ cylinders (for carbonated beverage systems)
- Pest control substances
- Dishwasher chemicals
- Any other hazardous product used in the workplace

*Access requirement:* SDS must be accessible to workers at all times during their shift. Common practice is to maintain an SDS binder at or near the chemical storage area. A worker who needs to use a cleaning chemical must be able to find the SDS for that product within minutes.

> **Regulatory Requirement:** Employers are legally required under WHMIS 2015 to ensure that current SDS are available for every hazardous product used in the workplace, that all containers are properly labelled, and that workers receive WHMIS training. This is a legal obligation under federal and provincial legislation.

**Temperature Logs**

*What they are:* Written or electronic records of temperature measurements taken at defined monitoring points in the food safety system.

*Types of temperature logs:*

| Log Type | What It Monitors | Monitoring Frequency | Critical Limit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Refrigerator/walk-in cooler log | Air temperature of unit | 2× per shift (common industry practice) | ≤4°C |
| Freezer log | Air temperature of unit | 1–2× per shift | ≤-18°C |
| Hot-holding log | Temperature of food in steam table/bain-marie | Every 2–4 hours during service | ≥60°C |
| Cooking/CCP log | Internal temperature of food at the CCP | Every batch at the cooking CCP | Varies by product (74°C for most cooked foods) |
| Receiving temperature log | Temperature of incoming refrigerated and frozen deliveries | Every delivery | Refrigerated ≤4°C; frozen ≤-18°C |
| Cooling log | Temperature at defined intervals during cooling process | At start, 2-hour mark (must be ≤20°C), and 6-hour mark (must be ≤4°C) | Two-stage cooling standard |
| Re-thermalization log | Internal temperature of reheated food | Each reheating event | ≥74°C within 2 hours |

*Data loggers (electronic temperature monitoring):* Electronic devices that continuously record temperatures and store them digitally. Advantages over manual logs include:
- Continuous rather than spot-check monitoring
- Automatic timestamping (cannot be altered retroactively)
- Alarm notification when temperature exceeds set limit
- Downloadable records for audit purposes

---

**Sanitizer Strength Logs**

*Why sanitizer concentration is monitored:*

Sanitizer that is too weak fails to kill pathogens — the food contact surface is cleaned visually but not microbiologically safe. Sanitizer that is too strong leaves chemical residues on food contact surfaces that can contaminate food.

*Common sanitizer types and target concentrations:*

| Sanitizer Type | Typical Working Concentration | Test Method | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chlorine (sodium hypochlorite) | 100–200 ppm for food contact surfaces | Chlorine test strips | Effective against a broad spectrum of pathogens; degrades rapidly at high temperatures and in the presence of organic matter |
| Quaternary ammonium (quat) | 200–400 ppm (product-specific) | Quat test strips | More stable than chlorine; effective in moderately hard water; requires product-specific test strips |
| Iodine (iodophor) | 12.5–25 ppm | Iodine test strips | Visible (brown colour) allows visual check of approximate concentration; pH-sensitive |

> **Regulatory note:** Sanitizer concentrations for food contact surfaces are specified in provincial food premises regulations and on product labels. The values above reflect common Canadian commercial kitchen practice. Always follow the specific label directions for the product being used — these constitute the manufacturer's specifications and have regulatory standing.

*Sanitizer log entries:* Date, time, sanitizer type, measured concentration, pass/fail against critical limit, employee initials, corrective action if below critical limit (e.g., make fresh solution).

**Job Hazard Analysis (JHA)**

*What it is:* A document that systematically identifies the hazards associated with each step of a specific job or task, and describes the safe work procedures to eliminate or control those hazards. Also called a Job Safety Analysis (JSA) or hazard assessment.

*Why it exists:* Required as part of workplace health and safety management systems under provincial OH&S legislation. A JHA demonstrates that the employer has identified hazards and implemented controls — an obligation under the general duty clause of most OH&S acts.

*High-risk kitchen tasks typically requiring a JHA:*
- Operating a mandoline slicer or deli slicer
- Using a meat grinder or tenderizer
- Working with a tilt skillet or steam-jacketed kettle
- Deep fryer operation
- Using high-temperature ovens (deck ovens, combi-ovens)
- Receiving and handling CO₂ cylinders
- Handling and storing concentrated cleaning chemicals

*JHA structure:*
1. Job steps (break the task into discrete steps)
2. Potential hazard at each step (what could go wrong?)
3. Control measures (what safe work procedure prevents the hazard?)

*Example — Mandoline Slicer:*

| Job Step | Potential Hazard | Control Measure |
|---|---|---|
| Retrieve mandoline from storage | Lacerations from blade contact | Store with blade guard in place; transport with blade facing away from body |
| Assemble for use | Blade contact during assembly | Wear cut-resistant gloves during assembly; handle blade only by spine |
| Set blade depth | Incorrect setting causes inconsistent thickness or blade contact | Set depth before use, not during; use blade guard |
| Slice product | Fingertip laceration from blade contact | Always use hand guard/food pusher; never use bare hand in last 7 cm of product |
| Clean after use | Laceration during cleaning | Remove blade before cleaning; clean blade from spine outward with appropriate cloth, never bare hands |

---

**Waste Logs**

*What they track:* The quantity and type of food discarded at each stage of production. Categories typically tracked:

- **Prep waste:** Trim, peels, tops removed during preparation (some is unavoidable; excessive amounts indicate poor knife skills or wrong product grade)
- **Cooking waste:** Product that fails quality during cooking — overcooked, dropped, contaminated
- **Plate waste / return waste:** Uneaten food returned from guest (tracked less rigorously in food service, more in institutional settings)
- **Spoilage/expiry waste:** Product that became unusable before production (FIFO failure, storage temperature failure, over-purchasing)
- **Re-thermalization failures:** Product discarded because it could not be safely reheated and served

*Why waste logs are valuable:*
- Identify patterns of spoilage that indicate purchasing, storage, or rotation problems
- Quantify the true food cost impact of production losses
- Track re-thermalization failures as a food safety monitoring tool
- Support environmental compliance where municipal organic waste diversion is required

---

**Prep Lists and Cleaning Lists**

*Prep lists:* The daily production schedule documenting what needs to be prepared, by whom, and by when (see B-4.02 for full detail on production scheduling).

*Cleaning lists (cleaning schedules):* Document required cleaning tasks, frequency, responsible person, and sign-off confirmation. A cleaning schedule may include:
- Daily tasks: wipe surfaces, clean stovetop, sweep and mop floors
- Weekly tasks: degrease hood filters, clean walk-in cooler interior, descale coffee equipment
- Monthly tasks: deep clean ovens, clean under and behind equipment, check refrigerator door seals

Under HACCP prerequisite programs, cleaning and sanitation schedules are a required documented procedure. An inspector reviewing a HACCP plan will ask to see the cleaning schedule and verify that it is actually being followed (through inspection of cleanliness and review of signed completion records).

#### Deep Dive: The Regulatory Framework — WHMIS, HACCP, OH&S, and Public Health

**WHMIS 2015**

WHMIS 2015 is Canada's national hazard communication standard. Its three pillars are:

1. **Classification** — hazardous products are classified into categories with defined hazard criteria (physical hazards, health hazards, environmental hazards) according to GHS standards
2. **Labelling** — supplier labels on hazardous product containers must include: product identifier, hazard pictogram(s), signal word (DANGER or WARNING), hazard statements, precautionary statements, and supplier information
3. **SDS** — Safety Data Sheets must be provided by suppliers for every hazardous product; must be made available to workers; must be retained and kept current

*WHMIS pictograms (GHS symbols):* Eight standardized pictograms identify hazard categories including: exploding bomb (explosive), flame (flammable), flame over circle (oxidizing), skull and crossbones (acute toxicity), exclamation mark (health hazard), health hazard (silhouette), corrosion, and environment. Kitchen workers are most likely to encounter flame, corrosion (for concentrated acids and bases), and exclamation mark or health hazard symbols on cleaning chemicals.

*Workplace labels:* When a hazardous product is transferred from its original container into a secondary container (e.g., decanting sanitizer into a spray bottle), a **workplace label** must be applied. At minimum, a workplace label must identify the product and provide applicable safety information. A label from a permanent marker writing the product name is the absolute minimum; a printed label with hazard information is better practice.

**HACCP as a Regulatory and Documentation Framework**

HACCP (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points) is both a food safety management methodology and, in certain contexts, a regulatory requirement. Documentation is integral to HACCP — without records, there is no way to demonstrate that the system is functioning.

*The seven HACCP principles and their documentation requirements:*

| HACCP Principle | Documentation Produced |
|---|---|
| 1. Conduct hazard analysis | Written hazard analysis document identifying all biological, chemical, and physical hazards |
| 2. Identify CCPs | CCP decision tree and CCP list |
| 3. Establish critical limits | Critical limits documented for each CCP (e.g., ≥74°C for cooking CCP) |
| 4. Establish monitoring procedures | Monitoring procedures documented; forms designed for data collection |
| 5. Establish corrective actions | Written corrective action procedures for each CCP deviation |
| 6. Establish verification procedures | Verification schedule and records (equipment calibration, supervisor review of logs, periodic product testing) |
| 7. Establish record-keeping | All of the above, plus ongoing monitoring logs, corrective action records, verification records |

*When HACCP is a regulatory requirement in Canada:*
- **Federally registered food establishments** (those producing food for interprovincial or international trade) are required to have a written, functioning HACCP-based food safety plan under the **Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR)**, administered by the **Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA)**
- **Provincially regulated food premises** (restaurants, cafeterias, catering operations) are subject to provincial food premises regulations; many provinces require HACCP-based food safety plans for higher-risk operations
- Many large foodservice operators implement HACCP voluntarily regardless of regulatory requirement

**OH&S Framework in Commercial Kitchens**

Provincial OH&S legislation in Canada creates three fundamental worker rights:
1. **The right to know** about hazards in the workplace (WHMIS is the mechanism for hazardous products)
2. **The right to participate** in health and safety activities (joint health and safety committees for larger workplaces)
3. **The right to refuse** unsafe work without reprisal

Documentation supports all three rights. WHMIS training records prove workers have been informed. Safety meeting minutes and JHA reviews demonstrate participation. Refusal documentation protects the worker who refused.

*Internal Responsibility System (IRS):* Canadian OH&S law is built on the principle that employers and workers share responsibility for maintaining a safe workplace — this is not solely the employer's burden. Every cook who correctly completes a temperature log, files an SDS for a new chemical, or reports a hazard is fulfilling their part of the IRS.

**Public Health Regulations**

Provincial and territorial food premises regulations govern commercial kitchens through health inspections conducted by public health authorities. Key documentation items that health inspectors review:

- Temperature logs (are they current? do they show corrective actions when deviations occurred?)
- HACCP monitoring records (is the food safety plan being implemented?)
- Shellfish tags (are they present and properly retained?)
- SDS binder (are current SDS available for all chemicals?)
- Pest control records (is a licensed pest control operator conducting inspections?)
- Cleaning schedules (are they signed and up to date?)

An inspection that finds missing, incomplete, or falsified records is a serious violation that may trigger follow-up inspections, fines, or licence action — regardless of whether the physical kitchen itself is clean.

## Electronic vs. Written Documentation

Both methods are widely used in Canadian commercial kitchens. Each has advantages:

| Feature | Written (Paper) | Electronic |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost** | Low — forms are inexpensive | Higher initial cost (software, hardware) |
| **Accessibility** | Always available without power or network | Requires device, power, and potentially internet access |
| **Tamper resistance** | Vulnerable to alteration; corrections visible | Audit trail; edits logged; timestamps automatic |
| **Alarm capability** | None | Can send alerts when critical limits are exceeded |
| **Storage** | Physical space required; risk of damage/loss | Cloud storage; easily searched and backed up |
| **Regulatory acceptability** | Universally accepted | Generally accepted; verify electronic records meet regulatory requirements for your jurisdiction |
| **Continuity** | No system failure risk | Requires backup and recovery planning |

> **Operational Recommendation:** Whether using paper or electronic systems, the key requirements are the same — records must be complete, accurate, timely, legible, retained for the required period, and accessible during inspections. A well-maintained paper system outperforms a poorly-implemented digital one.

## Common Mistakes in Kitchen Documentation

| Mistake | Risk | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Filling in temperature logs without taking measurements ("pencil whipping") | Fabrication of records; no actual food safety monitoring; serious regulatory and legal violation | Every entry must represent an actual measurement; use calibrated thermometers; record what you measure |
| Using correction fluid (white-out) on official records | Appears to conceal original entries; undermines trust in record authenticity | Single line through error, correction beside it, initials and date |
| Separating shellfish tag from container before product is fully used | Loss of traceability; violation of shellfish sanitation requirements | Tag stays with container until last product consumed |
| Storing SDS binder in a locked office | Workers cannot access safety information during their shift; WHMIS violation | SDS must be accessible to workers at all times, at all times |
| Filing a corrective action of "refridge temp fixed" with no detail | Insufficient documentation; what action was taken? What happened to the food in the unit? | Corrective action must state: action taken (service call made/product moved), product disposition (product transferred to other unit/product discarded), person responsible, outcome |
| Accumulating weeks of logs before filing | Risk of loss; difficulty retrieving records during an unannounced inspection | File at end of each shift or each day; maintain current binder |
| Not obtaining SDS for newly purchased cleaning products | WHMIS violation; workers exposed to unknown hazards | Request SDS at time of purchase; do not use product until SDS is on file |
| Ignoring apprenticeship hours documentation | Loss of approved training hours; inability to challenge Red Seal exam | Record and have hours signed off at regular intervals (monthly minimum); do not wait until end of apprenticeship |
| Completing banquet orders without verifying dietary requirements | Serving an allergen to a guest who declared a restriction; potential anaphylaxis | Always cross-check dietary requirements before production; confirm with event coordinator if ambiguous |

## Memory Aids for Apprentices

**"STAR" — The Four Steps for Any Document**
> **S**ecure (keep it with the product or in its designated location)
> **T**rack (record the required information completely at the time of the event)
> **A**ct (take corrective action if a critical limit is not met; document the action)
> **R**etain (file and keep for the required period)

**The SDS Sections You Must Know Cold**
> In a kitchen emergency, you need three sections immediately:
> - **Section 2:** What are the hazards?
> - **Section 4:** What first aid do I give right now?
> - **Section 7:** How should this have been stored to prevent this?

**"SHELLS" — Shellfish Tag Essentials**
> **S**pecies | **H**arvester ID | **E**area (harvest area) | **L**ot date | **L**ength of retention (90 days) | **S**tay with the product

**WHMIS Labels: Signal Words**
> - **DANGER** = more severe hazard
> - **WARNING** = less severe hazard
> When you see DANGER on a label, read the SDS before using.

**The Temperature Log Check**
> For every entry ask: *Date? Time? What? Reading? In range? If not — what did I do?*
> If any of these is missing, the entry is incomplete.

**Never White-Out**
> "One line, write beside, initial, date." This is how professionals correct errors in legal records.

## Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge

This section summarizes the most commonly tested concepts from this subtask for Red Seal examination preparation.

### Critical Regulatory Facts

| Fact | Regulatory Context |
|---|---|
| Shellfish tags must remain with product until last piece is consumed, then retained (commonly 90 days) | Canadian Shellfish Sanitation Program (CSSP); SFCR; verify with provincial authority |
| SDS must be accessible to all workers at all times during their shift | WHMIS 2015 / Hazardous Products Act and Regulations |
| An SDS contains 16 standardized sections under the GHS format | WHMIS 2015 alignment with GHS |
| Workplace labels are required when decanting hazardous products into secondary containers | WHMIS 2015 |
| HACCP documentation is legally required for federally registered food establishments | SFCR, administered by CFIA |
| Workplace injuries must be reported and documented under provincial OH&S legislation | Provincial OH&S Acts; WCB requirements |

### Likely Exam Questions and Concepts

1. **What is a shellfish tag and why must it be retained?**
   → A traceability document that accompanies every container of live bivalve shellfish, enabling health authorities to trace product back to the harvest area in the event of a recall or illness outbreak. Shellfish can harbour pathogens (Vibrio, norovirus) and biotoxins from their growing water; the CSSP monitors growing areas, and tags are the link between the growing area and the point of service.

2. **What does SDS stand for and under which Canadian regulation are they required?**
   → Safety Data Sheet. Required under WHMIS 2015, implemented through the Hazardous Products Act and Hazardous Products Regulations (federal), and corresponding provincial OH&S legislation.

3. **Name four sections of an SDS and their content.**
   → Any four from: Section 1 (identification), Section 2 (hazard identification), Section 4 (first aid measures), Section 7 (handling and storage), Section 8 (exposure controls/PPE), Section 16 (other information including revision date) — and the others.

4. **What information must a HACCP temperature log entry include?**
   → Date, time, product or equipment monitored, temperature value, employee identification (initials or name), corrective action if critical limit was not met.

5. **What is the correct method for correcting a documentation error in an official record?**
   → Draw a single line through the error so the original is still readable, write the correction beside it, initial, and date. Never use correction fluid (white-out) on legal or regulatory records.

6. **Name five documents listed in the Range of Variables for B-5.02.**
   → Shellfish tags, SDS, temperature logs, waste logs, sanitizer strength logs, recipe files, job hazard analysis, prep list, banquet orders, OH&S documents, cleaning list (any five)

7. **What are the three fundamental worker rights established by Canadian OH&S legislation?**
   → The right to know (about hazards in the workplace), the right to participate (in health and safety activities), the right to refuse (unsafe work without reprisal)

8. **What does WHMIS stand for and when was it last updated?**
   → Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System. Last major update was WHMIS 2015, which aligned Canada's system with the Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals (GHS).

9. **What are the two signal words on a WHMIS label and what do they indicate?**
   → DANGER (more severe hazard) and WARNING (less severe hazard)

10. **What is a workplace label and when is it required under WHMIS?**
    → A workplace label is required whenever a hazardous product is transferred from its original supplier container into another container (secondary container) for use in the workplace. It must at minimum identify the product and provide applicable safety information.

11. **Name three types of temperature logs maintained in a commercial kitchen and their critical limits.**
    → Refrigerator/walk-in cooler log (≤4°C); hot-holding log (≥60°C); re-thermalization log (≥74°C within 2 hours); freezer log (≤-18°C); cooking CCP log (typically ≥74°C for most cooked foods) — any three with correct critical limits

12. **What is the CSSP and which federal agencies administer it?**
    → The Canadian Shellfish Sanitation Program. Administered jointly by the Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA), Environment and Climate Change Canada (ECCC), and Fisheries and Oceans Canada (DFO). It monitors shellfish growing areas and classifies them as approved, conditionally approved, restricted, or closed based on water quality and biotoxin testing.

13. **What is the Internal Responsibility System (IRS) and how does documentation relate to it?**
    → The IRS is the foundational principle of Canadian OH&S law — that employers and workers share responsibility for workplace safety. Completing accurate temperature logs, maintaining SDS files, and reporting hazards are worker contributions to the IRS. Documentation is the evidence that both parties are meeting their shared responsibilities.
