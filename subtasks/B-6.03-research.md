# B-6.03 | Stores Products — Enhanced Learning Resource

**Major Work Activity:** B — Common Occupational Skills
**Task:** B-6 | Manages Products

> **What this subtask is about:** Storage is where most commercial kitchen food safety failures originate. A product that arrives in perfect condition at receiving can become unsafe or unusable within 24 hours if stored at the wrong temperature, in the wrong container, in the wrong location, without a date label, or next to an incompatible product. Conversely, well-managed storage extends shelf life, prevents waste, protects against cross-contamination, and maintains the quality that justifies the purchase cost. This document enriches the Red Seal competency with the food science, regulatory framework, container knowledge, and operational systems an apprentice needs to manage storage as a professional.

---

## Why Storage Is a Food Safety System — Not Just Organization

Storage management is a continuous food safety function that runs 24 hours a day, 7 days a week — including when the kitchen is closed. The bacteria growing on improperly stored product don't stop multiplying because the kitchen isn't open.

**The three storage-related food safety failures that cause the most foodborne illness:**

1. **Temperature abuse in storage** — refrigerators holding above 4°C; product placed in warm areas of the walk-in; overcrowded units blocking air circulation; door gaskets failing
2. **Cross-contamination** — raw protein dripping onto ready-to-eat (RTE) food; allergen-containing products contaminating allergen-free preparations; chemical storage next to food
3. **Rotation failure** — oldest product not used first; spoiled or expired product remaining in service; date labels missing or ignored

All three are preventable through the skills and knowledge in this subtask.

**Performance Criteria:** Ensure clean, organized and secure storage area

**Evidence of Attainment:** Storage area is clean, organized and secure according to manufacturers' specifications, jurisdictional regulations and company policies

---

#### Deep Dive: Clean, Organized, and Secure Storage

**Cleanliness of Storage Areas**

A storage area that is not clean is not safe. Dirt, spills, and organic residue in storage areas:
- Provide nutrients for bacterial and mold growth
- Attract and harbor pests (insects, rodents)
- Can contaminate food through contact or drip
- Create conditions for cross-contamination between stored products

**Cleaning Requirements by Storage Area**

| Storage Area | Cleaning Frequency | Key Cleaning Tasks |
|---|---|---|
| **Walk-in cooler / refrigerator** | Daily wipe-down of surfaces; weekly or biweekly deep clean | Wipe shelves and walls; clean door gaskets (primary site of mold buildup); sweep and mop floor; check for spills at product level; deodorize drain; clean thermometer |
| **Freezer** | Monthly deep clean or as needed; defrost when ice buildup exceeds 6mm | Remove all product; defrost; clean interior; clean door gaskets; sanitize; restore product with FIFO |
| **Dry storage** | Weekly sweep and mop; wipe shelving monthly; inspect continuously | Sweep floor; mop; wipe shelves; remove expired product; inspect all product for pest evidence |
| **Chemical storage** | Clean immediately on any spill; weekly inspection | Check containment for leaks; wipe surfaces; verify labels are intact |

**Door Gaskets — The Hidden Contamination Point**

Refrigerator and freezer door gaskets (the rubber seals around the door frame) are one of the most frequently neglected food safety surfaces in a commercial kitchen. They:
- Collect condensation, food debris, and organic material in their folds
- Are warm enough (from room temperature contact) to allow mold and bacteria to grow
- When a door is opened and closed, gaskets flex — releasing microorganisms into the refrigerator air and onto stored product

A gasket that is visibly moldy, torn, or not sealing properly must be cleaned and sanitized immediately, or replaced. A failing gasket also allows warm air infiltration, raising the refrigerator's internal temperature.

> **Common Industry Practice:** Clean door gaskets during every daily walk-in clean. A stiff brush and sanitizer solution removes buildup from the folds. If a gasket is physically torn or no longer sealing, the refrigerator's temperature compliance is compromised — replace it.

**Organization**

A well-organized storage area prevents accidental contamination, supports FIFO rotation, and reduces the risk of allergenic ingredients coming into contact with allergen-free products.

**Organization principles:**

- **Designated locations for every product category:** Each product type (proteins, dairy, produce, cooked items, dry goods) has a defined location; staff can always find and return product correctly without improvisation
- **Labelled shelves:** Shelf labels state what goes where; reduces errors when FIFO rotation is done
- **Clear sight lines:** Never stack product so high that items at the back are invisible; concealed product is forgotten product
- **15 cm (6 inch) off the floor rule:** All food and food storage containers must be stored at least 15 cm above the floor in most Canadian provincial food premises regulations. This allows floor cleaning and prevents pest access. Shelving should be designed to meet this requirement — not temporary workarounds with product directly on the floor.
- **Walk-in cooler air circulation:** Do not block air circulation vents or pack the walk-in so tightly that cold air cannot flow around all items. Poor air circulation creates warm pockets where temperatures exceed 4°C — often without the thermometer reflecting this, as it only reads at the sensor location

**Security**

Chemical storage must be secured to prevent unauthorized access, especially in operations where the public, delivery personnel, or unsupervised staff may access storage areas:
- Locked cabinet for cleaning chemicals and sanitizers
- Secured access to areas with allergen-containing bulk ingredients
- Inventory controls to detect unexplained losses

#### Deep Dive: Food Storage Containers

Container selection directly affects food safety, food quality, and operational efficiency. The wrong container can introduce chemical contamination, allow bacterial growth, cause flavour transfer, or allow physical contamination.

**Food-Grade Plastic**

*What "food-grade" means:* A food-grade plastic container is manufactured from materials that will not migrate harmful chemicals into food under normal use conditions. Food-grade plastics are tested and certified to meet standards such as those set by NSF International (NSF/ANSI 2 — Food Equipment) or equivalent.

*Common food-grade plastics in commercial kitchens:*

| Plastic Type | Resin Code | Characteristics | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **HDPE** (High-Density Polyethylene) | 2 | Rigid, opaque; excellent chemical resistance; dishwasher safe | Buckets, cutting boards, milk jugs |
| **LDPE** (Low-Density Polyethylene) | 4 | Flexible; used for bags and film | Cling wrap, produce bags, food storage bags |
| **PP** (Polypropylene) | 5 | Rigid; excellent heat resistance (up to ~120°C); dishwasher safe; microwave safe | Food containers, deli containers, portion cups |
| **PET / PETE** (Polyethylene Terephthalate) | 1 | Clear, lightweight; single-use intended; not designed for repeated washing | Beverage bottles, single-use deli containers |
| **PC** (Polycarbonate) | 7 | Clear, very durable; was widely used for institutional food service containers | Being phased out due to BPA concerns; verify product is BPA-free if using |

*What disqualifies a plastic container for food storage:*
- Cracks, deep scratches, or chips — these harbour bacteria in recesses that cannot be reached by cleaning and sanitizing
- Discolouration or staining that will not clean out — indicates chemical or biological residue
- Originally used for non-food purposes (chemical containers, paint buckets) — chemical residue may remain even after washing
- No food-grade designation or markings

> **Regulatory/Safety Note:** Using a container that was previously used for chemicals to store food is a serious food contamination risk and a potential health regulation violation. In commercial kitchens, food storage containers must be food-grade and used exclusively for food.

**Stainless Steel**

Stainless steel (typically Grade 304 or 316 food-service grade) is the professional kitchen standard for long-term food storage containers for a reason:
- **Non-porous:** No scratches deep enough to harbour bacteria at a microscopic level; smooth surface is highly cleanable
- **Non-reactive:** Does not react with acidic foods (tomatoes, citrus, vinegars, wine reductions); does not impart flavour or metallic taste
- **Durable:** Lasts far longer than plastic with proper care
- **Temperature range:** Safe for use from freezing through oven temperatures (no plastic fumes or deformation)
- **Induction compatible:** Can be used on induction burners or placed in ovens

*Stainless steel hotel pan (gastronorm) sizes — the commercial kitchen standard:*

| Size | Approximate Dimensions | Depth Options | Common Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1/1 (full)** | 530 × 325 mm | 65, 100, 150, 200 mm | Main batch storage; hot-holding |
| **1/2 (half)** | 265 × 325 mm | 65, 100, 150 mm | Mid-size batches; steam table inserts |
| **1/3 (third)** | 176 × 325 mm | 65, 100, 150 mm | Smaller batches; cold prep table |
| **1/4 (quarter)** | 265 × 162 mm | 65, 100 mm | Garnishes; small mise en place |
| **1/6 (sixth)** | 176 × 162 mm | 65, 100 mm | Very small batches; sauces; condiments |
| **1/9 (ninth)** | 176 × 108 mm | 65 mm | Tiny quantities; individual service |

Using standardized gastronorm sizes across a kitchen enables containers, lids, and equipment (fridges, steam tables, combi-ovens) to be fully compatible — a major operational efficiency advantage.

**Non-Reactive Containers**

The concept of "non-reactive" refers to the container not chemically interacting with the food stored in it. This is particularly important for acidic foods.

| Container Material | Reactive with Acids? | Imparts Flavour? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stainless steel (food grade) | No | No | The professional standard |
| Food-grade plastic (HDPE, PP) | No | Possible if stained | Odour absorption can be a problem |
| Glass | No | No | Non-reactive, but heavy and breakage risk |
| Ceramic / glazed earthenware | No (if food-safe glaze) | No | Verify glaze is lead-free and food-safe |
| Uncoated aluminium | **Yes** | **Yes** — metallic taste | Reacts with acids (tomatoes, citrus, vinegar); produces metallic off-flavour |
| Uncoated copper | **Yes** | **Yes** | Toxic if used with acidic foods |
| Cast iron | Mildly with acids | **Yes** — iron flavour | Avoid storing acidic foods long-term in cast iron |

> **Practical example:** Storing tomato-based sauce overnight in an uncoated aluminium hotel pan will produce a noticeable metallic taste by the next morning. Use stainless steel or food-grade plastic for all acidic product storage.

**Vacuum-Packed Storage**

Vacuum packaging removes oxygen from around the food, significantly extending shelf life by:
- Slowing oxidation (a primary cause of rancidity in fats and colour change in proteins)
- Inhibiting aerobic bacterial growth (bacteria that require oxygen)
- Reducing desiccation (moisture loss) and freezer burn

*Food safety warning for vacuum storage:*

> **Critical Food Safety Warning — Anaerobic Conditions and *Clostridium botulinum*:** Vacuum packaging creates anaerobic (oxygen-free) conditions. *Clostridium botulinum* — the organism producing botulinum toxin — is an **anaerobic** bacterium that thrives in exactly these conditions. Vacuum-packed products **must be refrigerated at ≤4°C** (or frozen) to prevent *C. botulinum* growth. Never vacuum-pack raw garlic in oil and store at room temperature — this is a well-documented source of botulism outbreaks. Vacuum packaging is not a preservation method on its own; it must be combined with temperature control.

Vacuum-packed products under refrigeration still have a defined shelf life. Extended shelf life claims for vacuum packaging are only valid when:
- Proper refrigeration is maintained throughout
- The product is not from a spore-forming pathogen risk category without additional controls (cooking, pasteurization, or HACCP-validated process)

**Eco-Friendly Containers**

Compostable and sustainable containers are increasingly used in foodservice operations:
- **PLA (polylactic acid):** Bioplastic derived from corn starch or sugarcane; compostable in industrial composting facilities; not suitable for high-heat applications (deforms above 50–60°C)
- **Bagasse containers:** Made from sugarcane fibre; compostable; good for hot food but limited reuse durability
- **Reusable food-safe alternatives:** Durable containers that reduce single-use waste

> **Important:** "Eco-friendly" and "food-safe" are independent certifications. Always verify that a container is specifically designated food-safe for its intended use, regardless of its environmental credentials.

#### Deep Dive: Recognizing and Storing Hazardous Products

**Identifying Hazardous Products**

Under WHMIS 2015, a hazardous product is one that meets the classification criteria defined in the Hazardous Products Regulations. In a commercial kitchen, hazardous products typically include:

- Concentrated chlorine-based sanitizers and bleach solutions
- Quaternary ammonium (quat) sanitizers
- Oven and grill cleaners (highly alkaline; caustic)
- Degreasers
- Descaling agents (acid-based; e.g., phosphoric acid solutions)
- Floor cleaners and degreasers
- Pest control chemicals
- CO₂ cylinders (pressure hazard)
- Refrigerants (handled only by certified HVAC technicians; not typically managed by kitchen staff)

WHMIS-classified products are identified by:
- **Hazard pictograms** on the supplier label (skull and crossbones for acute toxicity; corrosion for caustics/acids; health hazard for carcinogens/sensitizers; exclamation mark for irritants)
- **Signal words:** DANGER (more severe) or WARNING (less severe)
- The existence of an **SDS** for the product

**Chemical Incompatibilities — The Most Critical Safety Rule in Chemical Storage**

Storing incompatible chemicals together creates a risk of violent reaction, toxic gas production, or fire if containers leak or break.

| Chemicals Combined | Hazardous Reaction | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Chlorine bleach + ammonia-based cleaners** | Produces chloramine gases (NH₂Cl, NHCl₂, NCl₃) | Respiratory damage; toxic fumes; can be fatal in enclosed spaces |
| **Chlorine bleach + acid-based cleaners (descalers, vinegar)** | Releases chlorine gas (Cl₂) | Highly toxic; irritates and damages respiratory tract and eyes |
| **Chlorine bleach + hydrogen peroxide** | Accelerated decomposition; potential violent reaction | Oxygen release; foaming; possible container rupture |
| **Concentrated nitric acid + organic solvents** | Fire or explosion risk | Severe fire hazard |

> **Practical rule for kitchen chemical storage:** Store bleach-based (chlorine) products and acid-based products (descalers, some drain cleaners, lime removers) in completely separate locations — different shelves, different cabinets, or different rooms. Label everything clearly. Never mix chemicals.

**Safe Chemical Storage Requirements**

Under WHMIS 2015 and provincial OH&S legislation, employers must:
- Ensure hazardous products are stored in accordance with the **SDS Section 7** (Handling and Storage) for that product
- Provide **secondary containment** for liquid chemicals (trays or shelving with raised edges to contain spills)
- Ensure chemical storage is **separated from food storage** — not in the same room, not above food or food equipment
- Ensure containers are **properly labelled** at all times
- Maintain **current SDS** accessible to workers
- Ensure workers are **trained** on the chemicals they work with

> **Regulatory Requirement:** Under most Canadian provincial food premises regulations, chemicals must be stored in a location completely separate from food storage. This is not a preference — it is a legal requirement. Chemical spills or vapour contamination of stored food require disposal of all affected product.

### B-6.03.04P
**Performance Criteria:** Store products

**Evidence of Attainment:** Products are stored in designated area according to jurisdictional regulations and company policies

#### Deep Dive: Storage Areas and Food Safety Requirements

**Refrigerated Storage (≤4°C)**

*Why 4°C is the critical threshold:*

At 4°C, the growth of most common foodborne pathogens is dramatically slowed. *Salmonella*, *E. coli* O157:H7, *Campylobacter*, and *Staphylococcus aureus* grow at a very slow rate at 4°C compared to their rates in the danger zone. This buys time — not indefinitely, but enough to safely manage inventory within defined shelf life windows.

*The important exception — Listeria monocytogenes:*

> **Critical Knowledge: *Listeria monocytogenes* Grows at Refrigerator Temperatures**

*Listeria monocytogenes* is unique among major foodborne pathogens because it can grow at **0°C to 45°C**, with slow but real growth occurring even at 2–4°C. This means:
- Refrigeration does not stop *Listeria* growth — it only slows it
- Shelf life management and rotation are food safety functions, not just quality functions
- Ready-to-eat foods that support *Listeria* growth (deli meats, soft cheeses, smoked fish) must be consumed within defined time limits **even when properly refrigerated**
- *Listeria* is particularly dangerous for pregnant women, elderly, immunocompromised individuals, and newborns

*Practical implication:* A deli meat product properly refrigerated at 4°C will still accumulate *Listeria* over its shelf life. This is why provincial regulations typically set maximum times for refrigerated RTE products and why FIFO rotation is a food safety obligation, not just an organizational preference.

**Shelf Life Guidelines for Refrigerated Storage (Common Industry Practice)**

> **Note:** These are widely used commercial kitchen reference points, not regulatory mandates. Actual shelf life depends on the specific product, initial microbial load, packaging, and exact refrigerator temperature. Always follow manufacturer's stated use-by dates when available, and provincial authority guidance.

| Product | Refrigerated Shelf Life (≤4°C) |
|---|---|
| Fresh beef, pork, lamb (whole cuts) | 3–5 days |
| Fresh ground meat | 1–2 days |
| Fresh poultry (whole or parts) | 1–3 days |
| Fresh fish | 1–2 days |
| Shellfish (live, in shell) | 1–2 days |
| Cooked meat and poultry | 3–4 days |
| Cooked fish | 1–2 days |
| Soups and stews (cooked) | 3–4 days |
| Stocks and broths | 3–4 days (or freeze) |
| Hard-boiled eggs | 1 week (in shell) |
| Fresh pasta (house-made) | 2–3 days |
| Soft cheeses | 1–2 weeks (check label) |
| Hard cheeses (opened) | 3–4 weeks |
| Cream and milk (opened) | Per label; typically 5–10 days |
| Butter (unsalted, opened) | 1–2 weeks (quality decline) |

**Freezer Storage (≤-18°C)**

*What happens to bacteria in the freezer:*

Freezing does not kill bacteria — it makes them **dormant**. When the product is thawed, any bacteria present before freezing revive and resume growth. This is why:
- Thawing methods matter enormously (never room temperature)
- Food that was frozen near the end of its refrigerator shelf life still has a reduced shelf life after thawing
- Freezing does not "reset" a product's safety clock

**Freezer Burn: A Quality (Not Safety) Issue**

Freezer burn occurs when moisture sublimes (transitions directly from ice to vapour) from the surface of a frozen food, leaving behind a dehydrated, oxidized layer. It appears as:
- White or grey opaque patches on the surface
- A dry, leathery texture in affected areas
- Off-flavours in severely affected areas

*Causes:*
- Improper packaging (not airtight; moisture can escape)
- Temperature fluctuations (partial thaw-refreeze cycles accelerate sublimation)
- Extended freezer storage beyond recommended times

*Prevention:*
- Wrap product tightly in airtight, moisture-vapour-resistant packaging
- Remove as much air as possible from the package before freezing (vacuum packing is optimal)
- Maintain consistent freezer temperature; minimize door opening
- Observe maximum frozen storage times

**Frozen Shelf Life Reference (Common Industry Practice)**

| Product | Recommended Maximum Frozen Storage |
|---|---|
| Beef (whole cuts) | 6–12 months |
| Ground beef | 3–4 months |
| Poultry (whole) | 12 months |
| Poultry (pieces) | 9 months |
| Fish (fatty: salmon, mackerel) | 2–3 months |
| Fish (lean: cod, halibut) | 6–8 months |
| Shrimp, scallops | 3–6 months |
| Soups and stews | 2–3 months |
| Bread and baked goods | 2–3 months |
| Ice cream and frozen desserts | 2–4 months |

**Dry Storage (10–21°C)**

*The four enemies of dry goods:*

1. **Heat** — temperatures above 21°C accelerate chemical reactions in food (rancidity, vitamin degradation, colour change, mold growth); dry storage should be away from ovens, steam pipes, and sun-exposed walls
2. **Moisture and humidity** — above 60% relative humidity, dry goods absorb moisture; this softens crackers and cookies, clumps hygroscopic ingredients (salt, sugar, baking powder), and encourages mold growth on grains and spices
3. **Light** — UV light degrades fats (causes rancidity), destroys certain vitamins (riboflavin, vitamins A and C), and causes colour change in sensitive products; dry storage should be shaded or use opaque containers
4. **Pests** — the primary vulnerability of dry storage; insects (pantry beetles, flour moths, weevils) and rodents can contaminate and destroy dry goods silently

*Dry storage best practices:*
- Store all dry goods in sealed containers after opening (original packaging rarely reseals adequately)
- Clean up spills immediately — organic matter on dry storage floors attracts pests
- Remove corrugated cardboard immediately — it is a harborage for cockroaches and their eggs
- Inspect incoming dry goods for pest evidence before putting away
- Space for cleaning behind and under shelves — pests cannot hide if there is no inaccessible space

**Cold-Sensitive Produce Storage (Ambient / 10–15°C)**

Some produce is damaged by standard refrigerator temperatures (≤4°C). Storing these items at 4°C causes **chilling injury**: cell membrane damage, loss of flavour, textural breakdown, and surface pitting/blackening. These items should be stored at ambient temperatures or in a dedicated slightly warmer cooler.

| Item | Optimal Storage Temperature | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Bananas | 13–15°C | Cell damage below 13°C causes blackening of skin and flavour loss |
| Tomatoes | 10–13°C | Below 10°C causes cell breakdown; loss of lycopene flavour compounds |
| Avocados (ripe) | 7–10°C | Cold slows ripening; use soon after reaching ripeness |
| Unripe avocados | 15–18°C | Allow to ripen at room temperature first |
| Potatoes | 7–10°C | Cold converts starches to sugars (undesirable sweetness; causes Maillard browning at lower temperatures during frying) |
| Winter squash, pumpkins | 10–13°C | Chilling injury causes soft spots and pitting |
| Mangoes, papayas | 13°C | Tropical fruits damaged by cold |
| Garlic, onions, shallots (whole) | Cool, dry, ventilated, 10–18°C | Not refrigerated; humidity causes rotting and sprouting |
| Bread | 18–21°C (or freeze) | Refrigerator temperatures (4°C) actually accelerate staling (retrogradation of starch) |

**Ethylene Gas and Produce Storage**

Ethylene is a naturally occurring plant hormone that triggers and accelerates ripening in fruit. Some produce produces significant amounts of ethylene; some is highly sensitive to it.

*High ethylene producers (should be stored away from sensitive items):*
- Apples, pears, bananas, avocados, tomatoes, cantaloupe

*Ethylene-sensitive items (avoid storing near high producers):*
- Leafy greens (lettuce, spinach, kale) — accelerated yellowing and wilt
- Broccoli, cauliflower — yellowing
- Carrots — bitterness from isocoumarins triggered by ethylene exposure
- Cucumbers — yellowing and softening
- Unripe berries — premature ripening

> **Operational Tip:** Do not store apples or bananas in the walk-in cooler with leafy greens or delicate vegetables. Ethylene exposure significantly shortens their usable shelf life.

#### Deep Dive: FIFO, Dating, and Labeling

**FIFO (First In, First Out)**

FIFO is the foundational principle of food inventory rotation. Its rule is simple and absolute: the **oldest product is always used first**. Newer product is placed behind or below existing stock so that the oldest product is always at the front and easiest to access.

*Why FIFO is a food safety principle, not just an organization principle:*

When FIFO is not followed:
- Newer product gets used repeatedly while older product remains at the back
- Older product eventually exceeds its shelf life; bacteria have had more time to accumulate
- Particularly dangerous for *Listeria*-supporting products (deli meats, soft cheeses, RTE items) where bacterial loads build over the shelf life even under refrigeration
- Food cost impact: older product that expires unused represents direct food waste and financial loss

**FIFO Implementation by Storage Area**

| Storage Area | FIFO Method |
|---|---|
| **Walk-in cooler shelves** | New stock goes **behind** existing stock; reach from the front |
| **Reach-in refrigerator** | New stock pushed to the **back**; existing stock moves to the **front** |
| **Dry shelving** | New stock placed **behind** older product; use from the front |
| **Freezer** | Date label all items; rotate newest behind oldest; use dated items in sequence; de-ice labels periodically to confirm dates |
| **Prep containers in service** | Use dated prep containers from the oldest rotation label first |

**Dating and Labeling Standards**

Every item placed into storage (other than commercially sealed products in original, legible packaging) should have a label. Minimum label content:

1. **Product name** — what is in the container (especially important when product is decanted from original packaging)
2. **Date prepared or date received** — when the product was produced or when it arrived
3. **Use-by date** — the calculated last safe date to use the product (= preparation date + shelf life days)
4. **Initials** — who prepared or received the product (accountability; allows follow-up questions)
5. **Allergen information** — if the product contains priority allergens not visually obvious from the label

*Optional but valuable additions:*
- Quantity (for batch items)
- Station or recipe the item belongs to
- Storage temperature requirement

**Rotation Label (Day Dot) Systems**

Many commercial kitchens use colour-coded **rotation labels** (commonly called "day dots"):
- One colour assigned to each day of the week (e.g., Monday = blue, Tuesday = red, etc.)
- Applied when the product is prepped or opened
- Staff immediately know when a product was made without reading a date
- Simplifies FIFO enforcement: only one colour should be in use for each product at a time; if two colours are present, the older colour is used first

*Limitation:* Day dot systems only communicate the day of preparation, not the date. In operations using a 7-day rotation, a Monday-blue label could be from this Monday or last Monday. Adding the full date is best practice.

**Calculating Use-By Dates**

```
Use-by date = Preparation date + Maximum refrigerator shelf life

Example:
  Chicken stock prepared: Tuesday, May 20
  Refrigerated shelf life (industry reference): 3–4 days
  Use-by date: Friday or Saturday, May 23–24
  Label should show: "Chicken stock — 05/20 — Use by 05/23"
```

### B-6.03.06P
**Performance Criteria:** Unpack and break down received products

**Evidence of Attainment:** Received products are unpacked and broken down in size as required and storage containers are used according to jurisdictional regulations

---

#### Deep Dive: Unpacking, Decanting, and Portioning

**Why Unpacking and Breaking Down Is a Food Safety Step**

When products arrive in their original packaging (cardboard boxes, transport crates), the packaging itself can be a contamination source:
- Corrugated cardboard harbors cockroaches, cockroach eggs, and other pests; bringing cardboard into food storage introduces pests into the kitchen
- Outer packaging surfaces have been handled by multiple people and contaminated by distribution center environments
- Product needs to be transferred to clean, food-grade containers for proper storage organization, FIFO labeling, and temperature management

**The Unpacking Process**

```
1. At the receiving dock/designated unpacking area:
   - Open cases and inspect product (per B-6.02)

2. Transfer product to food-grade storage containers:
   - Remove original cardboard packaging; do not bring into food storage
   - For fresh proteins: if vacuum-packed, leave in vacuum pack until use
     (vacuum packaging protects product; opening it starts the clock)
   - For produce: remove from transport crates; discard any damaged units;
     place into clean food-grade containers or cooler bins
   - For dry goods: inspect packaging; if intact, shelf-store in original
     packaging; if opened or torn, transfer to sealed food-grade containers

3. Break down into working quantities:
   - Large proteins (whole primals, bulk packs): portion for service if
     specification requires; place portions in dated containers
   - Large containers of dry goods (25 kg flour, 10 kg sugar): transfer
     working quantity into smaller, labelled, sealed containers for daily
     use; bulk stored separately with date label

4. Label and date immediately:
   - Apply rotation label to every container at the point of transfer
   - Never put an unlabeled container into storage

5. Store by priority:
   - Frozen first; fresh protein second; dairy; produce; dry goods
   - Follow correct storage order in refrigerators (per B-6.02)
```

**Maintaining Labels When Decanting**

When product is transferred from its original commercial packaging into an in-house container:
- The **allergen information** from the original label must be carried forward onto the in-house label
- The **date of production or opening** must be documented
- If the original product had a regulatory statement (e.g., a CFIA-inspected product label), the lot code or traceability information should be noted in case of a recall

**Proper Thawing Methods (Relevant to Storage Management)**

Frozen products that need to be prepared in advance require proper thawing. Thawing is part of the storage-to-production transition.

| Method | Safe? | Notes | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Refrigerator thawing** | **Yes — Safest** | ≤4°C throughout; allows full thaw over 24–48 hours; product can be refrigerated for 1–2 days after thawing before cooking | Large proteins (roasts, whole poultry); advance planning required |
| **Cold running water** | **Yes** | Fully submerged under running water ≤21°C; quick (1–2 hours for smaller items); cook immediately after thawing | Smaller proteins, fish portions; when time is limited |
| **Microwave** | **Yes — with conditions** | Creates hot spots; outside may begin cooking while centre thaws; must cook immediately after | Individual portions only; when cooking immediately |
| **As part of cooking** | **Yes — with conditions** | Cooking from frozen is safe if target internal temperature is reached; requires longer cooking time | Thin items (fish fillets, burger patties); verify internal temp |
| **Room temperature** | **NEVER** | Product sits in danger zone (4°C–60°C) throughout thawing; bacterial growth rapid | **Food safety violation in commercial kitchens** |

> **Regulatory context:** Room-temperature thawing is prohibited under most Canadian provincial food premises regulations. It is one of the most common causes of foodborne illness linked to commercial kitchens. Any cook observed thawing product on the counter should be immediately corrected.

#### Deep Dive: Cross-Contamination in Storage

**Types of Cross-Contamination in Storage**

| Type | How It Happens | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Drip contamination** | Raw protein juice leaks from container or drips from above onto food below | Store raw protein in covered, leak-proof containers; follow top-to-bottom storage order (see B-6.02 reference) |
| **Direct contact contamination** | Raw protein touches RTE food; unwashed produce contacts cooked product | Strict separation by shelf level and designated containers for each category |
| **Allergen cross-contact** | Allergen-containing product contacts allergen-free product; shared equipment; improperly cleaned containers | Dedicated, labelled containers for allergen-containing items; clean and sanitize between uses; separate storage location |
| **Chemical cross-contamination** | Cleaning chemical spills onto stored food; chemical vapours absorbed by nearby food | Complete physical separation of chemicals from food storage |
| **Odour transfer** | Strongly aromatic product (onions, cut garlic, pungent cheeses, durian) flavours nearby delicate items | Cover all products; store highly aromatic items in separate area or container |
| **Pest transmission** | Rodent or insect contact with stored product | Pest management program; eliminate harborage (cardboard); seal containers |

**Inventory Control Methods**

Effective inventory control ensures that the operation always has what it needs, minimizes waste, and maintains accurate food cost data.

| Method | Description | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Par stock system** | Maintain defined minimum quantity of each product | Ordering trigger: when on-hand falls to par level, order to restore par |
| **Perpetual inventory** | Continuous running count of all additions and subtractions to inventory | Large operations with inventory management software; accurate in real time |
| **Periodic inventory** | Regular physical count of all stock (weekly or monthly) | Most restaurant operations; actual physical count corrects any errors in perpetual tracking |
| **FIFO rotation** | First In, First Out — oldest used first | Embedded in daily storage practice; reduces spoilage; supports food safety |
| **ABC analysis** | Classify items by value/volume: A = high value (order carefully, track closely); B = moderate; C = low value (order in bulk, track loosely) | Prioritizes where to apply the most careful inventory management effort |
| **Food cost reconciliation** | (Opening inventory + purchases − closing inventory) ÷ revenue × 100 = food cost % | Monthly; identifies discrepancies between theoretical and actual food cost |

#### Deep Dive: Storage Techniques by Product State

| Product State | Storage Technique | Key Safety Points |
|---|---|---|
| **Raw proteins (meat, poultry, seafood)** | Bottom shelves of refrigerator; sealed containers; maximum labeled shelf life; separate from RTE | Follow poultry→ground→whole beef→fish→RTE vertical order |
| **Cooked foods** | Above raw proteins; covered, labeled, dated; rapid cool before storing if just cooked | Must cool from 60°C→4°C via two-stage cooling before storing; do not place hot food in refrigerator uncooled |
| **Ready-to-eat (RTE) foods** | Top shelves only; fully sealed; labeled with use-by date; maximum 3–4 days for most items | *Listeria* risk: strict shelf life adherence; no exposure to raw protein at any point |
| **Dairy** | Sealed; ≤4°C; away from strongly aromatic items; FIFO rotation | Cream especially sensitive to odour pickup |
| **Fresh produce** | Separate from proteins; most at ≤4°C; exceptions at ambient (see cold-sensitive list above); not touching floor | Separate ethylene producers from sensitive items |
| **Dry goods** | Sealed containers; cool, dark, dry; 15 cm off floor; no pest evidence | Inspect regularly; discard any product with pest evidence |
| **Frozen items** | ≤-18°C; wrapped airtight; dated; FIFO | Prevent freezer burn; manage frozen shelf life |

**The Critical Rule: Never Store Hot Food Directly in a Refrigerator**

Hot food placed directly into a refrigerator raises the internal temperature of the entire unit, potentially bringing other stored food into the temperature danger zone. The correct procedure is **two-stage cooling**:

```
Stage 1: Cool from 60°C → 20°C within 2 hours
         Methods: ice bath, blast chiller, ice wand, shallow containers,
                  dividing into smaller batches

Stage 2: Transfer to refrigerator; cool from 20°C → 4°C within 4 hours
         Standard refrigerator is efficient at cooling food already near ambient

Total: Must reach 4°C within 6 hours of cooking
```

> **Why not just put it in the fridge hot?** A standard commercial refrigerator is designed to maintain cold food at temperature — not to rapidly cool hot food. A large pot of soup at 80°C in a walk-in cooler can take 12+ hours to reach 4°C at the centre, spending the entire time at dangerous temperatures while also raising the temperature of everything else in the unit. This is one of the most common commercial kitchen food safety violations.

#### Deep Dive: Regulatory Framework for Storage

**Common Regulatory Requirements in Canadian Food Premises Regulations**

While specific requirements vary by province and territory, Canadian food premises regulations commonly address the following storage requirements:

| Requirement | Common Standard | Regulatory Context |
|---|---|---|
| Refrigerated storage temperature | ≤4°C | Most provincial food premises regulations |
| Frozen storage temperature | ≤-18°C | Most provincial food premises regulations |
| Off-floor storage | Minimum 15 cm (6 inches) | Most provincial food premises regulations |
| Chemical separation from food | Complete physical separation required | WHMIS 2015; provincial food premises regulations |
| Food storage containers | Food-grade materials only | Provincial food premises regulations |
| FIFO rotation | Required practice; supported by labeling requirements | Best practice embedded in HACCP prerequisite programs |
| Room-temperature thawing | Prohibited | Most provincial food premises regulations |
| Swollen / damaged cans | Must not be used | Canadian Food and Drug Regulations (adulterated food prohibition) |
| Pest control | Active pest management program required | Provincial food premises regulations |

**HACCP Prerequisite Programs and Storage**

Under HACCP, storage management is addressed through **prerequisite programs (PRPs)** — foundational practices that maintain a hygienic environment:
- **Temperature control PRP:** Refrigerator and freezer temperature monitoring logs
- **Cleaning and sanitation PRP:** Cleaning schedules for storage areas
- **Pest management PRP:** Documentation of pest control program and inspections
- **FIFO / stock rotation PRP:** Labeling and dating procedures
- **Chemical control PRP:** SDS management; chemical storage separation

For federally regulated food establishments (under SFCR), these PRPs are formally documented and audited. For provincial-jurisdiction food service operations, the same practices are expected under food premises regulations and health inspections.

## Common Mistakes in Storage

| Mistake | Risk | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Placing hot food directly into the walk-in cooler | Raises temperature of entire cooler; brings stored food into TDZ; standard refrigerator cannot cool food quickly enough | Two-stage cooling: ice bath to ≤20°C first, then refrigerator to ≤4°C within 6 hours total |
| Not labeling and dating containers when decanting | FIFO impossible; shelf life tracking impossible; allergen information lost | Every container gets a label at the moment of transfer; no exceptions |
| Storing raw chicken above beef in the refrigerator | Drip contamination with *Salmonella/Campylobacter* onto product requiring lower cooking temperature | Poultry always at the bottom; raw protein always below RTE foods |
| Vacuum-packing garlic in oil and storing at room temperature | *C. botulinum* ideal growth conditions (anaerobic + ambient temperature = botulism risk) | Garlic in oil must be refrigerated at all times; discard after 1 week |
| Storing cleaning chemicals on the same shelf as or above food products | Chemical contamination of food; potential poisoning | Chemicals must be physically separated from all food storage; locked cabinet preferred |
| Storing bleach and acid-based cleaners together | Chlorine gas production if containers leak or are combined; serious respiratory hazard | Store acid-based and chlorine-based products in separate locations; never combine |
| Leaving corrugated cardboard in food storage | Cockroach harborage; introduces pests into kitchen | Remove all cardboard at the receiving dock; decant into food-grade containers |
| Storing tomatoes in the walk-in cooler | Chilling injury: cell breakdown, loss of lycopene flavour, texture deterioration | Store tomatoes at 10–13°C, not at 4°C |
| Storing bread in the refrigerator | Accelerated staling from retrogradation of starch; bread goes stale faster at 4°C than at room temperature | Store bread at ambient (18–21°C); freeze for longer-term storage |
| Using a scratched or cracked plastic container | Cracks and scratches harbor bacteria; cannot be adequately cleaned or sanitized | Discard any cracked or deeply scratched food storage containers |
| Room-temperature thawing of frozen protein | Product surface enters TDZ while centre remains frozen; rapid bacterial growth | Refrigerator thaw (safest) or cold running water; never room temperature |

## Memory Aids for Apprentices

**"COLD" — The Four Enemies of Dry Storage Quality**
> **C**reatures (pests) | **O**xygen/light | **L**iquid/moisture | **D**egree of heat
> Protect dry goods from all four and shelf life is maximized.

**The Two-Stage Cooling Rule**
> "**60 to 20 in 2; 20 to 4 in 4."**
> 60°C → 20°C within 2 hours. 20°C → 4°C within 4 hours. Total: 6 hours maximum.

**"Listeria Laughs at Your Fridge"**
> *Listeria monocytogenes* grows at 0–4°C. Refrigeration slows it but does not stop it. FIFO rotation and shelf life management for RTE foods are food safety actions, not just organization.

**Ethylene Producers and Sensitives**
> Producers (ripen others): Apples, Pears, Bananas, Avocados, Tomatoes → "**A P B A T**"
> Sensitives (affected by ethylene): Leafy greens, Broccoli, Carrots, Cucumbers
> Keep producers and sensitives separated in produce storage.

**FIFO Physical Rule**
> "**New goes behind; old comes first.**"
> In every storage format — shelves, fridges, freezers — newest product is always placed behind or below existing stock.

**Temperature Numbers for Storage**

```
≤-18°C  → Freezer (frozen storage)
≤4°C    → Refrigerator (cold storage)
7–10°C  → Potatoes
10–13°C → Tomatoes, bananas, tropical fruit
10–21°C → Dry storage
60°C    → Minimum hot-holding (not storage, but the other end of the scale)
```

**Chemical Incompatibility Alert**
> "Bleach + Ammonia = Toxic gas. Bleach + Acid = Chlorine gas. Store separately. Always."

## Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge

This section summarizes the most commonly tested concepts from this subtask for Red Seal examination preparation.

### Critical Temperature Reference

| Storage Zone | Required Temperature | Key Note |
|---|---|---|
| Freezer | **≤-18°C** | Bacteria dormant, not dead; freezing does not reset safety clock |
| Refrigerator | **≤4°C** | Slows most pathogens; *Listeria* still grows slowly |
| Eggs | **≤7°C** | Once refrigerated, must remain refrigerated |
| Potatoes | **7–10°C** | Cold converts starch to sugar; affects quality and Maillard browning |
| Tomatoes | **10–13°C** | Chilling injury below 10°C; do not refrigerate at 4°C |
| Bananas | **13–15°C** | Cell damage below 13°C causes blackening |
| Dry storage | **10–21°C** | Cool, dark, dry, pest-free |

### Likely Exam Questions and Concepts

1. **What are the four storage areas in a commercial kitchen and their required temperatures?**
   → Refrigerator (≤4°C); Freezer (≤-18°C); Dry storage (10–21°C, cool and dry); Chemical storage (separate from food, as per SDS). Some operations also maintain a separate ambient area for cold-sensitive produce (10–15°C).

2. **What is FIFO and why is it a food safety principle, not just an organization practice?**
   → First In, First Out — oldest product is always used first. It is a food safety principle because certain pathogens (*Listeria*) grow slowly even under refrigeration, and because all perishable products accumulate bacterial load over their shelf life. FIFO ensures product is used before its safety-relevant shelf life is exceeded.

3. **What pathogen can grow at refrigerator temperatures (0–4°C) and what products does it present a risk in?**
   → *Listeria monocytogenes*. It is particularly associated with ready-to-eat products including deli meats, smoked fish, soft cheeses, and other RTE items that are not cooked before consumption. It is especially dangerous for pregnant women, elderly, and immunocompromised individuals.

4. **What is the two-stage cooling standard and why must food be cooled before refrigeration rather than placed hot into the walk-in?**
   → Stage 1: 60°C → 20°C within 2 hours; Stage 2: 20°C → 4°C within 4 hours; total maximum 6 hours. Hot food placed directly in a refrigerator raises the internal temperature of the unit, potentially bringing other stored food into the danger zone, and a standard refrigerator cannot cool a large hot item fast enough to meet the two-stage standard.

5. **What is freezer burn and what causes it?**
   → Freezer burn is dehydration and oxidation of the food surface caused by moisture sublimation (ice converting directly to vapour) from improperly wrapped frozen product. It appears as white or grey patches. Causes: inadequate airtight wrapping; temperature fluctuations in the freezer. It is a quality issue, not a safety issue.

6. **Why must vacuum-packed garlic in oil never be stored at room temperature?**
   → Vacuum packaging creates anaerobic (oxygen-free) conditions. *Clostridium botulinum* is an anaerobic organism that thrives in the absence of oxygen. Combined with ambient temperature and the moisture and nutrients in garlic and oil, this creates ideal conditions for botulinum toxin production. Garlic in oil must be refrigerated at all times.

7. **Name three chemical incompatibilities that create hazardous reactions in storage.**
   → Bleach + ammonia-based cleaners → chloramine gas (toxic fumes); Bleach + acid-based cleaners → chlorine gas (toxic); Bleach + hydrogen peroxide → accelerated decomposition, potential container rupture. Store these categories completely separately.

8. **What are the characteristics of a food-grade plastic container, and what disqualifies a plastic container from food use?**
   → Food-grade: manufactured from approved materials (HDPE, LDPE, PP, PET); NSF-certified or equivalent; no cracks, deep scratches, or staining; previously used only for food. Disqualified: cracked or scratched; previously used for chemicals; not manufactured from food-grade material; no food-grade designation.

9. **Why is bread stored at room temperature rather than in the refrigerator?**
   → Refrigerator temperatures (4°C) actually accelerate staling of bread through retrogradation — a process where starch crystals re-form, making the bread drier and firmer faster than at room temperature. Bread is stored at ambient (18–21°C) for short-term and frozen for long-term.

10. **What minimum height off the floor is food required to be stored at, and why?**
    → 15 cm (6 inches) minimum, as required by most Canadian provincial food premises regulations. This height allows floor cleaning beneath stored items and prevents pest access to food product.

11. **Name the four enemies of dry storage quality.**
    → Heat (accelerates chemical degradation and mold growth); Moisture/humidity (enables mold growth, causes ingredient clumping); Light (degrades fats, vitamins, colour); Pests (insects and rodents contaminate and destroy product).

12. **What is the correct procedure for thawing a large frozen roast?**
    → Refrigerator thawing — the safest method. Transfer the frozen roast from the freezer to the refrigerator 24–48 hours before it is needed. The product remains at ≤4°C throughout thawing. Room-temperature thawing is prohibited in commercial kitchens under most Canadian provincial food premises regulations.
