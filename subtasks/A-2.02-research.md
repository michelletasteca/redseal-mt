## Why Rapid Cooling Is a HACCP Critical Control Point

In Canadian foodservice, **slow cooling is the leading documented cause of bacterial foodborne illness traced to commercial kitchens.** The two main culprits — *Clostridium perfringens* and *Bacillus cereus* — are both **spore-forming bacteria**:

- **Spores survive cooking.** Even when food has been cooked to 74 °C, dormant bacterial spores can remain in the food.
- **Spores germinate as the food cools through the danger zone** (especially the 45–55 °C band where spore-formers thrive).
- The longer the food sits between **60 °C and 20 °C**, the more spores germinate and multiply.
- Some species (*B. cereus* emetic strains; *S. aureus* if cross-contaminated post-cook) produce **heat-stable toxins** — reheating does **not** make the food safe again.

This is the underlying reason for every cooling rule in this section.

> **Anchor sequence (Canadian standard, Health Canada / CFIA aligned):**
> **60 °C → 20 °C within 2 hours**
> **20 °C → 4 °C within an additional 4 hours**
> **Total ≤ 6 hours from hot to cold.**

This rule is codified in many provincial public-health food premises regulations and in CFIA's Preventive Control Plan (PCP) guidance. **[Regulatory / Best Practice — adopted across most Canadian jurisdictions]**

## The Physics of Cooling — Why Method Matters

Cooling is **heat removal**. Heat moves from hot to cold via three mechanisms:

| Mechanism | What it is | In the kitchen |
|---|---|---|
| **Conduction** | Heat through direct contact | Pan wall to ice water; product to a cold metal stir-paddle |
| **Convection** | Heat through fluid (air, water) movement | Blast chiller air, stirred ice bath, ice wand stirred through stock |
| **Radiation** | Heat as electromagnetic energy | Negligible at cooling temperatures |
| **Phase change** | Heat absorbed/released when matter changes state | **Ice → water absorbs ~334 J/g — a massive heat-sink. This is why ice baths beat refrigerators for active cooling.** |

### Three variables you can control to speed cooling

1. **Surface area-to-volume ratio.**
   - **Shallow pans (≤ 5 cm / 2 in deep)** cool 4–6× faster than the same volume in a deep cambro.
   - **Smaller portions** cool faster — the centre of a 20 L stockpot can take 12+ hours in a walk-in; the same stock in three 6 L hotel pans cools in 90 minutes.

2. **Temperature gradient.**
   - **Ice water (0 °C) vs walk-in air (2–4 °C)** is a tiny absolute difference, but the **heat-transfer coefficient of water is ~25× that of still air** — ice baths pull heat out dramatically faster.
   - **Adding ice as an ingredient** to stocks (instead of part of the water) cools and dilutes simultaneously.

3. **Movement (forced convection).**
   - **Stirring** breaks up the insulating boundary layer of hot product around the vessel wall.
   - **Blast chillers** use fans to move cold air aggressively — the air doesn't have to be colder than a walk-in's, just moving faster.
   - **Ice wand** moves cold from inside the product outward, not just the outside in.

> **Memory aid — "SAS-T":** **S**urface area, **A**gitation, **S**maller portions, **T**emperature gradient. Maximize all four to cool fast.

## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### A-2.02.01P — Select Cooling Methods and Vessels

**Performance Criteria:** select cooling methods and vessels
**Evidence of Attainment:** cooling methods and vessels are selected for rapid cooling.

#### The cooling toolkit — and when to use each

##### Ice bath
- **What it is:** sink, basin, or large insulated tub filled with **ice + water** (water is essential — ice alone is mostly air gaps and conducts poorly).
- **Best for:** stocks, soups, sauces, custards, ice-cream bases, brines, gels.
- **Use case:** stainless container of hot product submerged in the bath up to (not over) its rim; stirred continuously or periodically.
- **Vessel:** thin-walled **stainless steel** for fast conductive transfer; avoid thick plastic or glass.
- **Tips:**
  - **Salt the ice water** (~1 cup kosher salt per ice-bath) to drive water below 0 °C → much faster cooling (the same principle as homemade ice cream).
  - Stir every 5–10 minutes to break up the hot boundary layer inside the product.
  - Replenish ice as it melts; drain water as it warms.
  - Place the container on a wire rack or upside-down ramekin in the bath so cold water circulates underneath.

##### Divide into smaller / shallower portions
- **What it is:** transferring bulk hot product into multiple shallow pans or smaller containers.
- **Best for:** stews, chilis, sauces, casseroles, rice, beans.
- **Vessel:** **shallow hotel pans (≤ 5 cm / 2 in deep), full or half size**; vented covers or no cover during active cooling.
- **Why it works:** doubles or triples the surface-to-volume ratio.
- **Tip:** spread casserole-type dishes thinly; cut large roasts/proteins into smaller pieces before refrigerating.

##### Place in refrigerated unit (walk-in, reach-in)
- **What it is:** passive cooling using cold air convection.
- **Best for:** **second stage** of cooling (after the product has been pre-cooled to ≈ 20 °C) or for already-warm items.
- **Vessel:** uncovered or loosely covered, single-layer placement.
- **Why caution:**
  - **A walk-in is for storage, not active cooling.** Placing hot bulk product inside raises the unit's ambient temperature and threatens TCS items already inside.
  - Deep covered cambros in a walk-in are a textbook food-safety failure (centre stays > 20 °C for 8+ hours).
  - **[Operational]** Many ops dedicate a separate "cooling shelf" near the fan with maximum airflow.

##### Ice wand (cooling paddle)
- **What it is:** a hollow plastic paddle filled with water/glycol, frozen solid; placed into a tall vessel of hot liquid; stirred to pull heat from the core outward.
- **Best for:** **deep stockpots and large kettles** where dividing into shallow pans isn't practical mid-cooking.
- **Vessel:** the original pot — no transfer needed.
- **Why it works:** brings cold to the geometric centre of the product, where conventional cooling (outside in) is slowest.
- **Tips:**
  - Pre-freeze multiple wands; rotate as one melts.
  - Sanitize between uses.
  - Combine with ice bath for fastest cooling.

##### Blast chiller / blast freezer
- **What it is:** a refrigerated chamber with **high-velocity cold air** designed to drop food temperature aggressively.
- **Best for:** large-batch production, banquets, advance prep — the **HACCP gold standard**.
- **Vessel:** sheet pans, hotel pans, GN containers; products spread thinly.
- **Performance:** typically drops 90 °C product to 3 °C in **≈ 90 minutes** (most certified blast chillers meet a "90-in-90" or similar target). **[Best Practice — equipment spec varies]**
- **Modes:** soft chill (delicate), hard chill (dense), blast freeze.
- **Tips:**
  - Use a **product probe** for end-point control (chiller stops when product hits target).
  - Don't overload — blocks airflow.
  - Sanitize chiller racks and interior between cycles.

#### Selection logic — what to use when

| Situation | First-line method |
|---|---|
| **20 L pot of hot stock** | Ice wand in pot + ice bath in sink + stirring; transfer to shallow when ≈ 30 °C |
| **Casserole or lasagna** | Divide into half pans ≤ 5 cm deep; blast chiller or walk-in cooling shelf |
| **Roast meat / whole bird** | Slice/portion first; spread on sheet pan; blast chiller or uncovered in walk-in |
| **Sauces, gravies, soups (small batch)** | Stainless container in ice bath, stir |
| **Cooked rice, pasta, grains** | Spread thinly on sheet pan; blast chiller or walk-in cooling shelf; **never warm-hold cooked rice** |
| **Custards, crème anglaise, pastry cream** | Ice bath with stirring; cover surface with parchment to prevent skin |
| **Mass-quantity stew/chili** | Combination: split into shallow pans + ice bath underneath + blast chiller |

### A-2.02.02P — Monitor Cooling Process

**Performance Criteria:** monitor cooling process
**Evidence of Attainment:** cooling process is monitored to ensure that targeted temperature is reached within allotted time according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### The cooling log — a HACCP requirement

A typical Canadian cooling log captures:

| Field | Why |
|---|---|
| **Item / batch ID** | Traceability |
| **Start temperature (≥ 60 °C ideally)** | Establishes start of cooling timer |
| **Start time** | Time of removal from heat |
| **Method used** | Ice bath / shallow pan / blast chiller / etc. |
| **2-hour check: temperature at 2 h (target ≤ 20 °C)** | First HACCP milestone |
| **6-hour check: temperature at 6 h (target ≤ 4 °C)** | Second HACCP milestone |
| **Final action** | Cover, label, store / or corrective action |
| **Initials** | Accountability |

**[Regulatory]** Federally-registered operators under SFCR must document this in their PCP; provincial public-health programs increasingly require similar logs for restaurants. Pencil-whipped logs are a frequent compliance issue.

#### Probe technique reminder (carry-over from A-2.01)
- Insert into the **thickest / coldest part** — for cooling, that's typically the **geometric centre** of the vessel.
- Probe **multiple spots** for bulk product.
- **Sanitize the probe** between items and between checks.
- **Wait for the reading to stabilize.**

#### Monitoring cadence
- **Start time and initial temperature** logged immediately.
- **Check at the 2-hour mark** to verify ≤ 20 °C — this is the **most critical milestone** because it confirms the product is past the upper danger zone.
- **Check at 6 hours** to verify ≤ 4 °C.
- **For high-risk items** or large batches, check at hourly intervals.

#### Equipment-side monitoring
- Blast chillers with **product-probe end-point control** automate part of this (chiller stops when probe hits target).
- **Walk-in / reach-in temperatures** must also be monitored — overload during heavy cooling can push the walk-in above 4 °C and compromise other TCS items.

> **Operational tip:** post a laminated "Cooling Rules" sign in the prep area: **60 → 20 in 2, 20 → 4 in 4.** Reinforce daily; not optional.

### A-2.02.03P — Take Corrective Measures

**Performance Criteria:** take corrective measures
**Evidence of Attainment:** corrective measures are taken by adjusting the cooling process to ensure required temperature is reached using various methods or taking further actions according to company policies and jurisdictional regulations.

#### The HACCP corrective-action mindset
When monitoring shows a cooling critical limit is being missed, the cook must:
1. **Act immediately** to bring the cooling back into spec, *if possible*.
2. **Document** the deviation and the action taken.
3. **Inform the supervisor / chef on duty.**
4. **Investigate root cause** to prevent recurrence.

The original Range of Variables explicitly names: **recording, disposing of item, informing supervisor.**

#### Corrective-action decision tree

##### At the 2-hour check: product is ABOVE 20 °C
This is the **most savable** scenario.

1. **Intensify cooling immediately:**
   - Add fresh ice to the ice bath; refresh water.
   - Stir more aggressively / start ice wand.
   - **Divide into smaller / shallower portions** if not already.
   - Move to **blast chiller** if available.
2. **Re-probe in 30 minutes.**
3. **If still trending wrong:** the safer corrective option is to **reheat to ≥ 74 °C internal within 2 hours**, then **start the cooling cycle over from zero** with corrected methodology. This restarts the food-safety clock. **[Best Practice — accepted under Canadian public-health interpretations of the cooling rule]**
4. **Document** the deviation, the action, and the re-cool result.

##### At the 6-hour check: product is ABOVE 4 °C
This is much harder to recover.

1. **If product is at 5–7 °C and trending down:** consider intensifying cooling for a short window (e.g., ice bath at the final pan) and reassess at 30 minutes.
2. **If product is well above 4 °C (e.g., 10 °C+) at 6 hours:** the safer course is to **discard**. The cumulative time in the danger zone has likely already produced microbial/toxin risk.
3. **Reheating-then-recooling is NOT a routine remedy at the 6-hour stage** — it introduces another full danger-zone pass and stacks risk. Discard is the safer professional choice for most operators.
4. **Document and inform supervisor.**

##### Discovered after the fact: product was cooled overnight in a deep covered cambro
- Treat as if it never properly cooled.
- **Discard TCS items.**
- Document and use as a training case for the cooling rule.

#### When to discard outright (rapid cooling failures)

- Product temperature **> 20 °C at 2 hours** *and* corrective intensification fails to bring it ≤ 20 °C within the original 2-hour total window.
- Product temperature **> 4 °C at 6 hours**, particularly if > 10 °C.
- Product cooled in **a deep covered vessel in the walk-in** with no monitoring.
- Product exposed to **temperature abuse during cooling** (sat at room temp for an extra hour during a service rush, then forgotten).
- **Spore-former-risk products** (stews, gravies, cooked rice, refried beans) cooled outside the rule — discard with no second-guessing. **[Best Practice]**

#### Further actions — the "what else" of corrective action

| Action | When |
|---|---|
| **Record the deviation** | Always — required under HACCP/PCP |
| **Inform supervisor / chef** | Always — chain of accountability |
| **Inform public health unit** | If a customer illness might be linked, or if a power outage caused a major event (province-specific reporting thresholds) **[Regulatory]** |
| **Hold-and-investigate** | If product is borderline; isolate, label "DO NOT USE", supervisor decides |
| **Root cause analysis** | Was it volume? Vessel? Procedure? Equipment failure? Staff training? |
| **Adjust SOP / training** | Update the cooling SOP, retrain the team |
| **Equipment service request** | If blast chiller or walk-in is underperforming |

## 2. Knowledge Objectives — Deeper Dive

### A-2.02.01L — Cooling Methods, Time/Temperature Targets, Cold Holding, Danger Zone

> The original learning outcome is densely worded. Broken down, it covers: cooling methods, the 60 → 4 °C cooling envelope, cold-holding temperatures, and danger-zone temperatures.

#### Methods recap (Range of Variables)
- **Ice bath** — submersion cooling; water as the heat-transfer medium.
- **Divide product into smaller batches** — increase surface-to-volume ratio.
- **Place product in refrigerated unit** — passive air-convection cooling (use for second-stage cooling, not from boiling).
- **Use ice wands** — internal cooling for deep vessels.
- **Use blast chiller** — forced-air refrigeration; gold standard.

#### Time and temperature targets

| Stage | Target time | Target temperature | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Start of cooling** | t = 0 | ≥ 60 °C | Establishes the clock |
| **First milestone (upper danger zone exit)** | ≤ 2 hours | ≤ 20 °C | Limits time in 60–20 °C band where pathogens grow fastest |
| **Second milestone (full cool)** | ≤ additional 4 hours | ≤ 4 °C | Returns product to safe cold storage |
| **Total** | ≤ 6 hours | from 60 °C to 4 °C | Health Canada / CFIA / provincial standard |

> **Important nuance:** Some Canadian provincial codes phrase this as **"cool to 4 °C in 6 hours or less"** without explicitly stating the 2-hour midpoint, but Health Canada / CFIA guidance and most operator HACCP plans include the midpoint as the auditable Quality / safety milestone. **[Best Practice — universally taught]**

#### Cold-holding temperatures (carry-over from A-2.01)

| Storage | Required temperature |
|---|---|
| **Refrigerated cold hold (TCS food)** | **≤ 4 °C / 40 °F** |
| **Freezer storage** | **≤ −18 °C / 0 °F** |
| **Raw-fish parasite control (Health Canada guidance)** | **≤ −20 °C for ≥ 7 days** (or validated alternative) |

#### Danger zone (recap)
- **4 °C – 60 °C (40 °F – 140 °F).**
- Vegetative pathogens can multiply, including *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter*, *E. coli* O157:H7, *Staphylococcus aureus*.
- Spore-formers — *C. perfringens*, *B. cereus* — germinate in this band, especially **45–55 °C**, which is exactly the band a deep stockpot lingers in for hours when cooled improperly.
- *Listeria monocytogenes* — the cold-zone outlier — grows slowly **even at refrigeration temperatures down to ≈ 0 °C**. This is why cold-chain integrity for RTE foods (deli meats, soft cheese, smoked fish) matters as much as cooling.

#### Cooling-specific microbiology

| Pathogen | Why cooling matters |
|---|---|
| ***Clostridium perfringens*** | Spores survive cooking; germinate at 45–55 °C; very fast doubling (~7 min in ideal conditions). Classic outbreak: large-volume stews, gravies, refried beans cooled overnight. |
| ***Bacillus cereus*** | Spores survive cooking; two strains — emetic (heat-stable toxin in cooked rice held warm) and diarrhoeal (toxin produced in gut). Classic outbreak: cooked rice/pasta at room temp; refried beans. |
| ***Clostridium botulinum*** | Anaerobic spore-former; toxin in low-oxygen, low-acid, warm conditions; relevant when vacuum-packing or canning at home; not typically a fast-cooling concern but related microbiology. |
| ***Staphylococcus aureus*** | Cross-contaminated from food handler post-cook; produces heat-stable enterotoxin in temperature-abused food. Reheating does **not** destroy the toxin. |
| ***Listeria monocytogenes*** | Grows at fridge temps; once in the cold chain, only time can be controlled. Strict cold hold + dating + sanitation. |

#### Maintaining quality during cooling

Rapid cooling preserves quality as well as safety:

- **Texture** — quickly chilled sauces and gels set cleaner; slow-cooled mayonnaise can break.
- **Colour** — chilled green vegetables stay vibrant if shocked in ice water immediately after blanching.
- **Flavour development** — fast cooling reduces over-oxidation of fats; slow-cooled stocks can develop "long-held" off-flavours.
- **Crystallization in custards and ice-cream bases** — rapid cooling produces fine, smooth ice crystals; slow freezing creates large, gritty crystals.
- **Browning compound stability** — Maillard products degrade with prolonged warm holding.
- **Moisture retention** — prolonged warm cooling allows evaporation and skin formation; cover or surface-film once at < 20 °C.

### A-2.02.02L — Jurisdictional Regulations

#### Canadian regulatory layers for cooling

| Authority | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Health Canada** | Sets national food-safety policy and the **2-hour/4-hour cooling guidance** (60→20→4 °C). |
| **CFIA — SFCR** | Federally-registered operators must implement a **Preventive Control Plan (PCP)** that names cooling as a CCP and specifies the time/temperature critical limits and monitoring. **[Regulatory]** |
| **Provincial Public Health Food Premises Regulations** | Restaurants and food service. Most provinces codify the 4 °C cold hold and a cooling timeline aligned with Health Canada guidance. Examples: Ontario *Food Premises Regulation*; BC *Food Premises Regulation*; Alberta *Food Regulation*; Québec *Règlement sur les aliments* (MAPAQ). **[Regulatory]** |
| **Provincial Public Health Acts** | Outbreak reporting and illness exclusion. |
| **Municipal health authorities** | On-site inspection and enforcement. |

#### Practical regulatory expectations from a public-health inspector
When inspecting cooling practices in a Canadian commercial kitchen, an inspector will typically check:

1. **Cooling logs** — completed, signed, with realistic numbers (not all rounded to the minute).
2. **Calibrated thermometers** — verified ice-point or boiling-point test.
3. **Active cooling in progress** — looking for deep cambros in the walk-in (red flag).
4. **Vessel selection** — shallow pans for active cooling, not deep containers.
5. **Ice baths in use** — particularly for stocks and sauces.
6. **Blast chiller usage** — if present, expect it to be in active use, not parked empty.
7. **Storage organization** — cooled product covered, labelled, dated, on appropriate shelves.

#### Connection to HACCP

**Cooling is a recognized Critical Control Point (CCP)** in virtually every HACCP plan that includes cooked-then-cooled TCS items. The CCP elements:

| CCP element | Cooling-specific application |
|---|---|
| **Hazard analysis** | Spore-former growth, *Listeria* growth in slow-cooled cooked RTE, toxin formation |
| **Critical limits** | 60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h |
| **Monitoring procedure** | Probe at start, 2 h, 6 h; logged with initials |
| **Corrective action** | Intensify cooling; reheat-and-restart (at 2 h only); discard (at 6 h) |
| **Verification** | Thermometer calibration; log review; periodic data-logger audit |
| **Records** | Cooling logs retained per operation policy (often 6–24 months) |

## 3. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — End-of-shift 20 L stockpot of beef stock
1. Pull from heat at ~93 °C.
2. Strain (if applicable).
3. Place pot in a **sink ice bath** (deep enough to surround the lower half of the pot).
4. Insert **two pre-frozen ice wands** in the stock, stirring periodically.
5. **Log start time and temperature.**
6. At **30 minutes**: probe — typically ~ 40–50 °C.
7. At **2 hours**: probe — should be ≤ 20 °C. **Log.**
8. **Transfer to shallow stainless containers** (≤ 5 cm deep) for final cooling.
9. Place uncovered in walk-in on a dedicated rack.
10. At **6 hours total**: probe — should be ≤ 4 °C. **Log.**
11. Cover, label "Beef stock — [date]", store with stocks/sauces.

### Scenario 2 — Banquet production: 40 portions of beef bourguignon for tomorrow
1. Finish cooking; pull at 90 °C+.
2. **Divide immediately** into **half-hotel pans (≤ 5 cm)**.
3. Load into **blast chiller** set to soft-chill program with product probe.
4. Probe one of the pans — chiller auto-stops at 3 °C target.
5. Verify by probing other pans at the end of the cycle.
6. **Log** start time, end time, end temperature.
7. Cover with vented lids, label, refrigerate.
8. Day of service: reheat to 74 °C in combi oven, hold ≥ 60 °C, serve.

### Scenario 3 — Cooked rice for tomorrow's fried rice
1. **Critical**: cooked rice is a top *B. cereus* risk if mishandled.
2. **Do not warm-hold rice longer than 2 hours total.**
3. Spread cooked rice **thinly on sheet pans** at end of service (1–2 cm depth).
4. Place uncovered in walk-in or blast chiller.
5. Probe and log; cool to 4 °C within 6 hours (typically much faster with this surface area).
6. Cover, label, refrigerate.
7. Next day: cool rice goes directly into a hot wok over high heat → reheat to ≥ 74 °C very rapidly during stir-fry. (Never reheat rice slowly.)

### Scenario 4 — Roast turkey for sandwiches tomorrow
1. Allow turkey to cool to ~ 50 °C on the counter (no longer than 30 minutes; ambient kitchen heat speeds initial drop but starts the danger-zone clock).
2. **Slice / pull / portion** before walk-in storage — this dramatically increases surface area.
3. Spread sliced meat thinly on sheet pans or hotel pans.
4. Cool to 20 °C within 2 h (check), to 4 °C within 6 h (check).
5. Cover, label, store with RTE storage hierarchy (top shelf).

### Scenario 5 — At the 2-hour check, the lentil soup is still at 32 °C
1. **Stop and act:**
   - Move from current cooling location to a **fresh ice bath**.
   - Stir continuously for the next 20 minutes.
   - Pre-freeze additional ice wands; rotate in.
2. **Re-probe** in 20–30 minutes.
3. If now < 20 °C: continue normally; document the deviation and the corrective action.
4. If still > 20 °C: **reheat to ≥ 74 °C within 2 hours, then restart the cooling cycle** using shallow pans and blast chiller. Document the full event.
5. Inform supervisor.
6. Investigate: was the vessel too deep? Was the ice bath shallow? Was the walk-in too crowded?

## 4. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Deep cambro of stock in the walk-in with a lid on.** The single most-cited cooling failure in commercial kitchens.
- **"It'll cool overnight"** — no, the centre stays in the danger zone for 8+ hours.
- **Skipping the 2-hour midpoint check** — the 6-hour finish doesn't help if hours 1–4 were spent at 30 °C.
- **Cooling with the lid on** — traps heat. Cover only after reaching 4 °C.
- **Ice bath with no water** — ice alone is a poor conductor; add water (and salt for sub-zero baths).
- **Hot product on top of cold product in the walk-in** — heats the cold product into the danger zone.
- **Hot product blocking the walk-in fan** — disrupts the entire unit's circulation.
- **Cooled product without a date label** — invisible inventory; eventual spoilage or service of expired food.
- **Reheating temperature-abused product to "fix it"** — reheating does **not** inactivate spore germination products or heat-stable toxins.
- **Slow-cooling cooked rice at room temperature** — *B. cereus* paradise.
- **Refreezing thawed proteins without a cooking step** — adds a danger-zone pass.
- **Stacking shallow pans during active cooling** — turns them back into a deep container.
- **No cooling log** — you cannot prove it was cooled correctly if a public-health investigator asks.

## 6. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with A-2.02.

1. **The Canadian cooling rule:** **60 °C → 20 °C in 2 hours; 20 °C → 4 °C in an additional 4 hours; total ≤ 6 hours.**
2. **Why the rule exists:** to minimize time in the **45–55 °C** spore-germination band where *Clostridium perfringens* and *Bacillus cereus* thrive.
3. **Cooling is a HACCP Critical Control Point (CCP)** with documented monitoring, corrective action, and records.
4. **Cooling methods (Range of Variables):** ice bath, divide into smaller batches, place in refrigerated unit, ice wands, blast chiller.
5. **Blast chiller** is the gold standard — drops product from 90 °C to 3 °C in ~ 90 minutes for shallow pans.
6. **Ice bath** uses water (not just ice) for fast conductive transfer; salt the water for sub-zero baths.
7. **Maximum pan depth for active cooling: ~ 5 cm / 2 in.**
8. **Three speed levers:** Surface area-to-volume, agitation, temperature gradient.
9. **Phase-change physics:** ice → water absorbs ~334 J/g — why ice baths beat walk-in air for active cooling.
10. **Cold hold ≤ 4 °C; freezer ≤ −18 °C.**
11. **Food Danger Zone:** 4 °C – 60 °C. Pathogen growth window. Bacterial doubling time as fast as ~ 20 min for many species, faster for spore-formers.
12. **Cover only after cooling** — covering during active cooling traps heat and slows cooling.
13. **Spore-formers** (*C. perfringens*, *B. cereus*) survive cooking; cooling speed is the primary defence.
14. **Heat-stable toxins** (*Staph aureus*, emetic *B. cereus*) are NOT inactivated by reheating; prevent formation via temperature/hygiene.
15. **Probe technique:** geometric centre, sanitize between checks, wait for stable reading.
16. **Cooling log** (HACCP record): item, start time/temp, 2-h check, 6-h check, method, action, initials.
17. **Corrective action at 2-h failure:** intensify cooling; if still failing, reheat to 74 °C within 2 h and restart cooling cycle. Document.
18. **Corrective action at 6-h failure (still > 4 °C):** discard. Document. Inform supervisor.
19. **Further actions (Range of Variables):** recording, disposing of item, informing supervisor.
20. **Cooked rice** is a top *B. cereus* risk — never warm-hold cooked rice > 2 h; cool rapidly on sheet pans.
21. **Stews, gravies, refried beans** are top *C. perfringens* risk — never cool in deep covered containers.
22. **Ready-to-eat refrigerated cooked foods** are top *Listeria* risk — strict cold-chain integrity and date control.
23. **Storage during cooling:** uncovered shallow pans, single layer, RTE hierarchy, do not stack.
24. **CFIA SFCR PCP** mandates documented cooling CCPs for federally-registered operators.
25. **Provincial public-health regulations** codify the 4 °C cold hold and a cooling timeline aligned with Health Canada guidance.
26. **Health Canada / CFIA** are the federal source for the 60 → 20 → 4 °C cooling guidance.
27. **Cooling quality benefits:** preserves texture, colour, flavour, and prevents skin/crust formation.
28. **Operational discipline:** Stir-Check-Log — the rhythm that prevents most cooling failures.
