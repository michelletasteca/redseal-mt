## Why Sauce Cooking Is Where Technique Becomes Identity

Selection and processing build the parts. Cooking is where:

- **Flavours bloom** through Maillard browning, caramelization, infusion, reduction.
- **Emulsions form or break** based on temperature, agitation, and order of addition.
- **Thickening happens** as starches gelatinize, gelatin concentrates, eggs coagulate, fats emulsify.
- **Food safety is locked in or compromised** — pathogens controlled or allowed to grow during hot hold.
- **Identity is fixed** — a sauce becomes itself in this stage; no later step can salvage a fundamentally botched cook.

Every cooking method named in the Range of Variables — **tempering, reduction, blending, infusion, coagulation, caramelization** — is a distinct chemical event with its own rules.

## The Six Cooking Methods (Range of Variables) — Chemistry in Brief

### 1. Tempering
**Definition:** gradually raising the temperature of a delicate ingredient (egg yolks, cream, chocolate) by slowly incorporating hot liquid in small additions.
- **Mechanism:** prevents shock-induced coagulation. Eggs cook at ~ 65–70 °C; abrupt exposure to 90 °C sauce = scrambled eggs. Gradual warming gives proteins time to reorganize.
- **Applications:** liaison incorporation; custard / crème anglaise; chocolate temper for sauce gloss; caramel + cream integration.

### 2. Reduction
**Definition:** evaporating water from a liquid to concentrate solids, flavour, and gelatin.
- **Mechanism:** at ~ 100 °C atmospheric, water evaporates; dissolved gelatin, sugars, salts, and flavour compounds remain. Body builds as gelatin concentration rises.
- **Applications:** demi-glace, jus, pan sauces, balsamic glaze, wine reductions, beurre blanc base, béarnaise reduction.

### 3. Blending
**Definition:** incorporating ingredients into uniform suspension or emulsion using mechanical force (whisk, immersion blender, stand blender, food processor).
- **Mechanism:** breaks up clumps, disperses particles, can emulsify two normally-immiscible liquids (oil/butter and water) into stable suspension.
- **Applications:** vegetable purée sauces, coulis, salsa verde, vinaigrette, hollandaise (whisk-based emulsification), mounted-butter sauces.

### 4. Infusion
**Definition:** transferring flavour from solid aromatic into liquid through heat and time.
- **Mechanism:** heat extracts volatile and soluble flavour compounds from herbs, spices, dried mushrooms, citrus peels, chilies into the surrounding liquid.
- **Applications:** béchamel oignon piqué; sachet d'épices in espagnole; chili-and-spice paste in curry; tea-infused dessert sauces; vanilla in crème anglaise.

### 5. Coagulation
**Definition:** denaturation and binding of protein chains into a semi-solid or gel structure.
- **Mechanism:** proteins (egg, milk, soy) unfold and cross-link with heat, acid, or salt. In sauces, coagulation is **controlled** to produce body (liaison, hollandaise) without curdling.
- **Critical temperatures:**
  - Egg white: ~ 62 °C.
  - Egg yolk: ~ 65–70 °C.
  - Mixed yolk + cream: ~ 80 °C tolerable; above 85 °C scrambles.
  - Milk proteins (casein) coagulate with acid even at low temperatures (cheese-making, ricotta).
- **Applications:** liaison-finished velouté; hollandaise sabayon; mornay (egg yolk + cheese); curdled-sauce rescue (avoiding over-coagulation).

### 6. Caramelization
**Definition:** non-enzymatic browning of sugars at high temperature.
- **Mechanism:** **sugars decompose at ~ 160 °C+** into hundreds of new compounds — colour, aroma, bitter undertones.
- **Stages by sugar temperature:**
  - **~ 160 °C** — light gold.
  - **~ 170 °C** — amber.
  - **~ 180 °C** — deep amber, mahogany.
  - **~ 190 °C** — dark, edging into burnt; bitter.
  - **> 200 °C** — black, acrid, burnt.
- **Applications:** caramel sauce, gastrique, demi-glace background notes, brown roux dark-end, candy work, dessert sauces.

#### Caramelization vs Maillard

| Property | Caramelization | Maillard reaction |
|---|---|---|
| **Reactants** | Sugar alone | Amino acids + reducing sugars |
| **Temperature** | ~ 160 °C+ | ~ 140 °C+ |
| **Products** | Caramel compounds, furans | Pyrazines, melanoidins, hundreds of aroma molecules |
| **Examples in sauce work** | Caramel sauce, gastrique, dark roux end-stage | Brown roux, espagnole bones, sucs from roasting |

> **Memory aid — "TR-BICC":** **T**empering, **R**eduction, **B**lending, **I**nfusion, **C**oagulation, **C**aramelization.

## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### E-16.03.01P — Combine Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** combine ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** ingredients are combined in sequence according to recipe specifications.

#### General sauce-cooking sequences

##### Espagnole

1. **Make brown roux** (butter + flour, cook to mahogany; 15–30 min on medium-low).
2. **Add brown stock** in stages, whisking to incorporate (hot-cold rule from [E-15.02](E-15.02-research.md)).
3. **Add mirepoix** (typically sweated separately in a small amount of fat) + **tomato product** (paste or purée).
4. **Add sachet d'épices.**
5. **Simmer 1–2 hours** to develop depth, skim regularly.
6. **Strain** through fine chinois.
7. **Either** (a) use as is; or (b) combine with brown stock and reduce 50% to **demi-glace**.

##### Velouté

1. **Make blond roux** (5–10 min on medium-low).
2. **Add hot or cold white stock** per hot-cold rule.
3. **Simmer 30 min**, skim.
4. **Strain** through fine chinois.
5. **Adjust seasoning lightly** (some velouté goes into derivatives unseasoned).

##### Béchamel

1. **Make white roux** (3–5 min on medium-low).
2. **Add cold or warm milk** per hot-cold rule.
3. **Add oignon piqué** (peeled onion with bay leaf pinned by a clove).
4. **Simmer gently 20–30 min**, stirring occasionally to prevent surface skin.
5. **Strain** through fine chinois; discard oignon piqué.
6. **Season with salt, white pepper, nutmeg.**

##### Sauce tomate (classical French)

1. **Render salt pork or bacon** in a heavy pot.
2. **Sweat mirepoix and garlic.**
3. **Add tomato product + white stock + sachet d'épices** + optional small roux for body.
4. **Simmer 1–2 hours.**
5. **Pass through food mill** (mouli) for classical texture, OR purée and strain through fine chinois.
6. **Adjust seasoning** — salt, pepper, possibly sugar to balance tomato acidity.

##### Hollandaise

1. **Combine egg yolks + cold water (or wine reduction for béarnaise) + lemon juice** in a stainless bowl over a bain-marie.
2. **Whisk vigorously and continuously**, sabayon-style, over barely-simmering water — bowl should not touch water, and the bowl bottom must not exceed ~ 70 °C.
3. **Whisk** until the mixture **triples in volume** and forms ribbons that hold for 2 seconds.
4. **Remove from heat.**
5. **Drizzle warm clarified butter** (~ 50–60 °C) in a slow steady stream, whisking constantly — the emulsion builds as butter incorporates into the yolk-water network.
6. **Adjust** with salt, cayenne, additional lemon.
7. **Hold at 60–80 °C** — never colder (food safety), never hotter (breaks).

##### Beurre blanc

1. **Reduce wine + vinegar + minced shallot** in a small saucepan to ~ ⅓ volume (syrupy reduction; ~ 100 mL from 300 mL).
2. **Optional:** add 50 mL heavy cream and reduce briefly (modern "stabilized" beurre blanc).
3. **Off heat or on very low heat**, **whisk in cold cubed butter, a few cubes at a time**, allowing each addition to fully emulsify before the next.
4. **The temperature should stay 50–65 °C** during mounting — too hot and the butter melts to oil (no emulsion); too cold and the butter won't blend.
5. **Strain** if shallot is not desired in the final sauce.
6. **Season** with salt, white pepper.
7. **Hold 50–65 °C; serve immediately** — beurre blanc breaks above 65 °C and won't reform.

##### Pan sauce (à la minute, e.g., after sautéing chicken)

1. **Pour off excess fat** from the pan.
2. **Sweat aromatics** (shallot, garlic) in the remaining fat.
3. **Deglaze** with wine, stock, or other liquid.
4. **Scrape sucs** with a wooden spatula.
5. **Reduce** to desired consistency.
6. **Adjust** with mustard / cream / herbs as recipe dictates.
7. **Monter au beurre** (whisk in cold butter cubes off heat for gloss and body).
8. **Serve immediately** — pan sauces don't hold.

##### Pan gravy

1. **Pour off most pan fat**, retaining ~ 50 g.
2. **Add equal-weight flour** to make a roux in the pan.
3. **Whisk in stock** gradually.
4. **Simmer 5–10 min** to thicken and cook out flour.
5. **Season** salt, pepper.
6. **Strain** if desired.

##### Curries

1. **Bloom spice paste** in oil over medium-low heat (releases aromatic compounds, deepens flavour).
2. **Add aromatic base** (onion, garlic, ginger).
3. **Add tomato product** (if recipe uses tomato).
4. **Add protein + vegetables** if directly in the sauce.
5. **Add liquid** (coconut milk, stock, water).
6. **Simmer** until protein is cooked and flavours integrate.
7. **Finish** with acid (lime, tamarind), fresh herb (cilantro), garnish.

##### Soy-based sauces (stir-fry, glaze)

1. **Pre-mix** sauce ingredients in a small bowl (soy, vinegar, sugar, ginger, garlic, cornstarch slurry).
2. **Build wok stir-fry** to the point sauce-addition.
3. **Pour sauce in**, stir-toss aggressively.
4. **Cornstarch gelatinizes** in 1–2 minutes to glossy translucent thickness.
5. **Serve immediately.**

##### Coulis

1. **Cook fruit or vegetable** with sugar (for fruit) or aromatics (for vegetable).
2. **Blend** smooth.
3. **Strain** through fine sieve.
4. **Adjust** seasoning.
5. **Chill** for cold service; reheat gently for warm.

##### Chocolate sauce (ganache style)

1. **Bring heavy cream just below simmer.**
2. **Pour over finely chopped chocolate** in a stainless bowl.
3. **Let stand 1 minute** (chocolate melts gently).
4. **Whisk from the centre out** until smooth and glossy.
5. **Optional:** finish with butter, vanilla, liqueur.
6. **Serve warm** or hold for service.

##### Caramel sauce

1. **In a heavy-bottomed saucepan**, melt sugar dry OR with a small amount of water (the "wet" method is more forgiving).
2. **Watch colour** carefully — pale gold → amber → mahogany.
3. **At target colour (~ 180 °C)**, **remove from heat** — caramel continues to darken on residual heat.
4. **Slowly whisk in room-temperature heavy cream** — caramel will steam violently; expect bubbling.
5. **Return to low heat** and stir until smooth.
6. **Whisk in cold butter** off heat for gloss.
7. **Salt** at the end for salted caramel.

##### Compound butter

1. **Soften unsalted butter** to room temperature (not melted).
2. **Mix in finely chopped flavouring** + salt and other seasonings.
3. **Roll into a log** in plastic wrap or parchment.
4. **Refrigerate** until firm (or freeze for longer storage).
5. **Slice into disks** at service; melt onto hot food.

#### The order-of-addition principle

For every sauce, **the order in which ingredients enter the pot matters**:
- **Fats first** for sweating and roux.
- **Aromatics next** to bloom in the fat.
- **Featured solids** or spice pastes after aromatic foundation.
- **Liquid stages** with starches and slow-extracts.
- **Delicate finishes** (cream, butter, fresh herbs, acid) **at the end** to preserve top notes.
- **Egg-based finishes** (liaison) **off heat** to control coagulation.

### E-16.03.02P — Adjust Temperature of Sauce

**Performance Criteria:** adjust temperature of sauce
**Evidence of Attainment:** temperature of sauce is adjusted according to desired results.

#### Sauce temperature targets

| Sauce / stage | Temperature | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Sweat / sauté aromatics** | 70–110 °C | Soften without browning (sweat) or develop colour (sauté) |
| **Building roux (white)** | ~ 110–130 °C | Cook out raw flour without browning |
| **Building roux (brown / dark)** | 130–170 °C | Develop Maillard colour |
| **Simmering mother sauces (béchamel, velouté, espagnole, tomate)** | 85–95 °C | Gentle simmer; full gelatinization without breaking |
| **Reduction (atmospheric)** | ~ 100 °C | Active evaporation |
| **Holding cream sauces** | 70–80 °C | Hot enough for safety + service; cool enough to preserve emulsion |
| **Hollandaise sabayon-cooking** | Bowl bottom ≤ 70 °C; sauce ~ 65–75 °C | Yolk coagulation point |
| **Hollandaise hold** | 60–80 °C | Above 60 °C for food safety; below 85 °C to prevent breaking |
| **Beurre blanc hold** | 50–65 °C | Above this, butter melts to oil (loses emulsion) |
| **Liaison incorporation** | Sauce 80–85 °C; never above 85 °C after addition | Yolks already cooked at this point; preserves emulsion |
| **Caramel target** | 160–180 °C | Light gold to amber |
| **Hot hold (regulatory)** | ≥ 60 °C | **[Regulatory]** food-safety floor |
| **Reheat target** | ≥ 74 °C internal within 2 h | **[Regulatory]** |

#### How to adjust temperature

- **Reduce heat** if the sauce is approaching a hard boil, scorching, or breaking.
- **Move the pan off direct heat** for delicate stages (hollandaise sabayon, liaison addition).
- **Add cold liquid** (small amounts of stock, water, or cream) to cool an over-hot sauce — but only if recipe allows the dilution.
- **Increase heat** if reduction needs to accelerate (carefully; scorch risk).
- **Use a bain-marie** for very gentle hold and slow re-warming.

### E-16.03.04P — Deglaze Cooking Vessel

**Performance Criteria:** deglaze cooking vessel
**Evidence of Attainment:** cooking vessel is deglazed by adding liquid to base to capture flavour profile.

#### What deglazing is (carry-over from D-13.03)

The **sucs / fond** — the brown caramelized residue left on the pan after sautéing or roasting protein — are a concentrated flavour source. Deglazing dissolves these into liquid to incorporate them into the sauce.

#### Deglazing procedure

1. **Pour off excess fat** from the pan (leave a small amount for flavour).
2. **Heat the pan medium-high** if not already hot.
3. **Add liquid** (water, wine, stock, juice, vinegar) — typically 30–250 mL depending on pan size.
4. **Scrape every brown bit** off the pan with a wooden or silicone spatula.
5. **Reduce briefly** to drive off raw alcohol (if wine) and concentrate flavour.
6. **Strain** if desired before incorporating into the sauce.

#### Deglazing liquids

| Liquid | When |
|---|---|
| **Water** | Neutral lift |
| **Stock** | Most common for savoury sauces |
| **Dry white wine** | Fish, chicken, veal pan sauces |
| **Dry red wine** | Beef, lamb, game pan sauces |
| **Vermouth** | Refined white pan sauces; substitute for wine |
| **Madeira / port / sherry** | Sweeter pan sauces; demi enrichment |
| **Brandy / cognac** | Flamed briefly to burn off raw alcohol; rich pan sauces |
| **Vinegar** | Sharp acid lift; lambic vinegars, sherry vinegar, balsamic |
| **Fruit juice (apple, citrus, pomegranate)** | Sweet-acid lift; pork and game pan sauces |
| **Beer** | Mussels and shellfish sauces; some regional braises |

#### Common deglazing mistakes

- **Pan too hot** — liquid evaporates instantly; sucs char before lifting.
- **Pan too cool** — sucs harden and stick; deglazing fails.
- **Discarding the deglazed pan unwashed** — losing the most flavour-dense element.
- **Using metal utensils on coated pans** — scratches.
- **Over-aggressive scraping of burnt fond** — bitter compounds extract too.

### E-16.03.05P — Determine Sauce Qualities

**Performance Criteria:** determine sauce qualities
**Evidence of Attainment:** sauce qualities are determined according to desired results.

From Range of Variables: **consistency, colour, flavour, texture.**

#### Quality assessment checklist

##### Consistency (body / thickness)

- **Nappant** — coats the back of a spoon with a clean line drawn by a finger; the standard mother-sauce body.
- **Demi-glace** — slightly thicker; falls in slow ribbons.
- **Glace** — syrupy; gels firmly when cold.
- **Velouté** — light, pourable; coats but isn't gluey.
- **Béchamel** — silky, opaque, smooth.
- **Hollandaise / béarnaise** — thick, glossy, fluffy from sabayon; pours slowly.
- **Beurre blanc** — silky, thick, holds a soft peak briefly when whisked.
- **Pan sauce** — between nappant and slightly thicker; mounts visibly when butter is added.

##### Colour

- **Espagnole / demi-glace** — rich mahogany.
- **Velouté** — pale ivory to straw.
- **Béchamel** — bright white to ivory; never grey.
- **Sauce tomate** — bright tomato red (slightly muted from cook).
- **Hollandaise** — pale to bright yellow.
- **Béarnaise** — slightly green-tinted from tarragon.
- **Pan sauces with red wine** — deep ruby brown.
- **Caramel sauce** — amber to deep mahogany.

##### Flavour

- **Balanced** — salty, sour, sweet, bitter, umami all present at appropriate levels.
- **Characteristic** — matches the sauce identity (mushroom in chasseur, wine in bordelaise, mustard in robert).
- **Free of off-notes** — no scorch, no metallic, no bitter from over-extraction, no raw flour.

##### Texture

- **Smooth** (mother sauces, hollandaise) — no lumps, no graininess.
- **Slightly chunky** (chunky tomato sauce, fresh salsa) — by design.
- **Glossy** — emulsion intact; light reflects evenly.
- **Mounted** — visible silkiness from added cold butter.

#### Tasting protocol

- **Sanitized tasting spoon per taste.**
- **Never re-dip.**
- **Taste at serving temperature** — sauces taste different hot vs cool.
- **Adjust in stages** — small additions, taste again.

### E-16.03.06P — Apply Thickening Technique

**Performance Criteria:** apply thickening technique
**Evidence of Attainment:** thickening technique is applied according to desired results.

From Range of Variables: **reduction, adding thickening agents (roux, liaison, slurry), emulsification.**

> Full thickening-agent procedures are in [E-15.02-research.md](E-15.02-research.md). Here's the sauce-stage application.

#### Reduction
- **Wide shallow pan; steady simmer; skim; stir as concentration rises; pull at target volume.**
- **Don't season early** — salt concentrates aggressively.
- **Cream reductions never boil hard** — break risk.

#### Roux (carry-over)
- Hot roux + cold liquid OR cold roux + hot liquid → smooth.
- Hot + hot → lumps.
- Cook out 15–20 min for full gelatinization.

#### Liaison (carry-over)
- 3 yolks + 250 mL heavy cream per L; tempered with hot sauce; pour back; **hold ≤ 85 °C; never boil.**

#### Slurry (carry-over)
- Starch + cold water 1:1 to 1:2; whisk into simmering sauce; cook 1–2 min until clear and thickened; don't boil hard after.

#### Emulsification

Two main families of sauce emulsion:

##### Stable warm emulsion (hollandaise, béarnaise)
- **Egg yolks + acid + clarified butter** at 60–75 °C.
- **Yolks provide lecithin (emulsifier).** Lecithin has both polar (water-loving) and non-polar (fat-loving) ends; it bridges water and fat into a stable emulsion.
- **Sabayon** (whisking yolks + water over heat to a foamy ribbon) creates the protein-network structure that holds the fat.
- **Butter incorporates in a slow steady stream** — too fast and the emulsion breaks.
- **Hold 60–80 °C** — outside this window, the emulsion fails.

##### Mounted-butter emulsion (beurre blanc)
- **Wine + acid + shallot reduction** as the water-phase base.
- **Cold cubed butter** whisked in one piece at a time.
- The **milk proteins and water in butter** form the emulsion; **fat is suspended in the water phase, stabilized by milk proteins.**
- **Temperature 50–65 °C** — outside this, the emulsion fails.

##### Cold emulsions (mayonnaise, vinaigrette)
- **Egg yolk + oil + acid** for mayonnaise (lecithin-stabilized).
- **Mustard + oil + acid** for some vinaigrettes (mustard contains emulsifiers).
- **Cold or room temperature** throughout.

#### Recovering a broken sauce

| Sauce | Break cause | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| **Hollandaise** | Too hot, too fast butter addition, water-phase imbalance | In a clean bowl, whisk a fresh egg yolk + 1 tsp warm water until foamy; **slowly whisk in the broken sauce** as if it were melted butter. The new yolk rebuilds the emulsion. |
| **Béarnaise** | Same | Same yolk-rebuild method |
| **Beurre blanc** | Too hot (butter melts to oil) or too cold (won't mount) | Reduce heat or warm gently; if completely broken, restart with a small amount of fresh reduction and whisk the broken sauce in gradually |
| **Cream sauce** (boiled, broken) | Boiled or over-acidic | Off heat; whisk in 1–2 tbsp fresh cold cream; gentle re-warm |
| **Cream reduction with curdled milk** | Acid + heat | Often unsalvageable; sometimes saved by adding a starch slurry, whisking smooth, straining |
| **Curdled liaison** | Boiled after liaison | Mostly unsalvageable; blend with immersion blender and strain for emergency salvage |

### E-16.03.07P — Adjust Sauce Qualities

**Performance Criteria:** adjust sauce qualities
**Evidence of Attainment:** sauce qualities are adjusted according to desired results.

#### Adjusting consistency

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| **Too thick** | Add hot stock, milk, cream, or water — matching the sauce's character — and re-warm |
| **Too thin** | Reduce briefly; add slurry; add beurre manié pinches; add purée |
| **Lumpy** | Strain through fine chinois; blend with immersion blender |
| **Broken cream** | Whisk in cold cream off heat; gentle re-warm |
| **Broken emulsion (hollandaise / beurre blanc)** | See recovery techniques above |

#### Adjusting colour

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| **Too pale** (béchamel, velouté) | Often left as-is — pale is correct for these sauces. For brown sauces: re-introduce some sucs or a touch of demi-glace |
| **Too dark / scorched** | Often unsalvageable in classical sauces; sometimes saved by transferring to a clean pan and diluting |
| **Grey / oxidized** (cream sauce held too long) | Discard; not recoverable |
| **Off-colour from reactive cookware** | Discard; replace cookware |

#### Adjusting flavour

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| **Bland** | Salt; umami (soy / fish sauce / glace); acid (lemon, vinegar); aromatic herb |
| **Too salty** | Dilute with unsalted stock or water; add a small amount of cream or butter |
| **Too acidic** | Pinch of sugar; cream/butter to mellow |
| **Too sweet** | Acid (vinegar, lemon) for balance |
| **Too rich / heavy** | Acid; stock dilution; fresh herb finish |
| **Flat / lacking depth** | Aromatic finish (fresh herb, citrus zest, glace); deglaze new fond to add |
| **Bitter from over-extraction or scorch** | Sometimes masked with cream + acid; severe bitterness is unsalvageable |
| **Raw flour taste** | Continue simmering longer |
| **Raw alcohol from wine** | Reduce longer |

#### Adjusting texture

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| **Grainy from over-puréed starch** | Strain through fine chinois |
| **Skin on top** | Skim off; cover with parchment when holding |
| **Surface fat** | Skim |
| **Curdled / scrambled liaison** | Generally unsalvageable; emergency salvage by blender + strain |

#### The "balance check" before service

**Five qualities, every plate:**
1. **Salt** — at service temperature.
2. **Acid** — bright enough?
3. **Sweet** — appropriate for the sauce type.
4. **Bitter / depth** — for brown sauces and reductions.
5. **Umami** — meaty, savoury notes.

If any quality is missing or excessive, the sauce reads as flat or off.

### E-16.03.01L — Sauces and Their Applications (Cooking Lens)

#### Factors to consider when cooking sauces

| Factor | Cooking-stage implication |
|---|---|
| **Final sauce identity** | Sequence and method align to the recipe |
| **Stock and dairy quality** | Mediocre base = mediocre sauce |
| **Service style** | Plated à la minute vs buffet hot-hold vs banquet pre-prep |
| **Hold time** | Hollandaise ~ 1–2 h max; demi can hold for days; pan sauces serve immediately |
| **Allergen requirements** | Dedicated tools and equipment for severe-allergen orders |
| **Production scale** | Single portion vs banquet of 200 — heat distribution and equipment choice differ |
| **Operator skill level** | Hollandaise / béarnaise / beurre blanc demand precision; reductions are more forgiving |

#### Ingredients and ratios (carry-over from E-16.01 / E-16.02)

See E-16.01 and E-16.02 for the per-litre ratio tables.

#### Dietary requirements (carry-over)

- **Celiac / gluten-free** — cornstarch slurry, rice-flour roux, vegetable purée; verify modified starch source.
- **Dairy-free** — plant cream, oil-based roux, vegetable purée; no béchamel or hollandaise.
- **Egg-free** — no hollandaise, béarnaise, liaison; substitute butter-emulsion or starch-thickened.
- **Vegan** — vegetable purée, plant cream, plant butter, vegetable stock; mushroom + miso umami.
- **Halal / kosher** — no pork; no meat-dairy mixing (kosher); certified supply.
- **Sulphite-sensitive** — sulphite-free wine or non-alcoholic.

### E-16.03.02L — Procedures Used to Prepare Sauces

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to prepare hot and cold sauces.

#### Hot sauces — procedure summary

##### Espagnole / demi-glace
- Brown roux → brown stock incorporation → mirepoix + tomato + sachet → simmer 1–2 h → strain → (for demi: combine with stock and reduce 50%).

##### Velouté
- Blond roux → white stock → simmer 30 min → strain.

##### Béchamel
- White roux → milk → oignon piqué → simmer 20–30 min → strain → season.

##### Sauce tomate (classical)
- Render salt pork → sweat mirepoix + garlic → add tomato + stock + sachet → simmer 1–2 h → food mill or purée + strain.

##### Hollandaise
- Yolks + water/acid sabayon over bain-marie → whisk to ribbons → off heat → drizzle warm clarified butter → adjust → hold 60–80 °C.

##### Béarnaise
- Tarragon-shallot-vinegar reduction → strain or leave whole → sabayon with yolks → mount with butter → finish with fresh tarragon.

##### Beurre blanc
- Wine + vinegar + shallot reduction to ⅓ → optional cream → whisk in cold cubed butter at 50–65 °C → strain → season → hold 50–65 °C.

##### Pan sauce
- Pour off fat → sweat aromatics → deglaze → reduce → finish (mustard / cream / herbs) → monter au beurre → serve.

##### Pan gravy
- Retain fat → roux in pan → whisk in stock → simmer → strain.

##### Reductions
- Strain liquid → wide pan → simmer → skim → reduce to target → season late.

##### Curries
- Bloom spices → aromatic base → tomato (if used) → protein → liquid → simmer to integrate → finish with acid + herb.

##### Soy-based stir-fry sauces
- Pre-mix sauce ingredients → add to stir-fry near end → cornstarch gelatinizes → serve immediately.

##### Chocolate sauce (ganache)
- Bring cream just below simmer → pour over chopped chocolate → rest → whisk smooth → adjust.

##### Caramel sauce
- Caramelize sugar to ~ 180 °C → off heat → whisk in room-temp cream → return to low heat → finish with butter and salt.

#### Cold sauces — procedure summary

##### Mayonnaise
- Egg yolk + mustard + vinegar/lemon → drizzle oil while whisking → emulsifies into a thick sauce → adjust.

##### Vinaigrette
- Acid + emulsifier (mustard, honey, shallot purée) + seasoning → drizzle oil while whisking.

##### Salsa fresca (pico de gallo)
- Dice ingredients; combine; rest 30 min to meld; serve cold.

##### Salsa verde
- Pound or chop herbs + capers + anchovy + garlic + olive oil + acid → no cooking → adjust → serve cold.

##### Romesco
- Roasted red pepper + tomato + almond + garlic + olive oil → process smooth → adjust acid and seasoning → serve cool to room temperature.

##### Mango chutney (raw style)
- Combine diced mango + ginger + chili + lime + cilantro → rest → serve.

##### Compound butter
- Soften butter → mix in flavouring → roll in log → chill → slice as needed.

##### Coulis (cold service)
- Cook fruit + sugar → blend → strain → chill → serve.

##### Mayonnaise-based sauces (aioli, tartar, remoulade, ravigote)
- Build mayonnaise first; fold in additional flavour components; rest in fridge.

#### Sanitation during cooking (carry-over)
- **Hot hold ≥ 60 °C; cold hold ≤ 4 °C.**
- **Hot-blender safety** for puréed sauces.
- **Sanitize tasting spoons** between every taste.
- **Allergen-isolated tools** for severe-allergen orders.

## 3. Food-Safety Considerations During Sauce Cooking

### Hot hold of sauces

- **Mother sauces (espagnole, velouté, béchamel, tomate):** **≥ 60 °C** in bain-marie or hot well during service. **[Regulatory]**
- **Hollandaise / béarnaise:** **60–80 °C** — narrow window; food-safety floor of 60 °C, emulsion-break ceiling of ~ 85 °C; **maximum service hold ~ 1–2 h** for both safety and quality. **[Best Practice]**
- **Beurre blanc:** **50–65 °C** — outside this, the emulsion fails. **Note:** 50 °C is below the food-safety floor of 60 °C. For commercial service, beurre blanc is **made fresh and held briefly**, or held in a warm spot with the understanding that it has limited safe-hold time. **[Best Practice]**

> ⚠️ **Beurre blanc at 50–55 °C is technically in the danger zone (4–60 °C).** Industry handles this by:
> - Making fresh and serving immediately (the safest approach).
> - Holding briefly (under 2 hours) in a covered warm spot.
> - Some operations heat slightly higher (60–65 °C) accepting a more delicate emulsion.

### Reheating sauces

- **Reheat to ≥ 74 °C / 165 °F within 2 hours** for refrigerated mother sauces. **[Regulatory]**
- **Reheat gently** to preserve emulsion and body — high heat can break cream and seize starches.
- **Never reheat emulsified butter sauces** (hollandaise, béarnaise, beurre blanc) once cooled — they don't come back. Make fresh.

### Impacts of incorrect re-thermalization on sauce quality and safety

**Safety:**
- **Cold spots survive cooking** in thick cream sauces; pathogens may persist.
- **Heat-stable toxins** (*Staph aureus*) not destroyed by reheating — prevention via hygiene + temperature is the only defence.
- **Cross-contamination** if reheating equipment isn't sanitized between cycles.

**Quality:**
- **Cream sauces break** when reheated aggressively — emulsion collapses, fat separates.
- **Roux-thickened sauces thin** with repeated heat cycles — starches break down.
- **Reductions get gummy or sticky** — gelatin over-concentrates.
- **Hollandaise / béarnaise / beurre blanc are not reheatable** — always serve fresh.
- **Tomato sauces darken** with repeated heat — colour and flavour decline.
- **Caramelized sauces (caramel, demi-glace background)** lose their nuanced caramel notes; bitter scorch notes emerge.
- **Fresh herb top notes evaporate** with each reheat.

> **Operational rule:** plan sauce production so each portion is heated **once to service temperature**. Mother sauces can re-warm; emulsions cannot.

### Cooling sauces (carry-over from A-2.02)

- **60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.**
- **Cool uncovered in shallow stainless containers**; cover after ≤ 4 °C.
- **Cream and butter sauces** especially benefit from rapid cooling — quality decay starts within minutes of dropping below hot-hold.
- **Tomato and demi-glace sauces** cool more forgivingly.

### Allergen cross-contact

- **Soy sauce in the wok** then used in a celiac order's stir-fry → cross-contact.
- **Hollandaise whisk** used in the next sauce without sanitation → egg cross-contact for an egg-allergic guest.
- **Roux pot** used between gluten-free and standard production → wheat residue.
- **Cream pot** used between dairy-free and dairy sauces → milk residue.
- **Dedicated tools** for severe-allergen orders; see [A-2.04-research.md](A-2.04-research.md).

## 4. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Cooking hollandaise for 12 portions at brunch
1. **Mise en place** complete (see [E-16.02-research.md](E-16.02-research.md) Scenario 1).
2. **Set up bain-marie**: saucepan with water at gentle simmer; stainless bowl over it not touching the water.
3. **Combine yolks + 30 mL water + 30 mL lemon juice** in the bowl; whisk briefly.
4. **Sabayon stage:** whisk vigorously over the bain-marie for 3–5 minutes, until the mixture **triples in volume** and **forms ribbons** that hold 2 sec.
5. **Remove from heat.**
6. **Drizzle 250 g warm clarified butter** in a slow steady stream, whisking continuously.
7. **Adjust** with salt, pinch cayenne, additional lemon.
8. **Hold in a warm spot at 60–80 °C** (over warm water, in a thermos, or in a low-temperature bain-marie).
9. **Service:** ladle onto eggs Benedict or asparagus.

### Scenario 2 — Beurre blanc à la minute for grilled snapper
1. **Wine + vinegar + shallot reduction** done in advance; held warm.
2. **At service:** small saucepan over low heat; combine reduction + optional 50 mL cream.
3. **Whisk in cold cubed butter** a few cubes at a time, swirling the pan and whisking.
4. **Strain** through fine chinois (optional, if shallot pieces aren't wanted).
5. **Hold 50–65 °C** for the brief service window.
6. **Spoon over fish** at plating.

### Scenario 3 — Demi-glace production for the week
1. **Espagnole already cooked** (or use a high-quality commercial reference).
2. **Combine 4 L espagnole + 4 L brown veal stock** in a wide rondeau.
3. **Simmer gently 2–3 h**, reducing to 4 L final volume (50%).
4. **Skim** regularly.
5. **Strain** through cheesecloth-lined fine chinois.
6. **Cool per A-2.02** (60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h).
7. **Portion into containers**, label, date.
8. **Refrigerate** (3–5 days) or freeze (months).

### Scenario 4 — Pan sauce for sautéed chicken breast
1. **Chicken sautéed** to ≥ 74 °C internal.
2. **Pour off excess fat**; leave ~ 1 tbsp in the pan.
3. **Sweat 2 minced shallots** in the fat 1 min.
4. **Deglaze** with 60 mL white wine; scrape sucs; reduce by half.
5. **Add 250 mL chicken stock + 1 sprig thyme**; reduce to ~ 100 mL.
6. **Off heat, whisk in 30 g cold butter** (monter au beurre) for gloss.
7. **Season** salt, pepper.
8. **Spoon over the chicken** at plating.

### Scenario 5 — Recovering a broken hollandaise mid-service
1. **Observation:** sauce has gone oily, with butter separating out.
2. **Action:**
   - **In a clean stainless bowl**, whisk 1 fresh egg yolk + 1 tsp warm water until foamy.
   - **Slowly drizzle the broken sauce** into the fresh yolk, whisking constantly — re-builds the emulsion.
3. **Continue** until all the broken sauce is incorporated.
4. **Hold at 60–80 °C** for the remaining service window.
5. **Lesson:** monitor temperature more carefully; add butter more slowly next time.

### Scenario 6 — Celiac guest needs a velouté-style sauce on fish
1. **Standard chicken velouté** uses wheat-flour roux.
2. **Substitution:**
   - **Cornstarch slurry** (1 tsp + 1 tbsp cold water per 250 mL liquid).
   - **Or rice-flour roux** for closer texture.
3. **Use allergen-isolated whisk, pot, and ladle.**
4. **Verify** stock has no hidden wheat (some commercial bases contain wheat).
5. **Document** the substitution.
6. **Server confirms** at table.

### Scenario 7 — Caramel sauce production
1. **In a heavy-bottomed stainless saucepan**, combine 250 g granulated sugar + 60 mL water.
2. **Heat over medium**, swirling (do NOT stir — crystallization risk).
3. **Cook to amber colour** (~ 180 °C, or just past mahogany).
4. **Off heat**, slowly whisk in 200 mL room-temperature heavy cream — expect steam and bubbling.
5. **Return to low heat** for 1–2 min, stirring until smooth.
6. **Whisk in 50 g cold butter** off heat.
7. **Salt** at the end.
8. **Cool to room temperature**; refrigerate for storage.

## 5. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Boiling cream sauce** → breaks; emulsion fails; fat separates.
- **Cold cream into hot caramel** → sugar seizes; steam explosion; severe burns.
- **Hollandaise held cold** → falls below food-safety floor (60 °C) AND breaks emulsion. Make fresh.
- **Hollandaise above 85 °C** → breaks.
- **Beurre blanc above 65 °C** → breaks (butter melts to oil).
- **Beurre blanc below 50 °C** → solidifies; won't pour.
- **Liaison added to boiling sauce** → scrambled.
- **Slurry into non-simmering sauce** → starchy aftertaste; thickening incomplete.
- **Roux + hot + hot** → lumps.
- **Salt added at start of long reduction** → over-salted; concentrates aggressively.
- **Reduction in deep narrow pot** → slow evaporation; scorching at bottom.
- **Reactive cookware (aluminum) for tomato/wine sauces** → metallic flavour, discolouration.
- **Eggs cracked directly into the sabayon bowl** → bad egg ruins the batch.
- **Cold butter dropped into broken beurre blanc** → won't recover; restart.
- **Reheating hollandaise / béarnaise / beurre blanc** → not recoverable; always serve fresh.
- **Repeated reheating of cream sauce** → cumulative quality decay; pathogen-spore risk if cooled improperly between cycles.
- **Pan sauce in a cold pan** → no fond extraction.
- **Pan-sauce deglaze on a too-cold pan** → fond doesn't release.
- **Tasting with the cooking spoon and re-dipping** → cross-contamination.
- **Plastic spatula in caramel** → melts.
- **No probe thermometer for hollandaise** → guessing at the yolk-coagulation window.
- **Not skimming reductions** → cloudy, greasy finish.

## 7. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with E-16.03.

1. **Six cooking methods (Range of Variables):** tempering, reduction, blending, infusion, coagulation, caramelization.
2. **Tempering:** gradually raise temperature of delicate ingredients (eggs, cream, chocolate) by slowly incorporating hot liquid.
3. **Reduction:** evaporate water to concentrate solids, flavour, and gelatin.
4. **Blending:** mechanical incorporation into uniform suspension or emulsion.
5. **Infusion:** transfer flavour from solid aromatic into liquid via heat and time.
6. **Coagulation:** controlled protein denaturation into semi-solid structure (egg yolks ~ 65–70 °C; mixed with cream tolerates ~ 80 °C; above 85 °C scrambles).
7. **Caramelization:** sugar decomposition at ≥ 160 °C — light gold → amber → mahogany → burnt.
8. **Maillard vs caramelization:** Maillard requires both amino acids and reducing sugars at ≥ ~ 140 °C; caramelization requires only sugar at ≥ 160 °C.
9. **Mother sauce sequences:**
   - **Espagnole:** brown roux → brown stock → mirepoix + tomato + sachet → simmer 1–2 h → strain.
   - **Demi-glace:** espagnole + brown stock reduced 50%.
   - **Velouté:** blond roux → white stock → simmer 30 min → strain.
   - **Béchamel:** white roux → milk → oignon piqué → simmer 20–30 min → strain.
   - **Sauce tomate (classical):** render salt pork → sweat mirepoix → tomato + stock + sachet → simmer → mill / strain.
   - **Hollandaise:** sabayon yolks over bain-marie → drizzle warm clarified butter.
10. **Hollandaise temperature window:** **60–80 °C** for service hold; **maximum 1–2 h** hold.
11. **Beurre blanc temperature window:** **50–65 °C** for emulsion stability.
12. **Hot-cold rule for roux:** hot + cold or cold + hot → smooth; hot + hot → lumps.
13. **Cook-out** roux-based sauces 15–20 min for full gelatinization and raw-flour removal.
14. **Slurry technique:** cold-water dispersion of starch; whisk into simmering sauce; cook 1–2 min; never boil hard after.
15. **Liaison:** 3 yolks + 250 mL heavy cream per L; temper into hot sauce; pour back; hold ≤ 85 °C; never boil.
16. **Beurre blanc emulsion:** wine + acid + shallot reduction (water phase) + cold cubed butter (fat phase, suspended by milk proteins).
17. **Hollandaise emulsion:** egg yolk lecithin + sabayon protein network + clarified butter.
18. **Recovering a broken emulsion:** fresh yolk + warm water as a new base; drizzle the broken sauce in.
19. **Deglaze every suc** — pan sauces and brown sauces depend on caramelized residue.
20. **Sauce qualities (Range of Variables):** consistency, colour, flavour, texture.
21. **Nappant:** coats the back of a spoon — the standard mother-sauce body.
22. **Thickening techniques (Range of Variables):** reduction, adding agents (roux, liaison, slurry), emulsification.
23. **Monter au beurre:** whisk cold butter cubes off heat for gloss and body.
24. **Salt held late in reductions** — concentrates aggressively.
25. **Hot hold ≥ 60 °C; cold hold ≤ 4 °C; reheat to ≥ 74 °C within 2 h.**
26. **Hollandaise, béarnaise, and beurre blanc are not reheatable** — make fresh.
27. **Cooling rule** (carry-over from A-2.02): 60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.
28. **Reactive cookware ban for acidic sauces** — aluminum, unlined copper, bare cast iron, bare carbon steel.
29. **Heavy-bottomed stainless** is the workhorse sauce pot.
30. **Pasteurized egg yolks** for hollandaise / béarnaise / liaison in high-risk populations (immunocompromised, elderly, pregnant, very young).
