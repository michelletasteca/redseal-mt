# A-2.03 | Re-thermalizes Foods — Enhanced Learning Resource

**Major Work Activity:** A — Hygiene, Sanitation and Safety
**Task:** A-2 | Practices Food Safety Procedures

> **What this subtask is about:** Re-thermalization means reheating previously cooked and cooled food to a safe internal temperature for service. It is one of the highest-risk steps in commercial food handling — done incorrectly, it can allow bacterial toxins to accumulate to dangerous levels or fail to kill bacteria that multiplied during cooling. This document preserves all Red Seal competency content and adds the food science, safety reasoning, and operational knowledge an apprentice needs to understand *why* these standards exist, not just what they are.

---

## Understanding Re-thermalization

### What Re-thermalization Is

Re-thermalization (also called "reheating" in everyday language) is the process of bringing previously cooked and cooled food back up to a safe serving temperature. In a commercial kitchen, this applies to:

- Soups, stocks, and sauces made in advance and refrigerated
- Batch-cooked proteins (roasts, braises, stews) reheated per order or for hot-holding
- Pre-portioned entrées reheated for banquet or cafeteria service
- Leftovers from previous service brought forward to the next meal period
- Sous vide pouches brought from cold storage to service temperature

### Why It's a Critical Control Point (CCP)

Re-thermalization is not just "warming food up." Because cooked food has already been exposed to the temperature danger zone once (during initial cooling), every subsequent re-entry into that zone creates another window for bacterial multiplication. Most foodborne illness outbreaks in commercial kitchens involve one of two failure modes:

1. **Inadequate reheating** — food never reaches the temperature needed to kill bacteria that multiplied during improper cooling
2. **Slow reheating** — food spends too long passing through the danger zone, allowing surviving organisms to multiply further before the temperature becomes lethal

Re-thermalization is therefore a **Critical Control Point (CCP)** under HACCP — a step where a control measure can be applied to prevent or eliminate a food safety hazard.

#### Deep Dive: Tools and Equipment for Re-thermalization

Selecting the right equipment is not simply a matter of convenience — it directly affects how quickly and evenly heat penetrates food, which determines both safety and quality outcomes.

**Equipment Reference**

| Equipment | Best For | Heat Transfer Method | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stove / range (gas or electric) | Liquids, soups, sauces, small batches | Conduction (pot bottom) + convection (liquid circulation) | Stir frequently; hot spots at bottom; fast and controllable |
| Conventional oven | Roasts, casseroles, sheet-pan proteins | Convection (air) + radiation | Slower penetration; cover pan to retain moisture; use probe thermometer |
| Combi-oven (steam + convection) | Proteins, vegetables, plated meals, banquet trays | Convection + steam (forced moist heat) | Fastest and most even penetration; retains moisture; preferred for large-scale reheating |
| Microwave | Small portions, individual servings | Dielectric heating (electromagnetic radiation excites water molecules) | Uneven heating common; requires standing time and stirring; not recommended for large or dense items |
| Bain-marie / steam table | Holding after reheating, finishing hot soups/sauces | Indirect moist heat (water bath) | **Not suitable for primary reheating from cold** — too slow; use only for holding food already at 74°C+ |
| Thermal circulator (sous vide) | Proteins, vacuum-sealed products, precision reheating | Convection (circulating water bath) | Very precise temperature control; typically used to bring food to 60–74°C; confirm target temperature matches food safety requirements |
| Pots and pans | Liquids, braised items, stews | Conduction (metal contact) + convection | Choose heavy-gauge metal for even heat distribution; avoid rapid boiling of delicate sauces |
| Food service containers / hotel pans | Holding product during or after reheating | Conduction from surrounding equipment | Use appropriately sized containers; avoid overfilling (reduces heat penetration to centre) |

> **Industry Best Practice:** Match container size to batch size. A large hotel pan filled 5 cm deep reheats far faster and more evenly than the same pan filled 15 cm deep. In commercial kitchens, dividing product into smaller batches is a standard technique to reduce re-thermalization time and improve safety.

> **Operational Tip:** A combi-oven in steam-injection mode at 100–150°C with a probe is the most reliable tool for banquet-scale reheating — it heats food simultaneously from all surfaces, maintains moisture, and halts automatically when the probe reaches the target temperature.

---

#### Heat Transfer Science (Why Some Methods Are Faster)

Understanding heat transfer explains why equipment choices matter for food safety:

- **Conduction** — heat moves through direct contact (metal pan → food surface). Speed depends on thermal conductivity of the material and surface area in contact.
- **Convection** — heat moves through a circulating fluid (water or air). Forced convection (fan-assisted ovens, circulating water baths) is significantly faster than natural convection because it continuously replaces the cooled boundary layer at the food's surface.
- **Dielectric heating (microwave)** — electromagnetic waves penetrate food and excite water molecules throughout the mass simultaneously, which is why microwaves are fast. However, energy distribution is uneven, creating hot and cold spots — a major food safety risk unless the food is stirred and allowed to stand.
- **Steam** — has very high heat transfer coefficient compared to dry air. This is why combi-ovens using steam reheat denser items much faster than dry-heat ovens at the same nominal temperature.

#### Deep Dive: Required Temperatures and Timeframes

**The Critical Temperature: 74°C (165°F)**

In most Canadian jurisdictions, previously cooked foods must be reheated to an **internal temperature of 74°C (165°F)**. This temperature is the standard reference point in many provincial food premises regulations and is consistent with Health Canada's Safe Food Handling guidelines for commercially cooked foods.

> **Regulatory note:** Specific temperature requirements are set by provincial and territorial food premises regulations. While 74°C is the most widely cited standard across Canadian jurisdictions, always verify the requirements of the specific province or territory in which you are working. The standard referenced in Canadian professional cook training is 74°C.

**The Timeframe Requirement**

Food must reach 74°C within **2 hours** of beginning the reheating process. This window exists because:

- Once food is removed from refrigeration and begins warming, it enters the temperature danger zone (4°C–60°C)
- Every minute spent in the danger zone is a window for bacterial multiplication
- Keeping re-thermalization time to 2 hours or less limits the total cumulative time a food product has spent in the danger zone across its entire life cycle

> **Industry Best Practice:** The 2-hour reheating window is the widely observed commercial standard. Many operators use a more conservative internal target — aiming to hit 74°C in under 60 minutes — to provide margin and ensure consistent food quality.

#### The Temperature Danger Zone

```
Temperature Scale (°C)      Food Safety Status
───────────────────────────────────────────────────
  74°C+ (165°F+)     ◄──── SAFE: re-thermalization target
  ─────────────────
  60°C  (140°F)      ◄──── MINIMUM hot-holding temperature
  │                        (Bacteria grow slowly above 60°C)
  │  DANGER ZONE           
  │  (4°C – 60°C)          Rapid bacterial multiplication
  │  Bacteria can           Most pathogens double every
  │  double every           20 minutes at optimal temp
  │  20–30 minutes          (~37°C body temp)
  │
  4°C   (40°F)       ◄──── MAXIMUM cold-holding temperature
  ─────────────────
  0°C – 4°C                Bacteria slow significantly
                            (Listeria can still grow)
  ─────────────────
  -18°C (0°F)        ◄──── FREEZING: bacteria dormant
```

> **Common Industry Practice:** The 2-hour total rule — food should not accumulate more than 2 hours in the danger zone across its entire handling history (cooking → cooling → cold storage → reheating). Some jurisdictions allow up to 4 hours as the outer limit before disposal, but 2 hours is the professional kitchen standard.

---

#### Bacteria Relevant to Re-thermalization

Understanding which bacteria create risk during reheating — and why — helps apprentices apply good judgment beyond simply following rules:

**Clostridium perfringens**
- Forms heat-resistant spores that survive normal cooking temperatures
- Vegetative cells are destroyed by heat, but spores survive and germinate when food cools slowly
- Grows extremely rapidly between 43°C–47°C (optimal range) — can double in under 10 minutes
- Common in large batches of stews, gravies, meat pies, roasts — the exact foods most often held for re-thermalization
- **Implication:** Even food that was properly cooked may carry viable C. perfringens spores. Proper rapid cooling followed by rapid reheating to 74°C is essential to prevent dangerous toxin loads.

**Bacillus cereus**
- Also forms heat-resistant spores
- Produces two types of toxins: one emetic (vomiting) toxin that is **heat-stable** and survives reheating; one diarrheal toxin that is heat-labile
- Common in rice dishes, starchy foods, cereals, pasta
- **Implication:** If food was improperly cooled and B. cereus produced its heat-stable emetic toxin, **reheating will NOT destroy that toxin**. This is why proper cooling is the actual point of control — not reheating.

**Staphylococcus aureus**
- Produces heat-stable enterotoxins that survive 100°C for 30 minutes
- **Implication:** As with B. cereus emetic toxin, reheating cannot save food that was handled improperly and allowed to develop S. aureus toxins. Reheating addresses vegetative bacterial cells, not preformed heat-stable toxins.

**Salmonella, E. coli O157:H7, Listeria monocytogenes**
- All destroyed by reaching 74°C throughout the food
- These vegetative pathogens represent the microbiological targets that the 74°C standard is designed to eliminate

> **Key insight for apprentices:** Re-thermalization destroys *live bacteria* but **cannot destroy heat-stable toxins**. If food was allowed to sit in the danger zone too long before reheating, it may be contaminated with preformed toxins that make people sick even after proper reheating. Correct cooling is the first line of defense; correct re-thermalization is the second.

---

#### Impact of Incorrect Re-thermalization on Food Quality

Re-thermalization affects food quality as well as safety. Understanding the quality impacts helps maintain professional standards:

| Failure Mode | Quality Impact |
|---|---|
| Reheating too slowly (low heat, extended time) | Moisture loss, textural degradation, overcooking of proteins; also a safety failure |
| Overheating / exceeding 74°C excessively | Sauces break (emulsion failure), proteins toughen, delicate vegetables lose structure and colour |
| Reheating in wrong equipment (e.g., soup in dry oven) | Uneven heating, scorching, skin formation, quality loss |
| Reheating dense item without dividing batches | Centre remains under-temperature while exterior overcooks |
| Repeated reheating of the same product | Progressive quality degradation; food should never be reheated more than once |

> **Operational Recommendation:** Reheat only the quantity needed for service. Reheating in smaller batches reduces re-thermalization time, improves consistency, and prevents unnecessary quality loss on food that will be reheated a second time (which should be avoided entirely).

#### Deep Dive: Temperature Verification

**Why thermometers are mandatory — not optional**

Visual cues (steaming, bubbling, browning) are unreliable indicators of internal temperature. A large pot of soup may boil vigorously at the surface while the thermal centre of a dense piece of meat submerged within it is still at 50°C. Only a calibrated probe thermometer provides the data needed to confirm a CCP has been met.

**Types of Thermometers Used in Commercial Kitchens**

| Type | How It Works | Best Use | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bimetallic stem thermometer | Metal coil expands with heat, moves dial | General-purpose; soups, roasts, stews | Slow response; reads average over sensor length (~2.5 cm zone) |
| Digital instant-read probe | Thermocouple or thermistor reads at tip | Precise spot-check; thin items | Reads a single point; must be inserted to correct depth |
| Oven-safe leave-in probe | Thermocouple with cable to external display | Monitoring centre temperature during oven reheating | Fixed position; must not be dislodged during handling |
| Combi-oven integrated probe | Same as above, built into equipment | Auto-stop at target temperature | Requires correct placement at thermal centre |
| Infrared (IR) gun thermometer | Reads surface radiation | Spot-check surface temperatures; receiving inspection | **Cannot measure internal temperature** — not suitable for verifying re-thermalization |

> **Regulatory / Industry Standard:** Thermometers used in commercial food preparation should be accurate to ±1°C. Calibrate regularly using the ice-water method (0°C) and/or boiling water method (accounting for altitude). In Canada, most food premises regulations require thermometers to be available and used at CCPs.

**Correct Measurement Technique**

1. **Insert the probe into the thermal centre** — the geometric middle of the densest, thickest part, away from bones, fat pockets, and container edges. The coldest point in reheated food is at the centre.
2. **Wait for the reading to stabilize** — especially with bimetallic thermometers (up to 15–20 seconds).
3. **Take multiple readings** on large batches (different locations, including centre of multiple containers).
4. **Sanitize the probe before and after each use** — cross-contamination via probe is a real risk.
5. **Record the temperature** — documentation is part of HACCP compliance and provides a defence if food safety is later questioned.

> **Common Mistake:** Inserting the probe into the hottest point (e.g., directly at the edge of a bubbling pot) rather than the thermal centre. This gives a false reading — the exterior or liquid surface may be 90°C while the dense centre of solid product is 58°C.

#### Deep Dive: Corrective Measures

**When re-thermalization fails to reach 74°C within the 2-hour window, three decisions must be made in sequence:**

```
Re-thermalization process in progress
         │
         ▼
Internal temp check at 2-hour mark
         │
    ─────┴─────
    │          │
  74°C+      Below 74°C
reached        │
    │          ▼
  Safe      Is total danger zone
  proceed   time still under
            acceptable limit?
               │
         ──────┴──────
         │            │
       Yes           No
         │            │
         ▼            ▼
  Divide into    DISCARD product
  smaller         + Record
  batches          + Notify supervisor
  Apply more
  heat / higher
  temperature
  Re-check temp
```

**Corrective Methods (Adjusting the Process)**

- **Divide into smaller batches** — the most effective corrective measure. A 20-litre pot of soup will take far longer to reheat than four 5-litre batches. Dividing reduces the mass that heat must penetrate.
- **Increase the heat source** — raise oven temperature, increase burner intensity, switch to a higher-output appliance.
- **Change the method** — if a conventional oven is too slow, transfer to a combi-oven, stovetop, or steam kettle.
- **Stir or agitate** — for liquids, stirring moves cooler product from the centre to the heated periphery and distributes heat rapidly (exploiting forced convection).

**Further Actions (When Product Cannot Be Saved)**

If the product cannot reach 74°C within the safe window:

- **Record** the incident in the food safety log, including product type, quantity, temperature readings, time, and disposition
- **Dispose of the item** — do not attempt to cool and re-store food that has failed re-thermalization and exceeded safe time limits
- **Inform the supervisor** — allows investigation of process failures, documentation for audit purposes, and communication to service staff

> **HACCP Documentation Note:** Corrective action records are a required component of a HACCP plan. When a CCP (like re-thermalization) is not met, the corrective action taken must be documented. This record demonstrates that the food service operation has a functioning food safety management system and took appropriate steps when a deviation occurred.

> **Important Regulatory / Operational Point:** Food that has been reheated must not be allowed to cool and be reheated a second time. Repeated cycling through the danger zone compounds bacterial multiplication risk. Product reheated but not served must be discarded, not returned to cold storage for later use.

#### Deep Dive: Principles of Re-thermalization

**The Impact of Quantity (Mass) on Re-thermalization**

This is one of the most practical principles in food service — and one of the most commonly violated. Quantity affects re-thermalization in two important ways:

1. **Thermal mass** — larger volumes hold more total heat energy, which means they also take longer to gain or lose heat. A 10-kg pot of chili takes 4–5× longer to reach 74°C at the centre than a 2-kg portion of the same product.

2. **Surface-area-to-volume ratio** — heat enters food through its surface. Smaller batches (or thinner layers) have a proportionally larger surface area relative to their volume, so heat penetrates to the centre much faster.

**Practical Guideline (Common Industry Practice):**
- Reheat liquid items (soups, sauces) in batches no more than **10–12 cm deep** in a pot or steam kettle for efficient heat transfer
- Reheat solid items (roasts, casseroles) in pans no more than **5–7 cm deep**
- When batch size cannot be reduced (banquet service for 300 covers), use high-capacity equipment (tilt skillet, steam-jacketed kettle, combi-oven) designed for even high-volume heating

#### Deep Dive: Required Internal Temperatures and Methods

**Internal Temperature Reference Table**

| Food Type | Minimum Safe Reheat Temperature | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| All previously cooked foods (general) | **74°C (165°F)** | Standard for most Canadian jurisdictions |
| Soups, sauces, gravies | **74°C** — typically brought to a full rolling boil (100°C) as a visible confirmation | Boiling provides an additional safety margin |
| Poultry (previously cooked) | **74°C** | Same as general standard |
| Pork, beef, lamb (previously cooked) | **74°C** | Already cooked; 74°C is the reheat standard regardless of original cook temp |
| Stuffed products | **74°C at geometric centre of stuffing** | Stuffing is insulated; takes longer to reheat |
| Sous vide / vacuum-packed (reheat in pouch) | **60°C–74°C depending on product and jurisdiction** | Confirm food safety plan; do not use as hot-holding without confirming temperature |

**Methods for Re-heating Foods**

| Method | Speed | Quality Preservation | Best Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stovetop (direct heat) | Fast | Good for liquids; poor for delicate items | Soups, sauces, gravies |
| Conventional oven (covered) | Moderate | Good for roasts, casseroles | Dense proteins, baked items |
| Combi-oven (steam + convection) | Fast | Excellent — retains moisture | Proteins, banquet trays, vegetables |
| Steam table / bain-marie | Very slow | Good — gentle heat | **Only for holding** food already at 74°C; not for initial reheating |
| Microwave | Fast for small batches | Variable — hot spots and moisture loss | Individual portions, quick service |
| Thermal circulator | Slow but precise | Excellent | Sous vide product; precision reheating of delicate proteins |

> **Common Mistake:** Using a steam table or bain-marie to reheat cold food. These units are designed to **maintain** hot food above 60°C, not to drive cold food up to 74°C. They typically operate at 80–90°C water temperature, which means the food may take 3–4 hours to reach safe internal temperature — far beyond the 2-hour window and a serious food safety hazard.

#### Deep Dive: Regulatory Framework in Canadian Food Service

**HACCP — Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points**

HACCP is a science-based, systematic approach to identifying, evaluating, and controlling food safety hazards. It is the internationally recognized framework used in Canadian commercial kitchens and is required for federally registered food establishments under the Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR).

The seven principles of HACCP:
1. Conduct a hazard analysis
2. Identify Critical Control Points (CCPs)
3. Establish critical limits for each CCP
4. Establish monitoring procedures
5. Establish corrective actions
6. Establish verification procedures
7. Establish record-keeping and documentation

Re-thermalization is a CCP under most HACCP plans. The critical limits are:
- **Temperature:** ≥74°C at the thermal centre
- **Time:** Reached within 2 hours

> **Regulatory Context:** HACCP is mandated for federally regulated food establishments (those processing food for interprovincial or international trade) under the SFCR administered by the Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA). Most provinces also incorporate HACCP principles into provincial food premises regulations, and many larger foodservice operations voluntarily implement HACCP-based food safety plans regardless of regulatory requirement.

**OH&S (Occupational Health and Safety)**

In the context of re-thermalization, OH&S concerns include:
- Burns from steam, hot liquids, and heated containers
- Scalding from splashing during stirring of hot liquids
- Strain injuries from lifting heavy pots of hot product
- Chemical hazards from sanitizing solutions used to clean thermometers (relevant to WHMIS)

**WHMIS (Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System)**

WHMIS applies to cleaning and sanitizing chemicals used on thermometers and equipment between re-thermalization tasks. Proper labelling, storage, and handling of these products is part of the food safety workflow.

**Public Health Regulations**

Each province and territory in Canada has food premises regulations administered by provincial or local public health authorities. These typically specify:
- Minimum reheating temperatures (commonly 74°C)
- Hot-holding temperature requirements (commonly 60°C)
- Maximum time food may remain in the danger zone
- Record-keeping requirements for food safety monitoring

> **Regulatory vs. Industry Practice Distinction:**
> | Type | Description |
> |---|---|
> | **Regulatory requirement** | 74°C internal temperature for reheated food — required by most provincial food premises regulations |
> | **Regulatory requirement** | Thermometer availability and use at CCPs — required under most provincial and HACCP-based food safety plans |
> | **Regulatory requirement** | HACCP documentation (corrective actions, temperature logs) — required for federally registered establishments |
> | **Industry best practice** | Dividing into small batches to meet the 2-hour timeframe — a recommended method, not typically prescribed in regulation |
> | **Industry best practice** | Reheating soups to a rolling boil as a visual confirmation — common practice, exceeds the minimum 74°C requirement |
> | **Operational recommendation** | Reheat only as much as needed for immediate service — not a regulation, but reduces waste and safety risk |

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

| Mistake | Risk | Correct Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Reheating in a steam table or bain-marie | Product may never reach 74°C within 2 hours; bacterial proliferation | Use steam table for **holding only** — reheat by stovetop, oven, or combi-oven first |
| Not stirring soups during stovetop reheating | Uneven heating; centre stays cool while edges boil | Stir frequently; use a full rolling boil as confirmation for soups and liquid items |
| Taking temperature at the surface or liquid top | Gives false reading; missing cold centre | Always probe at the geometric thermal centre |
| Reheating a full 20-litre batch pot | Takes too long; danger zone exposure excessive | Divide into smaller pots; reheat in batches |
| Reheating and then re-cooling to serve tomorrow | Second cooling/reheating cycle increases risk exponentially | Reheat only what will be served; discard unsold reheated product |
| Using infrared (IR) gun to verify reheat temperature | IR reads only surface temperature; interior may still be cold | Use a calibrated probe thermometer inserted to the thermal centre |
| Not recording temperature readings | No documentation if a food safety incident is investigated | Log temperature, time, product, and corrective actions at every CCP check |
| Assuming food is safe because it "looks" or "smells" fine | Bacterial toxins are odourless, colourless, tasteless | Always verify with thermometer; never rely on sensory assessment alone for safety |

## Memory Aids for Apprentices

**The 4-40-140 Rule (Canadian Metric Equivalent: 4-4-74)**
> - **4°C** — maximum cold-holding temperature (fridge)
> - **40 minutes** — some operations use this as an informal single-event time limit in the danger zone for high-risk preparations (not a universal regulation, but a conservative benchmark)
> - **74°C** — minimum reheat temperature

**"Two Hours, Two Inches" (Common Industry Mnemonic)**
> Re-heat within **2 hours**; reheat dense solids in layers no deeper than **5 cm (2 inches)** for safe heat penetration.

**HOTDOG Rule for Steam Tables**
> **H**old, **O**nly, **T**emperature already **D**one, **O**n **G**as/electric steam tables. Steam tables are for **holding** — not for initial reheating.

**"Divide and Conquer" for Large Batches**
> If you can't hit 74°C in time, divide the batch in half. Smaller mass = faster heat penetration = shorter danger zone exposure.

## Food Safety Principles Summary

| Principle | Application to Re-thermalization |
|---|---|
| Time-temperature control | Food must reach 74°C within 2 hours; every minute in 4°C–60°C is a risk window |
| Critical Control Points | Re-thermalization is a CCP; temperature must be measured, recorded, and verified |
| FIFO (First In, First Out) | Reheat oldest stock first to minimize storage duration |
| Corrective action protocol | When CCP is not met: adjust, re-measure, or discard + record + notify |
| Cross-contamination prevention | Sanitize probe between uses; don't reheat in equipment used for raw product without cleaning |

| Single reheat rule | Reheated food that is not served must be discarded — not re-cooled and reheated again |

### Likely Exam Questions and Concepts

1. **What is the minimum internal temperature food must reach when being re-thermalized?**
   → **74°C (165°F)**

2. **What type of thermometer is used to verify internal temperature during re-thermalization?**
   → A calibrated probe (bimetallic stem or digital instant-read thermocouple) inserted to the thermal centre of the food

3. **Why should a steam table not be used to reheat cold food?**
   → Steam tables maintain hot food above 60°C but cannot heat cold food to 74°C quickly enough — the food remains in the danger zone too long, allowing dangerous bacterial multiplication

4. **What is the most effective corrective measure when food is not reheating quickly enough?**
   → Divide product into smaller batches and apply appropriate heat — reduces mass and increases surface-area-to-volume ratio for faster, more even heating

5. **Which bacterium is most associated with failures in re-thermalization of large batch items like stews and gravies?**
   → *Clostridium perfringens* — a spore-forming organism that survives initial cooking, germinates during slow cooling, and grows extremely rapidly if food spends too long in the 43–47°C range during reheating

6. **Can reheating food to 74°C eliminate all food safety risks?**
   → No — heat-stable toxins produced by *Staphylococcus aureus* and *Bacillus cereus* (emetic type) survive normal reheating temperatures. Proper cooling is the critical control for preventing toxin formation; reheating cannot reverse toxin contamination

7. **What records should be kept when a re-thermalization CCP is not met?**
   → Product type and quantity, temperature readings (time and value), corrective action taken, disposition of product (discarded or salvaged), and name of person responsible — as required by HACCP documentation standards

8. **What does HACCP stand for, and why does it apply to re-thermalization?**
   → Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points. Re-thermalization is a CCP because it is a step where a measurable control measure (temperature) can prevent or eliminate a microbiological hazard. HACCP requires identifying this CCP, setting a critical limit (74°C/2 hours), monitoring it, and documenting corrective actions when the limit is not met

9. **Name three regulations included in the jurisdictional regulations for food handling in Canada.**
   → WHMIS, HACCP, OH&S, public health regulations (provincial food premises regulations)

10. **What is the recommended approach when food fails to reach 74°C within the 2-hour window and total danger zone time has been exceeded?**
    → The product must be **discarded**. The incident must be **recorded** in the food safety log. The supervisor must be **informed**. The product must not be held, re-cooled, or served.
