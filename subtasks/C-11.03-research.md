# C-11.03 | Processes Vegetables — Enriched Research Document

**Major Work Activity:** C — Produce
**Task:** C-11 | Prepares Vegetables
**Competency:** Processes vegetables

---

## Overview and Why This Matters

Processing vegetables is one of the most skill-intensive and time-sensitive areas of professional cooking. It encompasses everything from executing precise classical French knife cuts to shaping delicate garnishes, juicing, puréeing, and minimizing food waste. The skills in this competency:

- Directly affect **cooking consistency** — uniform cuts ensure even cooking; uneven cuts produce simultaneously over- and undercooked product
- Drive **food cost outcomes** — maximizing yield from each vegetable and intelligently using trim reduces waste cost
- Determine **visual quality** — classical cuts and shaped garnishes are marks of technical professionalism and are assessed in Red Seal practical evaluations
- Govern **food safety** — improperly stored cut vegetables have dramatically shorter safe shelf lives than whole, uncut product
- Affect **nutrition** — processing method, cut size, and surface area all affect how much nutritional value survives to the plate

Understanding the science and technique behind each knife cut, each processing method, and each quality-maintenance procedure gives an apprentice the ability to work accurately, quickly, and safely across every station.

## C-11.03.01P — Select and Use Tools and Equipment

### Performance Criteria
Tools and equipment are selected and used according to desired results and recipe specifications.

---

### Knife Selection for Vegetable Processing

| Knife | Blade Length | Best Vegetable Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Chef's knife** | 20–25 cm | Primary cut for all classical French cuts; rough chopping; large vegetable sectioning |
| **Paring knife** | 7–10 cm | Turning (tourné), scoring, fine detail work, garnish work, removing eyes from potatoes |
| **Tourné knife (bird's beak)** | 6–8 cm curved | Specifically designed for the 7-sided tourné cut; curved blade follows the oval contour |
| **Cleaver** | Heavy | Breaking through dense, thick-skinned squash, pumpkin, hard root vegetables |
| **Serrated bread knife** | 20–25 cm | Slicing tomatoes and other firm-skinned soft-fleshed vegetables without crushing |

> **Knife Sharpness Standard:** Every classical vegetable cut depends on a sharp knife. A dull knife compresses and tears cells instead of slicing cleanly, causing bruising, enzymatic browning, and jagged, inconsistent cut edges. Hone the knife before beginning any vegetable processing session. This is both a **quality requirement** and an **OH&S requirement** — dull knives require more force and are more likely to slip.

---

### Crinkle Cutters

**What they are:** Hand-held cutting tools with a wavy or serrated blade profile instead of a straight edge.

**What they produce:** Decorative wavy/crinkle edges on cut surfaces — adds visual texture and increases surface area slightly.

**Applications:**
- Crinkle-cut carrot rondelles for crudité platters and garnish
- Crinkle-cut cucumber slices for buffet presentation
- Crinkle-cut potatoes (can also be achieved on a mandolin with a wavy blade attachment for waffle cuts)
- Decorative vegetable chips

**Operational notes:** Crinkle cutters must be kept clean and sharp — the wavy blade is more difficult to sharpen than a straight blade. Food debris accumulates easily in the blade troughs; sanitize thoroughly after use.

---

### Mandoline (Mandolin Slicer)

**What it does:** Produces very thin, extremely uniform slices that would be impossible to achieve consistently with a knife — typically 0.5–5 mm thickness, adjustable.

**Applications:**
- Potato gratin (dauphinois): paper-thin, even slices cook uniformly in cream
- Fennel: paper-thin shaved fennel for salads (texture transformation — raw fennel is too hard to eat without shaving)
- Cucumber ribbons, shaved radish, shaved beet (raw beet salads)
- Vegetable crisps (beet, parsnip, carrot — slice thin, dehydrate or fry)
- Truffle shaving (same tool, extremely fine setting)

> **⚠ CRITICAL OH&S HAZARD — The Mandoline is the Most Dangerous Tool in the Kitchen**
>
> Mandoline injuries are among the most severe and frequent in professional kitchens. The exposed razor-sharp blade operates continuously throughout the slicing stroke, and the handler's hand passes over the blade with each push. A momentary lapse in technique (brief pause in forward momentum, shift in hand angle, or inattention) can result in immediate, deep lacerations — down to bone in severe cases.
>
> **Required safety practices:**
> - **Always use the hand guard** (the food-holding plate that comes with the mandoline) — never bypass it
> - **Wear a cut-resistant glove** on the hand holding the vegetable — this is a best practice even when using the guard
> - Maintain constant, controlled forward pressure; never pause mid-stroke
> - When the vegetable becomes too small to hold safely with the guard (shorter than the guard itself), **stop** — switch to a knife for the remainder or discard the stub
> - Never rush mandoline work; consistent, deliberate motion is faster and safer than hurrying

**Cleaning:** Disassemble before cleaning; handle the blade with a folded towel, not bare fingers; wash with hot soapy water; dry; reassemble. Never put unsheathed mandoline blades in a sink where someone might reach in blindly.

---

### Food Processor

**What it does:** Motorized multi-function tool with interchangeable blades and discs for chopping, slicing, grating, julienning, and puréeing in batch quantities.

| Attachment | Function | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **S-blade (chopping blade)** | Coarse or fine chopping; puréeing | Mirepoix chopping; rough vegetable soup puree; dips |
| **Slicing disc** | Uniform slices (set thickness) | Volume potato slicing; cucumber slices |
| **Shredding/grating disc** | Fine or coarse shreds | Cabbage for coleslaw; beet for salad; potato for rösti |
| **Julienne disc** (if available) | Thin matchstick strips | Volume julienne production |

**Key limitations:**
- Cannot achieve the precision of hand-cut classical cuts — food processor produces irregular results unsuitable for fine plating
- Over-processing risk: running too long turns vegetable to mush; pulse in short bursts and check frequently
- Underfilling creates uneven results (blade only contacts food at its base); do not fill less than ¼ capacity
- All parts (bowl, lid, blades) must be washed and sanitized between uses and between different food types

---

### Juicer

| Type | Mechanism | Best For | Nutrient Retention |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Centrifugal** | High-speed spinning grate; fast extraction | Most vegetables; production volume | Moderate — some heat from friction; some foam/oxidation |
| **Cold press (masticating)** | Slow crushing and pressing | Maximum quality; delicate produce; green juice | Higher — minimal heat; less oxidation |
| **Commercial citrus press** | Direct pressure on citrus halves | Lemon, lime, orange juice service | High |

**Vegetable juice applications in commercial kitchens:**
- Carrot juice: sauces, soups, glazes, nutritional side items
- Beet juice: natural colouring agent for pastas, risotto, reduction sauces
- Celery juice: cocktail mix, health-focused menu items
- Tomato water: obtained by straining pulped, salted tomatoes through cheesecloth; ultra-clean flavour; used in refined consommé-style applications and cocktails

> **Food Safety:** Juice residue ferments rapidly at room temperature. Clean juicer components **immediately** after use. Fresh-pressed vegetable juices must be refrigerated at ≤4°C and used within 24–48 hours; or pasteurized for extended service.

---

### Blender (Countertop and Immersion)

**Countertop blender:**
- Used for: smooth vegetable purées, soups, sauces; best for producing silky-smooth texture in volume
- Produces finer, smoother result than a food processor

> **⚠ Hot Liquid Hazard:** When blending **hot vegetable soups or purées** in a countertop blender:
> 1. Fill the blender jug no more than **half full**
> 2. Remove the centre cap from the lid and hold a folded kitchen towel over the opening to allow steam to vent
> 3. Start on the **lowest speed**; increase gradually
> 4. Never seal the lid completely on hot liquid — trapped steam creates rapid pressure build-up that can explosively blow the lid off, causing severe burns
>
> Alternatively: allow hot liquid to cool to approximately 60°C before blending with a sealed lid, or use an immersion blender.

**Immersion (stick) blender:**
- Used for: blending directly in the pot; less cleanup; ideal for soups and sauces
- Splashing hazard: keep blade submerged at all times while operating; lifting blade above the liquid surface causes hot liquid to spray
- The blade must be fully below the surface before starting and must be powered off before lifting

### Performance Criteria
Processing methods are identified according to recipe specifications and desired results.

### Method 1: Cutting (Classical French Knife Cuts)

The classical French knife cut system is the foundation of professional vegetable preparation. Mastery of these cuts is directly assessed in Red Seal practical examinations.

**The Progression Principle — Plank → Stick → Cube:**

All classical dice and strip cuts are derived from the same three-step process:
1. **Plank**: slice the vegetable into flat planks of the required thickness
2. **Stick**: stack planks and cut into sticks of the required width
3. **Cube**: align sticks and cut across at the same width to produce cubes

The only variable between a julienne and a brunoise is whether you stop at the stick (julienne) or continue to the cube (brunoise).

---

#### Julienne

| Attribute | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Dimensions** | 3 mm × 3 mm × 5–6 cm |
| **Shape** | Very thin matchstick strips |
| **Method** | Planks 3 mm thick → stacks → 3 mm wide strips → trim to 5–6 cm length |
| **Classic vegetables** | Carrots, celery, leeks, peppers, zucchini, beets, daikon |
| **Applications** | Salads, stir-fry, Asian noodle dishes, garnish, bouquet of vegetable garnish, raw crudité |
| **Cooking time** | 1–2 minutes in boiling water; 30–60 seconds sautéed in hot oil |
| **Why uniformity matters** | All 3 mm strips cook simultaneously; a mix of 2 mm and 5 mm strips means some burn while others are raw |

---

#### Brunoise

| Attribute | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Dimensions** | 3 mm × 3 mm × 3 mm |
| **Shape** | Very fine, tiny cubes |
| **Method** | Julienne strips → rotate 90° → cut across at 3 mm intervals |
| **Classic vegetables** | Carrots, celery, onion, leeks; any vegetable that can be julienned |
| **Applications** | Mirepoix brunoise for consommé, fine soups; sauce garnish; chilled dishes; vinaigrettes |
| **Fine brunoise** | 1.5 mm × 1.5 mm × 1.5 mm — even smaller; purely decorative garnish for consommé |

> **Technique Note:** Brunoise requires extreme consistency in the julienne step. If julienne strips vary in thickness, the brunoise cubes will be irregular — some too small (dissolve in heat), some too large (undercooked). Practice julienne consistency as the prerequisite to mastering brunoise.

---

#### Batonnet (Baton)

| Attribute | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Dimensions** | 6 mm × 6 mm × 5–7 cm |
| **Shape** | Thicker matchstick; sometimes called "French-cut" |
| **Method** | Planks 6 mm thick → 6 mm wide strips → trim to 5–7 cm |
| **Classic vegetables** | Carrots, celery, potatoes, turnips, parsnips |
| **Applications** | Crudité platters; sautéed or steamed vegetable sides; stir-fry (larger); vegetable garnish on plated dishes |
| **Note** | "Batonnet" in French means "little stick"; often referred to simply as "baton" in Canadian kitchens |

---

#### Dice (Three Sizes)

| Size | Dimensions | Derived From | Applications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Small dice** | 6 mm × 6 mm × 6 mm | Batonnet → cross-cut | Soups, sauces, relishes; faster cooking than medium dice |
| **Medium dice** | 1.2 cm × 1.2 cm × 1.2 cm | Medium sticks | Most common "diced vegetable" in cooking; stews, sautés, roasted vegetable sides |
| **Large dice** | 2 cm × 2 cm × 2 cm | Large planks and sticks | Rustic stews; long-braise preparations; grilled vegetable skewers |

> **Memory Aid — Cut Size Sequence (smallest to largest):**
> **Fine Brunoise → Brunoise → Small Dice → Medium Dice → Large Dice**
> 1.5 mm → 3 mm → 6 mm → 1.2 cm → 2 cm
>
> Each step roughly doubles the dimension of the previous.

---

#### Additional Cuts (Beyond the Core Four)

| Cut | Description | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Chiffonade** | Thin strips from rolled leafy vegetables; 1–3 mm wide | Cabbage, leafy greens, basil; garnish; salads |
| **Rondelle** | Round disc slices; straight (perpendicular to length) or diagonal (bias) | Carrots, cucumbers, zucchini; soups, stews, garnish |
| **Oblique/roll cut** | Angled bias cuts rotating the vegetable 90° between each cut | Cylindrical vegetables (carrots, parsnips); creates large irregular pieces with maximum surface area for browning; Chinese and roasting preparations |
| **Tourné** | Seven-sided football/barrel shape, 5–6 cm long; requires a tourné knife | Classic French garnish; demonstrates technical skill |
| **Paysanne** | Thin, flat, irregular pieces (squares, triangles, circles) ~1 cm; thinner than dice | Rustic soups, minestrone; country-style dishes |
| **Mince (haché)** | Very fine, irregular chop — finer than small dice | Garlic, shallots, ginger, aromatic vegetables; sauces |

### Method 2: Acidulating

**What it is:** Adding acid (lemon juice, white vinegar, or citric acid) to water or applying directly to cut vegetable surfaces to prevent enzymatic browning.

**Science:** Cut surfaces of browning-prone vegetables (artichokes, celeriac, parsnips, salsify, avocado) are immediately exposed to oxygen. Polyphenol oxidase (PPO) enzyme reacts with phenolic compounds and oxygen to produce brown melanin pigments within seconds to minutes. Acid inhibits PPO by lowering the pH below the enzyme's optimal range.

**Application:**
- **Acidulated water holding bath:** 1–2 tablespoons of lemon juice or white wine vinegar per litre of cold water; immediately submerge cut vegetables
- **Direct acid application:** brushing lemon juice directly on cut surfaces (avocado, artichoke hearts)
- **For potatoes:** cold water immersion (without acid) is sufficient — removes surface starch and limits oxygen contact

**Vegetables requiring acidulated water:**

| Vegetable | Reason | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Artichoke hearts | Extremely high PPO activity; browns within seconds | Rub with lemon immediately; hold in acidulated water |
| Celeriac | High PPO activity | Immediately into acidulated water after peeling/cutting |
| Parsnips | Moderate PPO activity | Acidulated water if not cooking immediately |
| Salsify | Very high PPO activity | Peel and immediately into acidulated water |
| Potatoes (cut) | Moderate starch browning | Cold water immersion (no acid needed) |

---

### Method 3: Juicing

**What it is:** Extracting the liquid from vegetables using mechanical pressure or centrifugal force.

**Yield considerations:**
- Hard vegetables (carrots, beets): lower juice yield; require significant pressure; centrifugal juicer works well
- High-water-content vegetables (cucumber, celery): higher yield; easier extraction
- Leafy greens (spinach, kale): very low yield from centrifugal; masticating juicer preferred

**Commercial kitchen applications:**
- Vegetable juice as a cooking liquid (beet juice in risotto; carrot juice in glazes)
- Natural food colouring (beet juice = deep red/purple)
- Tomato water (cold press through cheesecloth) for refined sauces and cocktails
- Juice reductions for concentrated vegetable flavour

---

### Method 4: Puréeing

**What it is:** Converting cooked (occasionally raw) vegetables into a smooth, uniform paste or liquid.

**Texture spectrum:**

| Consistency | Equipment | Technique | Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rough/rustic** | Food mill; potato masher | Press through food mill; hand mash | Chunky bisque; rustic potato side; textured sauces |
| **Smooth** | Food processor; standard blender | Process until uniform | Standard vegetable soups; hummus |
| **Ultra-smooth** | High-speed blender (Vitamix) | Blend at high speed; strain through fine chinois or tamis | Refined restaurant soups; silky squash purée; modernist applications |
| **Foam/espuma** | ISI siphon after puréeing | Blend smooth; strain; chill; charge with N₂O in siphon | Modernist garnish and presentations |

**The starch content effect on purée texture:**
- High-starch vegetables (potatoes): blend with a **food mill** or **ricer** — high-speed blending ruptures starch granules, releasing free starch, creating a gluey, paste-like texture (over-worked mashed potatoes). Never blend potatoes in a food processor or high-speed blender.
- Low-starch vegetables (carrots, squash, peas): can use blender or food processor without this risk

> **Fat enrichment in vegetable purées:** Many professional purées are enriched with butter or cream at the end of puréeing — a technique called **monter au beurre** in French. Fat coats starch molecules and acts as a lubricant between protein and starch structures, producing a richer, silkier mouthfeel. A standard restaurant-quality carrot or celeriac purée may contain 10–15% butter by weight.

---

### Method 5: Shaping

**What it is:** Forming vegetables into decorative geometric or organic shapes for garnish and presentation.

**Classic shaping techniques:**

| Shape | Technique | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Tourné** | 7-sided football/barrel using tourné knife; 5–6 cm length | Classic French garnish alongside protein; demonstrates technical level |
| **Parisienne spheres** | Parisienne scoop turned in a firm vegetable (potato, turnip, carrot, melon) | Elegant garnish; compressed vegetable salads |
| **Cucumber fans** | Thin bias slices not cut all the way through; fan open | Cold platter garnish; buffet presentation |
| **Carrot curls** | Paper-thin peeled strips; roll tightly; secure with toothpick; chill in ice water | Garnish; salad presentation |
| **Radish roses** | Score cross-hatch cuts from tip toward stem; chill in ice water to open | Buffet and cold platter garnish |
| **Spring onion curls/brushes** | Score green tops multiple times lengthwise; chill in ice water to curl | Chinese and pan-Asian garnish |
| **Tomato rose** | Very thin continuous spiral peel from a tomato, curled to form a rose shape | Classic cold plate garnish |

> **Ice Water in Garnish Work:** Cold water (ideally with ice) is used extensively in vegetable garnish work because:
> 1. Cold stimulates osmosis — cut cells absorb water and cells re-hydrate, causing the scored sections to open and curl
> 2. Cold slows enzymatic browning
> 3. Crisp texture is maintained during advance preparation
>
> Most cut garnishes can be prepared hours before service and held in ice water in the refrigerator, ready for plating.

---

### Method 6: Shaving

**What it is:** Producing extremely thin, nearly translucent slices or ribbons from vegetables, typically using a mandoline or very careful knife work (or a Y-peeler for ribbons).

**Applications:**
- Shaved fennel (salads — the thinness makes raw fennel tender and delicate rather than hard and fibrous)
- Shaved radish (garnish and salads)
- Asparagus ribbons (use Y-peeler along the length of a raw asparagus spear)
- Shaved raw beet (salads; pairs with goat cheese or citrus)
- Shaved Parmesan over vegetable dishes (shaving tool applies to cheese as well)

**Why shaving changes eating quality:** Shaving raw vegetables that would otherwise be too firm or fibrous to eat comfortably reduces the cellular structure to such a thin layer that chewing resistance is minimal — the vegetable becomes palatably tender without cooking.

---

### Method 7: Grating

**What it is:** Reducing vegetables to fine shreds, threads, or powder using a box grater, microplane, or food processor grating disc.

| Grating Style | Tool | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Coarse grate** | Box grater, coarse side | Rösti (potato); coleslaw; carrot rémoulade; zucchini bread; latkes |
| **Fine grate** | Box grater, fine side | Horseradish; ginger; hard cheese over vegetables |
| **Microplane/rasp** | Rasp grater | Garlic paste (grated garlic); ginger; nutmeg; citrus zest combined with dishes |
| **Food processor disc** | Shredding disc | Volume grating for coleslaw, rösti, carrot salad production |

**Key quality rule for grated potato (rösti, latkes):**
After grating, excess moisture must be removed — **squeeze grated potato in a clean towel until most liquid is pressed out**. Excess moisture prevents the Maillard browning reaction from occurring (surface steams instead of browns) and produces a soft, soggy product instead of a crispy one.

---

### Method 8: Slicing

**What it is:** Cutting vegetables into flat, uniform cross-sections at a consistent thickness — the most universal processing method.

**Thickness applications:**

| Thickness | Tool | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Paper-thin (0.5–1 mm)** | Mandoline | Potato gratin; vegetable crisps; garnish |
| **Thin (2–3 mm)** | Mandoline or sharp knife | Cucumber rounds; radish; fennel; potato chips |
| **Standard (4–6 mm)** | Knife | Carrot rounds; zucchini; onion rings |
| **Thick (8–15 mm)** | Knife | Onion rings for caramelizing; tomato steak cuts; squash planks |

**Bias (diagonal) slicing:**
Cutting at a 45° angle to the vegetable's length produces oval slices with greater surface area than straight cross-sections. This is widely used for carrots, asparagus, and zucchini — the increased surface area means more contact with the pan surface for browning, and the angular cut is visually more interesting.

---

### Method 9: Drying/Dehydrating

**What it is:** Removing moisture from vegetables to change texture, intensify flavour, and extend shelf life.

| Technique | Temperature/Method | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Oven-drying (slow)** | 80–100°C; 2–6 hours | Oven-dried tomatoes; dehydrated mushroom; root vegetable chips |
| **Dehydrator** | 55–70°C; 4–12 hours | Uniform, consistent results; vegetable powders; concentrated flavours |
| **Air-drying** | Room temperature; several days | Herbs; garlic |
| **Flash-frying (a different approach to "drying")** | 185°C oil; seconds | Crispy vegetable garnish — beet chips, leek strands, parsnip crisps |

**Why dehydrating intensifies flavour:** Removing water concentrates all soluble flavour compounds (sugars, acids, aromatic molecules) — 1 kg of fresh tomatoes produces approximately 100–150 g of oven-dried tomatoes with dramatically concentrated umami and sweetness.

---

### Texture Modification Methods

The knowledge objectives include identifying methods used to **modify textures of vegetables**:

| Desired Texture | Method | Science |
|---|---|---|
| **Crisp-tender (blanched)** | Blanch in boiling salted water; shock in ice water | Heat gelatinizes some starch; ice bath halts cooking; maintains colour and nutrients; partial cooking for later finishing |
| **Caramelized/soft** | Roast at 200–220°C; long sauté | Maillard reaction and caramelization convert starch to sugar, create complex brown pigments |
| **Silky smooth** | Purée and strain through fine tamis | Cell walls completely broken; fibre removed; emulsified with fat |
| **Pickled/acidulated** | Pickle in acid brine | Acid weakens pectin in cell walls; osmosis draws moisture from cells; texture softens slightly; colour changes |
| **Compressed** | Vacuum-seal with flavouring liquid | Vacuum removes air from cellular structure; liquid fills the space; rapid flavour absorption; translucent appearance |
| **Roasted firm** | High heat roasting with fat; do not cover | Maillard browning; Outer caramelization; interior still has structure |

## C-11.03.03P — Maintain Uniform Cuts

### Performance Criteria
Uniform cuts to vegetable are maintained for consistency in appearance and cooking.

---

### Why Uniformity Is Non-Negotiable

| Effect of Uniform Cuts | Effect of Non-Uniform Cuts |
|---|---|
| All pieces cook at the same rate | Small pieces are overcooked while large pieces are raw — simultaneously |
| Even colour development on all surfaces | Uneven browning; burnt small pieces |
| Professional, consistent plating | Unprofessional appearance; guest perception of quality reduced |
| Accurate cooking time estimates | Inconsistent service timing; stations miss pick-up windows |
| Accurate food costing (yield calculations) | Inconsistent yield makes food cost calculation unreliable |

**Building uniform cut skill:**

- During training: measure cuts with a ruler periodically to develop accuracy awareness
- Work to the **plank standard first** — if the plank is not exactly 3 mm, the julienne cannot be 3 mm
- Square up vegetables properly before cutting — create a flat base before proceeding
- Use a **bench scraper** to collect and organize cut pieces without disturbing them
- Maintain the knife's rocking point (the tip stays on the board for most cuts) for consistent slice thickness

**Squaring technique (creating a rectangular block):**
Most vegetables are round or irregular. To make uniform cuts, first trim four sides to create a rectangular block (a process called **blocking out** or **squaring**). The trim from this step is usable for stocks, soups, or purees.

## C-11.03.05P — Cut and Shape Vegetable for Garnishes

### Performance Criteria
Vegetable is cut and shaped for garnishes according to desired result and appearance.

---

### Principles of Vegetable Garnish Work

1. **Garnishes must be edible** — an inedible decoration on a plate is an amateur mistake; every garnish element should contribute flavour, texture, or visual context
2. **Garnishes must be related to the dish** — a beet chip on a beef dish makes sense; a random decorative carrot curl that has no connection to the dish's flavours does not
3. **Garnishes must be prepared well in advance** — delicate carved or shaped garnishes require time in ice water to set and open; rush work is visible in the result
4. **Uniformity applies to garnish work** as much as to functional cuts — three carrot curls on a plate must be the same size

**Classic garnish prep guide:**

| Garnish | Vegetable | Technique | Advance Prep Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tourné vegetables** | Potato, carrot, turnip, zucchini | 7-sided turning with tourné knife; 5–6 cm length | Up to 24 hours (in acidulated water for browning vegetables) |
| **Radish rose** | Red radish | Score cross-hatch pattern from tip toward base; do not cut through stem | 30–60 minutes in cold water to open |
| **Spring onion curl** | Green onion/scallion | Score green top 5–6 times lengthwise; refrigerate in ice water | 30–60 minutes |
| **Carrot curl** | Carrot | Peel long thin strips with peeler; roll tightly; pin with toothpick; refrigerate | 30–60 minutes in ice water |
| **Cucumber fan** | Cucumber | Thin diagonal slices not quite cut through at one end; spread open | Immediate or 15 minutes |
| **Tomato rose** | Tomato | Continuous thin spiral peel; roll tightly to form rose | Immediate use |
| **Vegetable crisp** | Beet, parsnip, carrot | Mandoline slice paper-thin; toss in oil; dehydrate at 80°C or flash fry at 185°C | Up to 24 hours stored in dry, airtight container |

## C-11.03.06P — Maintain Quality of Vegetable During Processing

### Performance Criteria
Quality of vegetable is maintained during processing.

---

### Quality Maintenance Principles

**Temperature control during prep:**
- Maintain cold work surface environment — in hot kitchens, wet a cloth with cold water and wipe the cutting board periodically to keep the surface cool
- Return processed vegetables to cold storage between batches
- Do not leave cut, high-moisture vegetables at room temperature for more than 30 minutes (they warm into the Temperature Danger Zone — 4°C to 60°C)

**Sharp tools:**
As covered in the cleaning competency: sharp tools produce clean cellular cuts, minimizing PPO enzyme activation, bruising, and cellular fluid release. Dull tools compress and tear, triggering enzymatic browning within minutes.

**Batch processing strategy:**
- Process in quantities aligned to hold time — if cut vegetables hold quality for 4 hours at 4°C, do not cut 8 hours of supply in one batch
- For service: time the processing of the most delicate items (tomato, herbs, microgreens) to the closest period before pick-up

**Preventing browning in cut vegetables:**
- Browning-prone vegetables → acidulated water or direct lemon application immediately
- Potatoes → cold water immersion (see C-11.02 section)
- Cut avocado, artichoke, celeriac: work quickly; acid immediately; cover with plastic wrap pressed directly onto surface (excludes oxygen)

**Covering cut surfaces:**
- When cut vegetables must be held briefly during a prep session (e.g., while finishing a batch): cover with damp paper towel or plastic wrap pressed to the surface
- This limits oxygen contact (slowing PPO browning) and moisture loss

## C-11.03.07P — Minimize Waste and Utilize Trim

### Performance Criteria
Waste is minimized and trim is utilized to maximize yield.

---

### Yield Management Framework

**Yield percentage** determines how much usable product a cook actually gets from each kilogram of purchased vegetable:

> **Yield % = (EP weight ÷ AP weight) × 100**

| Vegetable | Approximate Yield % | Primary Trim Loss |
|---|---|---|
| Potatoes (peeled) | 78–82% | Skin, eyes, blemishes |
| Carrots (topped, peeled) | 80–84% | Tops, skin |
| Broccoli (florets only) | 55–60% | Stems, leaves, main stalk |
| Cauliflower (florets only) | 50–55% | Core, leaves, outer stem |
| Onions (peeled, trimmed) | 88–92% | Outer skins, root end, top |
| Leeks (white and light green only) | 40–50% | Dark green tops, roots, outer layer |
| Fennel (trimmed bulb) | 65–70% | Fronds, stalks, outer tough layer |
| Cabbage (cored, outer leaves removed) | 75–80% | Outer leaves, core |

> **Costing Principle:** If a vegetable has a 70% yield, you actually need to purchase 1.43 kg to get 1 kg of usable product. This significantly affects recipe costing. A chef who calculates cost on AP price without accounting for yield is systematically undercosting every dish using that vegetable.

---

### Trim Utilization — Turning Waste into Value

The professional kitchen operates on the principle that **trim has value**. Every trimmed vegetable part should be evaluated for secondary use before being discarded.

| Vegetable | Trim Portion | Secondary Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Carrot tops** | Feathery green fronds | Herb-like garnish; carrot top pesto; chimichurri |
| **Carrot peel** | Thin skin strips | Vegetable stock; dehydrate for powder |
| **Celery leaves** | Inner leafy tops | Stock flavouring; salad garnish; herb-like addition to soups |
| **Onion skins and root ends** | Outer papery skins and tops | Stock (skins add colour and flavour to dark stocks) |
| **Broccoli/cauliflower stems** | Inner white stems (after outer skin removed) | Slice thinly and sauté; add to stir-fry; purée into soup |
| **Asparagus trimmings** | Tough lower stalk ends | Asparagus bisque or cream soup |
| **Leek green tops** | Dark green upper portions | Bouquet garni wrapping; stock flavouring (strong flavour; discard after) |
| **Fennel fronds and stalks** | Top fronds and tough outer stalks | Fronds: garnish; stalks: stock or braising aromatics |
| **Corn cobs** | After kernels removed | Corn stock; smoked corn stock; corn milk (simmer in cream) |
| **Tomato cores and seeds** | Core and gel with seeds | Tomato water (strain through cheesecloth); added back to sauces |
| **Potato peels** | Outer skin (washed) | Baked/fried potato skin chips (if clean); stock |
| **Beet skin and trimmings** | After roasting | Dehydrate to powder for garnish; stock (note: will heavily colour the liquid) |
| **Mushroom stems** | Removed from caps for stuffed mushroom prep | Mushroom duxelles; stocks; soups |

**Trim labelling and management:**
- In a properly organized kitchen, two trim containers are maintained: **stock-quality trim** (clean, no spoilage, suitable for stock) and **discard** (spoiled, heavily soiled, truly non-usable)
- Stock-quality trim containers must be labelled, dated, and stored at ≤4°C until used; maximum 2 days
- Trim used for stock should produce a noticeably flavour-rich stock — bland trim from water-content-only vegetables is not worth the storage space

## C-11.03.08P — Store Cut Vegetables

### Performance Criteria
Cut vegetables are stored according to type and procedure to preserve quality.

---

### Why Cut Vegetables Have Shorter Safe Shelf Life

Cut vegetables have significantly higher spoilage risk than whole vegetables because:
1. **Surface area exposed to oxygen** — PPO browning and oxidative deterioration accelerates
2. **Cell walls ruptured** — intracellular fluid released; bacterial nutrients more accessible
3. **Protective skin removed** — natural barrier against pathogens gone
4. **Increased water activity at cut surfaces** — ideal for microbial growth

**The Temperature Danger Zone (TDZ: 4°C–60°C) applies to cut vegetables** when they contain sufficient nutrients and moisture to support bacterial growth. Cut vegetables stored at room temperature for extended periods present a genuine food safety risk — particularly if combined with protein components or dressings that further support growth.

---

### Secondary Storage Procedures for Cut Vegetables

| Vegetable / Cut Type | Temperature | Container | Moisture Control | Maximum Hold |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Julienne/brunoise carrots** | ≤4°C | Airtight container | Slightly damp paper towel underneath | 24–48 hours |
| **Diced/cut onions** | ≤4°C | Sealed airtight container (strong odour — prevents cross-absorption) | Dry; no water needed | 24–36 hours |
| **Cut potatoes** | ≤4°C | Submerged in cold water | Water bath prevents browning and contact with oxygen | 24 hours max; change water daily |
| **Brunoise mirepoix (mixed)** | ≤4°C | Airtight container | Dry paper towel lining | 24 hours |
| **Shaved fennel** | ≤4°C | Airtight container | Toss in small amount of lemon juice first to slow browning | Same day; 12 hours max |
| **Blanched vegetables (ready to finish)** | ≤4°C | Airtight container; well drained | Dry before storing | 24–48 hours |
| **Julienne/brunoise beets** | ≤4°C | Separate sealed container (heavy staining) | Slightly damp; separate from other vegetables | 24–48 hours |
| **Tourné vegetables** | ≤4°C (in acidulated water if browning-prone) | In cold water (turnip, potato, celeriac) | Acidulated water for browning-prone; plain water for others | 24 hours |
| **Puréed vegetables** | ≤4°C | Airtight container; plastic wrap pressed to surface | Cover surface to prevent skin formation (oxidative layer) | 24–48 hours |
| **Vegetable chips/crisps (fried or dehydrated)** | Room temperature | Airtight container; cool dry location | Must be completely dry; any residual moisture causes sogginess within hours | 12–24 hours (fried); 2–5 days (dehydrated) |

---

### Labelling Requirements for Stored Cut Vegetables

> **Industry Best Practice (required in most Canadian commercial kitchen operations and explicitly by HACCP programs):**
> Every container of stored cut vegetables must be labelled with:
> - **Product name** (e.g., "julienne carrots," "brunoise mirepoix")
> - **Preparation date AND time**
> - **Use-by date AND time** (calculated from the appropriate hold time for that vegetable)
> - Preparer's name (required in some jurisdictions and HACCP-managed operations)
>
> Unlabelled containers in a walk-in must be treated as unknown age and either used immediately (if they look and smell fresh) or discarded if there is any doubt.

### Common Mistakes in Vegetable Processing

| Mistake | Consequence | Correct Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Non-uniform cuts | Some pieces overcooked, others raw; unprofessional appearance | Practice the plank → stick → cube progression; develop accuracy through repetition |
| Using a dull knife for julienne/brunoise | Tearing cells; browning; inconsistent sizes; injury risk | Hone knife before every cutting session |
| Failing to square up the vegetable before cutting | Irregular shapes, uneven thicknesses | Create a flat base on vegetable before starting cuts |
| Using mandoline without hand guard and cut-resistant glove | Severe laceration — one of the most common serious kitchen injuries | Always use hand guard AND cut-resistant glove; stop before vegetable stub becomes too small |
| Blending hot liquids in sealed blender | Steam pressure explosion; severe burns | Fill only half; remove center cap; start on low; use towel over opening |
| Blending high-starch potato in food processor or high-speed blender | Gluey, paste-like overworked mashed potato | Use food mill or ricer for potatoes |
| Not acidulating browning-prone vegetables immediately | Rapid, irreversible browning | Have acidulated water bath ready before cutting artichokes, celeriac, etc. |
| Leaving cut vegetables at room temperature during prep | TDZ exposure; bacterial growth accelerates | Keep in cold water or return to refrigerator in batches |
| Discarding all vegetable trim | Food cost waste; missed flavour opportunities | Evaluate trim for stock, purée, or garnish use; label and store |
| Storing cut onions in non-airtight containers | Onion odour penetrates all other food in the refrigerator | Always seal cut onions in airtight containers |
| Wet vegetables stored in sealed containers | Trapped moisture → rapid bacterial growth and sliming | Dry cut vegetables before sealing; paper towel liner absorbs excess |
| Not labelling cut vegetable containers | FIFO failure; safety risk from unknown age | Label every container with product, prep date/time, use-by |

### Exam Q&A — 12 Practice Questions

**Q1: What are the dimensions of a julienne cut and a brunoise cut? How is brunoise derived from julienne?**
A: Julienne: 3 mm × 3 mm × 5–6 cm (thin matchstick strips). Brunoise: 3 mm × 3 mm × 3 mm (tiny cubes). Brunoise is derived from julienne by rotating the julienne strips 90° and cross-cutting at 3 mm intervals — so a brunoise is effectively a julienne cut a third time in the remaining dimension.

**Q2: Why is uniform cut size critical for vegetable cooking, beyond just appearance?**
A: Uniform cut size ensures all pieces cook at exactly the same rate. If a batch contains both 2 mm and 6 mm julienne strips, the smaller pieces are overcooked and mushy while the larger pieces remain underdone — all at the same moment. Uniform cuts are a functional cooking requirement, not only an aesthetic one.

**Q3: What is the most critical safety requirement when using a mandoline, and why is this injury so common?**
A: Always use the hand guard AND a cut-resistant glove. Mandoline injuries are common because the blade is continuously exposed and the operator's hand passes directly over it with each stroke. A momentary lapse — pausing mid-stroke, shifting grip angle, or losing attention — results in immediate deep laceration. The hand guard must be used even when it feels awkward; the operator must stop before the vegetable becomes too short to hold safely with the guard.

**Q4: Why should potatoes never be puréed in a food processor or high-speed blender?**
A: High-speed mechanical action ruptures potato starch granules and releases free amylose starch into the mixture. This free starch forms a tight gel that creates a gluey, paste-like texture — called overworked or "glue-pot" mashed potatoes — that cannot be corrected. Potatoes must be processed through a food mill or ricer, which applies gentle pressure without agitating the starch structure.

**Q5: What is the correct safety procedure for blending a hot vegetable soup in a countertop blender?**
A: Fill the blender jug no more than half full; remove the centre cap from the lid; hold a folded towel over the opening to allow steam to vent while preventing splatter; start on the lowest speed. Never seal the lid completely on hot liquid — steam builds pressure and can explosively lift the lid, causing severe burns.

**Q6: What is acidulated water, which vegetables require it, and why?**
A: Acidulated water is cold water with a small amount of acid (lemon juice or white vinegar). It is required for artichokes, celeriac, parsnips, and salsify — vegetables with high polyphenol oxidase (PPO) enzyme activity that brown within seconds of cutting. The acid lowers pH below PPO's optimal range, inhibiting the browning reaction. Cut vegetables must be placed in acidulated water immediately.

**Q7: How do you calculate yield percentage, and why does it matter for food costing?**
A: Yield % = (EP weight ÷ AP weight) × 100. If broccoli has a 60% yield, you must purchase 1 kg to obtain 600 g of usable florets. Failing to account for yield means every dish using that vegetable is systematically undercosted — actual food cost is higher than the calculated cost if costing is based on AP price alone.

**Q8: What is the plank → stick → cube progression and how does it apply to all classical dice cuts?**
A: All classical dice cuts are made by first slicing the vegetable into flat planks of the desired thickness, then cutting the planks into sticks of the same width, then cutting the sticks into cubes of the same dimension. This produces geometrically uniform cubes at any size — small (6 mm), medium (1.2 cm), or large (2 cm) — depending on the thickness established in the first plank step.

**Q9: What is the difference between a batonnet and a julienne cut?**
A: Batonnet: 6 mm × 6 mm × 5–7 cm — thicker, larger sticks used for crudité, vegetable sides, and stir-fry with larger components. Julienne: 3 mm × 3 mm × 5–6 cm — thin matchstick strips used for salads, garnish, and delicate stir-fry. Both are stick cuts but at different dimensions; batonnet is twice the thickness and width of julienne.

**Q10: Give four examples of vegetable trim and describe a secondary use for each.**
A: (1) Broccoli stems — peel outer layer; slice thinly; use in stir-fry or purée into soup. (2) Leek green tops — use as flavouring in stocks and as the outer layer of a bouquet garni. (3) Corn cobs — simmer in cream or stock to produce corn-flavoured liquid for sauces and soups. (4) Fennel fronds — use as an herb-like garnish in salads and on plated fish dishes.

**Q11: Why must cut onions be stored in an airtight container?**
A: Onions release volatile sulphur compounds (thiosulfinates and their breakdown products) that readily permeate all other foods stored in the same refrigerator. Airtight storage prevents these strong odours from being absorbed into milk, cream, butter, and other delicate items. Onion-scented butter or cream cannot be used in service.

**Q12: What is the maximum hold time for cut potatoes stored in cold water, and what are the two food quality purposes of the water bath?**
A: Maximum 24 hours with daily water changes. The two quality purposes are: (1) Cold water immersion limits oxygen contact at the cut surface, significantly slowing enzymatic browning (PPO reaction). (2) It washes surface starch from the cut surface, which improves the texture of boiled potatoes (less gluey) and increases crispiness in fried applications (less surface starch = less steam barrier, better browning).
