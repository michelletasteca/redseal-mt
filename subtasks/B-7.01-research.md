## Overview: Why Recipes Matter in Professional Kitchens

A recipe is far more than a list of ingredients — it is a **standardized system** that delivers consistency, controls food cost, ensures food safety, and communicates intent across an entire team. In commercial kitchens, a recipe is a **legal and operational document** that also supports allergen management, HACCP compliance, nutritional disclosure, and training.

Following a recipe precisely is not about creative limitation. It is the professional discipline that makes large-scale, consistent, safe food production possible. A Red Seal Cook must understand not only how to follow a recipe, but **why** each step is sequenced the way it is, how to scale production intelligently, and when substitutions are appropriate.

### B-7.01.01P — Follow Recipe Sequence

**Performance Criteria:** Follow recipe sequence
**Evidence of Attainment:** Recipe sequence is followed according to recipe specifications

---

#### Why Sequence Matters

A recipe's sequence is not arbitrary — each step is designed to optimize:

| Purpose | Example |
|---|---|
| **Food safety** | Cook proteins before adding delicate vegetables to ensure safe internal temperatures |
| **Flavour development** | Brown aromatics before adding liquid (Maillard reaction requires dry heat) |
| **Texture integrity** | Add starch slurries at the end to prevent over-thickening during cooking |
| **Emulsion stability** | Add oil to egg yolks slowly to prevent breaking a mayonnaise |
| **Leavening chemistry** | Fold whipped egg whites at the end to preserve air incorporation |
| **Cross-contamination control** | Work with ready-to-eat foods before raw proteins in prep sequence |

> **Operational Recommendation:** Read the entire recipe before beginning. Mise en place (everything in its place) — all ingredients measured, prepped, and organized before starting — is the professional standard.

---

#### The Standardized Recipe: Structure and Purpose

A **standardized recipe** is one that has been tested, adjusted, and validated for a specific kitchen operation, equipment set, and customer base. It consistently produces the intended quantity, quality, and cost.

**Components of a Complete Standardized Recipe:**

| Component | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Recipe name and code | Identification and menu linking |
| Total yield and portion size | Quantity control and portioning |
| Ingredient list with precise quantities | Consistency and purchasing |
| Preparation method (step-by-step) | Consistency, training |
| Equipment required | Preparation and safety |
| Internal cooking temperatures | Food safety / HACCP CCP |
| Plating/presentation instructions | Quality and consistency |
| Critical allergens | Allergen management / legal disclosure |
| Storage instructions | Food safety and shelf life |
| Food cost per portion | Cost control |

> **Industry Best Practice:** A HACCP-integrated standardized recipe includes cooking temperatures, cooling procedures, and time-temperature thresholds embedded within the method steps — not just at the end.

---

#### Cross-Contamination Risks in Prep Sequence

Recipe sequence directly affects cross-contamination risk:

- Prep ready-to-eat foods **before** raw proteins using the same board
- Follow colour-coded board protocols (red = raw beef, yellow = raw poultry, green = produce, white = RTE)
- If using the same equipment across ingredient types, **wash, rinse, sanitize** between uses
- Never prepare allergen-containing components in shared equipment without sanitization and disclosure

> **Regulatory Note (Industry Practice):** Provincial food premises regulations require prevention of cross-contamination. HACCP Prerequisite Programs (PRPs) govern sanitation procedures. Verify with your provincial authority for specific requirements.

### B-7.01.02P — Measure Amounts of Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** Measure amounts of ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** Amounts of ingredients are measured according to recipe specifications

#### Measurement Systems Overview

Three systems are used in Canadian commercial kitchens. A Red Seal Cook must be fluent in all three and able to convert between them.

| System | Common Use | Units |
|---|---|---|
| **Metric** | Canadian standard; all modern recipes, labels, nutrition | g, kg, mL, L, °C |
| **Imperial (UK-derived)** | Legacy Canadian recipes, some supplier labels | oz, lb, pt, qt, gal, °F |
| **American Standard (US)** | Many cookbook and recipe imports | cups, tbsp, tsp, fl oz |

> **Key Distinction:** Canadian "Imperial" and US "Standard" differ — particularly for volume. A Canadian/Imperial gallon = 4.546 L; a US gallon = 3.785 L. This matters when scaling bulk recipes or interpreting imported recipe books.

---

| 1 pound (lb) | 454 grams (g) | × 454 |
| 1 pound (lb) | 16 ounces (oz) | — |
| 1 ounce (oz) | 28.35 grams (g) | × 28.35 |

**Volume:**

| From | To | Factor |
|---|---|---|
| 1 litre (L) | 1,000 mL | — |
| 1 Canadian cup | 250 mL | — |
| 1 US cup | ~237–240 mL | slightly less than Canadian |

| 1 teaspoon (tsp) | 5 mL | — |

| 1 US quart | 946 mL (~1 L) | — |
| 1 US gallon | 3.785 L | — |
| 1 Imperial gallon | 4.546 L | — |

**Temperature:**

| Celsius → Fahrenheit | °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32 |
|---|---|
| Fahrenheit → Celsius | °C = (°F − 32) × 5/9 |

Common reference points:

| °C | °F | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 0°C | 32°F | Freezing point |

| 175–190°C | 350–375°F | General baking/roasting range |

---

#### Weight vs. Volume: Why Professionals Weigh

**Volume measurement is density-dependent.** One cup of different ingredients has very different weights:

| Ingredient | 1 Cup Weight (approx.) |
|---|---|
| Water | 240 g |
| All-purpose flour (scooped) | 120–130 g |
| All-purpose flour (packed) | 150–165 g |
| Granulated sugar | 200 g |
| Brown sugar (packed) | 220 g |
| Honey | 340 g |
| Butter | 225 g |
| Cocoa powder | 85–100 g |

> **Why This Matters:** The difference between scooping and spooning flour into a cup can be 30–40 g — enough to make baked goods dramatically different in texture. Professional bakers **always use weight**. Volume is acceptable for liquids (density variation is minimal) and small quantities (spices, seasonings).

**Operational Recommendation:** In baking and pastry, use a kitchen scale for all dry ingredients over 30 g. In savoury cooking, volume is more forgiving, but weight is still preferred for consistency across a team.

---

#### Measuring Tools and Equipment

| Tool | Used For | Key Points |
|---|---|---|
| **Digital scale** | Weight of all dry/wet ingredients | Zero (tare) between each ingredient; ensure calibration |
| **Dry measuring cups** | Dry volume (flour, sugar, grains) | Level off with a straight edge; don't pack unless specified |
| **Liquid measuring cups** | Volume of liquids | Read at eye level at the meniscus (bottom of curve) |
| **Measuring spoons** | Small volumes (tsp, tbsp) | Level dry ingredients; fill to brim for liquids |
| **Ladles** | Liquid portioning (soups, sauces) | Standardized by size (2 oz, 4 oz, 6 oz, 8 oz) |
| **Portion scoops (dishers)** | Semi-solid portioning (scoops, cookies) | Numbered by portions per quart (e.g., #8 = 4 oz) |
| **Volume containers** | Bulk liquid measuring | Graduated in mL/L markings |

> **Common Mistake:** Reading a liquid measuring cup while it is held up rather than on a flat surface — parallax error makes the reading inaccurate.

---

#### HACCP Connection: Measurement Precision and Food Safety

Accurate measurement is a food safety issue in specific contexts:

- **Curing salts (sodium nitrite):** Even a small excess is toxic — precise weighing is critical
- **HACCP recipes** may specify pH targets (using phosphoric acid, citric acid) that require accurate measurement to achieve preservation
- **Brine concentration** for marinating or curing must be consistent for food safety
- **Allergen management:** Measuring and segregating allergen-containing ingredients prevents cross-contact

### B-7.01.03P — Maintain Consistency When Converting Recipes

**Performance Criteria:** Maintain consistency when converting recipes
**Evidence of Attainment:** Consistency is maintained when converting recipes

---

#### Recipe Yield Conversion: The Conversion Factor Method

The standard professional method for scaling recipes uses a **Conversion Factor (CF)**.

**Formula:**

```
CF = Desired Yield ÷ Original Yield
New Ingredient Amount = Original Amount × CF
```

**Example:**

- Original recipe makes 24 portions
- You need 60 portions
- CF = 60 ÷ 24 = **2.5**
- If original recipe uses 500 g flour, new amount = 500 × 2.5 = **1,250 g flour**

---

#### Non-Linear Scaling: The Critical Exceptions

> **Most common exam and practical mistake:** Treating ALL ingredients as linear when scaling.

Some ingredients and processes do **not** scale proportionally:

**1. Salt and Spices — Scale at 60–75% when multiplying by large factors**

When doubling or tripling a recipe, the palate perceives salt and bold spices disproportionately. The general rule:

- For CF ≤ 2: scale linearly and adjust at end
- For CF 3–5: start at 75% of calculated amount and taste
- For CF > 5: start at 50–60% and adjust

**Why:** Our salt perception is not linear — the relationship between concentration and perceived saltiness follows a logarithmic-like curve. A dish with double the salt tastes more than twice as salty.

**2. Leavening Agents (baking powder, baking soda) — Scale at 60–75%**

- Excess baking soda creates a soapy/metallic taste and darkens baked goods
- Excess baking powder makes goods collapse and taste bitter
- For every 1 tsp baking powder in the original, scale more conservatively when multiplying by 3+

**3. Cooking Time and Temperature — Do NOT scale**

- A dish that takes 30 minutes at 175°C still takes approximately 30 minutes at the same temperature whether you're making 1 portion or 10
- Larger batch volumes in a pot will take longer to come to temperature — but you **adjust by monitoring**, not by multiplying time
- Oven temperature remains constant regardless of quantity

**4. Pan Size and Depth — Must be adjusted**

- Scaling a baked recipe by 3× requires 3× the pan surface area, not just 3× the time
- A batter at double depth takes ~40–60% longer to bake at the same temperature

**5. Emulsions (mayonnaise, hollandaise, vinaigrette) — Handle carefully**

- Emulsification is technique-dependent, not purely quantity-dependent
- Very small-scale emulsions are harder (insufficient volume for tools)
- Very large-scale emulsions require commercial equipment (immersion blender, Robot Coupe)

**6. Gelatin and Agar — Scale linearly but verify set strength with tests**

- Different liquid compositions affect gel strength
- Always make a test batch when scaling significantly

---

#### Yield Loss and EP/AP in Recipe Conversion

When scaling, remember that recipe ingredient quantities are often **EP (edible portion)** — the amount after trimming, peeling, and waste removal.

**Key concept:**

```
AP Amount Required = EP Amount Required ÷ Yield %
```

**Example:**
- Recipe calls for 500 g EP carrots (CF = 2, so you need 1,000 g EP)
- Carrot yield % = 80% (20% waste from peeling/trimming)
- AP amount to purchase = 1,000 g ÷ 0.80 = **1,250 g (1.25 kg) AP carrots**

> **Industry Best Practice:** Standardized recipes should specify whether quantities are AP or EP. If unclear, clarify before purchasing or prepping.

---

#### Maintaining Quality Consistency When Scaling

Beyond ingredient math, consistency requires:

- **Same equipment type** — a convection oven behaves differently from a conventional oven; scaling assumes same equipment
- **Same technique** — a hand-mixed recipe scaled to 10× may need a stand mixer
- **Consistent ingredient temperature** — cold butter vs. room temperature butter behaves differently in pastry regardless of quantity
- **Batch size limits** — some sauces (e.g., beurre blanc) physically cannot be made in quantities above certain thresholds with standard equipment

#### Factors Governing Substitutions

Per the Range of Variables, substitutions are driven by:

| Factor | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Menu requirements** | Seasonal specials, dish profile, flavour compatibility |
| **Dietary requirements** | Allergens, religious dietary laws, vegetarian/vegan, medical diets |

| **Budget** | Premium ingredient → cost-appropriate alternative |
| **Availability** | Out-of-stock item, seasonal absence, supply disruption |

---

#### Common Ingredient Substitutions Table

**Dairy Alternatives:**

| Original | Substitution | Ratio | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heavy cream (35%) | Coconut cream | 1:1 | Alters flavour; use for vegan |
| Butter (fat function) | Margarine or coconut oil | 1:1 | Texture may vary in baking |
| Buttermilk | Milk + 15 mL lemon juice per 250 mL | 1:1 by volume | Let stand 5 min |
| Sour cream | Plain Greek yogurt | 1:1 | Lower fat; slight tang difference |
| Whole milk | Skim milk + 1 tbsp butter per cup | 1:1 | Adds back fat |

**Egg Alternatives (binding/structure):**

| Function | Substitution | Ratio per egg |
|---|---|---|
| Binding (meatballs, burgers) | 1 tbsp ground flaxseed + 3 tbsp water | 1:1 per egg |
| Leavening (baking) | 1 tsp baking soda + 1 tbsp vinegar | 1:1 per egg |
| Emulsification | Aquafaba (chickpea liquid) | 3 tbsp per egg |

**Leavening:**

| Original | Substitution | Ratio |
|---|---|---|
| 1 tsp baking powder | ¼ tsp baking soda + ½ tsp cream of tartar | 1:1 |
| 1 tsp baking soda | 3 tsp baking powder (excess; adjust liquid) | 1:3 |

**Herbs (Fresh → Dried):**

| Original | Substitution | Ratio |
|---|---|---|
| 1 tbsp fresh herbs | 1 tsp dried herbs | 3:1 (fresh:dried) |
| 1 tsp dried herbs | ½ tsp ground herbs | 2:1 (dried:ground) |

**Why:** Drying concentrates volatile flavour compounds (essential oils) approximately 3-fold. Ground herbs are even more concentrated due to increased surface area.

**Thickening Agents:**

| Agent | Thickening Power vs. Flour | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Cornstarch | 2× flour | Clear finish; breaks down with prolonged heat or acid |
| Arrowroot | 2× flour | Clear finish; freezes well; acid-stable |
| All-purpose flour | Baseline | Opaque; stable over heat; classic roux base |
| Tapioca starch | ~2× flour | Clear; elastic texture; great for fruit pies |
| Rice flour | ~1.5× flour | Slightly grainy if under-cooked |
| Potato starch | 2× flour | Freeze-thaw stable |

#### Allergen-Driven Substitutions: Food Safety Priority

Health Canada identifies 14 priority food allergens that must be declared on packaged food labels. In a kitchen, substitutions involving allergens carry **legal and safety implications**.

**Canada's 14 Priority Allergens:**

1. Peanuts
2. Tree nuts (almonds, cashews, walnuts, pecans, pistachios, Brazil nuts, hazelnuts, macadamias, pine nuts)
3. Sesame seeds
4. Wheat and triticale
5. Gluten sources (wheat, rye, barley, oats, triticale)
6. Milk
7. Eggs
8. Fish
9. Shellfish/Crustaceans
10. Molluscs
11. Mustard
12. Sulphites (≥10 ppm)
13. Soy
14. Corn (as of November 2026 under updated regulations)

> **Regulatory Note:** Under the *Safe Food for Canadians Act* and Canadian food labelling regulations, all priority allergens must be declared. In food service, provincial regulations and best practices require clear communication about allergens in menu items.

**When making allergen-driven substitutions:**
- Verify the substitute is allergen-free (check labels and supplier specs)
- Avoid cross-contact — use dedicated equipment for allergen-free prep
- Document the substitution for traceability
- Communicate with front-of-house staff and guests

**Gluten-Free Substitutions:**

| Original | Gluten-Free Alternative | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| All-purpose flour | GF flour blend | Ratios vary by blend; add xanthan gum if blend lacks it |
| Breadcrumbs | GF breadcrumbs, crushed GF crackers, almond flour | |
| Soy sauce | Tamari (certified GF), coconut aminos | Read labels — some tamari contains wheat |
| Pasta | Rice pasta, corn pasta, quinoa pasta | Adjust cooking time (often shorter) |

---

#### Functional vs. Flavour Substitutions

A critical distinction:

**Functional substitution:** Replacing an ingredient that serves a structural, chemical, or safety role.
- Replacing salt in a brine (food safety function) requires ensuring the substitute provides equivalent water activity reduction
- Replacing baking soda (leavening chemistry) requires understanding the acid-base reaction
- Replacing egg in a custard (protein coagulation + emulsification) is complex

**Flavour substitution:** Replacing an ingredient primarily for taste.
- Swapping thyme for oregano, or lemon for lime
- More flexible; adjust quantities by taste

> **Operational Recommendation:** Always understand whether an ingredient is functional or primarily flavour-driven before substituting. Functional substitutions require more testing and care.

### B-7.01.05P — Adjust Seasoning

**Performance Criteria:** Adjust seasoning
**Evidence of Attainment:** Seasoning is adjusted according to recipe specifications and desired results

---

#### The Science of Seasoning

Seasoning is not just adding salt — it is the management of **five basic tastes** and flavour-enhancing techniques that make food more balanced, complex, and satisfying.

**The Five Basic Tastes:**

| Taste | Primary Sources | Function in Seasoning |
|---|---|---|
| **Salt (salty)** | Table salt, kosher salt, soy sauce, miso | Enhances all other flavours; suppresses bitterness |
| **Acid (sour)** | Lemon juice, vinegar, wine, fermented ingredients | Brightens flavours; balances richness; can substitute for some salt |
| **Sweet** | Sugar, honey, fruit, caramelized onions | Balances acid and bitterness; rounds sharp flavours |
| **Bitter** | Coffee, dark chocolate, radicchio, burnt elements | Adds complexity; balanced by fat, sweetness |
| **Umami (savory)** | Meat stocks, parmesan, mushrooms, tomatoes, soy sauce, MSG | Deepens flavour perception; increases saliva response |

> **Industry Best Practice:** Most seasoning problems are a lack of salt, acid, or both. When a dish tastes "flat," add a small amount of salt, then taste. If still flat, add a few drops of acid (lemon, vinegar). This two-step check solves the majority of seasoning issues.

---

#### Salt: Types and Applications

| Salt Type | Grain Size | Uses | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Table salt** | Fine | Baking (precise measurement) | Contains iodine; anti-caking agents |
| **Kosher salt** | Coarse flake | Cooking, seasoning meats | No additives; larger grain = less dense than table salt |
| **Sea salt (fine)** | Fine | All-purpose cooking | Mineral trace notes vary by source |
| **Fleur de sel / flaky sea salt** | Large flake | Finishing salt only | Expensive; melts on contact with heat, losing texture value |
| **Pickling salt** | Fine | Brines, pickling | No iodine (prevents discolouration); no anti-caking agents |
| **Curing salt (Prague powder)** | Fine, pink-dyed | Cured meats only | Contains sodium nitrite — **not a culinary seasoning; strictly functional** |

> **Critical Note:** Kosher salt is significantly less dense than table salt. 1 tsp table salt ≠ 1 tsp kosher salt by weight. If a recipe says "1 tsp salt" and you use kosher salt, you will under-season. **Always season to taste, not just to quantity.**

**Kosher salt density comparison:**
- 1 tsp fine table salt ≈ 6 g
- 1 tsp Diamond Crystal kosher salt ≈ 2.8 g
- 1 tsp Morton kosher salt ≈ 4.8 g

---

#### Temperature and Salt Perception

**Hot food tastes less salty than cold food at the same salt concentration.**

**Why:** Taste receptor sensitivity changes with temperature. At higher temperatures, volatile aromatic compounds are more active (perceived flavour is higher), but salt ion transport through taste receptors is less efficient. As food cools, it will taste saltier.

**Operational Impact:**
- Season hot soups and sauces to taste slightly underseasoned — they will taste more balanced once cooled to serving temperature
- Cold preparations (potato salad, gazpacho) can tolerate more aggressive initial seasoning than hot
- Taste testing should be done at or near the serving temperature

---

#### Acid as a Seasoning Tool

Acid is one of the most underused seasoning tools in professional kitchens. Adding a small amount of acid:

- **Brightens** the perceived flavour intensity
- **Balances** richness in fatty preparations (cream soups, braised dishes)
- **Enhances perceived saltiness** (allowing reduction of actual salt)
- **Rounds out bitterness** in dark leafy greens, bitter chocolate

**Common Acidifying Agents:**
- Lemon juice (fresh): versatile, bright, fruity — use at the end of cooking
- White wine vinegar: mild, good for light sauces
- Sherry vinegar: nutty, complex — good for soups and braises
- Apple cider vinegar: fruity, moderate — BBQ applications
- Balsamic vinegar: sweet-acid — finishing glazes and garnishes
- Lime juice: stronger acid, more aromatic — Latin/Asian cuisine

> **Operational Recommendation:** When a dish seems complete but lacks depth, add ¼ tsp of lemon juice at a time, stir, and re-taste. This is a professional finishing technique used in Michelin-level kitchens and food truck operations alike.

---

#### Seasoning Sequence: When to Add What

| Stage | Seasoning Action | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Building the base** | Salt aromatics (onion, garlic) early | Draws out moisture, accelerates caramelization |
| **During cooking** | Add salt and pepper in layers | Allows seasoning to penetrate ingredients |
| **Before protein hits the pan** | Season meat/fish | Creates seasoned crust; draws surface moisture (15+ min ahead for thick cuts) |
| **Tasting mid-cook** | Adjust salt, herb, spice balance | Earlier correction is easier than later |
| **Finishing (off heat)** | Add fresh herbs, lemon, finishing salt | Preserves volatile aromatics; adds textural contrast |
| **At service/plating** | Light finishing seasoning only | Over-seasoning at plating is irreversible |

---

#### Seasoning Adjustment: Common Problems and Fixes

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Over-salted dish** | Too much salt added; reduction concentrated salt | Add starch (potato), add unsalted component, increase volume, add acid |
| **Flat/bland flavour** | Under-seasoned; low umami | Add salt in small increments, then acid (lemon), then umami (parmesan, soy sauce) |
| **Too acidic** | Too much vinegar or lemon | Add fat (cream, butter), sweetness (sugar, honey), or more base |
| **Too sweet** | Excess sugar or sweet vegetable | Add acid, salt, or bitter element |
| **Too bitter** | Over-extracted spices, burnt elements, excessive coffee | Add fat, sweetness; may need to dilute |
| **Too spicy (heat)** | Excess chili, pepper | Add dairy (fat + casein), sweetness, starch; dilute with unseasoned base |
| **Over-herbed** | Too many fresh/dried herbs | Dilute with unseasoned base; balance with acid and fat |

## Range of Variables

### Factors for Ingredient Substitutions

- **Menu requirements:** The substitute must fit the dish's flavour profile, colour, and texture expectations
- **Dietary requirements:** Medical diets (low sodium, low fat, diabetic), allergen restrictions, religious dietary laws (halal, kosher, jain, Hindu), lifestyle choices (vegan, vegetarian)
- **Demographics:** Customer age profile (low sodium for elderly), cultural background (certain flavour preferences), institutional settings (schools, hospitals, long-term care)
- **Budget:** Cost pressure may require substituting a premium ingredient for an equivalent; must not compromise food safety or quality below standards
- **Availability:** Seasonal absence, supply chain disruption, vendor shortage; must substitute with comparable functional and flavour properties

#### Volume to Weight Conversions

Volume measurement assumes a given density. Converting between volume and weight requires knowing the density (or specific gravity) of the ingredient.

**Key densities to know:**

| Ingredient | Volume | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Water | 1 mL | 1 g |
| All-purpose flour (spooned) | 250 mL (1 cup) | ~120–125 g |
| Granulated sugar | 250 mL (1 cup) | ~200 g |
| Butter | 250 mL (1 cup) | ~227 g |
| Vegetable oil | 250 mL (1 cup) | ~215–220 g |
| Honey | 250 mL (1 cup) | ~340 g |

> **Operational Note:** For water-based liquids, 1 mL ≈ 1 g (density = 1). For everything else, you need the conversion factor or a scale.

---

#### Converting Between Metric and Imperial/American Standard

**Quick conversion chain:**

```
1 cup = 250 mL (Canadian) = 16 tbsp = 48 tsp
1 tbsp = 15 mL = 3 tsp
1 tsp = 5 mL
```

**Worked Example — Scale a recipe from US cups to metric grams:**
- Recipe calls for 2½ cups all-purpose flour
- 2.5 cups × 250 mL/cup = 625 mL (volume)
- But 1 cup AP flour ≈ 120 g → 2.5 × 120 = **300 g**
- Note: using mL as a proxy for g would give 625 g — wrong by more than 2×

> **This is why weighing is always preferred over volume measurement for dry ingredients.**

### B-7.01.02L — Procedures for Following Recipes

**Learning Outcomes:** Demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to follow recipes

---

#### Step-by-Step Recipe Execution Procedure

1. **Read the recipe completely** before starting any prep — identify unfamiliar techniques, special equipment, or long lead times (e.g., overnight marination)
2. **Mise en place:** Measure and organize all ingredients; prepare all sub-components (minced garlic, toasted nuts, blanched vegetables)
3. **Verify equipment:** Ensure correct pans, equipment sizes, and temperatures are achievable
4. **Check critical temperatures:** Identify all temperature checkpoints in the recipe (preheat oven, bring stock to simmer, chill component before proceeding)
5. **Follow sequence:** Execute steps in exact recipe order
6. **Monitor and measure:** Use thermometers, timers, scales throughout — do not estimate critical measurements
7. **Taste and adjust:** Season progressively; verify balance before plating
8. **Document deviations:** In a professional setting, any substitution or modification should be noted for food cost, allergen, and HACCP records

---

#### Recipe Yield Adjustment: Procedures

**Step 1:** Identify the original yield (e.g., "Makes 10 portions")
**Step 2:** Identify the desired yield (e.g., "Need 35 portions")
**Step 3:** Calculate CF: 35 ÷ 10 = **3.5**
**Step 4:** Multiply all ingredients by 3.5
**Step 5:** Apply non-linear scaling adjustments (salt, spices, leavening → start at 75%)
**Step 6:** Do NOT multiply cooking temperature
**Step 7:** Adjust cooking time based on actual mass (monitor with thermometer)
**Step 8:** Re-taste and adjust seasoning at the end

---

#### HACCP Integration in Recipe Following

Well-designed recipes embed HACCP Critical Control Points (CCPs):

| Recipe Step | Embedded CCP |
|---|---|
| "Cook chicken until internal temperature reaches 74°C" | CCP: cooking kill step |
| "Cool stock to 60°C within 2 hours, then to 4°C within 4 hours" | CCP: cooling |
| "Reheat soup to minimum 74°C before service" | CCP: re-thermalization |
| "Hold at minimum 60°C until service" | CCP: hot holding |

> **Industry Best Practice:** In HACCP-certified operations (required by SFCR for federally regulated facilities), recipe deviations at CCPs must be documented as corrective actions. In foodservice operations, the same discipline — even without formal HACCP certification — constitutes best food safety practice.

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Impact | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scaling all ingredients proportionally including spices | Assuming linearity | Over-seasoned, bitter, or soapy dish | Use 60–75% of calculated spice/salt and adjust to taste |
| Measuring flour by scooping (packed) | Speed; habit | 20–30% excess flour; tough baked goods | Spoon flour into measure and level, or use scale |
| Not reading recipe fully before starting | Rushing | Surprises mid-cook (overnight chill, missing equipment) | Complete read-through before any prep begins |
| Using finishing salt as cooking salt | Habit or substitution | Loses textural value; waste of expensive product | Reserve flaky salts for final plating only |
| Not adjusting seasoning for temperature | Lack of knowledge | Under-seasoned cold dish, over-seasoned hot dish | Taste at serving temperature; adjust accordingly |
| Ignoring EP/AP distinction | Confusion or unfamiliarity | Under-purchasing, running out mid-service | Clarify whether recipe uses EP or AP; calculate AP accordingly |
| Treating baking time as scalable | Applying same CF to time | Under- or over-cooked product | Monitor with thermometer; do not multiply time by CF |
| Substituting without checking allergens | Urgency, unfamiliarity | Allergen exposure; guest harm; legal liability | Check all substitute ingredients for allergen content before use |
| Skipping the tare function on scale | Rushing | Including container weight in ingredient weight | Always tare (zero) with empty or container-on scale before adding ingredient |
| Over-seasoning at plating | Habit | Irreversible; dish is too salty | Season progressively through cooking; light touch only at plating |

## Memory Aids

**CF Formula — "Divide the New by the Old"**
- Desired ÷ Original = CF
- "I **WANT** / I **HAVE** = my multiplier"

**Weight/Volume Anchors:**
- "1 mL water = 1 g" — everything else is denser or lighter
- "1 tbsp = 15 mL = 3 tsp" — the kitchen ratio you use every day
- "1 cup = 250 mL (Canada)" — not 240 mL (US)
- "1 lb = 454 g, 1 kg = 2.2 lb" — the cross-system bridge

**Seasoning Check Sequence — "SALT then ACID"**
- Flat dish? First: tiny pinch of salt → taste
- Still flat? Next: few drops of lemon or vinegar → taste
- Still flat? Check for umami — add a touch of soy, parmesan rind, or miso

**Non-Linear Scaling Mnemonic — "SLAB"**
- **S**pices and salt
- **L**eavening (baking powder/soda)
- **A**ddictive heat (chili, pepper)
- **B**aking time/temperature
→ All four of these do NOT scale proportionally with CF

**Herb Conversion — "3 Fresh = 1 Dried = ½ Ground"**
- 3 tbsp fresh herbs → 1 tbsp dried → ½ tbsp ground

**Temperature Conversion Quick Check:**
- 74°C = safe cooking (poultry, reheating) — "74 is the line"
- 60°C = hot holding minimum — "60 to hold, 4 to cold"
- 4°C = max refrigeration safe — "keep below 4 to stay alive"

### Exam Q&A

**Q1: What is the conversion factor to scale a recipe from 20 portions to 75 portions?**
A: CF = 75 ÷ 20 = **3.75**

**Q2: Why should salt and spices not be scaled proportionally when multiplying a recipe by 4 or more?**
A: Salt and spice perception is non-linear — doubling the concentration tastes more than twice as salty/spicy. Starting at 60–75% of calculated amounts and adjusting by taste prevents over-seasoning.

**Q3: What is the correct ratio for converting fresh herbs to dried herbs?**
A: 3 parts fresh = 1 part dried (3:1)

**Q4: A recipe uses 2 cups of all-purpose flour. When converting to grams, what error occurs if you assume 1 cup = 240 g?**
A: Flour does not weigh 240 g per cup. One cup of spooned, levelled AP flour weighs approximately 120–125 g. Using 240 g per cup would result in **double the flour needed**, making baked goods dense and tough.

**Q5: When measuring liquids in a graduated measuring cup, at what point on the meniscus should the reading be taken?**
A: At eye level, reading the **bottom of the meniscus** (the lowest point of the curved liquid surface).

**Q6: A large-batch soup recipe has been scaled by 5×. The recipe originally called for 30 minutes at 175°C. How long should the large batch cook?**
A: **Do not multiply time by CF.** Oven temperature stays the same. Cooking time is monitored with a thermometer — time will vary based on actual mass and vessel size, not the CF.

**Q7: What are the five basic tastes, and which two are most commonly used to rescue a flat-tasting dish?**
A: Sweet, salty, sour (acid), bitter, umami. The most common fixes for a flat dish are **salt** (first) and **acid** (second).

**Q8: What is the difference between EP and AP, and why does it matter when purchasing ingredients for a scaled recipe?**
A: EP (Edible Portion) is the weight after trimming; AP (As Purchased) is the raw weight. Recipes list EP amounts — you must calculate AP = EP ÷ yield% to know how much to order. Ignoring this leads to under-purchasing and running out during service.

**Q9: Name three factors that determine ingredient substitutions in a professional kitchen.**
A: Any three of: menu requirements, dietary requirements, demographics, budget, availability.

**Q10: Why does hot food taste less salty than the same food when cold?**
A: Temperature affects taste receptor sensitivity. Salt ion transport through taste receptors is less efficient at high temperatures. Dishes should be tasted at or near serving temperature to judge seasoning accurately.

**Q11: What is the Maillard reaction, and why does recipe sequence matter for achieving it?**
A: The Maillard reaction is a chemical browning reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars at temperatures above ~140°C, producing hundreds of flavour and colour compounds. It requires **dry heat** — the recipe must sequence adding liquid *after* the browning step; adding liquid prematurely stops the reaction and causes steaming instead of browning.

**Q12: A guest has a confirmed peanut allergy and the recipe normally uses peanut oil. You substitute sunflower oil. What additional steps must be taken?**
A: (1) Confirm the sunflower oil contains no peanut cross-contact (check supplier label/SDS); (2) use clean, allergen-free equipment and surfaces; (3) document the substitution; (4) communicate to front-of-house and the guest that peanut oil has been replaced. The substitution alone is insufficient without these steps.
