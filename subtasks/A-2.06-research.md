## Sanitizing vs Cleaning vs Disinfecting vs Sterilizing — Get the Words Right

These four terms are routinely confused. The Red Seal exam will test the distinction.

| Term | What it does | Microbial reduction | Where used |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cleaning** | Physical removal of visible soil (grease, food, dust) | Removes 90%+ microbes simply by removing the soil they live in, but does not "kill" by chemistry | Step 1 of every sanitation cycle |
| **Sanitizing** | Reduces microorganisms on a previously-cleaned **food-contact surface** to a **safe level** — typically a **5-log (99.999%) reduction** in 30 seconds | Kills/reduces vegetative bacteria; less effective against spores and some viruses | Cutting boards, knives, pans, prep surfaces, smallwares, dish machine |
| **Disinfecting** | Higher-level pathogen kill — typically a **6-log reduction** in seconds to minutes; for **non-food-contact** surfaces | Kills broader pathogen range; usually higher chemical strength | Washrooms, drains, garbage, floors |
| **Sterilizing** | Eliminates **all microbes** including spores | 100% | Canneries, autoclaves, retort processing — rarely used in restaurants |

> **Key apprentice rule:** *Restaurants sanitize food-contact surfaces and disinfect non-food-contact areas.* Sterilization is not normally a kitchen activity.

### What "5-log reduction" actually means

A "5-log" or "99.999%" reduction is the regulatory benchmark for food-contact sanitizers in Canada.

- 1 log = 90% kill (1 in 10 microbes survive)
- 2 log = 99% (1 in 100 survive)
- 3 log = 99.9% (1 in 1000)
- 4 log = 99.99%
- **5 log = 99.999% (1 in 100,000 survive)**
- 6 log = 99.9999%

If a surface starts with 1,000,000 bacteria per cm², a properly functioning sanitizer reduces that to ~ 10 — typically below an infectious dose for most pathogens (though far above zero).

## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### A-2.06.01P — Identify Utensils, Tools, Equipment and Appliances to Be Sanitized

**Performance Criteria:** identify utensils, tools, equipment and appliances to be sanitized
**Evidence of Attainment:** utensils, tools, equipment and appliances are identified.

#### What requires sanitizing in a commercial kitchen

Sanitizing follows cleaning. Anything that contacts food, hands, or food-contact surfaces should be sanitized between uses.

| Category | Sanitize because |
|---|---|
| **Cutting boards** | Direct contact with raw protein/produce/RTE; high cross-contamination risk |
| **Knives, peelers, zesters, mandolines** | Direct contact with raw and cooked foods |
| **Tongs, spatulas, whisks, ladles** | Touch food and surfaces |
| **Cambros, hotel pans, mixing bowls** | Hold food at varying temperatures |
| **Plates, cutlery, glassware** | Direct guest contact |
| **Smallwares** (sieves, china caps, chinois, measuring tools) | Food contact in prep |
| **Slicer blade, chopper blade, grinder parts** | Disassembled food contact; high biofilm risk in seams |
| **Ice scoops, ice machine interior** | RTE food contact (ice is food) |
| **Beverage equipment** (espresso steam wands, soda nozzles, juicer parts) | Direct contact with drinks |
| **Prep surfaces (table tops)** | Food contact during prep |
| **Sinks (prep, hand)** | Food and hand contact |
| **Sanitizer buckets and cloths themselves** | Re-contamination risk if neglected |
| **Refrigerator door handles** | Cross-contamination via hands |
| **Hood/exhaust filters** (separate cleaning) | Drip risk over food |

#### When to sanitize (triggers)

- **After cleaning** — sanitize is the chemical/thermal step that follows.
- **Between tasks** — especially between raw and ready-to-eat (RTE).
- **Between proteins** — chicken → beef → fish, etc.
- **Between allergens** — for allergen-isolated orders.
- **Every ~4 hours** during continuous use of a station.
- **Before service start** and **at end of service.**
- **After contamination** — spill, drop, suspected pest.
- **Per Master Sanitation Schedule (MSS)** — defined cadence per item.

#### Non-food-contact surfaces — disinfect, not sanitize

- Floors and walls (general daily)
- Trash bins, recycle bins
- Drain grates
- Washrooms (separate cleaning crew/schedule ideally)
- Mop heads, mop buckets

Use higher-strength **disinfectant**, not food-contact sanitizer concentrations.

> **Memory aid — "WHERE FOOD TOUCHES, SANITIZE":** food-contact surfaces, hands, hand-contact zones. Everything else is "disinfect."

### A-2.06.02P — Apply Sanitization Procedures

**Performance Criteria:** apply sanitization procedures
**Evidence of Attainment:** sanitization procedures are applied according to company policies and jurisdictional regulations.

#### The two main sanitizing methods

##### Chemical sanitizing
Approved chemical sanitizer applied at correct concentration, contact time, and temperature, on a clean surface.

##### Thermal (heat) sanitizing
- **Hot-water immersion** at **≥ 77 °C / 171 °F** for **≥ 30 seconds** in the third compartment of a 3-sink.
- **Mechanical dishwasher final rinse at ≥ 82 °C / 180 °F** (high-temp type).
- **Steam** for fixed equipment (combi-oven self-clean cycle includes steam phase).

Both methods are accepted under Canadian public-health regulations when correctly applied. **[Regulatory under provincial public-health food premises regulations]**

#### Standard sanitation sequence (recap from A-2.05)

1. **Scrape** — remove gross debris.
2. **Wash** — detergent + warm water (~ 43 °C).
3. **Rinse** — potable water, removes detergent (detergent residue deactivates sanitizer).
4. **Sanitize** — chemical at correct ppm with contact time, OR heat ≥ 77 °C for ≥ 30 sec.
5. **Air-dry** — never towel-dry food-contact surfaces.

#### The four C's of effective sanitizing

| Factor | Why |
|---|---|
| **Clean surface** | Soil deactivates chemical sanitizers (especially chlorine); biofilms shield underlying microbes |
| **Correct concentration** | Too low = no kill; too high = chemical residue + corrosion |
| **Contact time** | Sanitizer needs dwell time on surface — typically 7–60 seconds depending on chemistry |
| **Compatible temperature & pH** | Each sanitizer has an optimal range; outside it, effectiveness collapses |

#### Spray-on surface sanitizing (between-task)

1. Pre-wipe with detergent-dampened cloth (or full wash if soiled).
2. Spray sanitizer (correctly diluted) on the surface — enough to wet visibly.
3. **Allow contact time** per chemistry (typically ~30 sec for quats, ~10 sec for chlorine at 200 ppm).
4. Wipe excess with sanitizer-bucket cloth (or leave to air-dry).
5. Surface is now ready for next food task.

> **Common mistake:** spray and immediately wipe dry. Sanitizer needs **contact time** before being wiped or air-dried.

### A-2.06.03P — Apply Sanitizing Solution

**Performance Criteria:** apply sanitizing solution
**Evidence of Attainment:** sanitizing solution is applied according to company policies and jurisdictional regulations.

#### Application methods

| Method | Use |
|---|---|
| **Immersion** | Smallwares, utensils in 3-sink third compartment |
| **Spray** | Surfaces, equipment exteriors (workplace-labelled bottles) |
| **Wipe** | Surfaces using sanitizer-bucket cloth |
| **Foam** | Floor and wall in heavy-duty cleaning |
| **Mechanical** | Dish machine, automatic sanitizer dosing |
| **Steam / heat** | Fixed equipment self-clean |

#### Solution preparation

##### From a chemical dispenser (most reliable)
- Operate the auto-dispenser per protocol.
- **Verify with test strip** — dispensers can drift, fail, or run out.

##### Manual mixing (when dispenser unavailable)
- **Read the SDS/label** — never guess concentration.
- **Cold water for chlorine** — hot water gases off chlorine and weakens the solution.
- **Warm water for quats** — improves solubility.
- **Measure** with a graduated cup — never "glug."
- **Test with strip immediately.**
- **Label** the container (WHMIS workplace label: product, concentration, time made).

#### Concentration targets (food contact)

| Sanitizer | Concentration | Contact time (min) | Test strip |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chlorine (sodium hypochlorite)** | **100–200 ppm** | ≈ 7–10 sec | Chlorine strip |
| **Quaternary ammonium (quats)** | **200–400 ppm** per label | ≈ 30 sec | Quat strip |
| **Iodine (iodophors)** | **12.5–25 ppm** | ≈ 30 sec | Iodine strip |
| **Hot water immersion** | ≥ 77 °C / 171 °F | ≥ 30 sec | Thermometer |
| **High-temp dish-machine final rinse** | ≥ 82 °C / 180 °F | Per cycle | Thermolabel / max-registering thermo |

**[Regulatory]** Concentrations and contact times must match the **manufacturer's SDS/label** and the provincial public-health code. The numbers above are widely used in Canada; **always verify with test strips.**

#### Why each sanitizer chemistry behaves the way it does

##### Chlorine (sodium hypochlorite, ~ 5–6% household bleach)
- **Mechanism:** disrupts cellular membranes and proteins via oxidation.
- **Strengths:** fast, cheap, broad-spectrum (bacteria, viruses, fungi), effective against norovirus at appropriate concentrations.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - **Inactivated by organic matter** — soil rapidly consumes free chlorine.
  - **pH-sensitive** — works best at pH 6.5–7.5; loses effectiveness above pH 8.
  - **Temperature-sensitive** — degrades faster in hot water; gases off as chlorine vapour.
  - **Corrosive** to metals (especially aluminum, low-grade stainless).
  - **Photo-degrades** in sunlight.
- **Best for:** general food-contact sanitizing in a 3-sink; outbreak situations (norovirus) at elevated concentrations.

##### Quaternary ammonium ("quats")
- **Mechanism:** disrupts cell membranes; positively charged molecule binds to negatively charged microbial surfaces.
- **Strengths:** stable, leaves a brief residual film, no strong odour, mild to skin at use concentration, broad-spectrum.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - **Deactivated by hard water** (calcium and magnesium ions tie up quat).
  - **Deactivated by anionic detergents** — rinse thoroughly between wash and sanitize.
  - **Less effective against some viruses and *Pseudomonas*** than chlorine.
  - **Residual film** is a feature for some applications and a problem for others (rinse-required for direct food contact in many provinces).
- **Best for:** sanitizer buckets and spray bottles at prep stations.

##### Iodine (iodophors)
- **Mechanism:** iodine oxidizes microbial proteins.
- **Strengths:** visible (amber colour signals presence), broad-spectrum, less corrosive than chlorine.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - **Stains plastics and porous surfaces.**
  - **Temperature-sensitive** — degrades / sublimes above ~ 49 °C (don't use in hot water).
  - **pH range:** ~ 3–5; outside this, effectiveness drops.
  - Higher cost than chlorine.
- **Best for:** glassware sanitizing in bar settings; some dairy applications.

##### Hot water (thermal)
- **Mechanism:** heat denatures microbial proteins and disrupts cell membranes.
- **Strengths:** no chemical residue, no allergen carry-over, simple verification (thermometer).
- **Weaknesses:** energy cost, scald risk, requires verified thermometer or thermolabel.
- **Best for:** mechanical dish machines (high-temp); utensil immersion sanitizing.

#### Test strips — a brief but critical practice

##### What pH actually means here
Note: the original Range of Variables lists "using test strips to measure pH." **In Canadian commercial sanitation practice, sanitizer test strips primarily measure CONCENTRATION (ppm), not pH directly.** Sanitizer performance is pH-sensitive, but the strip you use at the bucket is concentration-specific:
- Chlorine strip: measures free chlorine ppm.
- Quat strip: measures quat ppm.
- Iodine strip: measures iodine ppm.

For systems where **pH itself is being verified** (e.g., acidified rice for sushi, fermented products), separate **pH strips or a digital pH meter** are used. **[Operational clarification]**

##### How to use a sanitizer test strip
1. Dip the strip in the sanitizer for the time specified on the package (usually 5–10 seconds).
2. Compare colour to the chart on the bottle.
3. Read concentration in ppm.
4. If below target → discard and remake the solution.
5. If above target → dilute (but note: most chemistry has a safe-upper-bound; over-concentration is a food-safety concern).

##### When to test
- **At setup** of each bucket / 3-sink / spray bottle.
- **Every 2–4 hours** during continuous use.
- **After visible soiling** of the bucket.
- **After significant top-up** (if remaking).
- **Whenever sanitizer effectiveness is in doubt** (e.g., post-power-failure dispenser).

### A-2.06.01L — Sanitizing Tools, Equipment, and Products

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of sanitizing tools and equipment, and their applications/procedures.

#### Sanitizing tools and equipment (Range of Variables)

| Tool | Function | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Dishwasher (mechanical)** | Continuous sanitizing via heat or chemical | High-temp ≥ 82 °C final rinse; low-temp uses chlorine ~ 50 ppm; verify each shift |
| **Thermometer** | Verifying hot-water sanitize and dish-machine rinse | Thermolabel, max-registering, or thermocouple plate; calibrated probe for hot-water immersion |
| **Spray bottles** | Surface sanitizing between tasks | Workplace label per WHMIS; correct dilution; never re-use a food container |
| **Sinks (3-compartment)** | Manual warewashing | Wash–rinse–sanitize–air-dry; sanitize compartment for chemical or hot water immersion |
| **Buckets** | Holding sanitizer for cloth use | Labelled (product, ppm, time); test strips; refresh every 2–4 h |
| **Cloths** | Wiping surfaces with sanitizer | Live in sanitizer bucket; colour-coded by task; never multi-use across raw/RTE |

##### Additional commonly used items

| Tool | Function |
|---|---|
| **Test strips (chlorine/quat/iodine)** | Concentration verification |
| **pH strips / pH meter** | For pH-controlled foods (sushi rice, fermentations); not the routine sanitizer test |
| **Thermolabel / max-registering thermometer** | Dish machine rinse verification |
| **Sanitizer dispenser** | Auto-mixed sanitizer at point of use |
| **Foamer / pressure sprayer** | Heavy-duty surface and floor sanitation |
| **CIP (Clean-In-Place) systems** | Larger or specialty operations — automated cleaning + sanitizing of fixed equipment |
| **Steam cleaner** | Combined cleaning + sanitizing for ovens, hoods, drains |

#### Sanitizing products — applications and procedures

| Product | Application | Procedure |
|---|---|---|
| **Chlorine bleach (food-grade)** | 3-sink, sanitizer bucket, spray, outbreak response | Cold water; 100–200 ppm; 7–10 sec contact; test with strip |
| **Quat (food-grade)** | Sanitizer bucket, spray | Warm water; 200–400 ppm per label; ~ 30 sec contact; test with strip |
| **Iodophor (food-grade)** | Bar glassware, dairy | Cool water (< 49 °C); 12.5–25 ppm; ~ 30 sec; test |
| **Peracetic acid (PAA)** | Larger ops, industrial settings | Low-temperature effective; biodegradable; strong odour; PPE required |
| **Hot water (thermal)** | Mechanical dishwasher, 3-sink hot immersion | ≥ 82 °C final rinse mechanical or ≥ 77 °C 3-sink immersion 30 sec |
| **Steam** | Equipment self-clean, surface decontamination | Per manufacturer cycle |
| **Disinfectants (non-food-contact)** | Washrooms, drains | Higher concentration; per label; not for food contact |

> ⚠️ **Never mix chemicals.** Bleach + acid = chlorine gas; bleach + ammonia = chloramines. Chlorine sanitizer + acidic delimer in a dish-machine cycle without proper sequencing has hospitalized cooks. **[Regulatory — WHMIS / OH&S]**

### A-2.06.02L — Sanitization Procedures

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to sanitize tools and equipment.

#### Disassemble and assemble — recap from A-2.05

Sanitizing follows cleaning, which usually requires disassembly:
- **Slicer:** unplug → blade out → manual sanitize each part → air-dry → reassemble dry.
- **Buffalo chopper / robot-coupe:** unplug → S-blade out (two-hand) → manual sanitize.
- **Meat grinder:** unplug → worm/plate/knife disassembled → sanitize each piece in 3-sink.
- **Combi-oven:** self-clean cycle (includes high-temp steam phase that is itself a sanitizing step).
- **Ice machine:** manufacturer-approved interior sanitizer applied per schedule.
- **Slicer/grinder seams** are biofilm hotspots — extra brush work + immersion.

#### Three-sink method of sanitization (the Red Seal staple)

| Sink | Step | Temperature | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Wash** | Detergent + water | ≈ 43–49 °C / 110–120 °F | Scrape first; change water as it soils |
| **2. Rinse** | Clean potable water | Warm | Removes detergent so it doesn't deactivate sanitizer |
| **3. Sanitize** | Chemical at ppm **or** hot water ≥ 77 °C / 171 °F | Per chemistry / heat | Verify with strip or thermometer |
| **4. Air-dry** | On rack | — | No towels; gravity drain only |

Volume control:
- **Don't overload** — bury items in detergent; soil load reduces effectiveness.
- **Sanitize compartment** items must be **fully submerged** for the chemical contact time.
- **Hot water 3-sink sanitizing** requires the water to be **maintained at ≥ 77 °C** (continuous heat source or frequent replacement) — this is harder than it sounds.

#### Sanitization procedures (Range of Variables) — use of chemicals, test strips, proper heat and drying techniques

##### Use of chemicals
- Right chemical for the surface and pathogen risk.
- Correct dilution from dispenser or label.
- PPE per SDS Section 8 (gloves + goggles for concentrated products).
- Contact time before wiping or air-dry.
- Never mix.
- Workplace label per WHMIS.
- Store below food, ventilated, locked where required.

##### Using test strips (concentration verification)
- Match chemistry — chlorine strips for chlorine, quat for quats, iodine for iodine.
- Test **at setup, every 2–4 hours, after soiling, after dispenser change**.
- Remake bucket when concentration is out of spec.
- **Document** in HACCP log.

##### Proper heat and drying techniques
- Hot-water sanitize at **≥ 77 °C surface** (utensils, immersion) or **≥ 82 °C final rinse** (mechanical dishwasher).
- Verify with thermometer or thermolabel.
- **Air-dry food-contact surfaces** — towel-drying re-contaminates.
- Items stored fully dry to prevent biofilm and gram-negative growth (*Pseudomonas* in particular).

### A-2.06.03L — Regulatory Requirements

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of regulatory requirements pertaining to sanitization of tools and equipment.

#### Canadian regulatory layers (sanitization)

| Authority | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Health Canada / PMRA** | Approves antimicrobials and sets allowable concentrations for food-contact use |
| **CFIA — SFCR** | Federally-registered operators: PCP with sanitation SSOPs and verification |
| **Provincial Public Health Food Premises Regulations** | Restaurants: prescribed methods (3-sink/dishwasher), temperatures, sanitizer concentrations |
| **WHMIS 2015** | Chemical hazard communication |
| **Provincial OH&S** | Worker safety; PPE per SDS |
| **Municipal public health units** | Inspection and enforcement |

> **[Regulatory]** Health Canada's **List of Permitted Food Additives** and its **Food and Drug Regulations** govern which sanitizers and concentrations are permitted in contact with food. Commercial products carry a **PCP (Pest Control Product) number** or are otherwise approved; using a non-approved sanitizer on food-contact surfaces is a violation.

#### Common provincial requirements

While exact wording varies by province, common required elements include:
- **3-compartment sink or equivalent** in foodservice operations.
- **Hot-water mechanical dishwasher final rinse ≥ 82 °C / 180 °F** (or chemical sanitizer at approved concentration).
- **Sanitizer test strips** on-site, matched to chemistry.
- **Approved sanitizer at proper concentration** with verifiable monitoring.
- **Air-drying** of food-contact items.
- **WHMIS-labelled** chemical containers.

#### What inspectors check

1. **3-sink setup** — water temperatures, sanitizer concentration, air-dry rack.
2. **Dish machine** — high-temp final rinse verified, or chemical concentration verified.
3. **Sanitizer bucket** — labelled, in date, in correct concentration.
4. **Test strips** — on-site, matched to chemistry, in use.
5. **WHMIS compliance** — labels, SDS, training records.
6. **Master Sanitation Schedule** — documented and signed.
7. **Equipment interior** — slicer, ice machine, walk-in gaskets, drains.

#### HACCP perspective — Sanitation as prerequisite

In a HACCP-aligned operation:
- **Sanitation is a prerequisite program (SSOP — Sanitation Standard Operating Procedure)**, not typically a CCP.
- The SSOP describes **who, what, when, how, with what** for each sanitation task.
- **Verification** activities (ATP swab testing, microbial swabs, visual inspections) confirm effectiveness.
- **Failures** in sanitation undermine every CCP — cooking, cooling, holding all assume sanitized surfaces.

##### ATP testing — the modern verification tool
- **Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP) bioluminescence** is increasingly used by Canadian operators to verify sanitation effectiveness.
- A swab + handheld luminometer measures organic residue (a proxy for cleanliness).
- Pass/fail thresholds are operation-specific (typically < 100 RLU for food-contact, but varies).
- Faster and more sensitive than visual inspection — catches biofilm and residue that look clean. **[Best Practice — increasingly required in audited operations]**

## 3. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Setting up a sanitizer bucket pre-shift
1. Confirm chemistry assigned for the station (typically quats or chlorine).
2. Fill bucket with appropriate-temperature water (cold for chlorine, warm for quats).
3. Add chemical from dispenser or measure per label.
4. Place a clean cloth in the bucket.
5. **Test with appropriate strip** — verify ppm.
6. Label bucket: product, concentration, time made.
7. Position off the floor, accessible but not on prep surface.

### Scenario 2 — Sanitizing the slicer mid-shift between proteins
1. Lockout — unplug.
2. Set blade to "0", engage guard.
3. Remove ring guard and blade (cut-resistant glove).
4. Pre-clean: brush off solid debris, wash with detergent.
5. Rinse with potable water.
6. **Sanitize:** immerse blade in chlorine 200 ppm for 10 sec OR spray quat at 200 ppm with 30-sec dwell.
7. Air-dry on rack.
8. Wipe motor housing with sanitizer cloth (do not spray onto controls).
9. Reassemble dry.
10. Plug in; resume.

### Scenario 3 — Test strip reads low at 10 a.m. (bucket made at 8 a.m.)
1. Stop using the bucket immediately.
2. Discard solution.
3. Rinse bucket.
4. Remake at correct concentration.
5. **Re-test with strip.**
6. **Re-sanitize** any surfaces or items wiped with the under-spec sanitizer in the last 2 hours, if practical.
7. Investigate root cause: dispenser drift? Soil load too high?
8. Document in sanitation log.

### Scenario 4 — High-temp dishwasher rinse reads 76 °C
1. Stop sending sensitive food-contact items through; switch to 3-sink.
2. Inspect: rinse aid level, booster heater operation, scale buildup.
3. Service call.
4. Verify with thermolabel after repair before resuming.
5. Document failure and corrective action.

### Scenario 5 — Suspected norovirus on premises (recent staff illness)
1. Exclude any symptomatic staff for **at least 48 hours symptom-free**. **[Regulatory]**
2. Notify public-health unit per provincial protocol.
3. **Elevate sanitation concentrations** — chlorine at higher concentrations (typically 1000–5000 ppm for environmental disinfection; food-contact surfaces re-sanitized at 200 ppm afterward with thorough rinse).
4. Focus on high-touch surfaces, washrooms, door handles, faucets.
5. **Alcohol hand sanitizer is LESS effective against norovirus** than soap-and-water — reinforce hand wash discipline.
6. Discard any food potentially contaminated.
7. Document.

### Scenario 6 — Newly installed quat dispenser at 3-sink
1. Read manufacturer documentation.
2. Run a cycle.
3. **Test with quat strip** — confirm 200–400 ppm.
4. Note time and concentration; train staff on testing schedule.
5. Schedule quarterly dispenser calibration with vendor.

## 4. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **"Sanitizer cleans it."** No — sanitizer kills microbes on clean surfaces. Soil deactivates sanitizer.
- **Spray-and-wipe (no dwell)** — sanitizer needs contact time before wiping.
- **Too strong is better** — over-concentration leaves chemical residue, can corrode equipment, and creates food-safety concerns.
- **Mixing chlorine + quat** — chemistries can be antagonistic; never mix.
- **Reusing bucket water all day** — concentration drops with soil load.
- **Topping up an old bucket** instead of remaking — never verifies correct concentration.
- **Skipping the rinse** between wash and sanitize — detergent residue deactivates sanitizer.
- **Using hot water with chlorine** — chlorine gases off; effective concentration drops.
- **Using cold water with quat** — slower dissolution and weaker action.
- **Using iodine in dish machine** — heat sublimes iodine away.
- **No test strips on-site** — assumes the system is working; major audit citation.
- **Wrong test strip for the chemistry** — chlorine strips do not measure quats and vice versa.
- **Sanitizer in unlabelled bottles** — WHMIS violation; safety risk.
- **Towel-drying after sanitizing** — re-contaminates.
- **Hand sanitizer treated as a sub for handwashing** — particularly fails against norovirus.
- **Pencil-whipping the sanitation log** — top inspection citation.
- **Ignoring biofilm in dish machine and ice machine** — they are sanitizer-resistant once established and require disassembly + mechanical removal.

## 6. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with A-2.06.

1. **Clean before sanitize.** Soil deactivates chemical sanitizers; biofilms shield microbes.
2. **Sanitizing = 5-log (99.999%) reduction** on a clean food-contact surface; standard regulatory benchmark.
3. **Sanitizing ≠ disinfecting ≠ sterilizing.** Restaurants sanitize food-contact and disinfect non-food-contact.
4. **Sanitizing methods:** chemical (chlorine, quat, iodine) and thermal (hot water, steam, dish-machine heat).
5. **Standard food-contact sanitizer concentrations:**
   - **Chlorine 100–200 ppm**, ~ 7–10 sec contact, cold water.
   - **Quats 200–400 ppm** per label, ~ 30 sec contact, warm water, hard-water sensitive.
   - **Iodine 12.5–25 ppm**, ~ 30 sec contact, < 49 °C, pH 3–5.
6. **Hot-water sanitizing:** ≥ 77 °C / 171 °F surface or immersion for ≥ 30 sec.
7. **High-temp dishwasher final rinse: ≥ 82 °C / 180 °F.** Verify with thermolabel.
8. **Low-temp / chemical dishwasher:** chlorine ~ 50 ppm in final rinse; verify with strip.
9. **3-sink method:** Wash → Rinse → Sanitize → Air-dry. Standard manual sanitization.
10. **Air-dry only** for food-contact surfaces; towel-drying re-contaminates.
11. **The Four C's:** Clean surface, Correct concentration, Contact time, Compatible temperature/pH.
12. **Test strips** are required on-site and matched to sanitizer chemistry; test at setup, every 2–4 hours, after soiling.
13. **Bucket discipline:** label with product/ppm/time; remake when out of spec; never top up; refresh every 2–4 h.
14. **Never mix chemicals:** bleach + acid → chlorine gas; bleach + ammonia → chloramine.
15. **Workplace labels (WHMIS 2015)** required on all decanted spray bottles and buckets.
16. **Disassemble equipment** (slicer, chopper, grinder, mandoline) for proper sanitizing of food-contact components.
17. **Reassemble dry** to prevent rust and biofilm.
18. **Health Canada / PMRA** regulates antimicrobials approved for food contact; products carry a PCP number or equivalent.
19. **CFIA SFCR** requires federally-registered operators to maintain a PCP with sanitation SSOPs.
20. **Provincial public-health regulations** prescribe the 3-sink/dishwasher requirements, temperatures, and sanitizer concentrations for restaurants.
21. **Sanitation is a HACCP prerequisite program (SSOP)** — fails silently if neglected; undermines every CCP.
22. **ATP testing** is a modern verification tool that detects organic residue (a cleanliness proxy) and is increasingly required in audited operations.
23. **Norovirus** response: elevated chlorine concentrations (1000–5000 ppm) for environmental disinfection; alcohol sanitizer less effective; exclude ill staff ≥ 48 h symptom-free.
24. **Spore-formers** (*B. cereus*, *C. perfringens*) are NOT killed by routine sanitizing — temperature control during cooling/holding is the defence.
25. **Heat-stable toxins** (*Staph aureus* enterotoxin) are not destroyed by sanitizing OR cooking — prevention via hygiene + temperature.
26. **Sanitizer effectiveness drops** with soil load, wrong pH, wrong temperature, or hard water — verify, don't assume.
27. **Master Sanitation Schedule (MSS)** — daily/weekly/monthly cadence documented and signed.
28. **Sensory + thermometer + test strip + log** — together, the system that demonstrates safe operation to inspectors.
