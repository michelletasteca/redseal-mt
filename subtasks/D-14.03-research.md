## Why the Cooking Stage Is Where Soups Are Made or Broken

Selection (D-14.01) and processing (D-14.02) build the parts. The cooking stage assembles them into a soup, with several outcomes simultaneously:

- **Flavour development** — sweating aromatics, building roux colour, deglazing fond, reducing for concentration.
- **Texture / body** — gelatin extraction (in stock-based hot soups), starch gelatinization (thickeners), emulsification (cream finishes).
- **Pathogen control** — cooking solids to safe internal temperatures; bringing the entire soup to ≥ 74 °C if reheating.
- **Service-readiness** — temperature, seasoning, garnish.
- **Cooling readiness** — anything not served immediately enters the most dangerous stage of its life (60 → 4 °C window).

A cook who controls heat, time, sequence, and seasoning at this stage produces a great soup. A cook who skips a step or pushes the heat produces something inedible — or unsafe.

## The Heat-Transfer Anchors

| Mechanism | In soup cooking |
|---|---|
| **Conduction** | Burner → pot bottom → liquid; aromatics sweating in butter |
| **Convection** | Stirred soup; rising bubbles in a simmer; air convection in a steam kettle's jacket |
| **Radiation** | Minor; the broiler/salamander for finishing French onion soup with melted cheese |
| **Phase change** | Water → steam at 100 °C; **steam kettles** use the latent heat of condensing steam in a jacket around the pot (very efficient and gentle) |

> **Anchor rule:** *Soups simmer; they do not boil* — except where boiling is intentional (emulsified ramen broth, certain reductions). A rolling boil agitates fat and proteins back into the liquid, breaks cream emulsions, scorches starches, and over-extracts aromatics. **[Best Practice]**

### Target simmer temperatures

| Stage | Temperature | What's happening |
|---|---|---|
| **Sweat / "low" cook** | 70–85 °C | Aromatics soften without colour |
| **Simmer (clear/cream/purée soup)** | 85–95 °C | Standard cook; gentle convection |
| **Boil (intentional reduction)** | 100 °C | Aggressive evaporation; risk of breaks |
| **Hot hold** | **≥ 60 °C** internal | **[Regulatory]** food-safety threshold |
| **Reheat target** | **≥ 74 °C / 165 °F within 2 hours** | **[Regulatory]** carry-over from A-2.01 |

### D-14.03.01P — Select Tools and Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** select tools and equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** tools and equipment are selected according to recipe specifications.

#### The soup-cooking toolkit (Range of Variables, expanded)

| Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| **Whisks** | Roux + liquid incorporation; smoothing cream soups; building emulsions; preventing scorching during initial heat |
| **Food processor / blender** | Puréeing cooked soups; making spice or aromatic pastes; finishing emulsions |
| **Cheesecloth** | Final straining of consommé and refined puréed soups; lining strainers; sachet retrieval |
| **Spoon (wooden or silicone)** | Stirring without scratching; tasting (single-use sanitized spoon) |
| **Spatula (heat-resistant rubber/silicone)** | Scraping pot walls; folding cream into soup; cleaning out the last drops |
| **Strainers (china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth-lined chinois)** | Staged straining for clarity and refinement |
| **Steam kettle** | Large-batch soup production; gentle, even heat; quick to bring up; some have integrated cooling and tilting features |
| **Soup pot** | Standard wide-and-shallower pot (vs the tall stockpot); easier stirring and even heat exposure |

##### Additional commonly used items

| Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| **Saucepan / saucepot (smaller)** | Building roux; making small batches; finishing reductions |
| **Stand or immersion blender** | Puréeing cream/purée soups (see hot-blender safety below) |
| **Spider / mesh skimmer** | Lifting solids out of broth (e.g., shellfish from chowder broth) |
| **Ladle (graduated)** | Portioning; service |
| **Bain-marie / hot well** | Holding soups at ≥ 60 °C during service |
| **Probe thermometer** | Verifying internal soup temperature for cooking, holding, and reheating |
| **Hotel pans + ice bath + ice wands + blast chiller** | Post-cook rapid cooling per A-2.02 |
| **Heat-resistant gloves and pads** | Handling hot pots — kept dry (wet pad = burn) |

#### Steam kettles — the workhorse of high-volume soup production

Steam kettles (jacketed kettles) circulate steam through a hollow outer wall surrounding the cooking vessel. Key features:

- **Even heat** — no hot spots; ideal for cream and milk-based soups that scorch easily on direct flame.
- **Gentle simmer control** — steam pressure regulated for precise temperature.
- **Large capacity** — typically 40, 60, 80, 100+ L; banquet and institutional kitchens depend on them.
- **Tilting models** — pour out finished soup safely without lifting.
- **Integrated cooling jackets** in some advanced models — switch from steam to cold water for rapid cooling.
- **Steam burn hazard** — phase-change energy of condensing steam is severe. PPE (heat-resistant gloves, eye protection) required during operation. **[Regulatory under provincial OH&S]**

> ⚠️ **Steam kettle safety:** Open the lid **away from the body** to vent steam. Never lean over the open pot. Steam burns are dramatically worse than dry-heat burns because steam delivers ~ 540 cal/g of latent heat as it condenses on skin.

#### Tool selection by soup type

| Soup | Critical tools |
|---|---|
| **Clear** | Soup pot, ladle, china cap, fine chinois, spider, probe thermometer |
| **Consommé** | Tall pot (raft needs depth), whisk (early stirring), spider, china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth |
| **Cream** | Sauce pot (roux), whisk, immersion/stand blender, fine chinois, sieve, dairy thermometer |
| **Purée** | Soup pot or steam kettle, immersion or stand blender, fine sieve, ladle |
| **Specialty (pho, ramen)** | Tall stockpot for broth phase, separate noodle pot, individual bowls with garnishes prepped |
| **Cold** | Stand blender, fine sieve, ice bath bowl, cold-storage containers |

### D-14.03.02P — Combine Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** combine ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** ingredients are combined in sequence according to recipe specifications.

#### The general soup-cooking sequence

Different soup types follow different macro-sequences. Below are the canonical patterns.

##### Clear soup
1. **Heat base broth** to a gentle simmer.
2. **Add featured solids** at the right time for desired doneness (proteins pre-cooked then added; vegetables cut to size for cook time).
3. **Skim** as needed.
4. **Add aromatic late** (green onion, herbs) for fresh notes.
5. **Adjust seasoning**.
6. Hold ≥ 60 °C or cool per A-2.02.

##### Consommé (clarification cook — D-14.03.03P below)
1. **Combine cold raft** (lean ground meat, egg whites, brunoise mirepoix, crushed ice, optional acid) **with cold stock**.
2. **Heat slowly** while stirring gently — the raft solids must dissolve into the liquid, then coagulate evenly.
3. **As proteins coagulate**, raft forms and floats — **stop stirring**.
4. **Cut a small vent hole** in the raft for steam escape.
5. **Simmer gently** 30–60 min — gentle convection percolates broth through the raft, which acts as a coagulation filter.
6. **Ladle** the now-crystal-clear stock from beneath the raft through a cheesecloth-lined chinois.
7. **Degrease** the surface (chilled-fat-cap method preferred).
8. **Adjust seasoning, hold ≥ 60 °C** or cool.

##### Cream soup
1. **Sweat aromatics in butter** (or oil) over low-medium heat — no colour.
2. **Add featured vegetable** if it benefits from sweating before liquid.
3. **Build roux** in a separate pan OR push aromatics to the side and add flour directly (the **"reverse roux" / vegetable-roux method**).
4. **Add stock gradually**, whisking to incorporate the roux smoothly — **hot roux + cold stock** or **cold roux + hot stock** for smooth (carry-over from D-14.02).
5. **Simmer** until vegetables are tender (typically 20–40 min depending on vegetable).
6. **Purée** with immersion or stand blender; strain through fine chinois for refinement.
7. **Temper in cream** — bring to just below the simmer; add cream off heat or below 80 °C to prevent breaking.
8. **Adjust seasoning** at service temperature (salt tastes different at different temperatures).
9. Hold ≥ 60 °C or cool.

##### Purée soup
1. **Sweat aromatics**.
2. **Add featured vegetable / legume + stock**.
3. **Simmer until tender** (legume soups can take 1–2 h; squash 30–45 min).
4. **Purée** in batches (or with immersion blender in-pot).
5. **Adjust consistency** — add stock to thin, reduce to thicken.
6. **Finish** with cream / acid / herbs.
7. Hold or cool.

##### Specialty / international
- Each cuisine has its own sequence — **pho** simmers aromatic broth separately, builds bowl with noodles + raw meat + hot broth at plating; **ramen** builds shoyu/miso/shio tare separately and combines at plating; **gumbo** starts with deep-cooked roux, builds with trinity (Cajun: onion + celery + bell pepper) and protein.

##### Cold
- **Hot-then-cooled** (vichyssoise): cook per cream/purée method, then chill per A-2.02 cooling rules.
- **Raw** (gazpacho, ajo blanco): process cold, infuse cold, serve cold — no cook step.

#### Why sequence matters

- **Aromatics cooked in fat** at the beginning release fat-soluble flavour compounds.
- **Roux built early** has time to cook out the raw-flour taste.
- **Featured solids added at the right time** are perfectly cooked at service.
- **Dairy added late and below boiling** stays intact (no breaking, no scorching).
- **Acids added last** preserve their brightness (long heat dulls citrus, vinegar).
- **Fresh herbs at service** for top-note aromatics.

### D-14.03.03P — Clarify Consommés

**Performance Criteria:** clarify consommés
**Evidence of Attainment:** consommés are clarified according to recipe specifications.

#### The science of consommé clarification

Consommé is a clear, intensely-flavoured broth produced by clarifying a stock through a **raft**:
- A mixture of **lean ground meat, egg whites, brunoise mirepoix, crushed ice, and optional acid (tomato, wine)**.
- When heated gently, **egg-white proteins denature and coagulate** in a network with the ground meat and mirepoix.
- This network rises to the surface and forms a **floating raft**.
- As the simmer continues, broth percolates **up through the raft**, leaving suspended particles trapped in the coagulated protein matrix.
- The raft is also a **flavour booster** — the meat releases additional protein and umami compounds into the broth.

#### Standard procedure

1. **Combine raft cold** in a tall narrow pot — ground meat (~ 500 g per 4 L broth), 4–6 egg whites, brunoise mirepoix (~ 200 g), crushed ice (~ 200 g), optional tomato/wine for clarity assistance and pigment.
2. **Whisk briefly** to combine, leaving no dry pockets.
3. **Add cold stock** (or barely warm) and whisk to disperse.
4. **Place over medium-low heat** — slow gentle heating is critical. **Aggressive heat denatures egg proteins too fast — they form clumps instead of a coherent raft.**
5. **Stir gently at first** to keep the raft from sticking to the pot bottom (a stuck raft burns and the consommé tastes scorched).
6. **As temperature reaches ~ 65–70 °C, the raft begins to coagulate** — stop stirring.
7. **The raft rises to the top** and forms a visible mass.
8. **Cut a small vent hole** in the centre of the raft for steam escape.
9. **Maintain a barely-trembling simmer** (~ 88–92 °C) for **30–60 minutes** — the gentle convection percolates broth through the raft for continuous clarification.
10. **Do not stir or break the raft** during the simmer.
11. **At end of cook**, ladle the broth **from beneath the raft** through a cheesecloth-lined fine chinois.
12. **Degrease** — easier when chilled (lift solidified fat off the top).
13. **Adjust seasoning** at service temperature.

#### What can go wrong

| Problem | Cause | Fix or prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Raft doesn't form** | Heat too aggressive too fast; not enough egg white; broth too hot at combine | Slow heat; verify egg-white quantity; combine cold |
| **Raft breaks apart during simmer** | Boiling instead of simmering; excessive stirring after raft formation | Maintain gentle simmer; don't disturb |
| **Burnt raft** | Pot too hot; raft stuck to bottom; not enough early gentle stirring | Lower heat; stir gently until raft floats |
| **Cloudy consommé** | Raft incomplete; broth not strained through cheesecloth at the end; particles bypassed the raft | Restart with a fresh raft if necessary, or accept reduced clarity for less-formal use |
| **Weak flavour** | Starting broth was weak; raft didn't contribute enough | Use a richer starting broth; build a more flavoursome raft |

#### Degreasing
- Chill the strained consommé in the walk-in until fat solidifies on the surface.
- Lift off the solidified fat layer with a spatula.
- Re-heat gently to service temperature.

#### Modern alternatives
- **Agar clarification** — gel-and-melt technique using agar to bind impurities into a gel that can be lifted out.
- **Gelatin clarification ("ice filtration")** — freeze a gelatin-set broth, then drip-thaw through cheesecloth; ice crystals lift impurities away as the gelatin filters.
- These are advanced techniques used in modernist or fine-dining operations; the classical raft remains the Red Seal exam standard. **[Classical]**

### D-14.03.04P — Monitor Cooking Time and Aromatics

**Performance Criteria:** monitor cooking time and aromatics
**Evidence of Attainment:** cooking time and aromatics are monitored according to recipe specifications and desired results.

#### Cooking-time guidelines by soup type

| Soup | Typical active cook time |
|---|---|
| **Clear soup** (assembly) | 15–30 min once broth is hot |
| **Consommé** (clarification) | 30–60 min |
| **Cream soup** | 30–45 min (sweating + simmering + finishing) |
| **Puréed vegetable soup** | 30–60 min depending on vegetable |
| **Legume / bean soups** | 60–120 min (longer for dried beans not pre-soaked) |
| **Specialty broths (pho, ramen)** | Broth itself is 4–24 h (see D-13); bowl assembly is minutes |
| **Cold soups** | Process minutes; chill 4+ hours |

> **[Best Practice]** Exact times depend on ingredient size, batch volume, and equipment.

#### Aromatic timing — when to add what

| Aromatic | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Mirepoix** | Sweat at start | Builds the flavour foundation; needs time to soften |
| **Bay leaves, peppercorns, dried herbs (in sachet)** | Add with liquid; remove at end of cook | Slow flavour release |
| **Fresh thyme, parsley stems** | With sachet or early | Robust; benefit from extraction |
| **Featured vegetable** | Right time for desired doneness | Match cook to vegetable type |
| **Wine for acid/depth** | After sweating, reduce alone briefly | Drives off raw alcohol |
| **Tomato product** | Mid-cook | Develops without scorching |
| **Cream / milk** | Last 5–10 min, off boil | Prevents breaking |
| **Delicate herbs (basil, tarragon, cilantro, mint)** | At service or final 1–2 min | Volatile aromatics |
| **Fresh citrus / acidic finish** | Off heat or at service | Long cook dulls citrus |
| **Garnish** | At plating | Visual freshness |
| **Salt** | Final adjust at service temperature | Reductions concentrate; cold mutes |

#### Tasting and adjusting

- **Use a single-use sanitized spoon** per taste.
- **Never re-dip** into the soup.
- **Taste at service temperature** — soups taste different hot vs lukewarm vs cold.
- **Adjust in stages** — small additions, taste again.
- **Document** new recipe iterations.

### D-14.03.05P — Incorporate Thickening Agents

**Performance Criteria:** incorporate thickening agents
**Evidence of Attainment:** thickening agents are incorporated to achieve desired results.

From Range of Variables: **roux, purées, modified starches, eggs.**

#### Thickener-by-thickener guide

##### Roux

- **What it is:** equal parts (by weight) of fat (butter, oil, lard) and flour, cooked together until the raw-flour taste is gone.
- **Mechanism:** wheat starch granules absorb water and swell; gluten proteins are dispersed in the fat to prevent gluten development; flour starch gelatinizes at ~ 70–95 °C.
- **Cook stages:** white (3–5 min) / blond (5–10 min) / brown (15–30 min) / dark brown (45+ min, Cajun gumbo).
- **Thickening power decreases** as roux is cooked darker — dark roux flavour is great but its thickening capacity drops noticeably.
- **Standard ratio for cream-soup body:** ~ 5–10% of liquid weight (roux total weight, fat + flour combined).
- **Incorporation rule:** hot roux + cold liquid (or cold roux + hot liquid), whisked, then brought to a gentle simmer to fully gelatinize and cook out raw flour notes (~ 15–20 minutes).

##### Purées (the soup itself as thickener)

- **Mechanism:** dissolved/dispersed solids — vegetable fibre, starches, pectin from cooked produce — provide body when blended into the liquid.
- **Best for:** vegetable purée soups, legume soups, squash soups.
- **Technique:** cook vegetable/legume tender in stock; blend in stand blender (or immersion); strain through fine sieve for refinement.
- **Adjusting:** add stock to thin; reduce or add additional puréed vegetable to thicken.
- **Advantage:** gluten-free, allergen-friendly, dairy-free options available.

##### Modified starches (cornstarch, arrowroot, tapioca, modified food starch)

- **Mechanism:** starch granules gelatinize in water and provide near-instant thickening.
- **Typical use:** Asian soups (corn-and-chicken, egg-drop, hot-and-sour), sauces, last-minute thickness correction.
- **Slurry technique:** mix starch with cold water (typically 1:1 or 1:2 starch:water) to a smooth paste; whisk into simmering soup; cook 1–2 min until thickened and clear (cornstarch) or stays cloudy (some modified starches).
- **Don't boil aggressively** after thickening — high heat can break the starch and thin the soup back out.
- **Cornstarch gives glossy translucent thickness; arrowroot is clearer; tapioca produces a slight stretch.**
- **Modified food starches** (cross-linked or pre-gelatinized) are formulated for stability under heat, acid, freeze-thaw — common in commercial production and frozen-soup products.
- **Gluten-free and allergen-friendly** in most cases (verify per product).

##### Eggs (liaison)

- **Mechanism:** egg yolk proteins coagulate at ~ 65–70 °C; combined with cream, they provide silky body and gloss without altering colour much.
- **Classical liaison:** ~ 3 egg yolks per 250 mL cream per litre of soup.
- **Technique:**
  1. **Beat yolks and cream together** in a bowl.
  2. **Temper** by gradually whisking in **small amounts of hot soup** (one ladle at a time) — bringing the egg mixture up to temperature slowly so the yolks don't scramble.
  3. **Pour the tempered liaison back into the soup** off heat (or just below simmer).
  4. **Whisk to incorporate.**
  5. **Hold below 85 °C** — boiling will scramble the eggs and curdle the soup.
- **Best for:** velouté, refined cream soups, avgolemono (Greek egg-lemon soup).
- **Allergen consideration:** egg is a Health Canada priority allergen; cross-contact controls apply.
- **Food safety:** holding egg-finished soups in the danger zone for prolonged periods is risky; serve fresh.

#### Comparing thickening power and characteristics

| Thickener | Thickening power per gram | Clarity / appearance | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cornstarch** | ~ 2 × flour | Glossy translucent | Asian soups, last-minute fixes |
| **Arrowroot** | ~ 2 × flour | Clearer than cornstarch | Same; less common in commercial Canadian kitchens |
| **Wheat flour (in roux)** | 1 × (reference) | Opaque | Cream soups, velouté |
| **Potato (puréed)** | Low–moderate per g, but generous body from native starch | Opaque | Purée soups, vichyssoise |
| **Bread (in cold soups)** | Low; volume-dependent | Opaque | Gazpacho, ajo blanco |
| **Egg yolk liaison** | Low (per gram) but elegant body and gloss | Slightly enriched colour | Refined velouté, refined cream soups |

#### Common thickener errors

- **Lumpy roux soup** — temperature mismatch (hot + hot); not enough whisking; flour added directly to hot liquid.
- **Raw-flour taste** — roux not cooked long enough.
- **Broken cream soup** — boiled after cream added.
- **Curdled liaison** — heat too high; eggs not tempered.
- **Slurry added to non-simmering soup** — starches need heat to gelatinize; just-warm soup leaves a starchy aftertaste.
- **Over-thickened soup** — too much thickener; need to thin with more stock or cream.
- **Stretchy / gummy** — too much arrowroot or tapioca, or wrong starch for the application.

### D-14.03.06P — Adjust Soup Characteristics

**Performance Criteria:** adjust soup characteristics
**Evidence of Attainment:** soup characteristics are adjusted to achieve desired results.

From Range of Variables: **taste, thickness, texture, appearance, colour, temperature.**

#### Adjustment-by-characteristic

##### Taste
- **Too bland:** add salt (small amounts at a time), umami booster (a splash of soy/fish sauce/miso/parmesan rind/glace), or acid (lemon, vinegar) for brightness.
- **Too salty:** add more stock or unsalted ingredient (potato slice to absorb, then remove); rebuild with diluting body if needed.
- **Too acidic:** add a pinch of sugar or a small amount of cream / butter.
- **Too sweet (rare in soup; common in tomato):** add acid (lemon, vinegar).
- **Too rich / heavy:** add stock or water; acid for cut.
- **Flat (lacking depth):** add aromatics, finish with herbs, deglaze new fond, build umami.
- **Bitter (over-extracted aromatics, scorched roux):** sometimes unsalvageable; a touch of cream and acid can mask mild bitterness.

##### Thickness
- **Too thin:** add starch slurry, knead in beurre manié (cold butter + flour pinch), reduce, or add more purée.
- **Too thick:** add hot stock, water, milk, cream, or a thin purée of the same featured vegetable.

##### Texture
- **Grainy from over-puréed starch** — strain through fine chinois.
- **Curdled (cream broken)** — sometimes recoverable by removing from heat, whisking in a small amount of cold cream/milk or a starch slurry, and gentle re-warming; severe breaks are unsalvageable.
- **Lumpy from poorly incorporated roux** — strain through fine chinois; may need to remake.

##### Appearance
- **Surface scum or fat** — skim.
- **Visible streaks** — whisk to homogenize.
- **Unappealing colour** — see below.

##### Colour
- **Too pale (cream of mushroom should be ivory-tan, not white)** — pre-brown some mushrooms separately and add back; deglaze with sherry for amber depth.
- **Too dark (over-roasted aromatics)** — sometimes balanced with a splash of cream or a brighter finishing element; severe over-darkness is unsalvageable.
- **Tomato-based discolouration over time** — colours fade in long hold; refreshen with a small addition of tomato paste or vibrant garnish.

##### Temperature
- **Hot hold ≥ 60 °C** in bain-marie or hot well.
- **Service temperature 70–80 °C** for hot soups.
- **Cold hold ≤ 4 °C** for cold soups.
- **Reheat to 74 °C within 2 h.**
- **Probe to verify** — visual cues alone can mislead.

#### Service-time finishing

- **Stir gently** before each plating to redistribute thickener that may have settled.
- **Wipe the bowl rim** clean before placing garnish.
- **Add garnish at the table side** (cream swirl, herb oil, croutons, chives) for visual freshness.
- **Pre-heat or pre-chill the bowl** to match the soup's temperature — a cold bowl on hot soup drops the soup's temperature significantly.

## 2. Knowledge Objectives — Deeper Dive

### D-14.03.01L — Soups and Their Applications (Cooking Lens)

#### Factors to consider when cooking soups

| Factor | Cooking-stage implication |
|---|---|
| **Featured solid character** | Match cook time and method (delicate veg vs hardy beans) |
| **Stock quality** | A great base is half the soup; weak stocks require flavour-building during cook |
| **Dairy stability** | Heavy cream (≥ 33% MF) holds heat better than milk; sour cream / yogurt added off heat |
| **Acidity** | Too much acid breaks cream; add acidic ingredients carefully |
| **Salinity** | Avoid over-salting at the cook stage; reductions concentrate; allow service-stage adjustment |
| **Intended hold time** | Soups for batch hot-hold need slightly under-cooked vegetables (carry-over cook); soups served à la minute can finish tighter |
| **Reheating expectations** | Soups that will be re-thermalized should be cooled rapidly and reheated once to 74 °C — avoid repeated cycles for quality |

#### Thickening agents (Range of Variables)

- **Roux** — wheat-flour-and-fat cooked mixture; cream soups, gumbo (dark), velouté.
- **Purées** — body from the soup's own solids; ideal for allergen-friendly and gluten-free production.
- **Modified starches** — cornstarch, arrowroot, tapioca, commercial modified food starches; ideal for Asian soups and last-minute corrections.
- **Eggs (liaison)** — yolks + cream tempered into the soup; refined finish; allergen consideration (egg).

##### Other (extension) thickeners worth knowing
- **Beurre manié** — equal parts cold butter and flour kneaded together, whisked into a simmering soup in pinches for last-minute thickening; faster than building a roux.
- **Cream reduction** — reduce cream alone to concentrate its body, then stir in.
- **Bread** — used in cold soups (gazpacho, ajo blanco); stale bread soaked in liquid then blended.
- **Cheese** — adds body and richness; melted into the soup off heat (e.g., French onion gratinée).
- **Gelatin** — used for cold consommé applications, sometimes for body in chilled soups.

#### Dietary requirements (recap)

| Need | Implication |
|---|---|
| **Allergens** | Egg liaison off-limits for egg allergy; roux off-limits for wheat allergy; cream soups off-limits for dairy allergy; bisques/chowders off-limits for fish/shellfish |
| **Vegan** | Vegetable stock + cashew/oat/coconut cream + roux made with vegan butter + slurry as a thickener |
| **Celiac** | Cornstarch slurry, rice flour, arrowroot instead of wheat-flour roux |
| **Halal / kosher** | Certified protein supply; no pork; kosher meat-dairy separation |
| **Low-sodium** | Salt held until very end; rely on aromatics, acid, and umami for flavour |
| **Diabetic** | Manage carbohydrate-heavy thickeners (potato purée, rice, noodles); favour cream + purée |

### D-14.03.02L — Procedures to Prepare Soups

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to prepare soups, clarify consommés, and thicken soups.

#### Procedures (Range of Variables): selecting cooking time and temperature, straining, reducing, deglazing, skimming, puréeing.

##### Selecting cooking time and temperature
- Match to soup type (table above).
- Use a probe thermometer to verify.
- Don't over-cook delicate aromatics or under-cook dense vegetables.

##### Straining
- Staged: china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth-lined chinois for clarity.
- Ladle, don't pour (carry-over from D-13.03).
- Don't press for refined soups.

##### Reducing
- Wide pan; gentle simmer; tracks volume.
- Skim continuously.
- Don't season at the reduction stage if downstream use is unknown.

##### Deglazing
- Lift caramelized fond with liquid; scrape with wooden/silicone spatula.
- Add deglazed liquid to soup.

##### Skimming
- Early and often.
- Wide flat skimmer; no stirring.
- Final degreasing easier when chilled.

##### Puréeing
- Cook ingredients tender; blend in stand blender or immersion.
- **Hot-liquid blender safety:** ≤ ⅔ full, vent, towel, pulse — never run continuously (see [D-14.02-research.md](D-14.02-research.md)).
- Strain through fine sieve for refinement.

## 3. Food-Safety Considerations Specific to Cooking Soups

### Hot holding during service

- **Soup must remain ≥ 60 °C in the hot well / bain-marie**. **[Regulatory]**
- **Probe periodically** to verify; visual cues can deceive.
- **Stir occasionally** to maintain even temperature distribution.
- **Replenish from a fresh batch** rather than topping up old soup with new — avoid stretching service over hours of partial-fill hot hold.

### Reheating

- **Reheat to 74 °C / 165 °F internal within 2 hours**. **[Regulatory]**
- **Stir during reheating** to ensure cold spots reach temperature.
- **Probe in multiple spots** to verify.
- **Reheat once** — repeated cycles damage quality (carry-over from A-2.01).
- **Steam tables, bain-maries, hot wells DO NOT reheat** — they hold. Use stovetop, combi, or steam kettle to reheat from refrigerated.

### Impacts of incorrect re-thermalization on soup quality and safety

**Safety:**
- **Surviving spores** (*B. cereus*, *C. perfringens*) germinate if cooling was slow; reheating doesn't always kill the resulting toxin.
- **Heat-stable toxins** (e.g., *S. aureus* enterotoxin) are not destroyed by reheating — prevention via hygiene + temperature is the only defence.
- **Pathogen survival in cold spots** — slow reheating in a thick cream soup or chowder can leave centre cold while edges scorch.
- **Cross-contamination** if reheating equipment wasn't sanitized between cycles.

**Quality:**
- **Cream soups break** — proteins denature unevenly under repeated heat cycles; emulsion fails.
- **Roux-thickened soups lose body** — repeated heat breaks down starch gelatinization; soup turns thin and watery.
- **Vegetables in clear soups overcook** — original textures lost; muddy appearance.
- **Aromatic top notes evaporate** — fresh herbs and citrus accents disappear with each reheat.
- **Fat separates** — oils and butter break out of suspension; surface slick.
- **Maillard / browning compounds degrade** — earlier roasted notes go bitter or stale.
- **Colour fades or darkens** — visible quality drop bowl-to-bowl.

> **Operational rule:** plan to reheat each portion or batch **once** to order. Don't repeatedly reheat the same pot of soup over a multi-day service window — split into single-use portions and reheat individually.

### Cooling (carry-over from A-2.02)

- **60 °C → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 °C → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.** **[Regulatory / Best Practice]**
- Methods: ice wand, ice bath, blast chiller, shallow vented pans.
- **Soups are top *C. perfringens* and *B. cereus* risk** because of:
  - Large volumes that cool slowly.
  - High protein/starch content (perfect bacterial growth medium).
  - Spore-formers surviving the cook.
- **Cover only after ≤ 4 °C.**

## 4. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Cooking cream of mushroom soup (10 L batch)
1. **Mise en place:** sweat aromatics ready (onion, leek, garlic); 2 kg mushrooms cleaned and chopped; 200 g butter + 200 g flour for roux; 7 L chicken stock; 1.5 L heavy cream; salt, white pepper, thyme, optional sherry.
2. **Sweat aromatics** in 100 g butter over medium-low heat 10 min.
3. **Add half the mushrooms**, sauté until golden (some browning here builds umami).
4. **Add remaining mushrooms** for second batch of sweating.
5. **Build the roux** in a separate pan (100 g butter + 200 g flour) until pale blond, ~ 5 min.
6. **Whisk in stock** gradually — hot roux + slowly added cold stock = smooth.
7. **Pour into the soup pot**, whisk to combine, bring to a gentle simmer 20–30 min.
8. **Probe at 88–92 °C** through the cook.
9. **Purée** with immersion blender (or stand blender in batches with hot-blender safety) — leave some texture or strain for refined version.
10. **Below simmer**, whisk in cream.
11. **Adjust seasoning** — salt, white pepper, finishing dry sherry.
12. **Hold ≥ 60 °C** or **cool per A-2.02** — shallow pans, ice bath, ice wand, blast chiller.

### Scenario 2 — Clarifying a 6 L consommé from beef stock
1. **Combine raft cold:** 750 g lean ground beef shin, 8 egg whites, 300 g fine brunoise (onion, carrot, celery), 300 g crushed ice, 100 g tomato paste, splash of dry red wine, 1 *oignon brûlé* for amber colour.
2. **Whisk gently** in a tall narrow pot.
3. **Add 6 L cold (degreased) beef stock**, whisk.
4. **Heat on medium-low**, stirring gently every minute to prevent the raft sticking.
5. **At ~ 65 °C**, raft begins to coagulate — stop stirring.
6. **Raft rises and forms a visible mass** at ~ 80 °C.
7. **Cut a vent hole** in the centre.
8. **Maintain barely-trembling simmer (~ 88–92 °C) for 45 min.**
9. **Ladle from beneath the raft** through cheesecloth-lined chinois into a clean container.
10. **Chill, lift fat cap, re-warm, adjust seasoning.**

### Scenario 3 — Tempering a liaison into a chicken velouté
1. **Velouté simmering** at ~ 85 °C; remove from direct heat.
2. **In a bowl:** 3 egg yolks + 250 mL cream, whisked.
3. **Ladle small amounts of hot velouté** into the yolk-cream mixture, whisking constantly. Add ~ 4–5 ladles to bring the egg mixture up to ~ 60 °C.
4. **Pour the tempered mixture back into the velouté**, whisking.
5. **Return to low heat** if needed; **never above ~ 85 °C** — boiling will scramble.
6. **Adjust seasoning, serve immediately.**

### Scenario 4 — Service rush: a guest reports their soup is cool
1. **Probe** the soup in the bowl — if < 60 °C, it has been in the danger zone.
2. **Discard the bowl** (don't reheat in the bowl).
3. **Probe the hot well** — verify ≥ 60 °C; investigate any drift.
4. **Re-plate from a freshly reheated batch** (74 °C verified).
5. **Pre-heat the bowl** for service.
6. **Document** the deviation and fix the hot-well issue.

### Scenario 5 — Stuck raft burning the consommé
1. **Smell:** scorched / acrid notes.
2. **Action:** if caught early enough, transfer the broth carefully (without disturbing the burnt bottom) to a clean pot.
3. **Don't scrape the burnt residue** — leave it in the original pot.
4. **Assess the broth** — if mild scorch flavour and otherwise clear, may be salvageable for less-formal use.
5. **If badly scorched, discard.**
6. **Lesson:** more frequent gentle stirring early; lower heat; thicker-bottomed pot.

### Scenario 6 — Vegan guest, dairy-free cream-of-cauliflower
1. **Substitute** vegetable stock for chicken; coconut cream or cashew cream for dairy; vegan butter or olive oil for butter.
2. **Sweat aromatics** in olive oil.
3. **Cook cauliflower** in vegetable stock until tender.
4. **Purée**.
5. **Stir in coconut cream off heat** for body and richness.
6. **Adjust** with salt, white pepper, lemon, nutritional yeast for cheesy notes.
7. **Use dedicated allergen-isolated tools** (purple cutting board, clean pot, etc.). See [A-2.04-research.md](A-2.04-research.md).

## 5. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Boiling cream soup** — emulsion breaks; soup separates.
- **Adding cream to a hard-simmering soup** — instant break.
- **Tempering omitted on liaison** — scrambled yolks.
- **Slurry added to non-simmering soup** — starchy aftertaste; thickener doesn't fully gelatinize.
- **Over-blended purée** — gummy or grainy depending on ingredient.
- **Steam kettle lid opened toward the body** — steam burns.
- **Stand blender filled past ⅔** — hot soup blowout.
- **Salting at the start** — concentrates if soup reduces or is held long.
- **Holding cream soup at hot well > 4 hours** — quality drops sharply; food-safety risk if temperature dips.
- **Re-thermalizing a soup multiple times** — quality decay + cumulative pathogen risk.
- **Reheating in the hot well from refrigerated** — stays in danger zone for hours.
- **Burning roux** — bitter, won't thicken; restart.
- **Skipping raft venting** in consommé — pressure builds; raft can rupture.
- **Stirring after raft formation** — raft breaks up, particles disperse, consommé clouds.
- **Forgetting to deglaze** the sauté pan after sweating — fond left behind = flavour lost.
- **Acidic finish added too early** — dulled brightness; broken cream.
- **Using table cream / coffee cream in cream soup** — curdles or thins.
- **Wet immersion blender / probe between dishes** — cross-contamination, sanitation failure.
- **Probe touching pot bottom** — false high reading; mistake heating control.
- **No cooling plan when cook ends** — finished soup sits in the danger zone.

## 7. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with D-14.03.

1. **Tools (Range of Variables):** whisks, food processor/blender, cheesecloth, spoon, spatula, strainers, steam kettle, soup pot.
2. **Steam kettle** — jacketed steam heat for high-volume gentle cooking; phase-change physics + serious burn risk; **open the lid away from the body**.
3. **Simmer (85–95 °C), don't boil**, for most clear, cream, purée, and clarification cooks. Boiling breaks cream and emulsifies fat.
4. **Cream soup sequence:** sweat aromatics → build roux → whisk in stock → simmer → purée → finish with cream off heat (below ~ 85 °C) → adjust season.
5. **Roux rule:** equal parts fat + flour by weight; **hot roux + cold liquid (or vice versa) = smooth; hot + hot = lumps.**
6. **Roux colour:** white / blond / brown / dark — darker has more flavour, less thickening.
7. **Heavy cream ≥ 33% MF** for cream-soup stability; **never boil** after adding cream.
8. **Four named thickeners (Range of Variables):** roux, purées, modified starches (cornstarch, arrowroot, tapioca), eggs (liaison).
9. **Slurry technique** (modified starches): mix with cold water to a paste; whisk into simmering soup; cook 1–2 min to gelatinize.
10. **Liaison (egg yolk + cream):** **temper** by adding hot soup to the yolk mixture gradually; pour back; **hold ≤ 85 °C** to prevent curdling.
11. **Consommé clarification** uses a **raft** of lean ground meat + egg whites + brunoise mirepoix + crushed ice + optional acid.
12. **Combine raft cold; heat slowly; gentle stirring until raft forms; stop stirring; vent the raft; barely-trembling simmer 30–60 min; ladle from beneath through cheesecloth-lined chinois; degrease.**
13. **Aromatic timing:** mirepoix at start; sachet with liquid; fresh delicate herbs at end / at service; acid finishes at service.
14. **Tasting** with single-use sanitized spoons; never re-dip into the soup.
15. **Soup characteristics (Range of Variables):** taste, thickness, texture, appearance, colour, temperature — each adjustable mid-cook and at service.
16. **Service temperatures:** clear / consommé > 70–75 °C; cream / purée 70–75 °C; cold ≤ 4 °C.
17. **Hot hold ≥ 60 °C** in bain-marie / hot well. **[Regulatory]**
18. **Reheat to 74 °C / 165 °F within 2 hours**, once only. Steam tables hold; they do **not** reheat.
19. **Cooling (carry-over from A-2.02):** 60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.
20. **Soups are top *C. perfringens* / *B. cereus* risk** during cooling; spores survive cooking; germinate in slow cooling.
21. **Heat-stable toxins** (*S. aureus*, *B. cereus* emetic) are **not destroyed by reheating** — prevent via hygiene + temperature.
22. **Re-thermalization quality losses:** broken cream, lost body, evaporated top notes, separated fat, faded colour. Plan single-reheat service.
23. **Hot-blender safety:** ≤ ⅔ full, vent the lid, towel over, pulse.
24. **Strain stages:** china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth-lined chinois; ladle, don't pour.
25. **Steam kettle workflow:** prep ingredients near; operate at gentle simmer; tilt-pour at end; cleanse interior immediately after.
26. **Dietary substitutions** (recap): celiac → cornstarch slurry / rice flour; vegan → vegetable stock + plant cream; halal/kosher → certified supply.
27. **Health Canada priority allergens** to scan for: eggs (liaison), milk (cream), mustard, peanuts, crustaceans/molluscs (bisque, chowder), fish, sesame, soy, sulphites, tree nuts (incl. coconut for labelling), wheat (roux).
28. **Probe thermometer** between every spot and protein; sanitize between uses.
29. **Garnish at plating** for fresh visual and aroma.
30. **Document** cook, reheat, hot-hold, and cooling milestones for HACCP-aligned operations.
