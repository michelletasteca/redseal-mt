# B-3.01 | Uses Knives — Enriched Research Document

**Major Work Activity:** B — Common Occupational Skills
**Task:** B-3 | Uses tools and equipment
**Competency Level:** Performance + Knowledge
**Applicable Regulations:** WHMIS, HACCP, OH&S, Public Health Regulations (provincial)

---

## Overview and Why This Competency Matters

The knife is the most fundamental tool in any professional kitchen. A Red Seal Cook must demonstrate mastery not only in cutting technique, but in selection, inspection, sharpening, sanitation, and storage — because errors in any of these areas have direct consequences for food safety, product quality, worker safety, and equipment longevity.

Knife-related injuries are among the most common workplace accidents in commercial kitchens (common industry practice awareness). A dull knife is significantly more dangerous than a sharp one: it requires more pressure, increasing the chance of slipping, and produces ragged, uneven cuts that extend food-contact time and increase the risk of bruising and bacterial entry into product.

Knives are also a **biological cross-contamination vector**. A knife used on raw poultry and then on ready-to-eat (RTE) vegetables without washing and sanitizing in between can transfer pathogens such as *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter* directly to food that will not be cooked again.

## B-3.01.01P | Select Knives

### Why Selection Matters

Using the wrong knife for a task increases effort, creates safety risk (forcing a blade), and degrades cut quality. The correct knife matches the task's required precision, the food product's density, and the desired cut dimension.

### Knife Types, Characteristics, and Applications

| Knife | Blade Length | Blade Profile | Primary Use | Why This Knife |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Chef's Knife (French Knife)** | 20–30 cm | Broad, slightly curved | All-purpose chopping, dicing, mincing, slicing | Curved belly allows rocking motion; broad blade can crush garlic, transfer food |
| **Paring Knife** | 6–10 cm | Narrow, pointed | Peeling, trimming, detail cuts, tournée, brunoise on small items | Short blade = maximum control for in-hand work and precision detail |
| **Boning Knife** | 13–16 cm | Stiff or flexible; narrow | Removing bones from meat, poultry, and fish | Narrow blade fits along bone contour; stiff = beef/pork; flexible = fish/poultry |
| **Filleting Knife** | 15–25 cm | Very flexible; thin | Filleting round and flat fish | Extreme flexibility allows blade to follow skeletal contours without tearing flesh |
| **Carving Knife** | 20–30 cm | Long, thin, slightly flexible | Slicing cooked roasts, poultry, ham | Length allows full draw-stroke slices; minimal pressure = cleaner cuts, less tearing |
| **Cleaver** | 15–20 cm | Rectangular; heavy spine | Splitting poultry, portioning bones, tenderizing | Weight generates force; not for precision; blade angle matters for bone splitting |

> **Practical Note:** In a busy commercial kitchen, the chef's knife performs approximately 80% of all cutting tasks. Mastery of the chef's knife is the single most valuable cutting skill for an apprentice.

### Blade Material Science

Understanding blade material helps apprentices select the right knife for the product and maintain it correctly.

| Material | Hardness | Edge Retention | Rust Resistance | Reactivity | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **High-Carbon Stainless Steel** | Medium-High (55–60 HRC) | Good | Excellent | None | Commercial kitchens (most common); balance of durability and edge performance |
| **Carbon Steel** | High (60–65 HRC) | Excellent | Poor — rusts easily | Reacts with acid, onions, fish | Fine-edge work; butcher shops; requires constant drying and oiling |
| **Ceramic (Zirconium Oxide)** | Very High (Mohs 8.5) | Exceptional | N/A (not metal) | None | Boneless slicing only; will chip on bones, frozen food, hard vegetables |
| **Stainless Steel (standard)** | Lower (54–58 HRC) | Moderate | Excellent | None | Budget knives; high-volume operations with heavy cleaning regimes |

#### Handle Materials

| Material | Hygiene | Durability | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Polypropylene (poly)** | Excellent — non-porous | High | Standard in commercial kitchens; dishwasher-tolerant handles; meets NSF food-safety standards |
| **Fibrox/Fibreglass composite** | Excellent | High | Victorinox standard; textured grip even when wet; preferred in many Canadian commercial kitchens |
| **Wood** | Poor if cracked — porous | Moderate | Traditional; if cracked, harbours bacteria and must be replaced; not recommended in food premises by many provincial health authorities |
| **Pakkawood/Stabilized wood** | Better than raw wood | High | Resin-impregnated; more resistant to moisture; still inspect for cracks |

> **Regulatory Note:** Provincial food premises regulations may restrict certain handle materials. Check your jurisdiction. Wood handles in a cracked or scored condition are a food safety violation in most Canadian provinces (common industry practice / provincial health standard).

## B-3.01.02P | Utilize Knives

### Grip and Hand Positioning

**The Pinch Grip (Standard Commercial Grip)**

The pinch grip is the professional standard. It provides maximum control, reduces fatigue, and places the hand safely on the blade for directed cuts.

1. Grip the blade between the pad of the thumb and the side of the bent index finger, just ahead of the bolster
2. Wrap the remaining three fingers around the handle
3. The pinch point is at the heel of the blade, not on the handle alone

**Why it works:** Gripping at the balance point reduces wrist strain and gives precise control of the blade angle. Gripping only the handle means the blade pivots from the wrist — less control, more effort, greater inaccuracy.

**The Claw Grip (Guide Hand)**

The non-dominant hand holds food in the claw grip at all times:

1. Curl the fingertips inward so fingernails touch the food
2. Knuckles protrude forward and act as the guide for the blade
3. The flat side of the blade rests lightly against the knuckles
4. The thumb is tucked behind the fingers

> **Safety Rule:** The blade should NEVER be able to reach fingertips because the knuckles act as a physical stop. The moment this grip is relaxed — the risk of laceration increases immediately.

### Core Cutting Techniques

| Technique | Motion | Knife Used | When |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rock chop** | Tip stays on board; heel rocks down in arc | Chef's knife | Mincing herbs, garlic; high-speed repetitive chopping |
| **Push cut** | Blade pushed forward and down through food | Chef's or slicing knife | Clean slices through dense product |
| **Pull cut (draw cut)** | Blade pulled toward body through food | Boning, filleting, carving | Following bone contours; delicate slicing |
| **Tap chop** | Blade lifted and brought straight down | Cleaver, chef's knife | Rough chopping; poultry joints |
| **Horizontal (accordion) cut** | Blade parallel to board, food held from above | Chef's knife | Splitting layers in onion; butterfly cuts |
| **Scoring** | Shallow cuts on surface | Chef's, paring | Cross-hatch on scored items; skin of fish |

### Knife Handling Safety Rules

- **Never try to catch a falling knife** — step back and let it fall. "A falling knife has no handle."
- **Never use a knife to open cans, pry lids, or scrape surfaces** — these actions damage the blade and cause slips
- **Always cut away from the body** — direction of force in case of slip
- **Announce your presence** when walking behind someone who is cutting: say "Behind!" or "Knife!"
- **Carry a knife with tip pointing down, blade turned back, close to your side** — never carry pointing outward or toward another person
- **Never leave a knife at the edge of a surface** or hidden under towels or food — laceration hazard
- **Never put knives in a sink of soapy water** — hidden blade is a severe laceration hazard

### Cross-Contamination Protocols — Critical Food Safety

A knife blade that contacts one food product and is then used on another without washing and sanitizing is a **cross-contamination event**.

| Scenario | Risk | Pathogen Concern |
|---|---|---|
| Raw poultry → raw salad greens | **CRITICAL** — salad is not cooked | *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter* |
| Raw beef → cooked beef | **HIGH** — cooked product contaminated | *E. coli* O157:H7 |
| Raw fish → RTE garnish | **HIGH** | Listeria, parasites |
| Peanut-containing product → allergen-free dish | **SEVERE** — anaphylaxis risk | Protein allergen cross-contact |
| Onion → fruit garnish | **LOW** food safety, but flavour cross-contamination | — |

**Rule:** Wash, rinse, and sanitize the knife (and cutting board) between every product type change, especially when crossing from raw to RTE or between allergen groups.

## B-3.01.03P | Inspect Knives

### Pre-Use Inspection Protocol

Inspect every knife before use. A damaged knife is a food safety hazard (physical contamination — metal fragments) and an OH&S hazard.

#### Sharpness Tests

| Test | Method | Result Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Paper test** | Hold sheet of paper by top edge; draw knife blade along edge | Clean slice = sharp; tearing or deflection = dull |
| **Tomato test** | Set ripe tomato on board; draw knife across skin with minimal pressure | Sharp knife glides through skin without pressing; dull knife slides |
| **Thumbnail test (careful)** | Lightly press edge perpendicular to thumbnail | Sharp edge catches slightly; dull edge slides off — light pressure only |
| **Visual inspection** | Look along edge in bright light at low angle | Light-reflecting flat spots indicate rolled or damaged edge |

> **Safety Note:** The arm-hair shave test is used in some training environments but is not appropriate in a commercial kitchen. Use the paper test or tomato test for professional assessment.

#### Damage Inspection Checklist

- **Chips or nicks in the blade edge** — small metal fragments can enter food (physical contamination); grinding required
- **Cracks in the blade** — remove from service immediately
- **Rust or pitting** — especially on carbon steel; deep pitting means food can be trapped; chemical contamination risk
- **Loose or cracked handle** — crevices harbour bacteria; structural failure risk mid-task; remove from service
- **Loose or missing rivets** — gap between handle and blade scales traps food debris
- **Bent tip** — common from improper use (prying); affects precision and safety
- **Damaged bolster** — rough edges are a laceration hazard

> **Regulatory Note (OH&S):** Under provincial occupational health and safety legislation, employers must ensure equipment is maintained in safe working condition. A damaged knife that causes injury may constitute a violation. Damaged knives must be tagged out of service, repaired, or discarded.

## B-3.01.04P | Select and Use Sharpening Tools

### Understanding Honing vs. Sharpening — A Critical Distinction

| Action | Tool | What It Does | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Honing** | Honing steel | Realigns the microscopic "teeth" of the blade edge — restores geometry without removing metal | Before or after each use; daily minimum |
| **Sharpening** | Whetstone, electric sharpener, pull-through | Removes metal to grind a new edge | When honing fails to restore cutting performance; approximately monthly in high-use kitchens |

**Why the distinction matters:** Many apprentices confuse honing and sharpening. Honing a dull knife will not fix it — only sharpening can restore a damaged or worn edge. Conversely, sharpening too frequently removes metal unnecessarily and shortens blade life.

### Sharpening Tools — Types and Applications

#### 1. Whetstone (Sharpening Stone)

- **What it does:** Abrasive surface grinds away metal to create a new edge
- **Grits:**
  - Coarse (220–400 grit): Chips, severely dull edges, resetting bevel
  - Medium (1000–3000 grit): Regular sharpening maintenance
  - Fine/Polish (4000–8000 grit): Finishing edge, polishing
- **Angle:** 15–20° for Western/European knives; 10–15° for Japanese-style knives
- **Medium:** Use water (water stone) or honing oil — never mix; oil clogs a water stone
- **Procedure:**
  1. Soak water stone 5–10 minutes before use
  2. Place stone on damp cloth or rubber base (non-slip)
  3. Hold knife at correct angle to stone
  4. Push blade across stone, edge-first, in smooth strokes — tip to heel in one motion
  5. Alternate sides with equal strokes
  6. Move to finer grit; finish on leather strop or steel
  7. Rinse and dry blade before use

> **Why the angle matters:** The sharpening angle determines the trade-off between sharpness and edge durability. A 15° angle produces a razor-sharp edge but is more prone to chipping. A 20° angle is more durable and better for hard-use knives (cleavers, heavy chef's knives).

#### 2. Honing Steel (Sharpening Steel)

- **What it does:** Realigns the microscopic rolled edge (called the "burr" or "wire edge") that forms during use
- **Types:**
  - **Smooth/polished steel:** Gentle realignment; best for maintaining a fine edge
  - **Ridged/grooved steel:** More aggressive; mild metal removal; better for slightly dull blades
  - **Diamond-coated steel:** Actually removes metal; used for light sharpening between whetstone sessions
  - **Ceramic rod:** Fine abrasion; between honing and sharpening; good for very fine edges
- **Angle:** Match the bevel angle of the knife (15–20° Western; 10–15° Japanese)
- **Methods:**
  - *Stationary method (commercial kitchen standard):* Tip of steel on a folded towel on the board, handle held up; draw knife down and across the steel
  - *Free-hand method:* Steel in one hand, blade drawn across — higher risk in a busy kitchen

> **Memory Aid — Honing Steel Angle:** "Point, elbow, wrist." Hold the steel at arm's length; your elbow, wrist, and the blade create the angle reference triangle.

#### 3. Pull-Through Sharpener

- **What it does:** Draws the blade through a fixed V-shaped abrasive slot; removes metal at a preset angle
- **Advantages:** Fast; no technique required; consistent for basic knives
- **Disadvantages:** Removes significant metal; preset angle may not match knife's original bevel; not appropriate for high-quality, Japanese, or ceramic knives; coarse abrasives can leave rough edge
- **Use when:** Quick restoration needed; budget knives; training environments where whetstone technique is not yet mastered

#### 4. Electric Sharpener

- **What it does:** Motorized abrasive wheels rotate and draw blade through staged slots (coarse → fine → polish)
- **Advantages:** Fast; consistent; good for high-volume operations
- **Disadvantages:** Removes the most metal per use; shortens blade life fastest; not for ceramic knives (will crack); not for Japanese single-bevel knives; motor generates heat that can affect temper of cheap steel
- **Manufacturers' specifications:** Always follow the manufacturer's angle recommendation and number of strokes — over-use removes excessive metal

### Sharpening Tool Selection Guide

| Knife Type | Recommended Sharpening | Honing Tool |
|---|---|---|
| Western chef's knife (stainless) | Whetstone (medium → fine) or electric | Ridged or ceramic steel |
| High-carbon steel knife | Whetstone (water) preferred | Smooth polished steel |
| Japanese knife | Whetstone (fine, 3000–8000) only — never electric | Ceramic rod only; angle-matched |
| Ceramic knife | Professional service only — diamond wheel | Cannot be honed conventionally |
| Serrated knife | Professional service only — not field-serviceable | Does not hone |
| Cleaver | Whetstone (coarse → medium); pull-through acceptable | Ridged steel |

## B-3.01.05P | Wash, Dry and Sanitize Knives

### Why Knives Require Special Washing Care

Knives must be washed separately by hand — **never in a commercial dishwasher** — for three reasons:

1. **Edge damage:** Dishwasher tumbling and aggressive spray impacts damage the fine edge geometry; harsh alkaline detergents accelerate corrosion of even stainless steel blades
2. **Handle damage:** High heat and water cycling causes wooden handles to crack, polypropylene handles to loosen at the rivets, and adhesives to fail — creating bacterial harborage
3. **Safety hazard:** In a dishwasher rack, a knife blade is invisible and unpredictably positioned — reaching in to unload is a severe laceration risk

> **Industry Standard:** In Canadian commercial kitchens, knives are hand-washed. Many operations explicitly include "no knives in the dishwasher" as a documented food safety policy.

### Hand-Washing Procedure

1. **Rinse** immediately after use under hot running water — removes loose food debris
2. **Wash** with warm water and dishwashing detergent; use a cloth or sponge, wiping from spine to edge (never dragging hand along edge); blade faces away from body
3. **Rinse** with clean warm water — all detergent must be removed (residue interferes with sanitizer)
4. **Sanitize:**
   - Chemical: Approved sanitizer (chlorine-based: 100–200 ppm; quaternary ammonium: per label); immerse or wipe blade; follow contact time
   - Heat: ≥77°C hot water for minimum 2 minutes (three-compartment sink method, province-dependent)
5. **Air dry** or immediately dry with a clean, dry cloth — do not leave wet; prevents rust on carbon steel; prevents scale buildup on stainless
6. **Store** immediately after drying (see B-3.01.06P)

> **Critical:** Cleaning must occur BEFORE sanitizing. Organic food residue (fat, protein, starch) physically shields bacteria from the sanitizer and rapidly depletes sanitizer concentration, rendering it ineffective. This is the same principle as all food-contact surface sanitation.

### Sanitizer Concentrations (Reference)

| Sanitizer Type | Effective Concentration | Contact Time | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chlorine (sodium hypochlorite) | 100–200 ppm | ≥1 minute | Effective; low cost; corrosive to carbon steel over time — rinse thoroughly |
| Quaternary ammonium (quats) | 200 ppm (label-dependent) | ≥1 minute | Less corrosive to metals; leave on (no rinse needed per label) |
| Heat sanitization | ≥77°C water | ≥2 minutes | No chemical residue; preferred by some operations |

> **Regulatory Note:** Acceptable sanitizers and concentrations are governed by provincial food premises regulations and the Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA) for federally registered establishments. Always use a sanitizer approved for use on food-contact surfaces.

### Between-Task Washing (Critical Food Safety)

Knives must be fully washed, rinsed, and sanitized — not just wiped on a towel — when switching between:

- Raw protein → another raw protein type (especially poultry → fish → beef)
- Raw protein → RTE food (this is the highest-risk scenario)
- Allergen-containing food → allergen-free food
- Dirty produce → cleaned produce
- Pungent aromatics → delicately flavoured products (quality, not safety)

**A towel wipe does not sanitize. It redistributes contamination.**

## B-3.01.06P | Store Knives

### Why Storage Method Directly Affects Safety and Edge Retention

Improper storage is the most common cause of edge damage in commercial kitchens. An improperly stored knife is also a laceration hazard to anyone who reaches without looking.

### Storage Methods

| Method | Edge Protection | Safety | Hygiene | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Magnetic strip (knife rail)** | Good — blade edge not contacted | Good if mounted correctly | Good — blades visible and not touching | Blade should contact spine/flat first, not edge; mount blade side down (edge toward user), spine up |
| **Knife block** | Excellent — slots protect edge | Excellent | Moderate — interior of slots must be cleaned; can harbour dust and bacteria if not maintained | Top-loading blocks (edge down) protect edges better than side-loading |
| **Knife roll/case** | Excellent — individual sleeves | Excellent — contained | Good | Required for transport; professional standard for cooks carrying their own kit |
| **Blade guard (scabbard)** | Excellent | Excellent | Good | Required for individual knife transport and storage in some operations |
| **Drawer (with blade guard)** | Moderate | Acceptable | Moderate | ONLY with individual blade guards — never loose in a drawer |
| **Loose in a drawer** | **POOR — edges contact each other and other utensils** | **DANGEROUS — laceration on retrieval** | Poor | **Prohibited practice in professional kitchens** |
| **Soaking in water** | **POOR — handles swell and delaminate** | **DANGEROUS — invisible blade** | **POOR — promotes bacterial growth** | **Never acceptable** |

> **OH&S Note:** Loose knives in drawers, on counters hidden under towels, or in sinks of water are all OH&S violations under most provincial workplace safety regulations. All sharp tools must be stored so that the hazard (edge) is protected and the tool can be retrieved without contact with the sharp portion.

### Edge Retention Best Practices

- Store knives where blades do not contact other metal surfaces
- Use pan protectors or blade guards for stacked storage
- Never store chef's knives tip-down in a container with other utensils — the tip contacts the bottom and bends or chips
- Carbon steel knives: apply a thin layer of food-safe mineral oil before long-term storage to prevent rust

## B-3.01.07P | Choose Cutting Surfaces

### Why the Cutting Surface Matters

The cutting surface affects:
1. **Blade longevity** — hard surfaces (glass, granite, ceramic tile) immediately destroy the knife edge
2. **Food safety** — porous or grooved surfaces harbour bacteria; colour-coding prevents cross-contamination
3. **Worker safety** — slipping boards cause cutting injuries
4. **Regulatory compliance** — provincial food premises regulations specify acceptable cutting surface materials

### Cutting Surface Materials

| Material | Edge Impact | Hygiene | Food Safety | Commercial Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Polypropylene (plastic)** | Minimal | Non-porous when new; grooves develop over time | Good — can go in dishwasher; replace when deeply grooved | **Standard commercial kitchen requirement** |
| **Wood (end-grain)** | Gentle — fibres close around blade | Porous; some natural antimicrobial properties claimed; harder to sanitize | Acceptable for some applications if properly maintained; not recommended for raw meat by many health authorities | Acceptable for bread, cooked product in some operations |
| **Bamboo** | Slightly harder than wood; minimal edge impact | Similar to wood | Similar to wood | Common in smaller operations; not preferred commercially |
| **Glass** | **Destroys edge immediately** | Easy to clean | No — edge contact = metal fragments in food | **Not acceptable for professional knife work** |
| **Granite/marble** | **Destroys edge immediately** | Easy to clean | No | **Not acceptable** |
| **Ceramic tile** | **Destroys edge immediately** | Easy to clean | No | **Not acceptable** |

> **Regulatory Basis:** Provincial food premises regulations (e.g., Ontario Food Premises Regulation 493/17, BC Food Premises Regulation) require food-contact surfaces including cutting boards to be smooth, non-absorbent, and cleanable. Deeply scored plastic boards and cracked wood boards no longer meet this standard and must be replaced.

### Colour-Coded Cutting Boards — The Commercial Kitchen Standard

Colour-coded cutting boards are the industry standard for **cross-contamination prevention**. They create a physical system that separates food types, reducing reliance on memory alone.

| Colour | Food Category | Food Safety Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Red** | Raw beef, raw pork, raw lamb | Prevents *E. coli* O157:H7, *Salmonella* cross-contamination |
| **Yellow** | Raw poultry (chicken, turkey, duck) | *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter* — highest poultry pathogen risk |
| **Blue** | Raw fish and seafood | Parasites, *Listeria*, *Vibrio* separation |
| **White** | Ready-to-eat foods, dairy, deli meat | RTE foods will not be cooked again — no kill step |
| **Green** | Fruits, vegetables, produce | Allergen and microbial separation from proteins |
| **Tan/Brown** | Cooked meats (some operations) | Separates cooked from raw products |
| **Purple** | Allergen-free foods (some operations) | Prevents allergen cross-contact |

> **Regulatory and HACCP Note:** Colour-coded board systems are often part of a HACCP plan or Food Safety Plan as a prerequisite program (PRP) for cross-contamination prevention. Some provincial food safety inspectors look for colour-coded boards as evidence of a cross-contamination prevention system.

### Board Stability and Safety

- Always **place a damp cloth or non-slip mat under a cutting board** before use
- A board that slips during cutting is a direct laceration hazard
- This is so basic it is rarely taught explicitly, but so important it appears on Red Seal practicals

### When to Replace a Cutting Board

- **Plastic:** Replace when deeply grooved, stained (indicates embedded organic material), or warped (creates instability)
- **Wood:** Replace when cracked, excessively scored, or if used for raw protein and no longer cleanable to a health standard
- A board that cannot be sanitized is a food safety violation

## Knife Safety — OH&S and Workplace Regulations

### Laceration Risk — The Leading Knife Injury Type

Knives cause more injuries in commercial kitchens than any other tool. The majority of knife injuries involve:

1. **Loss of concentration** — distraction during cutting
2. **Incorrect grip** — particularly the guide hand
3. **Dull blades** — requiring excess pressure, causing slip
4. **Improper transport** — walking with blade exposed or pointing forward
5. **Unsafe storage** — reaching into drawers or sinks

### Safe Transport of Knives

- Carry in a knife roll, case, or with blade guard
- If carrying a bare knife (short distances in kitchen only): blade pointing down, flat against the thigh, tip forward, announce "knife" when passing anyone
- Never run with a knife — walking injuries are bad enough; running injuries are severe

### WHMIS and Knife Cleaning Chemicals

Cleaning chemicals used in the knife-washing process (detergents, sanitizers, degreasers) are WHMIS-regulated hazardous products. All three WHMIS pillars apply:

1. **Labels** on all containers — must remain intact and legible
2. **Safety Data Sheets (SDS)** — 16-section documents available for all chemicals; relevant sections for knife washing: Section 2 (hazard), Section 8 (PPE — gloves when handling concentrated sanitizer), Section 11 (toxicological), Section 13 (disposal)
3. **Training** — workers must receive WHMIS training before handling chemicals

### HACCP Relevance

Knives are relevant to HACCP in two ways:

1. **Biological hazard control:** Proper washing and sanitizing between product types is a prerequisite program (PRP) that prevents cross-contamination — a biological CCP
2. **Physical hazard:** A chipped or cracked blade introduces a physical contamination risk (metal fragments in food). If a chip is found missing from a blade during inspection, the associated food product must be inspected and, if the fragment cannot be located, the batch may need to be discarded or screened with a metal detector (common HACCP protocol in food manufacturing; adapted to kitchen operations where feasible)

## Memory Aids and Operational Tips

### SHARP — Daily Knife Discipline
- **S**harpen/hone before service
- **H**old with pinch grip; guide hand uses claw
- **A**nnounce your presence when moving with a knife
- **R**inse and wash between every product type change
- **P**rotect the edge in proper storage

### Hone DAILY, Sharpen MONTHLY
The most common apprentice error: trying to hone a truly dull knife. Honing realigns; sharpening restores. If a freshly honed knife still won't cut a tomato without pressing, it needs sharpening.

### Colour Code Memory — "Raw Protein, RTE, Produce"
- **Red** = raw red meat
- **Yellow** = raw yellow-skinned (chicken) — think "yellow warning"
- **Blue** = blue water (fish)
- **White** = white (clean/RTE) — the "safe" colour
- **Green** = green garden (produce)

### The 3 Things That Ruin Edges
1. **Hard surfaces** — glass, granite, metal-on-metal contact
2. **Dishwasher** — alkaline detergent + tumbling action
3. **Improper storage** — loose in drawers, blades touching

### "A Dull Knife is a Dangerous Knife"
The physics: a dull blade requires more downward force → the food compresses before the blade enters → when the blade does enter, it moves faster and with more energy than intended → the chance of skipping off course = higher.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Consequence | Correction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Using a dull knife | Sharpening skipped; not inspecting | Increased injury risk; poor cut quality; more effort | Hone daily; sharpen monthly; inspect before use |
| Putting knives in the dishwasher | Habit from home kitchen | Edge damaged; handle loosened; laceration risk on retrieval | Hand wash only; strict kitchen policy |
| Wiping blade with bare hand | Force of habit | Severe laceration | Always wipe from spine side with cloth; blade faces away |
| Soaking in water | Laziness or habit | Handle damage; hidden blade; rust | Wash immediately; never soak |
| Leaving knife on edge of surface | Distraction | Falls, tips break, bystander injury | Centre knife on board or place on magnetic strip |
| Using wrong knife for task | Unfamiliarity | Difficulty, injury, poor result | Learn the knife selection table; use the right tool |
| Not washing between allergen groups | Lack of awareness | Allergic reaction in guest | Treat allergen changes like raw-to-RTE changes |
| Using glass or ceramic surface | Home habit | Instant edge destruction | Poly board only in commercial kitchen |
| Loose board — not stabilizing | Rushing | Board slips during cut; laceration | Damp cloth under board before any cutting |
| Incorrect sharpening angle | Lack of technique training | Wrong bevel; reduced edge quality | Learn and maintain consistent angle; use angle guide if needed |
| Not inspecting for chips | Rushing | Metal fragment contamination; ineffective cutting | Visual inspection before each use; report chips |
| Storing loose in drawer | Poor storage system | Edge damage; laceration hazard | Magnetic strip, block, or blade guards mandatory |

### 12 Key Q&A Pairs

**Q1: What is the difference between honing and sharpening a knife?**
A1: Honing realigns the existing edge using a honing steel without removing metal — done daily. Sharpening removes metal to create a new edge using a whetstone, pull-through, or electric sharpener — done when the edge is too worn to respond to honing.

**Q2: Why is a dull knife considered more dangerous than a sharp one?**
A2: A dull knife requires more pressure to cut, causing the blade to compress and then suddenly slip through the food unpredictably. A sharp knife cuts with controlled, minimal pressure and predictable movement.

**Q3: Which knife would you select to fillet a salmon?**
A3: A filleting knife — long (15–25 cm), extremely flexible blade that follows the skeletal contours without tearing the flesh.

**Q4: What are the two components of a safe knife grip?**
A4: The pinch grip on the knife handle (thumb and bent index finger pinch the blade at the bolster; remaining fingers wrap handle) and the claw grip on the food (fingertips curled in; knuckles as guide for the blade).

**Q5: Why should knives never be placed in a dishwasher in a commercial kitchen?**
A5: Dishwasher alkaline detergents and tumbling action damage the blade edge, heat and water cycling crack wooden handles and loosen polypropylene handles, and an exposed blade in a rack is a severe laceration hazard during unloading.

**Q6: What is the colour-coded cutting board for raw poultry, and why is it this colour?**
A6: Yellow — for raw chicken, turkey, and other poultry. The colour system prevents cross-contamination of pathogens such as *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter* from raw poultry to other food products.

**Q7: What does a chip in a knife blade represent in HACCP terms?**
A7: A physical contamination hazard — the metal fragment may have entered a food product. The knife must be removed from service, the chip located, and if not found, the associated food product must be assessed for possible contamination.

**Q8: What cutting surface material is required in most Canadian commercial kitchens, and what makes a board unacceptable for continued use?**
A8: Polypropylene (plastic). A board is unacceptable when deeply grooved, cracked, or warped — conditions that make it impossible to clean to food safety standards and that harbour bacteria in the cuts.

**Q9: What whetstone grit sequence would you use to sharpen a chef's knife that has developed nicks and chips?**
A9: Begin with coarse grit (220–400) to remove the damaged metal and reset the bevel, move to medium grit (1000–3000) to refine the edge, and finish with fine grit (4000–8000) to polish the edge.

**Q10: Name three situations when a knife must be fully washed, rinsed, and sanitized (not just wiped) before the next use.**
A10: (1) After cutting raw poultry before cutting any other product; (2) after cutting any allergen-containing food before cutting an allergen-free food; (3) after cutting raw protein before cutting RTE food that will not be cooked.

**Q11: How should a knife be transported safely across a commercial kitchen?**
A11: Blade pointing down, flat against the thigh, tip forward; carried close to the body; announce "knife" when passing behind or close to other workers. Preferably in a knife roll, case, or with a blade guard.

**Q12: What are the three pillars of WHMIS and how do they apply to the chemicals used to sanitize knives?**
A12: (1) Labels — all sanitizer containers must have intact, legible WHMIS labels; (2) SDS (Safety Data Sheets) — 16-section documents must be accessible for all chemicals, covering PPE requirements (gloves for concentrated sanitizer), hazards, and disposal; (3) Training — all workers must receive WHMIS training before using these chemicals in the kitchen.
