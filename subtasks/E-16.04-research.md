## Why "Finishing" Decides Whether a Sauce Sings

A perfectly built sauce can still die at the pass:
- **Held too long at the wrong temperature** — breaks, scorches, develops a skin, or drifts into the danger zone.
- **Wrong final touch** — added at the wrong moment, ruins the texture or flavour balance.
- **Sloppy garnish** — clutters the plate or hides the sauce.
- **Service-time errors** — wrong consistency for the plate, wrong temperature for the dish, wrong portion.

Finishing is also where **safety risks peak**:
- **Flambé** introduces open flame; mishandled, it causes serious burns and fires.
- **Hot hold** of emulsions, cream, and egg-based sauces walks a thin line between food safety (≥ 60 °C) and emulsion stability (often ≤ 85 °C).
- **Cooling and storage** of finished sauce determines pathogen control over the next 24–72 hours.

This subtask is the **integration point**: technique, food safety, presentation, and service-management discipline all converge.

## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### E-16.04.01P — Use Finishing Techniques

**Performance Criteria:** use finishing techniques
**Evidence of Attainment:** finishing techniques are used according to recipe specifications.

From Range of Variables: **monté au beurre, liaison, garnishing, flambé.**

#### Monté au beurre (mounting with butter)

**What it is:** whisking **cold cubed butter** into a warm sauce at the very end of cooking to add **gloss, body, and a silky finish**.

##### The science
- Butter is roughly **80–82% butterfat + 16–18% water + 1–2% milk proteins (caseins and whey)**.
- When cold butter is whisked into a warm (but not hot) sauce:
  - The **butter melts gradually**, releasing fat into the sauce in a controlled way.
  - The **milk proteins act as emulsifiers**, stabilizing the suspension of fat droplets in the water-based sauce.
  - The result is a **light, glossy, mounted texture** with concentrated buttery richness.
- If the sauce is **too hot (above ~ 80–85 °C)**, the butter "melts to oil" — the proteins denature, the emulsion fails, and the sauce becomes oily / greasy rather than silky.

##### Procedure
1. **Bring the sauce to the right temperature** — gentle simmer, then **off direct heat or on very low heat**.
2. **Cube cold butter** into 1–2 cm pieces.
3. **Whisk in butter 1–2 cubes at a time**, allowing each to fully incorporate before adding the next.
4. **Continuous gentle motion** — agitation maintains the emulsion.
5. **Stop when desired gloss and body achieved** — typically 30–80 g butter per 250 mL of pan sauce.
6. **Adjust seasoning** — butter dilutes salt; check.
7. **Serve immediately.**

##### Common applications
- **Pan sauces** (chicken, fish, beef).
- **Fish veloutés** and white wine sauces.
- **Reductions** — finished with cold butter for silkiness.
- **Beurre blanc** (a related technique — the entire sauce is built on this principle).

##### Common mistakes
- **Sauce too hot** → butter melts to oil; broken sauce.
- **Butter not cold** → won't emulsify; just melts in.
- **Adding too much butter too fast** → emulsion overwhelmed; breaks.
- **No agitation** → fat separates.
- **Holding the mounted sauce** → emulsion stability drops over time; ideally serve immediately.

> **Memory aid — "COLD CUBES, OFF HEAT, WHISK SLOWLY":** the three rules of monter au beurre.

#### Liaison (egg-yolk + cream finishing)

**What it is:** a **classical finishing thickener** that adds **silky body, gloss, and richness** to a sauce — egg yolks and heavy cream tempered into the sauce off heat.

> Full procedure and chemistry in [E-15.02-research.md](E-15.02-research.md) and [D-14.03-research.md](D-14.03-research.md).

##### Quick recap
- **Ratio:** ~ 3 egg yolks + 250 mL heavy cream (≥ 33% MF) per litre of sauce.
- **Temper:** ladle hot sauce into the yolk-cream mixture gradually; whisk continuously.
- **Pour back** into the sauce.
- **Hold ≤ 85 °C** — boiling curdles.

##### Sauces commonly finished with liaison
- **Sauce allemande** (velouté + liaison + lemon).
- **Sauce poulette.**
- **Refined cream soups.**
- **Avgolemono** (uses lemon instead of cream).
- **Classical cream-finished velouté.**

#### Garnishing

**What it is:** the visual and textural additions that complete a sauce's presentation.

| **Mornay (cheese)** | Grated cheese on top, gratinéed under salamander |
| **Suprême** | Whole mushroom caps; lemon zest |
| **Sauce vin blanc / fish velouté** | Fresh dill, chervil, or chives |
| **Beurre blanc** | Microgreens; lemon zest; fresh chives |
| **Pan sauce (red wine)** | Fresh parsley; cracked pepper; bone marrow (for beef) |
| **Sauce tomate (classical)** | Fresh basil chiffonade; olive oil drizzle |
| **Chasseur** | Sautéed mushroom slice; chiffonade tarragon |
| **Aurore** | Fresh herb; light cream swirl |
| **Curries** | Cilantro; fried shallot; toasted coconut; fresh chili |
| **Salsas** | Cilantro leaf; lime zest; raw onion |

| **Chocolate sauce** | Cocoa nib; flaked salt; edible gold (rare) |
| **Caramel sauce** | Flaked sea salt; toasted nuts (verify allergens) |

##### Garnish principles
- **Match the sauce's character** — don't add competing flavours.
- **Visual contrast** — colour against the sauce.
- **Texture contrast** — a delicate crunchy garnish on a smooth sauce.
- **Restraint** — a perfect dot of cream or a single basil leaf often beats a pile.
- **Add at plating** — herbs darken, garnishes soak.
- **Pre-portioned and ready** at the pass for service-time efficiency.

##### Allergen-aware garnishing (carry-over from D-14.04)
- **Nuts** on a tree-nut-allergy / peanut-allergy plate → cross-contact.
- **Cheese garnish** on a dairy-allergy plate.
- **Croutons** on a gluten-free plate.
- **Bone marrow** carries no allergen but is an animal-product issue for vegan and vegetarian guests.
- **Sesame** in dukkah or finishing oils.
- **Server flags the allergen plate; chef confirms; dedicated tools and prep.**

#### Flambé (flame-finish)

**What it is:** igniting alcohol in a sauce to **burn off some raw alcohol** while developing **toasted aroma compounds**. Done either pan-side in the kitchen or **tableside for theatrical service**.

##### The science
- **Ethanol vapours ignite** at ~ 13 °C above the alcohol's flash point.
- When alcohol is added to a hot pan, **vapour rises** and ignites — a brief flame burns the **vapour**, not the alcohol still in the liquid.
- **Flambé burns off ~ 25–75% of alcohol**, depending on the technique, duration, and pan size. **It does NOT eliminate all alcohol.** **[Best Practice]**
- A typical pan sauce held at simmer for several minutes after flambé can have **≤ 5% of original alcohol** remaining; brief flambé alone may leave 25%+.

##### Common flambé spirits

| Spirit | Use |
|---|---|
| **Brandy / cognac** | Steak Diane; lobster thermidor; pan sauces for beef and game |
| **Rum (dark or light)** | Bananas Foster; rum sauces |
| **Whiskey / bourbon** | Sauces with apple, pear, or stone fruit; some pan sauces |
| **Calvados** | Apple-based sauces; pork dishes |
| **Marsala** | Veal scallopini; chicken marsala |
| **Wine (high-alcohol fortified — Madeira, sherry, port)** | Specific traditional dishes |
| **Triple sec / Grand Marnier / curaçao** | Citrus-flavoured dessert sauces; crepes Suzette |
| **Tequila** | Some Mexican-influenced dishes |

##### Procedure (kitchen-side flambé)

1. **Build the dish** — sauce / protein cooked to the pre-flambé stage.
2. **Have a lid nearby** to smother flames if they get out of control.
3. **Move the pan off direct flame.**
4. **Add the spirit** to the pan, away from the flame.
5. **Return to the flame and tilt** to ignite the alcohol vapour — OR use a long lighter / match to ignite from above.
6. **Allow flames to burn down** (typically 5–15 seconds).
7. **Once the flames die**, return to cook and finish the sauce.

##### Tableside flambé (theatrical)

- **Strict OH&S protocol** — only trained servers / front-of-house staff under chef oversight.
- **Pre-warm spirit** for easier ignition (slightly warm, not hot).
- **Clear the table area** of flammable items (napkins, menus, candles).
- **No overhead obstructions** (no low-hanging fixtures, decorations).
- **Hold the pan at a safe distance** from the guest's face.
- **Have a lid or cloth** ready to smother flames.

##### Safety rules **[Regulatory under provincial fire codes and OH&S]**

- **Never flambé under the hood with a fan running on high** — flames can climb up the fan.
- **Never lean over the pan** during ignition — eyebrows and clothing catch fire instantly.
- **Don't add spirit from the bottle near the flame** — the bottle can flash back and explode. Always pour into a measuring cup first.
- **Keep hair restrained**, sleeves down/rolled appropriately, dry side towel ready.
- **Class K extinguisher** within reach for the cookline; **lid or cloth** is the first response for small flame.
- **No flambé near oxygen tanks, propane storage, or open flammable materials.**

> ⚠️ **Flambé burns are among the most common preventable kitchen injuries.** A 5-second mistake produces 6 months of recovery. Train deliberately; never improvise.

##### Common flambé mistakes
- **Too much alcohol** — fireball that doesn't die down.
- **Adding spirit from the bottle near flame** — bottle flash-back.
- **Leaning over the pan** during ignition.
- **No lid or cloth nearby** — no emergency-stop tool.
- **Insufficient pan size** — small pan, big spirit volume = flame everywhere.
- **Assuming all alcohol is burned off** — significant alcohol typically remains. Disclose to alcohol-sensitive / pregnant guests, halal / religious-observance guests, children, etc.

### E-16.04.02P — Evaluate and Adjust Final Qualities

**Performance Criteria:** evaluate and adjust final qualities
**Evidence of Attainment:** final qualities are evaluated and adjusted according to desired results.

From Range of Variables: **taste, appearance, flavour, texture, alcohol level.**

#### Final-quality assessment checklist

##### Taste (the balance test)
The five qualities to verify at service temperature:
1. **Salt** — appropriate level for the dish; not over-salted (irreversible).
2. **Acid** — bright but not harsh.
3. **Sweet** — appropriate (caramel notes in demi-glace, fruit sweetness in chutneys).
4. **Bitter / depth** — for brown sauces, sufficient roasted depth without scorch bitterness.
5. **Umami** — savoury fullness from stock, soy, miso, parmesan, glace.

##### Appearance
- **Colour** — characteristic for the sauce (mahogany demi, ivory béchamel, pale yellow hollandaise).
- **Gloss** — light reflects evenly off the surface.
- **No surface fat slick** unless intentional (some Asian broths).
- **No skin / film** on the surface.
- **No separated layer** (broken cream sauce).

##### Flavour
- **Characteristic of the sauce identity** — the apprentice should be able to name the sauce from the taste.
- **No off-notes** — scorched, metallic, raw flour, sulphur, ammonia.
- **Top notes present** — fresh herb, citrus, wine character (where intended).

##### Texture
- **Smooth** for mother sauces, hollandaise, beurre blanc.
- **Slightly chunky** for fresh salsas, chunky tomato sauces.
- **No grit, no graininess, no lumps** (unless intended).
- **Mounted** (glossy from added butter or cream) for finished sauces.

##### Alcohol level
This is a finishing-specific concern, especially for:
- **Sauces with wine, spirits, beer** used as flavour rather than fully reduced.
- **Flambé sauces** — significant alcohol may remain.
- **Late-addition alcohol** — for finishing notes without burn-off (rare).

##### Service-time considerations:
- **Halal guests** — alcohol-containing sauces require disclosure or substitution.
- **Pregnant guests** — alcohol disclosure especially for sauces that haven't been fully cooked down.
- **Children's portions** — alcohol disclosure or alcohol-free alternative.
- **Religious / recovery considerations** — disclosure.
- **Driving guests** — minor at sauce-portion levels, but theoretically possible to register on a breath test for sensitive ignition-interlock devices if the sauce had significant alcohol.

##### How much alcohol actually cooks off (USDA / Health Canada–aligned estimates)

| Cook method | Alcohol remaining |
|---|---|
| **Added cold, not heated** | ~ 100% |
| **Added to boiling liquid, removed from heat** | ~ 85% |
| **Flambéed briefly** | ~ 75% |
| **Cooked / baked 15 min** | ~ 40% |
| **Cooked / baked 30 min** | ~ 35% |
| **Cooked / baked 1 h** | ~ 25% |
| **Cooked / baked 2 h** | ~ 10% |
| **Cooked / baked 2.5 h** | ~ 5% |

> **[Best Practice]** Even long-cooked sauces retain trace alcohol — disclose to guests with strict dietary needs.

#### Adjustment techniques (carry-over from E-16.03)

| Quality | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| **Salt** | Add small amounts at a time; dilute with stock if over-salted |
| **Acid** | Lemon, vinegar, wine reduction; sugar to mellow over-acid |
| **Body too thin** | Slurry; beurre manié pinches; brief reduction; additional purée |
| **Body too thick** | Hot stock or cream to thin |
| **Lumpy** | Strain through fine chinois; blend with immersion blender |
| **Surface skin** | Skim; whisk to re-incorporate (cream sauce); cover with parchment to prevent |
| **Off colour** | Add a touch of demi-glace for brown sauces; cream for richness; herb oil for finish |
| **Flat flavour** | Acid, umami, fresh herb, citrus zest |
| **Bitter / scorched** | Often unsalvageable; try cream + acid to mask mild bitterness |

### E-16.04.03P — Hold Finished Sauce

**Performance Criteria:** hold finished sauce
**Evidence of Attainment:** finished sauce is held using techniques to preserve quality and integrity according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### The fundamentals of sauce hold

| Sauce category | Hot-hold temperature | Maximum hold time | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Espagnole / demi-glace** | ≥ 60 °C in bain-marie | Several hours; can be re-batched | Reheat from cold to ≥ 74 °C |
| **Velouté / béchamel** | ≥ 60 °C in bain-marie | 2–4 h ideal; skin forms; cover with parchment | Stir periodically |
| **Sauce tomate** | ≥ 60 °C | Several hours; rustic sauces hold longest | Stir periodically |
| **Cream-finished sauces** | 70–80 °C in bain-marie | ~ 2 h max for quality | Above 85 °C breaks |
| **Hollandaise / béarnaise** | 60–80 °C | **1–2 h max** for safety and quality | Make fresh; not reheatable |
| **Beurre blanc** | 50–65 °C | **30 min – 1 h max** | Make fresh; not reheatable; emulsion fragile |
| **Pan sauces** | Serve immediately | n/a | Not designed for hold |
| **Curries** | ≥ 60 °C | Several hours; coconut-milk-based may break with long heat | Stir; coconut milk sauces especially fragile |
| **Soy-based stir-fry sauces** | Made à la minute | n/a | Cornstarch breaks down with long hold |
| **Chocolate sauce** | Warm bain-marie (40–60 °C) | Hours OK | Reheat gently |
| **Caramel sauce** | Warm bain-marie (40–70 °C) | Hours OK | Reheat gently; can scorch on direct heat |
| **Cold sauces (mayonnaise, salsa, chutney)** | ≤ 4 °C | Per shelf life | Cover; date; mayonnaise-based highest *Salmonella* / *Listeria* risk |

#### Hot-hold techniques

##### Bain-marie / hot well
- **Most common** for service-line hot hold.
- **Internal sauce temperature ≥ 60 °C** verified periodically with probe.
- **Cover with vented lid** to prevent skin and dust; vented to allow evaporation control.
- **Stir periodically** to maintain even temperature.
- **Replenish from a fresh batch** rather than topping up partial-empty sauces (avoid mixing old + new and stretching service over hours).

##### Cartouche (parchment cap)
- A circle of parchment placed directly on the sauce surface.
- **Prevents skin formation** on cream sauces, béchamel, custard-based sauces.
- **Reduces oxidation.**

##### Steam table
- Holds at hot-well temperature; designed for service, not reheating.
- **Probe periodically.**

##### Heat lamp / warming drawer
- For sauces in plated service over short windows.
- Verify temperature; not for long hold.

##### Specialty hot hold for emulsions
- **Hollandaise** can be held in a wide-mouth thermos, in a warm spot (60–70 °C), or in a covered stainless container in a low-temperature bain-marie.
- **Beurre blanc** is sometimes held in a warm spot off direct heat, in a covered bowl, stirred occasionally — but **make fresh** is the safer practice for service quality.

#### Cooling sauces for storage (carry-over from A-2.02)

> **60 °C → 20 °C within 2 h; 20 °C → 4 °C within an additional 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.** **[Regulatory / Best Practice]**

##### Methods
- **Blast chiller** (fastest, gold standard).
- **Ice wand / ice paddle** in deep batches.
- **Ice bath** for smaller batches.
- **Shallow stainless pans (≤ 5 cm / 2 in deep), uncovered, on a wire rack** in the walk-in.

##### Sauce-specific cooling considerations
- **Cream sauces** cool quickly because of high fat content; but **emulsions can break** if cooled too fast in the wrong way (best practice: cool to ~ 30 °C before refrigerating).
- **Roux-thickened sauces** can form skin during cooling — **cover with parchment cartouche** or stir periodically.
- **Tomato sauces** cool forgivingly.
- **Reductions** cool quickly because of low volume; refrigerate uncovered until ≤ 4 °C.
- **Hollandaise / béarnaise / beurre blanc** — **DO NOT COOL FOR LATER USE.** Discard at end of service. Make fresh next service.

#### Storage of cooled sauces

| Sauce | Refrigerated shelf life (typical) | Freezer shelf life | Storage notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Espagnole / demi-glace** | 5–7 days | 3–6 months | Excellent freezer candidate; portion before freezing |
| **Velouté** | 3–5 days | 1–3 months | Quality drops on freeze |
| **Béchamel** | 3–4 days | 1–2 months | Quality drops noticeably on freeze; some operations don't freeze |
| **Sauce tomate** | 5–7 days | 6+ months | Freezes well |
| **Hollandaise / béarnaise / beurre blanc** | **Not stored** | **Not stored** | Make fresh |
| **Pan sauces** | **Not stored** | **Not stored** | Make fresh |
| **Curries** | 3–4 days | 2–3 months | Many curries improve next day |
| **Salsa fresca** | 1–2 days | Not recommended | Quality drops fast |
| **Mayonnaise** | 5–7 days commercial; 1–2 days house-made | Not recommended | Best made fresh |
| **Chutneys (preserved)** | Per sealed jar; weeks to months | n/a | Refrigerate after opening |
| **Chocolate sauce** | 1–2 weeks | 1–3 months | Reheat gently |
| **Caramel sauce** | 2–3 weeks | 1–3 months | Reheat gently |
| **Compound butter** | 5–7 days fridge | 1–3 months freezer | Wrap tightly; freezer for best storage |
| **Glace de viande** | 2–4 weeks fridge (gelatin acts as preservative) | 6+ months | Long shelf life when properly cooled and sealed |

##### Storage rules (carry-over from A-2.01)
- **Cover, label, date** every container.
- **Store on the RTE / top shelf** of the walk-in (raw-bottom / cooked-top hierarchy).
- **FIFO rotation.**
- **Use within shelf life.**

#### Reheating sauces

##### General rules
- **Reheat to ≥ 74 °C / 165 °F within 2 h.** **[Regulatory]**
- **Reheat gently** — high heat breaks emulsions, scorches reductions, splits cream.
- **Probe in multiple spots** for batch reheating.
- **Reheat once** — repeated cycles damage quality and stack risk.

##### Reheating method by sauce
| Sauce | Reheating method |
|---|---|
| **Espagnole / demi-glace** | Stovetop, gentle simmer; stir; verify 74 °C |
| **Velouté / béchamel** | Stovetop with whisk; verify 74 °C |
| **Sauce tomate** | Stovetop; stir; verify 74 °C |
| **Cream sauces (mornay, suprême, allemande)** | Gentle stovetop; do not boil; verify 74 °C |
| **Hollandaise / béarnaise / beurre blanc** | **Do not reheat — make fresh** |
| **Pan sauces** | **Do not reheat — make fresh** |
| **Curries** | Stovetop; stir; verify 74 °C; coconut-milk curries may need careful gentle heat |
| **Chocolate sauce** | Bain-marie / microwave low power; stir frequently |
| **Caramel sauce** | Bain-marie or gentle stovetop; can scorch on direct flame |
| **Glace de viande** | Gentle stovetop; small portions melt quickly |

#### Impacts of incorrect re-thermalization on sauce quality and safety

**Safety:**
- **Cold spots survive cooking** in thick cream sauces; pathogens may persist.
- **Heat-stable toxins** (*Staphylococcus aureus*, *Bacillus cereus* emetic) are NOT destroyed by reheating — prevention via hygiene + temperature is the only defence.
- **Cross-contamination** if reheating equipment isn't sanitized between cycles.
- **Multiple reheat cycles** accumulate pathogen-spore-germination risk during cooling phases.

**Quality:**
- **Cream sauces break** when reheated aggressively — emulsion collapses, fat separates.
- **Roux-thickened sauces thin** with repeated heat — starches break down (retrogradation reverses partially but body diminishes).
- **Reductions get gummy or sticky** — gelatin over-concentrates.
- **Hollandaise / béarnaise / beurre blanc are not reheatable** — make fresh.
- **Tomato sauces darken and lose fresh fruit notes** with repeated heat.
- **Caramel and chocolate sauces lose nuance** with repeated heat — bitter scorch emerges.
- **Fresh herb top notes evaporate** with each reheat.
- **Garnish quality drops** — pre-garnished held sauces lose visual freshness.

> **Operational rule:** **plan sauce production so each portion is heated once to service temperature.** Mother sauces can re-warm; emulsions cannot. Anything with fresh herb finish needs the herb added at the final reheat or at plating.

### E-16.04.01L — Sauces and Their Applications (Finishing Lens)

#### Factors to consider when finishing sauces

| Factor | Finishing implication |
|---|---|
| **Service style** | Plated à la minute vs buffet hot-hold vs banquet pre-prep — different finishing approaches |
| **Volume to be held** | Large batches need ongoing skim and stir; small batches can be made fresh |
| **Temperature stability** | Hollandaise / beurre blanc demand narrow windows; demi can hold for hours |
| **Garnish stability** | Fresh herbs add at plating; dried garnishes hold well |
| **Allergens of guests** | Substitutions and dedicated tools |
| **Alcohol disclosure** | Communicate to halal, pregnant, recovery, or other alcohol-sensitive guests |
| **Visual presentation** | The sauce is often the most visible part of the plate |
| **Re-thermalization plans** | Cool and store correctly; reheat to ≥ 74 °C; never reheat emulsions |

#### Dietary requirements specific to finishing (carry-over)

| Need | Finishing-stage implication |
|---|---|
| **Celiac / gluten-free** | Verify garnish (no flour-dredged crouton; no commercial sauces with hidden wheat) |
| **Dairy-free** | No monter au beurre; substitute olive oil; no liaison; no cream finish |
| **Egg allergy** | No liaison; no egg-based emulsions in the final finish |
| **Vegan** | No animal products in any finishing fat; no anchovy in beurre composé; plant-based |
| **Halal** | No pork-derived ingredients (no bacon-fat finishing); no alcohol-containing flambé unless cooked off; certified protein supply |
| **Kosher** | No pork; no shellfish (no XO finishing); no meat-dairy mixing |
| **Sulphite-sensitive** | Wine reductions and vinegar reductions; declare |
| **Sodium-restricted** | Hold back final salt; allow guest to add at table |
| **Alcohol-restricted (halal, pregnant, recovery, etc.)** | Disclose alcohol-containing sauces; offer substitutes |

### E-16.04.02L — Procedures to Finish, Hold, Cool, and Store Sauces

#### Hot-sauce finishing procedures

##### Monté au beurre
1. **Bring sauce to gentle simmer.**
2. **Off heat or very low heat.**
3. **Whisk in cold cubed butter** 1–2 cubes at a time.
4. **Continuous gentle motion** until desired body.
5. **Adjust seasoning.**
6. **Serve immediately.**

##### Liaison
1. **Bring sauce to ~ 85 °C; remove from direct heat.**
2. **Whisk yolks + cream in a bowl.**
3. **Temper** with hot sauce ladle by ladle.
4. **Pour tempered mixture back** into the sauce.
5. **Hold ≤ 85 °C; never boil.**
6. **Serve immediately.**

##### Garnishing
1. **Pre-prep garnish** to service spec.
2. **Hold cold or hot** per the garnish type.
3. **Add at plating** for fresh items.
4. **Sanitize tools** between allergen-isolated and standard plates.

##### Flambé
1. **Build dish to pre-flambé stage.**
2. **Pre-portion spirit** in a measuring cup.
3. **Lid or cloth ready.**
4. **Move pan off direct flame.**
5. **Add spirit.**
6. **Return to flame and ignite (or use long lighter).**
7. **Allow flames to die down.**
8. **Finish cooking the sauce.**
9. **Document any incident.**

#### Cold-sauce finishing procedures

##### Whisking cold sauce to consistency
- Mayonnaise, vinaigrette, salsa, chutney — whisked or mixed to final integration.

##### Resting
- **Cold sauces benefit from rest** — flavours meld over 30 min to several hours.
- **Hold ≤ 4 °C during rest.**

##### Garnishing cold
- **Add at plating** for visual freshness.
- **Herbs, microgreens, citrus zest, finishing salt** are common cold-sauce garnishes.

#### Holding procedures

##### Hot hold (carry-over)
- **Bain-marie at ≥ 60 °C** for mother sauces and standard cream sauces.
- **Narrow-window hold** (50–80 °C depending on sauce) for emulsions.
- **Stir periodically; cover with parchment** to prevent skin.

##### Cold hold
- **≤ 4 °C** in covered, labelled containers.
- **Top shelf of walk-in** (RTE hierarchy).
- **FIFO rotation.**

#### Cooling procedures (carry-over from A-2.02)

- **60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.**
- **Methods:** blast chiller, ice wand, ice bath, shallow stainless pans uncovered.
- **Cover only after ≤ 4 °C.**
- **Cooling log** for HACCP-aligned operations.

#### Storage procedures (carry-over from A-2.01)

- **Cover, label, date, store on RTE shelf.**
- **FIFO rotation.**
- **Refrigerated shelf life** typically 3–7 days for most sauces; longer for demi-glace and glace.
- **Freeze in portions** for sauces that freeze well (demi-glace, sauce tomate, curries, chocolate, caramel).

## 3. Food-Safety Considerations During Finishing and Hold

### Hot-hold of egg-based and dairy-based sauces

#### Hollandaise / béarnaise
- **60–80 °C** for service hold.
- **Maximum 1–2 hours** during service.
- **Discard at end of service** — do not save.
- **Pasteurized egg yolks** for high-risk populations (carry-over from E-15, E-16.01).

#### Beurre blanc
- **50–65 °C** for emulsion stability.
- **The 50–60 °C end is below the regulatory 60 °C hot-hold threshold** — manage by **making fresh and serving within 30 min – 1 h**, OR holding at the upper end of the window with awareness of emulsion fragility.
- **Discard at end of service.**

#### Cream-based sauces
- ≥ 60 °C; ≤ 85 °C for emulsion preservation.
- **2–4 h hot hold maximum**; beyond that, quality and safety risks accumulate.

#### Mother sauces (espagnole, velouté, béchamel, tomate)
- ≥ 60 °C hold; typically several hours of safe hold.
- **Stir periodically; skim**.

### Cold-hold of mayonnaise and other egg-based cold sauces

- **House-made mayonnaise** is a *Salmonella* risk if eggs are not pasteurized.
- **Hold ≤ 4 °C** at all times.
- **Make-up frequency** — small batches more often; discard at end of day or per operation policy.
- **For high-risk populations:** **pasteurized egg yolks** mandatory. **[Best Practice]**

### Allergen cross-contact during finishing

- **Garnishes are common cross-contact points** — nuts, cheese, croutons, sesame, shellfish-derived finishes.
- **Dedicated finishing tools** for allergen-isolated orders.
- **Server flags the allergen plate; chef confirms; final plating in an isolated zone.**

### Fire safety for flambé **[Regulatory under provincial fire codes and OH&S]**

- **Class K extinguisher** within reach of the cookline.
- **Fire blanket** for emergency smother.
- **Hood suppression system** charged and inspected (NFPA 17A) — semi-annual professional inspection.
- **Hood exhaust** inspected and cleaned per NFPA 96 — frequency tied to cooking volume.
- **No flambé under maximum-fan exhaust** — flames can climb.
- **Tableside flambé** requires trained staff, clear table area, no overhead obstructions, lid/cloth, spirit pre-portioned in a measuring cup.

### Allergen of alcohol

- **Health Canada labelling requires declaration of added sulphites ≥ 10 ppm** — wine, vinegar, some commercial bases.
- **Halal, pregnant, recovery, child, and some religious-observance guests** require alcohol disclosure even in cooked sauces.

## 4. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Finishing a pan sauce for sautéed chicken with monté au beurre
1. **Sauté chicken** to ≥ 74 °C internal; remove and rest.
2. **In the same pan, pour off excess fat.**
3. **Sweat 2 minced shallots** in remaining fat 1 min.
4. **Deglaze** with 60 mL white wine; reduce by half.
5. **Add 250 mL chicken stock + 1 sprig fresh thyme;** reduce to ~ 100 mL.
6. **Move off heat;** discard thyme sprig.
7. **Whisk in 30 g cold cubed butter** 1–2 cubes at a time — gloss appears.
8. **Adjust salt and pepper.**
9. **Spoon over chicken;** garnish with fresh chives.
10. **Serve immediately.**

### Scenario 2 — Tableside flambé for steak Diane
1. **Pre-portion 30 mL brandy** in a measuring cup at the kitchen.
2. **Move sauté pan with cooked steak + pan sauce to the dining-room cart.**
3. **At the table, position the pan away from guest and overhead lights.**
4. **Server explains briefly** (small piece of theatre).
5. **Pour brandy into pan, away from any flame source.**
6. **Tilt pan towards a long lighter** (or candle) to ignite vapours.
7. **Flames burn 5–10 sec.**
8. **Plate steak with sauce; garnish; serve.**
9. **Disclose** to guests that alcohol may remain.

### Scenario 3 — Holding hollandaise for a brunch service
1. **Make a batch** of hollandaise per E-16.03.
2. **Pour into a wide-mouth pre-warmed thermos** OR a covered stainless container set in a low-temperature bain-marie (~ 60 °C water).
3. **Probe** internal temp every 30 min; verify 60–80 °C.
4. **Stir gently before each use.**
5. **Limit hold to 1–2 hours.**
6. **Discard any remaining** at end of service.

### Scenario 4 — Cooling 4 L of demi-glace after Sunday service
1. **Strain demi-glace** from the bain-marie to a clean wide stainless container.
2. **Set in an ice bath** in the sink; stir to break up the boundary layer.
3. **At 2 hours**, probe — target ≤ 20 °C. Confirmed at 18 °C.
4. **Divide into smaller containers** (1 L each).
5. **Continue cooling in walk-in**, uncovered, on a wire rack.
6. **At 6 hours total**, probe — target ≤ 4 °C. Confirmed at 3 °C.
7. **Cover, label "Demi-glace — [date] — 4 L"**, store on RTE shelf.
8. **Log** all milestones.

### Scenario 5 — Mid-service service-line panic: roux-thickened cream sauce develops surface skin
1. **Action:** whisk gently to incorporate the skin back into the sauce; if discoloured chunks remain, strain through fine chinois.
2. **Cover with a parchment cartouche** going forward.
3. **Stir periodically** to prevent further skin.

### Scenario 6 — Vegan guest at a banquet of cream-of-asparagus soup, with sauce option
1. **Standard finishing** uses monter au beurre and cream.
2. **Substitution:**
   - **Olive oil drizzle** instead of butter mount.
   - **Cashew cream or oat cream** for cream finish.
   - **No dairy-based garnish** (no crème fraîche dollop; substitute with vegan crème).
3. **Dedicated allergen-isolated tools.**
4. **Server confirms at table.**

### Scenario 7 — Halal banquet, marsala-finished veal scallopini
1. **Standard sauce** uses dry marsala wine; flambé optional.
2. **Halal-compliant substitution:**
   - **Non-alcoholic substitute** — concord grape juice + balsamic vinegar + a splash of cooking liquid simulates the sweet-savoury complexity of marsala.
   - **No flambé.**
   - **Verify veal supplier** is halal-certified.
3. **Server confirms at table.**

## 5. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Monté au beurre with the pan too hot** → butter melts to oil; broken sauce.
- **Monté au beurre with room-temperature butter** → won't emulsify properly.
- **Liaison added to boiling sauce** → scrambled.
- **Hollandaise held below 60 °C** → food-safety risk.
- **Hollandaise held above 85 °C** → emulsion breaks.
- **Beurre blanc held above 65 °C** → emulsion breaks.
- **Pre-garnishing held sauces** → herbs darken, garnishes soak.
- **Flambé with too much alcohol** → fireball.
- **Flambé from the bottle near the flame** → bottle flashback / explosion.
- **Leaning over the flambé pan** → burned face / hair.
- **Assuming flambé burns off all alcohol** → significant alcohol may remain.
- **Holding sauces in deep covered cambros in the walk-in for cooling** → *C. perfringens* and *B. cereus* risk.
- **Repeatedly reheating cream sauces** → cumulative quality decay + pathogen-spore germination during cooling phases.
- **Reheating hollandaise, béarnaise, or beurre blanc** → don't; make fresh.
- **Mayonnaise from non-pasteurized yolks for high-risk service** → *Salmonella* risk.
- **Garnishing without checking allergens** — cross-contact on a flagged plate.
- **Holding pan sauce in a hot well** → pan sauces don't hold; make à la minute.
- **Skin on top of held béchamel** → cover with parchment to prevent.
- **Not labelling or dating stored sauce** — FIFO failure; eventual spoilage.

## 7. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with E-16.04.

1. **Four named finishing techniques (Range of Variables):** **monté au beurre, liaison, garnishing, flambé.**
2. **Final qualities (Range of Variables):** **taste, appearance, flavour, texture, alcohol level.**
3. **Monté au beurre:** cold cubed butter whisked into a warm (but not hot) sauce for gloss and body. Sauce must be off direct heat or very low heat; butter must be cold; whisk continuously; ~ 30–80 g butter per 250 mL of pan sauce.
4. **Liaison:** 3 yolks + 250 mL heavy cream per litre of sauce; temper into hot sauce; **hold ≤ 85 °C; never boil.**
5. **Garnishing:** match the sauce; restraint over clutter; add at plating; allergen-aware.
6. **Flambé:** ignites alcohol vapour; burns off ~ 25–75% of alcohol depending on technique; **does NOT eliminate all alcohol**.
7. **Flambé safety (carry-over from A-1.02):** pre-measure spirit; lid or cloth nearby; never lean over the pan; never pour from the bottle near flame; Class K extinguisher within reach.
8. **Hollandaise hold:** 60–80 °C; maximum 1–2 h; discard at end of service; not reheatable.
9. **Beurre blanc hold:** 50–65 °C; maximum 30 min – 1 h; discard; not reheatable.
10. **Cream-sauce hold:** 70–80 °C; ≤ 2 h ideal for quality.
11. **Mother sauces (espagnole, velouté, béchamel, sauce tomate):** ≥ 60 °C hot hold; can be held for several hours.
12. **Cartouche** (parchment on surface) prevents skin on béchamel and custard-based sauces.
13. **Cooling rule:** 60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.
14. **Refrigerated shelf life** (typical): demi-glace 5–7 days; velouté 3–5 days; béchamel 3–4 days; sauce tomate 5–7 days; cream sauces 3–4 days.
15. **Freezer-friendly sauces:** demi-glace, sauce tomate, curries, chocolate, caramel; portioned for service.
16. **Not stored / make fresh:** hollandaise, béarnaise, beurre blanc, pan sauces.
17. **Reheat:** to ≥ 74 °C within 2 h; gentle heat; stir; verify in multiple spots.
18. **Re-thermalization quality impacts:** broken cream, lost roux body, gummy reductions, lost top notes; emulsions are not reheatable.
19. **Re-thermalization safety:** heat-stable toxins not destroyed; cold-spot pathogens survive; cumulative spore risk with repeated cool-reheat cycles.
20. **Alcohol disclosure:** halal, pregnant, recovery, child, religious-observance guests — disclose for any sauce with significant alcohol (flambé or short-cook).
21. **Alcohol burn-off** (USDA/Health-Canada-aligned): ~ 25% remains after 1 h cook; ~ 5% after 2.5 h.
22. **Garnish allergen flags:** nuts, cheese, croutons, sesame, shellfish-derived finishes.
23. **Dedicated allergen-isolated tools** for severe-allergen orders at the finishing stage.
24. **Pasteurized egg yolks** for high-risk populations in hollandaise, mayonnaise, liaison.
25. **Service-line discipline:** probe internal temperature periodically; stir bain-marie sauces; replenish from fresh batches rather than topping up.
26. **Hot-hold equipment** (bain-marie, hot well, steam table) for holding only; do not use for reheating.
27. **Cooling methods** (Range of Variables): ice wands, venting (shallow uncovered pans), blast chiller, water bath / ice bath.
28. **Storage** in cold-storage hierarchy: RTE on top shelf; covered, labelled, dated; FIFO rotation.
29. **Cooling log** (HACCP-aligned operations): start time + temp, 2-h check, 6-h check, method, action, initials.
30. **Plan for single-reheat-to-service** per portion to preserve quality and minimize cumulative pathogen risk.
