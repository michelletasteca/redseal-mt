## Overview and Why This Skill Matters

Processing fruit encompasses a wide range of techniques — from precision knife cuts to decorative carving to machine-assisted puréeing and juicing. Unlike vegetable processing, fruit processing presents distinct challenges: most fruits are highly susceptible to enzymatic browning when cut, many are fragile and easily bruised, and nearly all cut fruit qualifies as a Temperature-Controlled for Safety (TCS) food that must be refrigerated immediately.

The fruit processing skills tested on the Red Seal exam cover precision (uniform cuts), technique (supremes, carving), science (acidulating, quality preservation), yield management (trim utilization), and food safety (storage, TCS rules). This document covers all of these in depth.

## Tools and Equipment: Selection, Science, and Safety

### Knives

Knives are the primary processing tool for nearly every fruit preparation. Selection depends on fruit size, firmness, and the desired result.

| Knife Type | Primary Fruit Use | Why This Knife |
|---|---|---|
| **Chef's knife (20–25 cm)** | Pineapple, melon, large fruit portioning, large dice | Weight and blade length enable efficient, controlled cuts through firm, large fruit |
| **Paring knife (7–10 cm)** | Peeling (tournée), detail work, citrus suprêmes, coring, small cuts | Short blade gives control for close-work and curved surfaces |
| **Serrated knife (bread knife)** | Citrus, tomatoes (technically a fruit), ripe stone fruits | Serrations grip and cut without crushing soft fruit; smooth blades compress ripe fruit |
| **Boning knife** | Mango (cutting around the flat pit) | Thin, flexible blade traces the pit contour |
| **Slicing knife (long, thin)** | Large melons, pineapple rings | Long blade completes cuts in single strokes for clean slices |

**Knife safety for fruit processing:**
- Always use a stable cutting board (non-slip mat or damp towel underneath)
- Curl fingers into a "bear claw" grip to protect fingertips
- Rock-chop method for soft fruit is NOT appropriate — use a clean pull-cut to avoid crushing
- A sharp knife is safer and produces cleaner cuts with less pressure required

### Mandolin

**Function:** Produces uniform thin slices, julienne, or waffle-cut slices of firm fruit rapidly.

**Primary fruit applications:**
- Apple and pear carpaccio (paper-thin slices for refined plating)
- Pineapple rings (uniform thickness for presentation)
- Firm kiwi slices
- Firm stone fruits (before full ripeness)

> **⚠️ CRITICAL SAFETY WARNING — MANDOLIN**
> The mandolin is one of the most dangerous tools in a commercial kitchen. The blade is extremely sharp and fully exposed during operation. Serious lacerations to fingers, palm, and thumb are the most common kitchen injuries involving mandolins.
>
> **Non-negotiable safety rules:**
> - The **hand guard must be used on every pass** — no exceptions
> - A **cut-resistant glove** on the guiding hand is strongly recommended (common industry practice)
> - Never reach for a falling mandolin
> - Fold the blade or use the guard cover when not in active use
> - Store with the blade folded or in a protective cover
>
> Fruit is often more slippery than vegetables (higher juice content, waxy skins). Slippage risk is elevated. The hand guard becomes even more critical for fruit than for vegetables.

**Operational tips:**
- Use firm, only slightly ripe fruit on the mandolin — very ripe fruit crushes under blade pressure
- Apply even, consistent pressure on each pass for uniform thickness
- Adjust blade height before use; test on first pass before committing to a full batch
- Stop before the last 2–3 cm of the piece — the hand guard cannot safely hold very small pieces; switch to knife for the final portion

---

### Shape Cutters

**Function:** Cookie-cutter style tools that punch precise shapes from sliced fruit.

**Primary applications:**
- Fruit skewers and platters with uniform star, heart, or round shapes
- Buffet and catering presentations
- Children's menus and banquet fruit platters
- Melon and watermelon shapes for garnish

**Best fruits:** Watermelon, honeydew, cantaloupe (sliced 1.5–2 cm thick), pineapple slices, firm kiwi, firm mango.

**Operational notes:**
- Cut fruit into even slabs of consistent thickness first (mandolin or slicing knife)
- Press cutter firmly straight down with even pressure — avoid rocking, which creates angled cuts
- Yield loss with shape cutters is higher than with knife cutting — plan trim utilization
- Trim from shape cutting: excellent for smoothies, purées, compotes, salsas

### Juicer

**Types and applications:**

| Juicer Type | How It Works | Best Fruit | Output Volume |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hand reamer (citrus)** | Manual twist over dome; extracts juice, holds seeds | Single citrus halves; small batches | Low |
| **Lever press (citrus)** | Inverted half placed over dome; handle forces press | Rapid citrus halving; moderate batch | Medium |
| **Electric citrus juicer** | Motorized dome; continuous operation | High-volume citrus juicing | High |
| **Centrifugal juicer** | High-speed spinning grates and separates; all fruits | Apples, pears, carrots, mixed | High |
| **Cold-press / masticating juicer** | Slow auger crushes and squeezes; no heat | All fruits; premium juice quality | High |

**Juice yield optimization — practical techniques:**
- Roll citrus firmly on a hard surface before cutting — this breaks the internal cell walls, increasing juice yield by 15–20%
- Bring citrus to room temperature before juicing — cold fruit yields significantly less juice than room-temperature fruit (pectin is firmer when cold, resists release)
- Microwave whole citrus for 10–15 seconds before juicing as an alternative to warming (common industry practice)
- Cut citrus as close to service time as possible — freshly squeezed juice oxidizes within 30–60 minutes at room temperature

**Seed management:**
- Strain juice through a fine-mesh sieve or cheesecloth for service
- Food-service juicers with built-in strainers catch seeds during extraction
- Always check juice for seeds before service — one seed in a glass of orange juice is a quality failure

### Melon Baller

**Function:** A semi-circular scoop that removes uniform spherical portions of melon or other soft fruit flesh.

**Sizes available:**
- **Small (Parisienne):** ~1.5–2 cm diameter — petit fours, micro-garnish
- **Medium:** ~2.5–3 cm — fruit salads, standard garnish
- **Large:** ~3.5–4 cm — platter presentations, structural elements

**Primary fruits:** Cantaloupe, honeydew, watermelon, jicama (technically a root but processed similarly), papaya, firm avocado.

**Technique for uniform balls:**
1. Halve the melon; scoop out and discard seeds
2. Press the melon baller firmly into the flesh at the start position
3. Rotate in a single smooth arc (approximately 270°) while maintaining pressure
4. Lift out — the ball should be fully formed
5. Arrange immediately; keep cold (≤4°C) to prevent browning and maintain texture

**Food safety — melon balls:**
Melon balls from cantaloupe or honeydew are TCS food and must be refrigerated at ≤4°C immediately. Any melon balls left on a buffet or platter at room temperature must not exceed 2 hours cumulative time in the Temperature Danger Zone. See Listeria warning from C-12.02: the melon must have been scrubbed before cutting.

### Spoon

**Uses in fruit processing:**
- Removing seeds and membrane from citrus halves (before supremes or juicing)
- Scooping papaya and passion fruit seeds
- Removing avocado flesh from skin intact
- Scooping kiwi flesh from skin using the spoon-insert-and-rotate technique
- Removing melon seeds (large spoon or ice cream scoop)

**The spoon-peel technique for kiwi:**
1. Trim both ends with a paring knife
2. Insert a tablespoon between the skin and flesh at one end
3. Rotate the spoon around the kiwi, keeping it pressed against the skin
4. The flesh slides out whole — no waste, round shape preserved for slicing

### Food Processor

**Function:** High-volume mechanical chopping, puréeing, and slicing using interchangeable blades and discs.

**Fruit applications:**
- Rough-chopping fruit for compotes, sauces, chutneys
- Puréeing cooked or soft fruit (with caution — see below)
- Slicing disc: uniform slices of firm fruit in large volume
- Grating disc: fine shredding of firm fruit (green mango, apple)

> **Important — Potatoes and starchy foods in a food processor:** This warning from vegetable processing also applies to starchy fruits — plantains processed in a food processor can become gluey due to starch damage. Use a food mill for starchy preparations.

**Food processor safety:**
- Ensure the bowl is fully locked before operating
- Do not overfill — maximum two-thirds full for solid fruit; much less for liquid or semi-liquid preparations
- Pulse for rough cuts; continuous for smooth purées
- Let the bowl come to a complete stop before removing the lid
- Blade is razor-sharp — handle by the hub, not the edges
- Wash blade separately from bowl — never leave in soapy water where it is invisible (laceration risk)

**Seeded fruit in the food processor:**
- Raspberries and blackberries: process briefly, then strain through a fine-mesh sieve or chinois to remove seeds — seeds produce a gritty texture in finished purées
- Passion fruit: scoop flesh and seeds into processor; pulse briefly; strain

### Blender

**Function:** Creates smooth purées, smoothies, and coulis from soft or pre-cooked fruit.

**Fruit applications:**
- Smoothies (raw fruit + liquid)
- Coulis (strained fruit purée for plating)
- Frozen fruit dessert bases (sorbet pre-freeze)
- Combining fruit with other ingredients

**Hot liquid in blender — critical safety procedure:**

> **⚠️ Blender Explosion Risk — Hot Liquid**
> Steam from hot liquid expands rapidly in the sealed blender jar. If the lid is in place and the blender is started at high speed, pressure can launch the lid and spray boiling liquid, causing severe burns.
>
> **Safe procedure for any hot fruit preparation (cooked compotes, sauces, warm purées):**
> 1. Fill blender no more than **half full**
> 2. Remove the centre cap from the lid (allows steam to escape)
> 3. Hold a folded **kitchen towel firmly** over the opening — this allows steam to vent while containing splatter
> 4. Start on the **lowest speed** setting
> 5. Gradually increase speed
> 6. Never start on high with hot liquid
>
> Most fruit preparations for blending are cold — but any warm compote or cooked fruit sauce must follow this procedure.

**Blender vs. food processor:**
- Blender: better for smooth, liquid-based purées and smoothies
- Food processor: better for rough chops, slicing, grating, and drier preparations

## The Eight Processing Methods

### 1. Cutting

All cutting methods for fruit involve the same fundamental safety principles: sharp knife, stable surface, controlled motion, correct grip. The specific cut chosen affects cooking time, appearance, texture, and the customer's eating experience.

**Why uniform cuts matter:**
- Uniform size = uniform cooking time (critical for sautéed or poached fruit)
- Uniform appearance = professional presentation
- Uniform texture = consistent eating experience
- Uniform cuts allow accurate recipe portioning and cost control

**Knife cut progression — the fruit equivalents of classical vegetable cuts:**

| Cut Name | Dimensions | Starting Point | Primary Fruit Applications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Julienne** | 3 mm × 3 mm × 5–6 cm | Plank → matchstick | Citrus zest julienne; firm green mango; apple salad |
| **Baton** | 6 mm × 6 mm × 5–7 cm | Plank → stick | Melon fingers; pineapple batons; kiwi batons |
| **Brunoise** | 3 mm × 3 mm × 3 mm | Julienne → cube | Fine fruit salsa; infused syrup garnish; fruit chutney |
| **Dice (small)** | 6 mm × 6 mm × 6 mm | Baton → cube | Fruit salsa; tartare topping; small fruit salad |
| **Dice (medium)** | 1.2 cm × 1.2 cm × 1.2 cm | Larger stick → cube | Standard fruit salad; compote base; plated dessert component |
| **Dice (large)** | 2 cm × 2 cm × 2 cm | Thick plank → cube | Fruit kebab components; large fruit bowl presentations |
| **Slices** | Variable thickness | Cross-sectional cuts | Plated dessert; garnish; pineapple rings; citrus wheels |
| **Suprême / Supreme** | Segment shape | Citrus-specific technique | Citrus salads, plated desserts, amuse-bouche |

**Plank → Stick → Cube derivation (same as for vegetables):**
- Trim fruit to flat, even sides (plank)
- Cut plank into uniform sticks (julienne, baton)
- Stack sticks and cut into cubes (brunoise, dice)
- Each stage doubles approximately in dimension: 3mm → 6mm → 12mm → 20mm

### The Suprême / Supreme — A Citrus-Specific Technique

The **suprême** (also spelled supreme, or called a "fillet") is a segment of citrus fruit cut free of all peel, pith, and membrane. The result is pure citrus flesh — no bitterness, no chewy membrane, clean appearance.

**Why supremes are important:**
- Membrane is bitter and tough — unpleasant texture in refined preparations
- Pith contributes no flavour and is visually unappealing
- Supremes showcase the citrus properly in plated desserts, salads, and amuse-bouche
- The technique also produces citrus juice as a byproduct — squeeze the membranes after cutting

**Supreme technique — step by step:**

1. **Wash the citrus** (mandatory — rind carries surface bacteria; the knife will contact it)
2. **Cut a flat base:** Trim 1 cm off the top and bottom; stand stable on the flat base
3. **Remove the peel and pith:** Starting at the top, cut downward following the natural curve of the fruit, removing all peel and white pith in smooth strokes — approximately 8–10 strokes around the full circumference. Remove any remaining pith with the paring knife
4. **Identify the membranes:** Hold the peeled citrus over a bowl (to catch juice). The white membranes dividing the segments are now visible
5. **Cut free each supreme:** Insert the paring knife on the inside edge of each membrane, then angle the blade toward the centre and cut alongside the opposite membrane — the segment falls free into the bowl
6. **Release juice:** When all segments are free, squeeze the remaining membrane skeleton over the bowl to extract all remaining juice
7. **Keep cold:** Supremes in juice, covered, at ≤4°C — use within 24 hours

**Common errors with supremes:**
- Not removing all pith (leaves bitterness and white residue)
- Cutting through segments rather than between membranes (ragged edges)
- Not washing the citrus before starting
- Not catching the juice (wasted yield)

### 2. Acidulating

**Definition:** Treating cut fruit surfaces with an acid solution to prevent or slow enzymatic browning (oxidation).

**The science of enzymatic browning:**

When fruit flesh is cut and exposed to air, oxygen activates the enzyme **polyphenol oxidase (PPO)**. PPO catalyzes the reaction between phenolic compounds in the fruit (chlorogenic acid, catechins) and oxygen, producing dark brown melanin pigments. This reaction:
- Is visible within minutes for apples, pears, avocado, and peaches
- Does not affect food safety — browning is a quality issue, not a safety issue
- Affects flavour (slight bitterness), appearance (brown colour), and texture (soft spots at cut surface)

**How acidulating works:**

| Mechanism | What Happens |
|---|---|
| **pH reduction (acid)** | Lowers pH below PPO optimal range (pH 5–7); slows enzyme activity |
| **Ascorbic acid (antioxidant)** | Preferentially oxidizes instead of phenolic compounds; depletes oxygen at cut surface |
| **Temperature (cold)** | Slows all enzyme reaction rates; cold acidulated water works better than room temperature |

**Acidulated water preparation:**
- Standard: **15 mL (1 tbsp) lemon juice per 1 litre cold water**
- Alternative: citric acid powder (food grade) at 2–3 g per litre
- Commercial anti-browning agents (sodium erythorbate, ascorbic acid blends): follow manufacturer dilution

**Effectiveness by acid type:**

| Acid | Source | Browning Inhibition | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Citric acid | Lemon juice, citric acid powder | Strong | Most common; also contributes fresh flavour |
| Ascorbic acid | Lemon juice, vitamin C powder | Strong | Antioxidant mechanism; no flavour impact |
| Tartaric acid | White wine, cream of tartar | Moderate | Wine-based preparations |
| Malic acid | Apple juice | Moderate | Mild; less effect than lemon |
| Phosphoric acid | Some commercial preparations | Strong | Industrial use only; not culinary |

**When to apply acidulating:**

| Fruit | PPO Activity | Acidulate? |
|---|---|---|
| Apple, pear | High | Yes — immediately after cutting |
| Avocado | Very high | Yes — immediately; also wrap tightly |
| Peach, nectarine, apricot | High | Yes |
| Banana | Moderate | Yes (especially for extended display) |
| Mango | Low | Optional |
| Citrus flesh | Very low (acid content) | Not needed |
| Melon | Very low | Not needed (low PPO) |
| Pineapple | Very low | Not needed |
| Berries | Very low | Not needed |
| Grapes | Very low | Not needed |

**Limitations of acidulating:**
- Does not permanently prevent browning — it delays it
- Over-acidulating affects flavour (too sour)
- Ascorbic acid is eventually depleted — replace solution regularly in high-volume operations
- Very ripe fruit browns faster regardless of acidulating

### 3. Juicing

**Definition:** Extracting the liquid content from fruit by mechanical pressing, reaming, or centrifugal force.

**Commercial kitchen juicing applications:**
- Fresh-squeezed citrus for beverages, sauces, vinaigrettes
- Apple or pear juice as a cooking liquid for poaching
- Berry juice/coulis base (cooked and strained)
- Pineapple juice for marinades (contains bromelain — tenderizes protein)

**Pineapple bromelain — culinary and food safety note:**

> Pineapple contains the enzyme **bromelain**, a protease that breaks down protein. This is why:
> - Fresh pineapple tenderizes meat (used in marinades)
> - Fresh pineapple juice prevents gelatin from setting (bromelain degrades gelatin — the same enzyme problem as in C-12.01)
> - Pineapple used in gelatin desserts must be **canned or cooked** (heat denatures bromelain)
> - Over-marinating protein in fresh pineapple juice makes the surface mushy — time control is critical (common industry practice: maximum 30–60 minutes for delicate proteins)

**Juice yield reference table:**

| Fruit | Approximate Yield per Piece | Optimizing Yield |
|---|---|---|
| Medium lemon | 30–45 mL (2–3 tbsp) | Room temp; roll firmly before cutting |
| Medium lime | 20–30 mL (1.5–2 tbsp) | Room temp; microwave 10 sec |
| Medium orange | 60–90 mL (¼–⅓ cup) | Room temp; lever press |
| Large grapefruit | 150–200 mL (⅔–¾ cup) | Room temp; lever press |
| Medium lemon (zested first) | Slightly less | Zest before juicing — easier and more yield |

**Juice storage:**
- Fresh citrus juice: ≤4°C, airtight container; best within 24 hours
- Juice held beyond 24 hours loses volatile aromatics and vitamin C rapidly
- Label with content, date, and time prepared
- Fresh-squeezed juice is a TCS food if other ingredients are added (particularly if diluted with water or mixed with proteins)

### 4. Puréeing

**Definition:** Reducing fruit to a smooth, uniform, semi-liquid or liquid consistency by mechanical action (blending, food processing, food mill) and optionally straining to remove seeds, skin, or fibrous particles.

**Equipment selection for puréeing:**

| Tool | Result | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Blender** | Very smooth; efficient at liquid incorporation | Smoothies, coulis, liquid-forward purées |
| **Food processor** | Smooth to chunky; less liquid needed | Thicker fruit purées, chutneys |
| **Food mill (moulin)** | Smooth; removes skin and seeds automatically through the disc | Applesauce, berry purée, cooked fruit sauces |
| **Immersion blender** | Convenient in-pot blending | Cooked compotes, soups with fruit |
| **Chinois / fine-mesh sieve** | Straining step after any purée method | Removing seeds (berry), fibres (mango), remaining skin |

**Coulis — the classical fruit purée:**
A **coulis** is a strained, smooth fruit purée used as a sauce for plated desserts. Key characteristics:
- **Consistency (nappe):** Thick enough to coat the back of a spoon; holds a clean line when drawn through
- **Colour:** Vibrant — correct puréeing and straining preserves colour
- **Flavour:** Concentrated — use ripe fruit; sweeten with simple syrup to taste
- **Strained** through a fine-mesh sieve or chinois to remove all seeds and fibrous particles

**Berry coulis technique:**
1. Rinse berries
2. Purée in blender or food processor
3. Pass through fine-mesh sieve — press and scrape with a rubber spatula; the seeds and skin remain in the sieve
4. Season: add sugar, lemon juice, and optional liqueur to taste
5. Adjust consistency with simple syrup or additional juice
6. Chill immediately; store ≤4°C; label

**Seeding considerations:**
- Raspberry and blackberry seeds are gritty and objectionable in finished purées — always strain
- Strawberry seeds are small and acceptable in rustic preparations; strain for refined service
- Passion fruit seeds: large; always strain or use whole seeds as texture element (they are edible)
- Mango fibres: purée thoroughly and strain through chinois for silky texture

### 5. Shaping

**Definition:** Forming fruit into specific three-dimensional shapes using melon ballers, shape cutters, molds, or hand-carving.

**Primary applications:**
- Melon balls (spheres) using melon baller
- Punched shapes from sliced fruit using shape cutters
- Hand-formed quenelles of soft fruit (avocado, mango) using two spoons
- Sculpted/carved fruit for display

**Quenelle technique:**
A **quenelle** is an elegant oval shape formed by drawing a warm, clean spoon through a soft preparation. For soft fruit:
1. Use two tablespoons
2. Scoop portion with one spoon
3. Draw the second spoon along the surface of the portion, transferring it between the two spoons to form a smooth oval
4. Place directly on the plate

Used for: avocado purée, mango gelée, parfait-style fruit preparations.

### 6. Shaving

**Definition:** Producing very thin, near-translucent slices or fine shreds of firm fruit using a mandolin, vegetable peeler, or fine grater.

**Applications:**
- Apple carpaccio (paper-thin slices, fan-arranged)
- Shaved coconut (fresh coconut, using vegetable peeler)
- Shaved citrus zest ribbons (vegetable peeler)
- Shaved pineapple (mandolin, for dehydrator preparations or raw salads)
- Green mango slaw (mandolin julienne attachment)

**Mandolin safety reminder:** See tool section above — hand guard is mandatory on every pass.

**Thickness guidelines:**
- Carpaccio presentation: 0.5–1 mm (thinnest mandolin setting)
- Garnish slices (transparent but intact): 1–2 mm
- Salad component slices: 2–3 mm
- Standard slices (structural): 3–5 mm

---

### 7. Grating / Zesting

**Definition:** Removing fine particles of fruit skin (zest) or reducing firm fruit to fine shreds using a grater.

**Zesting — the most common grating application:**

Citrus zest contains the essential oils (limonene, citral, linalool) that carry the intense citrus aroma and flavour. The **coloured outer layer** of citrus skin is all zest — the white inner layer (pith) is bitter and should not be included.

**Zesting tools:**

| Tool | Output | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Microplane (fine rasp grater)** | Very fine, dry, fluffy zest; no pith included | Most precise zesting; pastry, sauces, finishing |
| **Channel knife / cannelle knife** | Long continuous strips of zest | Decorative spirals, twists, citrus twists for cocktails |
| **Vegetable peeler** | Wide flat strips of zest | Infusions (sugar syrups, cream infusions); then julienne |
| **Box grater (fine side)** | Coarser zest; occasional pith included | Rustic applications; higher volume |

**Zesting sequence — always zest before juicing:**
- A whole citrus can be held easily for zesting
- After juicing, the collapsed half is difficult to zest cleanly
- Extract all required zest, then cut and juice

**Yield:**
- 1 medium lemon → approximately 1 tbsp (15 mL) zest
- 1 medium orange → approximately 2 tbsp (30 mL) zest
- 1 medium lime → approximately 1 tsp (5 mL) zest

**Grating firm fruit:**
- **Coconut:** Fresh coconut grated on box grater; fine for pastry; coarse for texture
- **Green mango/papaya:** Grated for Thai-style preparations (som tam)
- **Apple or pear:** Grated into batters (muffins, pancakes); prevents the starch-glue problem that blending would cause

### 8. Slicing

**Definition:** Cutting fruit into flat, even cross-sections of a specified thickness.

**Slicing applications by fruit:**

| Fruit | Common Slice Type | Typical Thickness | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Citrus** | Wheel (full cross-section) | 3–5 mm | Garnish, cocktails, presentation |
| **Citrus** | Half-moon | 3–5 mm | Platter, beverage garnish |
| **Strawberry** | Fan (partial cuts, base intact) | 2–3 mm cuts | Garnish (see below) |
| **Kiwi** | Round cross-section | 5–8 mm | Fruit salad, plated dessert |
| **Banana** | Bias (diagonal) cut | 5–8 mm | More surface area; better for presentations |
| **Pineapple** | Ring (core removed) | 1–1.5 cm | Plated dessert, glazed fruit |
| **Apple/pear** | Fan (parallel cuts, base intact) | 3–5 mm | Elegant garnish for desserts |
| **Mango** | Cheek, scored, inverted | Variable | Hedgehog cut presentation |

## C-12.03.03P — Maintain Uniform Cuts

**Evidence of Attainment:** Uniform cuts to fruit are maintained for consistency in appearance and cooking.

### Why Uniformity Matters — The Science and the Standard

- **Cooking uniformity:** Fruit pieces of different sizes cook at different rates. In a sautéed or poached preparation, small pieces overcook while large pieces remain underdone. Uniform cuts mean uniform heat penetration.
- **Portion control:** Uniform dice means uniform portion weight — critical for costing and serving consistency.
- **Visual standard:** Professional kitchens plate by visual standard. Guests notice when fruit pieces vary widely in size.
- **Customer experience:** Uniform pieces are easier to eat with a dessert fork and provide consistent flavour/texture in each bite.

### Maintaining Uniformity — Operational Techniques

1. **Square the fruit first:** Create flat, even surfaces (planks) before cutting sticks and dice. A round fruit cannot be cut uniformly until trimmed square.
2. **Use a guide cut:** Make one reference slice to establish thickness, then use that slice as your visual reference for the rest.
3. **Work in batches of consistent stage:** All planks cut to the same thickness before any stick cutting; all sticks cut before any dicing.
4. **Slow down:** Speed comes from efficiency of motion, not from rushing individual cuts. A second saved per cut is not worth the inconsistency.
5. **Self-audit during production:** Periodically compare finished pieces — if they are drifting in size, reset and adjust before completing the batch.

## Classical Fruit Garnish Techniques

### Strawberry Fan

One of the most common fruit garnishes in commercial kitchens:

1. Wash strawberry; leave hull intact (the hull is part of the garnish)
2. Place tip-up on cutting board
3. Make thin parallel cuts (2–3 mm apart) from near the hull to the tip, leaving the hull area intact so the berry does not fall apart
4. Gently press the slices apart to fan them out
5. Place immediately; apply only minutes before service (cut surface browns and dries quickly)

---

### Citrus Wheel and Half-Moon

**Citrus wheel:**
- Wash citrus
- Cut crosswise into 3–5 mm rounds
- Cut a slit from the centre to the edge on each wheel → the slit allows the wheel to be set on the rim of a glass

**Citrus twist (cocktail garnish):**
- Use a channel knife or peeler to cut a long continuous strip of zest
- Twist the strip around a skewer or your finger; hold for 30 seconds
- The strip retains the curl when released

---

### Citrus Basket

1. Cut citrus in half horizontally OR cut a shallow decorative V-zigzag around the equator
2. Scoop out interior (supremes or squeeze juice)
3. Fill the shell with fruit salad, sorbet, or other preparation
4. Serves as both container and garnish

---

### Melon Basket / Swan

**Melon basket:**
1. Mark a handle line across the top of the melon
2. Mark basket sides by cutting horizontally just below the equator on each side (not cutting through the handle)
3. Cut away the handle-adjacent sections
4. Scoop out the interior; zigzag the cut edge with V-gouge for decoration
5. Fill with melon balls, fresh fruit, or a frozen preparation

**Food safety note for melon carvings:**
- All carved melon surfaces are exposed cut surfaces — TCS food rules apply
- Assembled melon baskets must be kept at ≤4°C until service
- Display at ambient temperature not to exceed 2 hours cumulative

---

### Mango Hedgehog

1. Identify the flat pit running lengthwise through the mango
2. Cut a "cheek" from each side of the pit (two large halves)
3. Score the flesh in a crosshatch pattern (1–1.5 cm grid), cutting down to but not through the skin
4. Invert the cheek — the scored flesh fans outward into a "hedgehog" pattern
5. Serve immediately or brush with acidulated water/lime juice to prevent browning

---

### Apple or Pear Fan

1. Wash, peel if desired, halve, and core the fruit
2. Place flat side down
3. Make thin parallel cuts (3–5 mm) from the stem end to the base, leaving 1–1.5 cm at the stem end intact
4. Gently press the slices to fan outward and down
5. Apply immediately — both apple and pear brown rapidly after cutting
6. Brush with acidulated water or glaze with apricot nappage to retard browning and add sheen

## C-12.03.05P — Maintain Quality of Fruit During Processing

**Evidence of Attainment:** Quality of fruit is maintained during processing.

---

## Quality Dimensions and How to Protect Them

### Oxidation / Browning

See Acidulating section above. The key principle: **apply acid immediately after cutting** browning-susceptible fruit. Do not cut and leave.

### Colour

- Vibrant, natural colour is the primary quality indicator for fresh fruit
- PPO browning is the main threat — control with acid and cold
- Overripe fruit loses colour intensity — select and process at peak ripeness
- Citrus flesh colour is preserved by keeping cold and avoiding long air exposure

### Flavour

- Cut fruit loses volatile aromatic compounds over time — process close to service
- Acid (lemon juice) used for browning control also enhances perceived flavour brightness
- Over-acidulating masks natural fruit flavour — use the minimum effective amount
- Refrigeration slows volatile loss but does not stop it — freshness degrades even refrigerated

### Texture

- Mechanical damage (rough handling, wrong tool, excessive force) ruptures fruit cells
- Cell rupture releases juice, softens the piece, and accelerates browning at the damaged site
- Use appropriate tools: soft fruit = sharp knife with gentle pressure; firm fruit = appropriate mechanical device
- Cut surfaces dry out in refrigeration — cover all cut fruit with film or airtight lids

### Temperature

- All cut fruit is TCS food — hold at ≤4°C during processing and storage
- Do not allow cut fruit to sit at room temperature during extended prep sessions
- Work in batches: cut → immediately refrigerate → retrieve for plating
- In a hot kitchen, time at room temperature during active processing adds up — track it

## Trim Utilization

**Principle:** Every edible piece of trim has a culinary application. Trim that is discarded instead of utilized is lost food cost.

**Trim utilization by fruit type:**

| Trim Type | Utilization Options |
|---|---|
| **Citrus peel (no pith)** | Candied peel; zest strips for infusions; dried zest powder; compound butter; oil infusion; tea/tisane |
| **Citrus pith and membranes** | Squeeze for juice; discard pith (bitter) |
| **Citrus supreming juice** | Simple syrup base; sauce; dressing; vinaigrette; cocktail ingredient |
| **Pineapple core** | Simmer for juice extraction; steep in simple syrup; ferment (pineapple tepache) |
| **Pineapple rind** | Steep in boiling water for pineapple water/juice; discard after extraction |
| **Melon seeds** | Discard (not culinary); rinse and roast if desired (pepitas-style) |
| **Melon rind** | Pickled melon rind (classical preserve); rind in stock (mild flavour) |
| **Berry maceration liquid** | Syrup; sauce; coulis base; cocktail ingredient |
| **Apple/pear peels and cores** | Apple jelly stock; apple scrap vinegar; pectin extraction for jam-making |
| **Stone fruit pits** | Crack for kernel (bitter almond flavour in small amounts — use with extreme caution; amygdalin/cyanide risk); discard generally |
| **Shape-cutter off-cuts from melon** | Smoothies; purées; coulis; small dice for fruit salsa |
| **Mango skin** | Discard (contains urushiol — can cause dermatitis in sensitive individuals); do not use culinarily |

> **Safety Note — Mango Skin:** Mango is related to poison ivy and contains urushiol compounds in the skin and sap. Some individuals experience contact dermatitis from handling mango skin directly. Use gloves when peeling large volumes of mango (common industry practice).

> **Safety Note — Stone Fruit Pits/Kernels:** Pits of apricots, cherries, peaches, and plums contain amygdalin, which converts to hydrogen cyanide when metabolized. In the quantities found in normal cooking, incidental exposure is negligible. However, do NOT grind or use raw pits as a food ingredient.

**Trim labelling and storage:**
- All trim designated for use (not discard) must be labelled: "citrus trim — for juice/syrup," with date
- Trim deteriorates faster than whole fruit — use within 24–48 hours
- Trim stored beyond its use window must be discarded
- Separate stock-quality trim from discard trim at the point of production (two bins)

## C-12.03.07P — Store Cut Fruit

**Evidence of Attainment:** Cut fruit is stored according to type and procedure to preserve quality of stored fruit.

## Storage Quality Factors

The Range of Variables specifies that quality of stored fruit includes:
- **Minimal oxidation** (browning control — acid application, airtight containers, cold temperature)
- **Colour** (vibrant colour maintained by cold, acid, airtight storage)
- **Flavour** (volatile retention by cold, short storage time, airtight containers)
- **Texture** (firmness maintained by cold, no excess moisture, no mechanical compression)

## TCS Food Rules for Processed Fruit

- All cut fresh fruit is a **TCS (Temperature-Controlled for Safety) food**
- Must be held at ≤4°C or ≥60°C
- Maximum cumulative time in the TDZ (4°C–60°C): **2 hours**
- Fruit juice produced from fresh fruit is also TCS
- Coulis and purées mixed with dairy or other ingredients follow the most restrictive TCS guideline for any component

**Labelling requirements for stored processed fruit:**
- Content (specific fruit and form: "orange supremes," "strawberry coulis")
- Date and time of preparation
- Use-by date/time
- Initials of preparer (required in HACCP-managed kitchens)

## Common Mistakes — Fruit Processing

| Mistake | Consequence | Correct Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Cutting before washing | Surface bacteria carried to flesh by knife | Always wash before processing |
| Not applying acid immediately after cutting browning fruit | Visible browning within minutes | Apply acidulated water immediately on all cut surfaces |
| Zesting after juicing | Awkward; much lower zest yield | Always zest before juicing |
| Including pith in zest | Bitter flavour compounds in final dish | Use microplane or careful technique; stop at colour layer |
| Juicing cold citrus | 20–30% less juice yield | Room-temperature citrus before juicing |
| Leaving supremes in membranes | Bitter, tough eating experience | Clean cuts; all membrane removed |
| Shaving on mandolin without hand guard | Serious laceration risk | Hand guard on every pass; no exceptions |
| Puréeing seeded berries without straining | Gritty texture in finished sauce/coulis | Always strain seeded berry purées through fine-mesh sieve |
| Processing all fruit at once in a hot kitchen | TCS violation; browning; texture loss | Batch process; refrigerate promptly |
| Using same board/knife for raw protein and fruit without sanitizing | Cross-contamination of ready-to-eat food | Sanitize equipment between categories; use dedicated cutting boards |
| Storing cut avocado with air gap | Rapid surface browning | Press film directly on cut surface; include pit if halved |
| Discarding all trim | Increased food cost; waste | Utilize trim per available applications (juice, purée, coulis, infusion) |
| Blending hot fruit preparation at high speed without vent | Explosion/burn risk | Fill half; remove centre cap; towel over; start on low |

## 12 Q&A Pairs — Red Seal Exam Preparation

**Q1: What is a citrus supreme, and why is this technique used instead of simply segmenting the fruit?**
A: A supreme is a citrus segment cut free of all peel, pith, and membrane using a paring knife. The membrane is bitter and has a tough, fibrous texture that is unpleasant in refined preparations. Removing it produces a clean, smooth segment with pure citrus flavour. The juice released during supreming is also captured as a culinary byproduct.

**Q2: What enzyme causes enzymatic browning in cut fruit, and how does acidulating prevent it?**
A: Polyphenol oxidase (PPO) catalyzes the reaction between phenolic compounds in the fruit and oxygen, producing brown melanin pigments. Acidulating (applying lemon juice or citric acid solution) lowers the pH of the cut surface below PPO's optimal range (pH 5–7), slowing the enzyme's activity. Ascorbic acid in lemon juice also acts as an antioxidant, preferentially oxidizing before the phenolics do.

**Q3: Why should citrus always be zested before juicing?**
A: A whole, intact citrus is easy to grip and rotate against a grater for complete zest extraction. After juicing, the collapsed half-shell is difficult to hold and yields much less zest. Zesting first produces more zest with less effort and does not reduce juice yield.

**Q4: What are the dimensions of a brunoise, and what is the correct cutting progression to produce it?**
A: A brunoise is 3 mm × 3 mm × 3 mm. The correct progression: square the fruit into even planks → cut planks into julienne strips (3 mm × 3 mm × 5–6 cm) → cut julienne strips crosswise at 3 mm intervals to produce brunoise cubes.

**Q5: Why must pineapple be cooked or canned before use in gelatin-based desserts?**
A: Fresh pineapple contains the protease enzyme bromelain, which breaks down gelatin's protein structure and prevents it from setting. Heat denatures (deactivates) bromelain — canning and cooking both accomplish this. Canned pineapple can be used safely in gelatin desserts; fresh cannot.

**Q6: What is the safe procedure for blending a hot fruit preparation in a blender?**
A: Fill no more than half full; remove the centre cap from the lid; hold a folded kitchen towel firmly over the opening to allow steam to escape while containing splatter; start on the lowest speed; gradually increase speed. Never start with hot liquid at high speed in a covered blender — steam expansion can launch the lid and cause severe burns.

**Q7: What is the yield percentage of a pineapple after removing the crown, base, rind, and core?**
A: Approximately 50–55%. This means that approximately half of the as-purchased weight of a whole pineapple is usable edible flesh. This yield factor must be applied when purchasing pineapple to ensure sufficient EP (edible portion) quantity for a recipe.

**Q8: What are three uses for trim generated during the supreming of citrus?**
A: (1) Squeeze the membranes to extract remaining juice for sauces, dressings, or simple syrups. (2) Use peels (without pith) for candied citrus peel, infusions, or compound butter. (3) Juice and zest from peels can be used for cocktail ingredients or pastry applications.

**Q9: What OH&S rule applies to mandolin use, and why is it especially important for fruit?**
A: The hand guard must be used on every single pass — no exceptions. This is an OH&S requirement under safe work procedures. Fruit presents elevated risk compared to vegetables because the high juice content and waxy skins make the product more slippery, increasing the chance of the piece sliding out of the hand and exposing fingers to the blade.

**Q10: Why is cut fruit classified as a TCS food?**
A: Cut fruit has high water activity (sufficient moisture for bacterial growth) and a pH between approximately 3.5 and 6.0 — a range that overlaps with the dangerous zone for pathogens (4.6–7.5). The intact skin barrier is removed. These conditions allow pathogenic bacteria to grow rapidly if the fruit is not held at ≤4°C. Cut melon in particular is specifically named as a TCS food in most provincial food premises regulations.

**Q11: What is the difference between a blender and a food mill for puréeing fruit, and when is each appropriate?**
A: A blender produces very smooth purées and is efficient when liquid is present — best for coulis, smoothies, and liquid-forward preparations. A food mill uses a perforated disc to strain while puréeing — it automatically removes seeds and skins, making it ideal for applesauce, berry purées, and cooked fruit sauces where seeding and a smooth texture are both required without a separate straining step.

**Q12: What are the four quality characteristics of stored cut fruit listed in the Range of Variables, and what storage practice protects each?**
A: (1) **Minimal oxidation** — prevented by applying acidulated water immediately and using airtight containers. (2) **Colour** — maintained by cold temperature (≤4°C), acid treatment, and airtight storage excluding oxygen. (3) **Flavour** — preserved by cold storage, airtight containers retaining volatile aromatics, and short storage time. (4) **Texture** — maintained by cold temperature, gentle handling during storage (no compression), covering cut surfaces with film to prevent drying, and staying within defined shelf-life limits.
