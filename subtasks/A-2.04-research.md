## What Is Cross-Contamination — and Why It's the #1 Test Topic

**Cross-contamination** is the transfer of a contaminant from one food, surface, person, or piece of equipment to another. Public-health investigations of Canadian foodborne outbreaks repeatedly trace incidents to a small number of root causes:

1. **Bare-hand contact** with ready-to-eat food after handling raw protein.
2. **Shared cutting boards or knives** between raw protein and ready-to-eat (RTE) food.
3. **Drip from raw chicken** stored above RTE foods.
4. **Sanitizer not at proper concentration** — surfaces appear clean but aren't.
5. **Allergen cross-contact** — peanut residue on a "cleaned" pan transferring to a peanut-allergic guest's meal.

This subtask is fundamentally about **separating, sanitizing, and signalling** — keeping different categories of risk from meeting each other.

### Four categories of contamination

| Type | Examples | Primary defence |
|---|---|---|
| **Biological** | Bacteria (*Salmonella*, *E. coli*, *Listeria*), viruses (norovirus, Hep A), parasites (*Anisakis*), prions (BSE — extremely rare) | Hand hygiene, separation, temperature, sanitation |
| **Chemical** | Cleaners, sanitizers at wrong concentration, pesticide residue, cleaning chemicals in unlabelled bottles, allergens (a chemical contaminant for allergic guests) | Labelling, segregated storage (chemicals never above food), correct dilutions |
| **Physical** | Hair, fingernails, jewellery, glass, metal shavings from worn equipment, bandages, plastic, fruit pits, bones | Personal hygiene, equipment inspection, blue metal-detectable bandages |
| **Allergen (cross-contact)** | Peanut residue on a "cleaned" knife, gluten dust in fryer oil, shellfish protein in a sauté pan | Dedicated tools, fresh oil, deep clean + sanitize, communication |

> **Note on terminology:** **"Cross-contact"** is the food-allergy term that mirrors "cross-contamination." A peanut-free order contaminated with peanut residue is **cross-contact**, not cross-contamination. The distinction matters because **even microscopic allergen residue can trigger anaphylaxis** — far below microbial infectious doses.

### A-2.04.01P — Recognize Potential Food Contamination Risks

**Performance Criteria:** recognize potential food contamination risks
**Evidence of Attainment:** potential food contamination risks are recognized using sensory assessments and thermometers according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### What "risks" look like in a commercial kitchen

From the Range of Variables: *unsanitary work surfaces, improper storage, chemical, cross use of utensils and equipment, clothes, cell phones, soiled towels, gloves, biological, physical.*

| Risk | What to look for |
|---|---|
| **Unsanitary work surfaces** | Visible residue, sticky films, grease, dried blood, old food bits in seams, biofilm on faucets/drains |
| **Improper storage** | Raw chicken above produce, uncovered cooked items, food on floor, expired dates |
| **Chemical** | Unlabelled spray bottles, chemicals stored above food, spray drift onto produce |
| **Cross use of utensils** | Tongs that worked raw chicken still on the line, knife uncleared between proteins |
| **Clothes** | Soiled apron, dirty chef jacket, sleeves dipping into food |
| **Cell phones** | Phones on prep surfaces — heavily contaminated fomite |
| **Soiled towels** | Multi-purpose towel that has wiped raw juice, then a "clean" plate |
| **Gloves** | Torn glove, glove worn for 4+ hours, glove changed between raw → RTE without handwashing |
| **Biological** | Visible fly, rodent dropping, mould in walk-in, suspicious smell |
| **Physical** | Glass shard near food, missing bandage, broken cambro chip, jewellery on the line |

#### Sensory assessment — using all five senses

| Sense | Use |
|---|---|
| **Sight** | Discolouration, mould, slime, foreign objects, broken equipment, expired dates, soil on surfaces |
| **Smell** | Sour, ammonia, sulphur, "off" odours — pre-spoilage cues |
| **Touch** | Sticky residues on surfaces, slime on protein, temperature on receiving |
| **Sound** | Hissing gas leak, equipment grinding (worn seal → metal shavings) |
| **"Taste" — controlled** | Internal QA tasting from clean spoons only; never to validate questionable food |

> ⚠️ **Pathogens are usually invisible and odourless.** Sensory assessment catches **spoilage and gross contamination**, not the dangerous pathogens like *Salmonella* or *E. coli* O157:H7 that can cause severe illness in foods that look and smell fine. **Sensory + thermometer + procedure** together are the system.

#### Thermometer's role in contamination risk

A thermometer is a contamination detector by proxy:
- **Walk-in air temperature above 4 °C** → TCS food has been in the danger zone → growing pathogens.
- **Hot well temperature below 60 °C** → product is being held in growth conditions.
- **Internal cooked-product probe under target** → undercooked, surviving pathogens.
- **Receiving probe above 4 °C** on a cold delivery → cold-chain break → reject.

> **Memory aid — "SEE-SMELL-PROBE-ACT":** *See if it looks right, smell if it smells right, probe the temperature, act on what you find.*

### A-2.04.02P — Use Clean and Sanitized Work Surfaces, Tools and Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** use clean and sanitized work surfaces, tools and equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** clean and sanitized work surfaces, tools and equipment are used according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### Clean vs sanitize (recap from A-1.01)

| Term | What it does | Removes |
|---|---|---|
| **Cleaning** | Removes visible soil, grease, food residue | Physical contamination, organic matter |
| **Sanitizing** | Reduces microbes on a clean surface by 5-log (99.999%) | Biological |
| **Disinfecting** | Higher-level pathogen kill (non-food-contact) | Biological — used in washrooms, drains |

**Rule:** sanitize **only on already-clean surfaces.** Organic matter (grease, blood, protein) deactivates most chemical sanitizers, especially chlorine.

#### The food-contact sanitation sequence

1. **Scrape** — remove gross debris.
2. **Wash** — detergent + warm water (≈ 43 °C / 110 °F).
3. **Rinse** — potable water to remove detergent (residual detergent can deactivate sanitizer).
4. **Sanitize** — approved sanitizer at correct concentration + contact time.
5. **Air-dry** — towel-drying re-contaminates.

#### Standard Canadian sanitizer concentrations (food contact)

| Sanitizer | Typical concentration | Minimum contact time | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chlorine bleach (sodium hypochlorite)** | **100–200 ppm** | ≈ 7–10 sec | Most common; deactivated by organic soil; corrosive; ideal pH 6.5–7.5 |
| **Quaternary ammonium ("quats")** | **200–400 ppm** per label | ~ 30 sec | Residual film; deactivated by hard water and some detergents |
| **Iodine (iodophors)** | **12.5–25 ppm** | ~ 30 sec | Stains plastics; sensitive to heat > 49 °C |
| **Hot water (thermal)** | ≥ **77 °C / 171 °F** at surface (utensils); **≥ 82 °C / 180 °F** final rinse in high-temp dishwashers | ≥ 30 sec at temperature | No chemical residue; energy cost |

**[Regulatory]:** Concentrations and contact times must match the SDS/label and provincial public-health code. The numbers above are widely used; **always verify with the matching test strip chemistry.**

#### Test strips — non-negotiable

- Match the chemistry: chlorine strips for chlorine, quat strips for quats, iodine for iodine.
- Test **at setup, after 2–4 hours, and after visible soiling.**
- Re-make bucket when concentration drops below target — never "top up."

#### Surfaces, tools, equipment

- **Cutting boards** — wash, rinse, sanitize, air-dry after every protein change. Replace deeply scored boards (knife grooves harbour bacteria).
- **Knives** — sanitize handle and blade between raw and RTE tasks.
- **Slicers, mandolines, grinders, robots** — disassemble per manufacturer, clean each part, sanitize, reassemble dry.
- **Prep surfaces** — sanitize between tasks; rolling sanitizer bucket with cloth changed every couple of hours.
- **Ice scoops** — stored in a clean, dry, dedicated holder, not buried in the ice. **[Regulatory under public-health codes]**
- **Cloths/towels** — colour-coded (e.g., red for raw, blue for RTE, green for produce, yellow for cleaning) is an industry best practice.

### A-2.04.03P — Wash Hands and Change Gloves

**Performance Criteria:** wash hands and change gloves
**Evidence of Attainment:** gloves are discarded and hands are washed to avoid potential food contamination risks.

#### The 20-second handwash (recap from A-1.03)

1. Wet with warm water (≈ 38–43 °C).
2. Apply liquid soap.
3. Scrub all surfaces (palms, backs, between fingers, under nails) for **≥ 20 seconds**.
4. Rinse fingertips-down.
5. Dry with single-use towel.
6. Use the towel to turn off the tap.

#### When to wash hands AND change gloves — cross-contamination triggers

- Switching between **raw protein and ready-to-eat (RTE)** food.
- Switching between **different allergens** (e.g., peanut → peanut-free).
- After **touching face, hair, mouth, phone, money**.
- After **handling garbage** or dirty dishes.
- After **coughing, sneezing, blowing nose**.
- After **breaks, smoking, eating, drinking**.
- After **handling cleaning chemicals**.
- **Every ~4 hours** of continuous glove wear.
- Any time **gloves are damaged**.

#### The right order: wash → dry → glove
- Putting on gloves with wet hands traps moisture inside the glove → bacterial growth and glove tearing.
- **Gloves are NOT a handwashing substitute.** They are a barrier in addition.

#### Common cross-contamination from gloves
- Wearing the same gloves for 4+ tasks.
- "Just wiping" the glove on the apron between raw and RTE.
- Pulling new gloves onto unwashed hands ("the hands are clean inside the gloves").
- Using gloves to handle phones/wipes/garbage, then food.

> ⚠️ Gloves can produce **false security**. Cross-contamination still occurs when gloves move pathogens from one surface to another — gloves only protect food *if changed appropriately*.

### A-2.04.04P — Follow Storage Techniques

**Performance Criteria:** follow storage techniques
**Evidence of Attainment:** storage techniques are followed according to company policy and jurisdictional regulations.

#### The core rules (from Range of Variables)

- **Raw food on the bottom, cooked food on the top.**
- **Elevate** minimum distance above floor.
- **Separate meat items accordingly.**
- **Cover** all stored food.

#### Refrigerator/walk-in storage hierarchy (top → bottom)

| Shelf | Item | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Top** | Ready-to-eat (RTE) food, leftovers, drinks, prep | No further cooking — must not receive drips |
| | Whole seafood (cooks to 70 °C) | Higher cook temp than RTE but lower than meats |
| | Whole cuts beef, pork, lamb (cook to 63 °C) | |
| | Ground meats (cook to 71 °C) | |
| **Bottom** | Whole and ground poultry; marinating raw protein (cook to 74–82 °C) | Drips from above will be killed by the high cook temperature |

The principle: **the food with the highest cook-temperature kill step goes on the bottom**, so any drip from above goes to a food destined for high-temperature cooking. **[Best Practice — universally taught]**

#### Off-the-floor rule
- **Minimum ~15 cm / 6 in clearance** above the floor in storage areas (dry goods, walk-ins, freezers). **[Regulatory in most provinces]**
- Prevents pest access, allows cleaning underneath, and protects from mop-water splash.

#### Cover everything
- Cover prevents physical contamination (drips, debris, condensation).
- Cover during storage; **leave uncovered during active cooling** until ≤ 4 °C.
- Use food-grade plastic wrap, fitted lids, vented lids during cooling.

#### Separation of meat items
- Raw poultry separated from raw beef/pork/seafood (cross-contamination of *Campylobacter* and *Salmonella*).
- Designated bins/containers per protein; never stack raw chicken on raw beef.
- Drip trays for marinating proteins.

#### Dry storage
- Off floor; off walls (at least 5 cm / 2 in); spaced for airflow.
- FIFO rotation; labelled and dated.
- Pests and moisture controls (sealed containers for flour, sugar, rice).

#### Receiving and FIFO
- Inspect at delivery; reject if outside specs.
- Rotate so older stock is used first.
- Date-label everything moved out of original packaging.
- Cooked RTE refrigerated items typically used within ~7 days (varies by jurisdiction).

### A-2.04.05P — Use Designated Storage Containers, Cutting Boards and Utensils

**Performance Criteria:** use designated storage containers, cutting boards and utensils specific to products
**Evidence of Attainment:** designated storage containers, cutting boards and utensils specific to products are used according to company safety plan.

#### Colour-coded cutting boards — the Canadian industry standard

While not specified in federal law, a widely-adopted colour scheme is taught throughout Canadian apprenticeship and used in commercial kitchens. **[Best Practice]**

| Colour | Common designation |
|---|---|
| **Red** | Raw red meat (beef, pork, lamb) |
| **Yellow** | Raw poultry |
| **Blue** | Raw seafood |
| **Green** | Fruits and vegetables |
| **White** | Dairy, bread, bakery |
| **Brown** | Cooked meats |
| **Purple** | Allergen-isolated (gluten-free, peanut-free, etc.) |

> Some operations use simpler 4-colour systems; what matters is that the system is **documented, trained, and enforced** as part of the company safety plan.

#### Designated equipment principles

- **Tongs, spatulas, ladles** assigned per station / per task.
- **Container colours** for raw vs cooked; some ops use coloured gaskets or tags.
- **Allergen-isolated tools** — purple set, dedicated cleaning, stored separately.
- **Single-use items** (parchment, deli sheets) for high-risk allergen orders.
- **No sharing** between raw and RTE stations during service.

#### Cutting board care
- Replace deeply scored boards — knife grooves trap bacteria below sanitizer reach.
- Wash, rinse, sanitize, air-dry after each protein.
- Store on edge for airflow.
- Plastic / polyethylene preferred in many ops for dishwasher safety; wooden boards are permitted in many provinces under specific conditions, but plastic is the default for raw-protein stations.

#### Equipment hygiene — the "shared appliance" problem
- **Mixer, food processor, blender** used for one task, then another → must be fully disassembled and sanitized between, especially raw-to-RTE or allergen transitions.
- **Slicer** — full disassembly between proteins is best practice; allergen-isolated slicing tasks should be scheduled at start of shift before any allergenic product runs through.
- **Fryer oil** — gluten dust from breaded items contaminates oil; gluten-free cannot be fried in shared fryer oil.

### A-2.04.06P — Set Up Sanitizing System Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** set up sanitizing system equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** sanitizing system equipment is set up according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### The sanitizing system — three core pieces

From Range of Variables: **pails, spray bottles, gloves.**

##### Pails (sanitizer buckets)
- Typically two- or three-bucket system per station: detergent water, rinse water, sanitizer.
- **Volume:** 4–8 L common; clean and refill regularly.
- **Cloth in sanitizer**, not multi-purpose dishcloth.
- **Label** with sanitizer name, concentration, and time made.
- **Test** with strips at setup and every 2–4 hours.
- **Re-make** when soiled or concentration drops.
- **Store** off the floor when not in use (raised cart, sink basin), never on prep surfaces.

##### Spray bottles
- **Workplace label** required under WHMIS 2015 (product ID + hazard pictogram + safe handling). **[Regulatory]**
- Filled with sanitizer at correct concentration; no diluting from concentrate without verification.
- Stored away from food (below prep, in chemical caddy).
- Never re-use a food container (e.g., dressing bottle) for chemical — known poisoning pathway.

##### Gloves
- **Rubber/nitrile** gloves for handling chemicals — never the disposable food-handling glove (no chemical resistance).
- **Goggles** for any decanting or splash-prone task (see A-1.02).

#### Sanitizing system setup checklist (pre-shift)

1. Mix sanitizer to correct concentration per dispenser/SDS.
2. Test with matching strip.
3. Label buckets and spray bottles.
4. Place sanitizer cloths in buckets, not on counters.
5. Verify hand-washing sink stocked (soap, towels).
6. Verify dish machine running at correct temperature/ppm.
7. Verify SDS binder/QR is accessible.

#### Common errors
- **Mixing sanitizer with detergent** — most chemical sanitizers are deactivated by detergent residue.
- **Bleach + acid (delimer)** → **chlorine gas**.
- **Bleach + ammonia** → **chloramine gas**. Both have hospitalized cooks.
- Too-strong sanitizer = chemical residue + corrosion.
- Too-weak sanitizer = scented water; pathogens survive.

## 2. Knowledge Objectives — Deeper Dive

### A-2.04.01L — Cross-Contamination Principles and Practices

#### Types of food contamination (recap)

| Type | Examples | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Biological** | Bacteria, viruses, parasites, mould toxins | Hygiene, separation, temperature, sanitation |
| **Chemical** | Cleaners, sanitizer overdose, pesticides, machine lubricant, allergen residue | Labelling, segregation, training, certified equipment |
| **Physical** | Hair, fingernails, glass, metal, wood splinters, jewellery, bandages, plastic | Hygiene, blue bandages, equipment maintenance, foreign-object protocols |
| **Allergen (cross-contact)** | Peanut, gluten, sesame, dairy, soy, egg, fish, shellfish | Dedicated tools, communication, cleaning, training |

#### Sanitizing equipment (carry-over) — pails, spray bottles, gloves

See A-2.04.06P above for setup detail. Key principle: **the sanitizing system itself must be a controlled, monitored process** — it is not "spray and wipe."

#### Sanitizing products and concentrations (carry-over from A-1.01)

| Sanitizer | Food-contact concentration | Verification |
|---|---|---|
| **Chlorine** | 100–200 ppm | Chlorine test strip |
| **Quats** | 200–400 ppm | Quat test strip |
| **Iodine** | 12.5–25 ppm | Iodine test strip |
| **Hot water** | ≥ 77 °C utensil / ≥ 82 °C dish-machine rinse | Thermolabel / thermometer |

#### Communicable illnesses (Range of Variables)

| Illness | Pathogen | Transmission in kitchens | Control |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Norovirus (Norwalk virus)** | Norovirus | Fecal-oral, vomit aerosols, contaminated hands, shellfish | Handwashing (alcohol sanitizer LESS effective than soap), exclude ill workers ≥ 48 h symptom-free, chlorine sanitation at higher concentrations |
| **Hepatitis A** | HAV | Fecal-oral, infected handler, contaminated shellfish | Hand hygiene; vaccination of food handlers (industry-recommended); exclude jaundiced workers |
| **Salmonellosis** | *Salmonella* | Raw poultry/eggs, fecal contamination | Cooking, separation, hygiene |
| **STEC / *E. coli* O157:H7** | STEC | Raw ground beef, leafy greens, fecal | Cook to temp, hygiene, separation |
| **Campylobacteriosis** | *Campylobacter* | Raw poultry, unpasteurized milk | Cook, separation, hygiene |
| **Shigellosis** | *Shigella* | Fecal-oral, infected handler | Hygiene, exclusion |
| **Hand-foot-mouth, common cold, influenza** | Various viruses | Coughing, sneezing, hand contact | Cough into elbow, exclude ill workers, hygiene |
| **Listeriosis** | *Listeria monocytogenes* | RTE foods, cold deli, soft cheese | Cold chain, sanitation, environmental control |

**[Regulatory]** Provincial public-health acts require food handlers with **vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice, fever + sore throat, or confirmed reportable pathogen** to be excluded from food handling. Common return-to-work: **48 hours symptom-free** for vomiting/diarrhea; medical clearance for reportables.

#### Personal hygiene and food safety relating to cross-contamination

- Clean uniform daily; **apron off when leaving the kitchen.**
- **Hair restraint** (full coverage).
- **Nails short**, no polish/false nails.
- **No jewellery** (plain wedding band only).
- **Cuts covered** with blue waterproof bandage + glove.
- **No phones, no food, no drinks** on prep surfaces (lidded water + straw permitted in many ops, away from food).
- **Handwashing discipline** — frequent, before donning gloves, between tasks, after washroom (two-stage wash convention).

#### Storage techniques (Range of Variables)

Recap from A-2.04.04P:
- Raw bottom, cooked top.
- 15 cm / 6 in off floor.
- Separate meats by type.
- Cover everything (after cooling).
- FIFO rotation.
- Dated labels.

### A-2.04.02L — Dietary Requirements

This learning objective recognizes that the cook is responsible for guests' diet-specific needs, where cross-contact can cause harm. From Range of Variables: *cultural, allergies, ethical, religious, health-related, environmental, preferences.*

#### Allergies — the highest-stakes category

**Health Canada Priority Allergens, Gluten, and Added Sulphites (2024-aligned list):**

1. **Eggs**
2. **Milk** (dairy)
3. **Mustard**
4. **Peanuts**
5. **Crustaceans & molluscs** (shellfish)
6. **Fish**
7. **Sesame seeds**
8. **Soy**
9. **Sulphites** (≥ 10 ppm)
10. **Tree nuts** (almonds, Brazil nuts, cashews, hazelnuts, macadamia, pecans, pine nuts, pistachios, walnuts)
11. **Wheat and triticale** (and other gluten sources)

> **[Regulatory]** Federal labelling under the *Food and Drug Regulations* requires declaration of these allergens, gluten sources, and added sulphites at ≥ 10 ppm on packaged foods. In foodservice, the operator's obligation is to **disclose accurately** and **prevent cross-contact** for guests who declare allergies.

##### Allergen cross-contact controls
- **Communication first:** when a guest declares an allergy, escalate to chef/manager immediately.
- **Read the recipe and ingredient list** — including marinades, brines, batters, sauces, garnishes, oils.
- **Dedicated tools and surfaces** — purple boards/utensils, clean sauté pan, fresh oil, clean fryer (or no fryer if shared with breading).
- **Hand wash + glove change** before allergen-isolated prep.
- **Cover food** during plating and transport.
- **Server communication** — flag the plate, deliver separately, confirm guest at table.
- **Cross-contact from shared cooking media:** gluten-free items must NOT be fried in oil that has fried gluten-containing breading.
- **Cross-contact from air/dust:** flour dust travels; bake stations are an allergen risk for celiac-strict orders.
- **Documentation** — allergen menu matrix and training records.

##### Severity
- Allergens can trigger **anaphylaxis** in minutes, requiring epinephrine (EpiPen) and 911. Cross-contact doses are **invisibly small** (milligrams or less).
- This is fundamentally different from microbial cross-contamination, which usually requires larger doses.

#### Religious dietary requirements

| Tradition | Key requirements |
|---|---|
| **Halal (Islam)** | No pork or pork products; alcohol restrictions; meat from animals slaughtered per Islamic rite (*zabihah*); certified halal sources |
| **Kosher (Judaism)** | No pork, shellfish, or mixing meat with dairy; separate utensils and cooking surfaces for meat (*fleishig*) and dairy (*milchig*); certified kosher (hechsher) |
| **Hindu (vegetarian / no beef)** | Many Hindus avoid beef and may follow vegetarian diet |
| **Buddhist (varies)** | Some practice vegetarianism; others have specific abstinence days |
| **Jain (strict vegetarian)** | No meat, eggs, root vegetables (onion, garlic, potato in strict observance) |
| **Sikh (varies)** | Some avoid meat in religious contexts |

> Religious dietary needs often require **certification chains** (halal slaughter, kosher supervision). The cook's responsibility is to use designated ingredients and prevent cross-contact. **[Operational — varies by establishment]**

#### Health-related diets

| Diet | Considerations |
|---|---|
| **Celiac disease / gluten-free** | No wheat, barley, rye, triticale, or contaminated oats. Cross-contact threshold is very low. Treat like an allergen. |
| **Diabetic** | Carbohydrate management; portion and ingredient awareness; sugar-free options |
| **Low-sodium** | For cardiovascular/renal conditions; avoid added salt, soy sauce, brines, cured items |
| **Low-fat / heart-healthy** | Avoid frying, butter, cream-based sauces; lean protein, vegetables |
| **Renal diet** | Restricted potassium, phosphorus, protein — complex; usually managed by dietitian |
| **Phenylketonuria (PKU)** | Severe protein restriction; specialized — typically institutional foodservice |
| **Lactose intolerance** | Dairy substitutes; less life-threatening than dairy allergy |

#### Ethical / environmental / preference diets

| Diet | Description |
|---|---|
| **Vegetarian** | No meat, poultry, fish; may include dairy and eggs |
| **Vegan** | No animal products at all (no honey, gelatin, dairy, eggs, etc.) |
| **Pescatarian** | Fish but no other meat |
| **Flexitarian / reducetarian** | Mostly plant-based, occasional meat |
| **Sustainable seafood** | Ocean Wise, MSC, ASC certifications |
| **Local / seasonal** | Sourcing-driven |
| **Organic** | Certified production methods |

For these, cross-contact is usually a **values issue** rather than a safety issue — but the kitchen must still meet the guest's request honestly.

### A-2.04.03L — Regulatory Requirements

#### Canadian regulatory framework (cross-contamination focus)

| Authority | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Health Canada** | Allergen labelling policy, food safety guidance, communicable disease policy |
| **CFIA — SFCR** | Federally-registered operators must have a Preventive Control Plan addressing cross-contamination CCPs and allergen control |
| **Provincial Public Health Food Premises Regulations** | Restaurant cross-contamination requirements: separation, sanitation, storage, hygiene, illness exclusion |
| **Provincial Public Health Acts** | Communicable disease reporting, worker exclusion |
| **WHMIS 2015** | Chemical hazard communication — directly affects chemical contamination prevention |
| **Provincial OH&S** | Worker safety including chemical handling and PPE |
| **Municipal public health units** | Inspections, enforcement |

#### Inspection checkpoints — what an inspector looks for on cross-contamination

1. **Storage hierarchy** — raw bottom, RTE top, off-floor.
2. **Cross-use of tools** — observe a service period.
3. **Cleaning cloths and sanitizer** — concentration, freshness.
4. **Glove and hand hygiene** — change frequency, handwash sink usage.
5. **Allergen plan** — do staff know how to handle an allergic guest's order?
6. **Cooling and reheating logs** — proper separation through the cooling chain.
7. **Pest control** — IPM logs.
8. **Worker illness** — exclusion policy.
9. **Chemical storage** — labelled, locked, below food.
10. **Cutting boards** — colour scheme, condition, sanitation.

#### HACCP and cross-contamination CCPs

Cross-contamination is typically managed through a combination of **prerequisite programs** (sanitation, hygiene, pest control) and **CCPs** (cooking, cooling, allergen control where applicable).

| Prerequisite program | Cross-contamination relevance |
|---|---|
| **GMPs (Good Manufacturing Practices)** | Facility design, equipment, cleaning, hygiene |
| **SSOPs (Sanitation Standard Operating Procedures)** | Cleaning and sanitation schedule and methods |
| **Allergen control program** | Dedicated tools, training, communication, label review |
| **Pest control / IPM** | Exclusion, monitoring, licensed pesticide |
| **Receiving program** | Supplier approval, inspection, temperature logging |
| **Training program** | Annual food-safety, allergen, hygiene training |

## 3. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Breaking down a case of raw chicken
1. Set up dedicated station: **yellow board, raw-chicken-only knife**, sheet pan for trim, sanitizer bucket.
2. Wear apron over chef jacket; gloves on.
3. Process all chicken in one batch — minimize the number of times you handle the station.
4. **Before doing anything else:**
   - Discard gloves.
   - Wash hands thoroughly (including forearms).
   - Sanitize board, knife handle, station, sink (if drained chicken juice).
   - Change apron if soiled.
   - Don new gloves.
5. Only then move to the next task.

### Scenario 2 — Guest declares a severe peanut allergy
1. **Server flags** the allergy to chef/manager.
2. Chef reviews the order against the menu's allergen matrix.
3. Cook receives the allergen ticket — flagged colour or symbol.
4. **Wash hands, change apron**, don fresh gloves.
5. Move to allergen-isolated station: **purple board, clean tools, fresh sauté pan**.
6. **Verify all ingredients** — including marinades, sauces, garnishes, oils (peanut oil in fryer would be disqualifying; refined peanut oil is technically lower-risk but most ops avoid entirely).
7. Cook the dish; cover during plating and transport.
8. Server delivers separately and confirms at table.
9. If unsure at any step: stop and consult the chef.
10. **EpiPen** known location; staff trained on anaphylaxis response.

### Scenario 3 — Discover a fly in the walk-in
1. **Investigate** — single fly or infestation?
2. Check for source — uncovered product, open door, drain issue.
3. **Discard exposed RTE products** if there's evidence of contact.
4. Sanitize affected surfaces.
5. Contact licensed pest control if more than one fly or rodent evidence found.
6. **Document** in the pest log. **[Regulatory under public-health codes]**

### Scenario 4 — Sanitizer test strip reads pale at mid-service
1. Discard bucket.
2. Rinse and refill at correct concentration.
3. Re-test.
4. Check dispenser if auto-mixed — service call if defective.
5. **Re-sanitize** any recently used surfaces — the previous bucket was sub-spec.

### Scenario 5 — Cook arrives at shift with bandaged finger from yesterday
1. Inspect the bandage — is it intact, blue, no signs of infection?
2. If wound is **clean and healing**: blue bandage + glove, OK to continue.
3. If wound is **red, swollen, draining**: reassign to non-food-contact role; medical evaluation.
4. **Staph aureus** infection can produce heat-stable toxin in food — this is not a "tough it out" situation.

### Scenario 6 — Spilled raw beef juice on a prep surface used for vegetables
1. Stop work in the area.
2. Place signage if floor is also wet.
3. Discard any RTE products in splash zone.
4. **Wash, rinse, sanitize** the surface.
5. Change gloves and wash hands.
6. Replace tools (knife, board) with clean sanitized set.
7. Resume only after the area is verified clean.

## 4. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **"I changed gloves, I don't need to wash hands."** Cross-contamination from glove exterior is exactly the issue — wash before donning new gloves.
- **"This towel only touched a clean plate."** One swipe of raw juice followed by one swipe of a plate transfers bacteria. Use single-use or sanitizer-bucket cloths only.
- **Raw chicken stacked on top of beef in the walk-in.** Chicken drips through packaging; cross-contamination guaranteed.
- **"Sniff test" on chicken to decide if it's safe.** Pathogens are usually odourless; rely on use-by dates and temperature.
- **Re-using a "cleaned" peanut-contact knife on a peanut-free order.** Microscopic residue can trigger anaphylaxis.
- **"Gluten-free" fries from the same fryer as breaded items.** Gluten contamination in the oil — not gluten-free.
- **Sanitizer + detergent in the same bucket.** Detergent deactivates many sanitizers.
- **Allergen sauces stored above non-allergen prep.** A peanut sauce dripping onto a "peanut-free" vegetable prep below is a textbook cross-contact event.
- **Refilling chemical containers without re-labelling.** WHMIS violation; also poisoning risk.
- **Eating, drinking, or smoking near prep surfaces.**
- **Phone or watch on the line.** Phones are some of the dirtiest surfaces in a kitchen.
- **"My cut's fine, I don't need a glove."** *Staph* contamination + heat-stable toxin.
- **Ice scoop buried in the ice bin.** Handle in food contact = contamination.
- **Tasting with the cooking spoon and putting it back in the pot.** Use a separate tasting spoon, discarded after each taste.

## 6. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with A-2.04.

1. **Four contamination types:** Biological, Chemical, Physical, Allergen (cross-contact).
2. **Cross-contact (allergen)** is distinct from cross-contamination — even **microscopic residue** can trigger anaphylaxis.
3. **Storage hierarchy (top → bottom):** RTE → seafood → whole red meats → ground meats → poultry. Drip risk dictates direction.
4. **15 cm / 6 in** off the floor for stored food.
5. **Cover all stored food** — uncovered only during active cooling.
6. **Colour-coded cutting boards (industry standard):** red (raw red meat), yellow (poultry), blue (seafood), green (produce), white (dairy/bread), brown (cooked), purple (allergen-isolated).
7. **Clean before sanitize** — organic matter deactivates most sanitizers.
8. **Sanitizer concentrations (food contact):** chlorine 100–200 ppm; quats 200–400 ppm; iodine 12.5–25 ppm.
9. **High-temp dishwasher final rinse ≥ 82 °C / 180 °F.**
10. **Test strips** at setup, every 2–4 hours, and after soiling.
11. **Hand-wash → dry → glove**; gloves are not a wash substitute.
12. **Glove change triggers:** raw → RTE, allergen change, contamination, every ~4 hours, when damaged. Wash hands at each change.
13. **Personal hygiene:** clean uniform, hair restraint, no jewellery (except plain band), no polish/false nails, blue waterproof bandage + glove on cuts.
14. **Apron off when leaving the kitchen.**
15. **Phones, food, drinks** off prep surfaces.
16. **Communicable illnesses** that exclude from food handling: vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice, fever + sore throat, confirmed reportables. ~48 h symptom-free return convention.
17. **Norovirus (Norwalk)** — fecal-oral, low infectious dose, alcohol sanitizer LESS effective than soap-and-water; chlorine sanitation at elevated concentrations for outbreaks.
18. **Hepatitis A** — fecal-oral, vaccination commonly recommended for food handlers, jaundice excludes from food handling.
19. **Health Canada Priority Allergens, gluten sources, and added sulphites ≥ 10 ppm** must be declared on labels; in foodservice, the cook must prevent cross-contact and disclose accurately.
20. **Allergen list (Canada):** eggs, milk, mustard, peanuts, crustaceans/molluscs, fish, sesame, soy, sulphites, tree nuts, wheat/triticale.
21. **Allergen response:** escalate to chef → check recipe → dedicated tools/oil/pan → wash hands + new gloves → cover plate → server confirms.
22. **Religious diets:** halal (no pork/alcohol; *zabihah*); kosher (no pork/shellfish, no meat-dairy mix, certified); Hindu (commonly no beef); Jain (strict vegetarian, no roots).
23. **Health diets:** celiac/gluten-free (treated like an allergen); diabetic, low-sodium, renal, lactose intolerance.
24. **Ethical diets:** vegetarian, vegan, pescatarian, sustainable.
25. **Sanitizing system:** pails (labelled), spray bottles (WHMIS workplace label), gloves (chemical-resistant); test strips matched to chemistry.
26. **Never mix chemicals:** bleach + acid = chlorine gas; bleach + ammonia = chloramine.
27. **CFIA SFCR PCP** governs cross-contamination CCPs in federally-registered operators; **provincial public-health regulations** govern restaurants.
28. **HACCP prerequisite programs** for cross-contamination: GMPs, SSOPs, allergen control, pest control / IPM, receiving, training.
29. **Pest indicators:** flies, droppings, gnaw marks — discard exposed RTE, sanitize, call licensed pest control, document.
30. **Sensory + thermometer + procedure** — together, the system. Sensory alone catches spoilage but misses pathogens.
