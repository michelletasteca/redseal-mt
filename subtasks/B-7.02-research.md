## Overview: Why Portion Control Is a Professional Standard

Portion control is the practice of measuring or dividing food to a predetermined, consistent size for every serving. It is simultaneously a **food cost tool**, a **quality standard**, a **food safety mechanism**, and a **guest experience guarantee**.

In a commercial kitchen, inconsistent portioning is one of the fastest routes to financial loss. If a protein portion is 25 g over the standard, and the kitchen serves 200 covers per shift, that is 5 kg of food given away for free — every single service. At CAD $28/kg for chicken breast, that is $140 of waste per shift, or over $50,000 per year in a busy restaurant.

Beyond cost, portioning affects:
- **Food safety:** Pre-portioning reduces unnecessary handling and speeds cooling
- **Allergy management:** Standardized portions support accurate allergen declarations
- **HACCP compliance:** Portioned items in cook-chill operations cool more safely
- **Guest satisfaction:** Guests expect the same portion every time they order the same dish
- **Legal compliance:** Menu descriptions implying a specific weight may create consumer expectations under Canadian consumer protection standards

### B-7.02.01P — Portion Product

**Performance Criteria:** Portion product
**Evidence of Attainment:** Product is portioned according to recipe specifications

---

#### What Portioning Means in a Professional Setting

Portioning is the act of dividing prepared or raw food into **standardized serving units** based on the recipe's specified weight, volume, or count. Every plate, bowl, or package must match the standard — not just on average, but **every time**.

A portion is defined by:
- **Weight** (e.g., 170 g beef tenderloin, 150 g salmon fillet)
- **Volume** (e.g., 240 mL soup, 90 mL sauce)
- **Count** (e.g., 3 scallops, 5 shrimp, 2 crab cakes)
- **Geometric division** (e.g., 12 slices per cake, 8 wedges per pie)
- **Scoop size** (e.g., #16 scoop of potato salad)

---

#### Foods That Require Portioning

| Category | Portioning Method | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Proteins (beef, pork, poultry, lamb)** | Scale by weight | Highest food cost; most susceptible to over-portioning loss |
| **Fish and seafood** | Scale by weight | Expensive; delicate; over-portioning compounds rapidly |
| **Deli meats and charcuterie** | Scale by weight or slice count | Consistent build; food cost; allergy declaration |
| **Soups, stocks, sauces** | Ladle by volume | Consistency of portion; prevents under- or over-filling bowls |
| **Salads and cold sides** | Scoop or weight | Consistency; presentation |
| **Starchy sides (mashed potato, rice, grains)** | Scoop by volume | Consistent plating; fast service |
| **Cakes, pies, tarts** | Geometric cut (cake cutter, marker) | Equal servings; accurate food cost per slice |
| **Baked goods (cookies, rolls)** | Scoop or scale | Consistent size = consistent bake time |
| **Butter, cream, cheese** | Scale or pre-portioned ramekins | Accurate accompaniment; cost control |
| **Frozen desserts (ice cream, sorbet)** | Disher/scoop | Consistent serving; easy speed of service |
| **Plated sauces and gravies** | Squeeze bottle, ladle, or spoon measured | Consistent presentation and flavour balance |

---

#### The Food Cost Impact of Portioning Errors

**Formula:**
```
Actual Food Cost % = (Recipe Cost + Over-portion Cost) ÷ Menu Price × 100
```

**Worked Example:**
- Portioned chicken breast standard: 150 g
- Actual average portion: 175 g (25 g over)
- AP cost of chicken: $12/kg = $0.012/g
- Cost of over-portion per plate: 25 × $0.012 = **$0.30 per plate**
- Busy Friday service: 200 covers × $0.30 = **$60 wasted per service**
- Monthly impact (25 services): **$1,500 in waste**

**Over-portioning on the cost percentage:**
- Menu price: $24.00
- Target food cost: $6.00 = 25%
- With 25 g over-portion adding $0.30: actual cost = $6.30 ÷ $24.00 = **26.25%**
- Seemingly minor — but across an entire menu, a 1–2% food cost drift is significant at volume

> **Operational Recommendation:** Calibrate portioning at the start of each service. Use a scale to verify first 3–5 plates when training new staff on a portioning station. Weight-check spot inspections during service prevent drift.

---

#### Portion Control and Food Safety

**Pre-portioning reduces temperature abuse risk:**

- Pre-portioned proteins can be pulled directly from refrigeration to the line, minimizing time in the temperature danger zone (4°C–60°C)
- A 150 g portion of fish cooks in approximately 8–10 minutes; a non-portioned 500 g fillet takes 18–22 minutes — longer cooking = more time management complexity and higher chance of over- or under-cooking

**Pre-portioning accelerates cooling:**

- In cook-chill operations, a 150 g pre-portioned chicken breast cools from 60°C to 4°C significantly faster than an intact roast or large batch
- Smaller, shallower portions have greater surface-area-to-mass ratio, allowing heat to dissipate faster
- This directly supports the **two-stage cooling protocol** (60°C → 20°C within 2 hours; 20°C → 4°C within 4 hours)

**Allergen control:**

- Pre-portioning into labelled containers supports accurate allergen declaration per serving
- Cook-to-order portioning allows allergen substitution to be flagged per plate before cooking begins

### B-7.02.02P — Convert Volume, Weight and Temperature

**Performance Criteria:** Convert volume, weight and temperature
**Evidence of Attainment:** Volume, weight and temperature are converted according to metric, Imperial and American standard conversions

---

#### Why Conversion Fluency Matters for Portion Control

Most portioning utensils in Canadian kitchens are labelled in **Imperial units** (oz ladles, oz-based disher numbers), while recipes and food labels are increasingly in **metric**. A cook must convert fluidly between systems without error — an incorrect conversion at portioning affects every plate served.

Additionally, some supplier-sourced protein cuts are specified in Imperial pounds/ounces, while kitchen portioning targets are in grams. The conversion must be accurate to avoid consistent over- or under-portioning.

---

#### Volume Conversions: Practical Reference

**Metric Foundation:**
```
1 L = 1,000 mL
1 cup (Canadian) = 250 mL
1 tbsp = 15 mL
1 tsp = 5 mL
```

**Imperial/US to Metric:**

| Imperial/US | Metric |
|---|---|
| 1 fl oz | ~30 mL |
| 1 cup (US) | ~237–240 mL |
| 1 cup (Canadian) | 250 mL |
| 1 pint (US) | 473 mL |
| 1 quart (US) | 946 mL (~1 L) |
| 1 US gallon | 3.785 L |
| 1 Imperial gallon | 4.546 L |

> **Critical distinction for portion control:** A US quart = 946 mL, not 1,000 mL. The disher numbering system is based on the US quart (946 mL ÷ disher number = approximate volume per scoop). Using 1,000 mL introduces small but cumulative errors when calculating batch yields.

---

#### Weight Conversions: Practical Reference

| From | To | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| Pounds (lb) | Grams (g) | × 454 |
| Pounds (lb) | Kilograms (kg) | ÷ 2.2 |
| Kilograms (kg) | Pounds (lb) | × 2.2 |
| Ounces (oz) | Grams (g) | × 28.35 |
| Grams (g) | Ounces (oz) | ÷ 28.35 |

**Quick Reference for Common Protein Portions:**

| Weight (g) | Weight (oz) | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| 85 g | 3 oz | Canapé / tapas protein |
| 113 g | 4 oz | Breakfast protein (egg + 4 oz option) |
| 140 g | 5 oz | Lunch entrée protein |
| 170 g | 6 oz | Standard dinner protein |
| 225 g | 8 oz | Premium cut (beef, salmon) |
| 285 g | 10 oz | Large-format cut |
| 340 g | 12 oz | Full rack / large feature cut |

---

#### Temperature Conversions: Practical Reference

**Formulas:**
```
°F = (°C × 9/5) + 32
°C = (°F − 32) × 5/9
```

**Key Temperatures in Portioning and Service Contexts:**

| °C | °F | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| −18°C | 0°F | Minimum freezer standard |
| 0°C | 32°F | Freezing point of water |
| 4°C | 40°F | Maximum refrigeration safe temperature |
| 57–60°C | 135–140°F | Minimum hot-holding temperature |
| 63°C | 145°F | Minimum safe internal temp: whole beef, pork, fish, veal, lamb (with 3-min rest) |
| 68°C | 155°F | Ground beef, mechanically tenderized beef |
| 74°C | 165°F | Poultry; reheated foods; stuffed proteins |
| 82°C | 180°F | Poultry joints, stuffed whole poultry |
| 100°C | 212°F | Boiling point (sea level) |
| 160°C | 325°F | Low oven |
| 175–190°C | 350–375°F | Standard baking/roasting |

### B-7.02.03P — Use Portion Control Utensils

**Performance Criteria:** Use portion control utensils
**Evidence of Attainment:** Portion control utensils are used according to recipe specifications

---

#### Portion Control Utensils: Complete Reference

---

##### 1. Scales (Portioning Scales)

**Types:**

| Type | Description | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| **Digital portion scale** | Electronic; 0.1 g to 5 kg range; tare function | Most accurate; preferred for all weight portioning |
| **Spring scale** | Mechanical; less accurate; slower | Older kitchens; rough portioning only |
| **Balance scale** | Uses counterweights; highly accurate | Specialty applications; rare in modern kitchens |
| **Hanging/crane scale** | For large quantities (side of beef, whole fish, bulk) | Receiving dock; large-batch prep |

**How to Use a Digital Portion Scale:**
1. Place clean, dry scale on a flat, level surface
2. Power on; allow to stabilize
3. Place the portioning container (plate, bag, pan) on the scale
4. **Tare (zero) the scale** — the display should read 0.0 g with the container on it
5. Add the ingredient until the target weight is reached
6. Remove; re-tare for the next portion
7. If scale is used for multiple products, wipe platform between uses

> **Operational Recommendation:** Calibrate portion scales weekly with a known reference weight (often a calibration weight set). A scale off by even 5 g causes consistent over- or under-portioning across all portions. CFIA regulations require accurate weights on pre-packaged goods — commercial operations producing retail-labelled portions must use certified legal-for-trade scales.

**Common Scale Problems:**

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Scale reads positive without ingredient | Residue on platform | Clean platform; re-zero |
| Scale drifts while being read | Vibration, drafts | Shield from drafts; wait for stable reading |
| Inconsistent readings | Low battery | Replace batteries; check calibration |
| Scale reads over capacity | Exceeds max weight | Use hanging scale; weigh in smaller batches |

---

##### 2. Ladles

Ladles are the standard portioning utensil for **liquids and semi-liquids**: soups, stocks, sauces, gravies, batters, dressings.

**The Commercial Ladle Sizing System:**

Ladles are sized by the number of **fluid ounces** they hold when filled to the brim:

| Ladle Size (US fl oz) | Approx. mL | Common Use |
|---|---|---|
| 1 oz | 30 mL | Sauces, small condiments, dressings |
| 2 oz | 60 mL | Sauces, glazes, small sides |
| 3 oz | 90 mL | Dressings, gravies |
| 4 oz | 120 mL | Soups, chili, pan sauces |
| 6 oz | 180 mL | Standard soup portion (small bowl) |
| 8 oz | 240 mL | Standard soup portion (full bowl) |
| 12 oz | 355 mL | Large bowl soups, chowder |
| 16 oz | 480 mL | Bulk service, stew, large portions |

> **Operational Recommendation:** Ladle size must be matched to the service standard. If the recipe specifies a 240 mL soup portion and you use a 8 oz (240 mL) ladle filled level, every guest receives a consistent portion. Ladles should be hung or racked with size labels visible for line staff.

**How to Use a Ladle Correctly:**
1. Dip into the liquid at an angle
2. Scoop with a level, consistent motion
3. Lift straight up — do not tip to drain back into the pot
4. **Fill to the brim** for an accurate measured portion — or to the marked fill line on the ladle shank
5. Pour into service vessel in a single smooth motion

> **Common Mistake:** Tilting the ladle before pouring, losing 5–15 mL of liquid. Over 100 portions, this equals 500–1,500 mL of under-portioned product.

---

##### 3. Measuring Cups

Used for both recipe preparation and portioning of semi-solid and dry foods.

**Two Types:**

| Type | Purpose | Correct Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Liquid measuring cups** (with pour spout, graduated sides) | Measuring liquids by volume | Set on flat surface; read at eye level at the bottom of the meniscus |
| **Dry measuring cups** (flat-topped, nested sets) | Measuring dry or semi-solid foods | Fill to heaping; level off with straight edge |

> **Key Principle:** Never use a liquid measuring cup for dry ingredients, or vice versa. A liquid measuring cup cannot be levelled accurately. A dry cup cannot be read while liquid is poured — parallax error and drip loss make it imprecise.

**Canadian Cup Standard:** 1 cup = **250 mL**
**US Cup:** ~237–240 mL — a small but cumulative difference in large-batch portioning

---

##### 4. Portion Scoops (Dishers)

Dishers (also called portion scoops or ice cream scoops with a spring release mechanism) are the standard utensil for **semi-solid portioning**: mashed potatoes, rice, cookie dough, salads, ice cream, sandwich fillings.

**The Disher Numbering System:**

The number stamped on the disher or color-coded on the release spring represents the **approximate number of level scoops required to fill one US quart (946 mL)**.

```
Approximate Volume per Scoop (mL) = 946 ÷ Disher Number
```

**Complete Disher Reference:**

| Disher # | Approx. Volume | Approx. Weight (water equiv.) | Common Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| #6 | ~157 mL | ~157 g | Large muffin, large salad, entrée starch |
| #8 | ~118 mL | ~118 g | Muffins, large cookies, ice cream, salads |
| #10 | ~94 mL | ~94 g | Standard muffin, burger patty, potato scoop |
| #12 | ~78 mL | ~78 g | Cupcake batter, crab cake, sauce portioning |
| #16 | ~59 mL | ~59 g | Cookies, appetizer portions, small salads |
| #20 | ~47 mL | ~47 g | Canapé filling, cookie (medium) |
| #24 | ~39 mL | ~39 g | Small cookies, filling portioning |
| #30 | ~31 mL | ~31 g | Truffle/candy, garnish scoops |
| #40 | ~24 mL | ~24 g | Small garnish, demi-tasse dessert |
| #60 | ~16 mL | ~16 g | Mini portions, amuse-bouche, petit fours |
| #100 | ~9 mL | ~9 g | Very small garnish, decorative scoops |

> **Memory Aid:** "The higher the number, the smaller the scoop." A #100 is tiny; a #6 is large.

**Color-Coding System (Industry Standard):**

Many kitchen supply brands use a colour convention for quick visual identification:

| Colour | Disher # (approximate) |
|---|---|
| White | #6 |
| Blue | #8 |
| Orange | #10 |
| Teal/Green | #12 |
| Light green | #16 |
| Yellow | #20 |
| Red | #24 |
| Purple | #30 |
| Black | #40 |

> **Note:** Colors may vary by manufacturer — always confirm by reading the number stamp on the mechanism. Use color as a quick reference, not an absolute.

**How to Use a Disher:**
1. Dip into product; press down firmly and drag through product to fill
2. Level the scoop against the side of the container or with a straight edge if needed
3. Dispense by pressing the release thumb lever into the bowl or pan
4. For uniform baked goods: use consistent fill pressure; do not pack or under-fill
5. Clean release mechanism regularly — dried batter jams the spring

##### 5. Portion Bags

Portion bags are sealed, single-use containers pre-filled with a measured weight or volume of product. Common in **cook-chill, cook-freeze, and high-volume production operations**.

**Types:**
- **Vacuum-sealed portion bags:** Pre-portioned proteins (chicken breast, fish fillet, steak), sealed with a vacuum chamber or chamber sealer
- **Zip-lock portion bags:** Semi-sealed; sauces, marinades, batters
- **Piping bags:** Pre-filled sauces, purées, whipped cream — volume controlled by bag size and fill level
- **Pre-portioned retail-style pouches:** Sealed with a clip sealer; for take-out, grab-and-go

**Food Safety Advantages of Portion Bags:**
- Vacuum-sealed bags prevent oxidation and freezer burn
- Eliminate repeated handling — bag goes from freezer/fridge directly to bath or pan
- Each bag is labelled with: product name, date packaged, weight, use-by date, allergen warnings
- FIFO (First In, First Out) is easily enforced with dated bags stacked by date

> **Regulatory Context (Industry Practice):** HACCP-compliant kitchens use labelled portion bags as part of their traceability system. Provincial food safety regulations require that food stored in secondary containers be labelled. Verify specifics with your provincial health authority.

**Vacuum Packaging — Food Safety Warning:**

> **WARNING:** Vacuum-packaged foods create an **anaerobic (oxygen-free) environment** that supports the growth of *Clostridium botulinum*, a spore-forming bacterium that produces the deadly botulinum toxin. Garlic-in-oil, reduced-oxygen packaged proteins, and sous vide preparations all carry this risk.
>
> - Always refrigerate vacuum-packaged products at ≤4°C immediately after sealing
> - Never store vacuum-packaged items at ambient temperature
> - Follow validated time-temperature parameters for sous vide cooking

---

##### 6. Portion Cake Cutters

Portion cake cutters are **wire or blade dividers** used to cut cakes, cheesecakes, pies, and sheet baked goods into equal portions. They ensure geometric precision and consistent serving size.

**Types:**

| Tool | Description | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Wire cake cutter/divider** | Metal frame with multiple taut wires at equal spacing; pressed through a cake | Rounds, loaves; 10, 12, 14, 16 slice options |
| **Pie marker** | Pie-shaped stamper that presses indent lines into a pie before cutting | Ensures equal wedge cuts before service |
| **Sheet cake cutter** | Grid-pattern cutter for rectangular sheet pans | Cuts 20, 24, 30, 48 or other counts from a hotel pan |
| **Pizza wheel/rocker** | Rotating or rocking blade | Clean portion cuts for flatbreads, tarts |
| **Straight portion guide** | Ruler-style marker with notches at regular intervals | Marking cut lines before cutting; ensures equal slice width |

**Portion Counts from Standard Pan Sizes:**

| Pan Size | Common Portion Count | Portion Dimensions |
|---|---|---|
| 9-inch round | 8–12 slices | Wedge |
| 10-inch round | 10–14 slices | Wedge |
| Half sheet pan (18"×13") | 24–54 pieces | Depends on item size |
| Full sheet pan (26"×18") | 48–108 pieces | Depends on item size |
| Quarter sheet pan (9"×13") | 12–24 pieces | Depends on item size |

> **Operational Tip:** Pre-mark (score) cuts before the cake or pie is fully chilled — soft product cuts cleanly while slightly warm. For perfectly clean cheesecake cuts, dip the blade in hot water and wipe dry between each cut.

### B-7.02.01L — Portion Control Procedures and Applications

**Learning Outcomes:** Demonstrate knowledge of performing portion control and its associated applications

---

#### Procedures for Adjusting Recipe Yields

See B-7.01.03P for the Conversion Factor method. In the context of portioning specifically:

**Step 1:** Confirm whether the recipe yield is expressed as:
- Total weight or volume (e.g., "Yield: 3 kg mashed potato")
- Number of portions (e.g., "Yield: 20 portions")
- Both (preferred: "Yield: 20 portions × 150 g each = 3 kg total")

**Step 2:** If only total yield is given, divide to find portion weight:
```
Portion Size = Total Yield ÷ Number of Portions
```

**Step 3:** Multiply by CF to get new total yield; verify with equipment capacity (does it fit in the pot? in the oven?)

**Step 4:** Divide new total yield by portion size to confirm correct number of portions

---

#### Foods That Require Portioning: Detail

**Proteins:** Always portion by weight. Use scale for every portion — eyeballing protein weight is unreliable and consistently over-portions. Pre-portioning before service (during prep) is faster and more accurate than portioning during service rush.

**Soups and Sauces:** Use a ladle sized to match the exact serving volume. The ladle must be filled to the brim to be accurate. Using a smaller ladle and filling it high is inconsistent.

**Starchy Sides:** Disher portioning is fast and consistent. Select the disher number that gives the correct target weight. Test by weighing 5 consecutive scoops with the scale to verify the disher delivers the intended weight — disher volumes are approximate and density of the food affects actual weight.

**Baked Goods and Desserts:** For cakes/pies, use portion cutters for accuracy. For cookie dough, the disher ensures identical portions that bake evenly in the same time — irregular sizes produce some over-baked and some under-baked cookies in the same batch.

---

#### Calibration: Verifying Portioning Utensil Accuracy

> **Industry Best Practice:** Periodically verify that portioning utensils are delivering the intended portion weight. This is especially important for:
> - Dishers (density of product affects actual weight per scoop)
> - Ladles (manufacturer tolerances vary)
> - Scales (drift over time, especially with temperature and humidity changes)

**Calibration Process:**
1. Fill the utensil in normal operating method
2. Weigh or measure the contents on a calibrated scale
3. Record actual vs. target
4. If outside ±5% tolerance, adjust technique or replace utensil

### B-7.02.02L — Measurement Systems and Conversions

**Learning Outcomes:** Demonstrate knowledge of systems of measurement and their conversions

---

#### Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Tare** | Zeroing a scale with a container on it so only the ingredient weight is measured |
| **Meniscus** | The curved surface of a liquid in a container; read at the bottom of the curve |
| **Conversion Factor (CF)** | Desired yield ÷ original yield; used to scale recipes |
| **Portion** | A single standardized serving of a food item |
| **Yield** | Total output of a recipe, expressed as weight, volume, or count |
| **Resolution** | The smallest increment a scale can read (e.g., 0.1 g, 1 g, 5 g) |
| **Legal-for-trade scale** | A scale certified for commercial transactions involving weight-based pricing |

---

#### Systems of Measurement: Quick Reference

**Metric (SI):**
- Mass: milligram (mg), gram (g), kilogram (kg)
- Volume: millilitre (mL), centilitre (cL), litre (L)
- Temperature: Celsius (°C)
- Length: millimetre (mm), centimetre (cm), metre (m)

**Imperial:**
- Mass: ounce (oz), pound (lb)
- Volume: fluid ounce (fl oz), pint (pt), quart (qt), gallon (gal)
- Temperature: Fahrenheit (°F)

**American Standard:** Similar to Imperial but with different gallon size (3.785 L vs. 4.546 L Imperial)

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Impact | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Eyeballing portion size for proteins | Rushing; overconfidence | Consistent over-portioning; food cost drift; guest inequality | Always use scale for proteins; pre-portion during prep |
| Using incorrect ladle size | Grabbing nearest available ladle | Under- or over-portioning by 30–100% | Ladles must be labelled and organized by size; use recipe-specified size |
| Not taring scale before weighing | Forgetting the step | Container weight added to ingredient weight | Always tare; make it automatic habit |
| Using disher without verifying weight | Assuming volumetric = weight | Different food densities give different actual weights | Verify with scale for each new product (mashed potato ≠ ice cream by weight for same scoop number) |
| Packing dry measuring cups | Speed | 20–30% excess ingredient | Fill using spoon-and-level method; or weigh instead |
| Reading liquid measure at an angle | Standing above the cup | Parallax error — reading appears lower than actual | Crouch to eye level; read at the meniscus |
| Using a dished (heaped) ladle | Inconsistent technique | Over-portioning by 15–25% | Level the ladle; fill to brim only |
| Cutting cake without a portion guide | Freestyle cutting | Unequal slices; food cost and guest perception issues | Use portion marker or cutter |
| Storing vacuum-sealed protein at room temp | Unfamiliarity with anaerobic risk | C. botulinum growth risk | Refrigerate ≤4°C immediately after sealing |
| Ignoring scale calibration | Assuming accuracy | Systematic over- or under-portioning across all portions | Calibrate weekly with known reference weight |

## Memory Aids

**Ladle Sizes — "The oz is the fill"**
- A 4 oz ladle holds 4 fl oz = 120 mL when filled level
- "The number on the ladle = the fl oz it holds"

**Disher Formula — "946 ÷ Number = mL"**
- #8 scoop: 946 ÷ 8 = ~118 mL
- #16 scoop: 946 ÷ 16 = ~59 mL
- "Higher number = smaller scoop"

**Scale Use — "Tare, Fill, Read"**
- Three-step sequence every time: tare first, add ingredient, read weight

**Conversions — The Bridge Numbers:**
- 28 g = 1 oz (practical rounding of 28.35)
- 454 g = 1 lb
- 2.2 lb = 1 kg
- 250 mL = 1 Canadian cup
- 30 mL = 1 fl oz (practical rounding of 29.57)

**Temperature Anchors — "4 to Store, 60 to Hold, 74 to Safe"**
- Refrigerate at ≤4°C
- Hot-hold at ≥60°C
- Internal cooking target (poultry/reheating): 74°C

**Over-Portioning Math — "25 g × 200 covers = 5 kg lost per service"**
- Visualize the entire shift's over-portioning as a single pile — makes the loss tangible

### Exam Q&A

**Q1: What is the approximate volume of a #12 disher?**
A: 946 mL ÷ 12 = **~79 mL** (approximately 2.7 fl oz)

**Q2: A ladle is labelled "6 oz." How many mL does it hold when filled to the brim?**
A: 6 fl oz × 30 mL/fl oz = **~180 mL**

**Q3: Why is weight measurement preferred over volume measurement for protein portioning?**
A: Weight is density-independent and directly reflects the actual amount of food. Volume measurement of irregularly shaped proteins (like fillets) is impractical and inaccurate. A scale gives an exact, verifiable, consistent result for every portion.

**Q4: A recipe yields 2.4 kg of mashed potato and is portioned with a #8 disher (~118 mL per scoop). Assuming mashed potato density is approximately 0.85 g/mL, how many portions does the recipe yield?**
A:
- Volume per scoop ≈ 118 mL
- Weight per scoop: 118 × 0.85 = ~100 g
- Total portions: 2,400 g ÷ 100 g = **24 portions**

**Q5: What is the food safety risk specific to vacuum-packaged foods stored improperly?**
A: The anaerobic (oxygen-free) environment created by vacuum packaging supports the growth of *Clostridium botulinum*, which produces the deadly botulinum toxin. Vacuum-packaged products must be stored at ≤4°C at all times.

**Q6: A deli scale reads 128 g when only the empty tray is on it (tare was not done). The cook weighs what appears to be a 170 g portion. What is the actual ingredient weight?**
A: 170 g − 128 g = **42 g** — significantly under-portioned. The tare function must always be used.

**Q7: A new cook consistently uses a 6 oz ladle instead of the specified 4 oz ladle for soup. The recipe specifies 120 mL (4 oz) per serving. What is the over-portioning percentage?**
A: Actual portion: 180 mL. Target: 120 mL. Over-portion: 60 mL ÷ 120 mL = **50% over-portioned per bowl**.

**Q8: What is the colour-code system for portion scoops, and why should a cook not rely solely on colour?**
A: Colour-coding (e.g., blue = #8, orange = #10) provides fast visual identification. However, colours vary by manufacturer — the number stamped on the release mechanism is the only reliable indicator. Always verify by reading the number.

**Q9: Convert 6 oz of sauce to mL.**
A: 6 × 30 mL = **180 mL** (exact: 6 × 29.57 = 177.4 mL; 180 mL is the practical professional rounding)

**Q10: What is the minimum internal temperature for reheated food in a commercial kitchen?**
A: **74°C (165°F)**, reached within 2 hours of beginning the reheating process (common industry practice; verify with provincial authority for regulatory specifics).

**Q11: Why should dishers be weight-verified rather than trusting the volume number alone?**
A: The disher number reflects an approximate volume based on water. Denser foods (mashed potato, hummus, ground meat) will weigh more per scoop than lighter foods (whipped cream, soft mousse). The scoop volume is constant, but the weight per portion varies by product density. Only a scale confirms the actual weight delivered.

**Q12: A pastry chef is cutting a 10-inch round cake into 12 equal slices. What tool ensures consistent portions, and what is the angle of each slice?**
A: A **wire cake divider or portion marker** set for 12 slices. Each wedge angle = 360° ÷ 12 = **30° per slice**.
