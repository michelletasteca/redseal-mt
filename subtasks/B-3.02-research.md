## Overview and Why This Skill Matters

The selection and correct use of pots, pans, and utensils is not a trivial operational detail — it directly affects food safety, food quality, worker safety, and equipment longevity. Using an aluminium pot to make a tomato sauce for an extended period will produce a metallic off-flavour. Using a metal spatula in a non-stick pan damages the coating and creates a physical contamination hazard. Heating an empty non-stick pan on high heat can release toxic fumes. A pot with a loose handle is a serious burn and spill hazard.

The Red Seal exam tests whether a cook understands the material science behind cookware, the food safety implications of reactive metals, the correct matching of utensils to pan surfaces, and the regulatory requirements for washing, sanitizing, and storing cookware. This document covers all of these in depth.

---

## Heat Transfer — The Science Behind Cookware Choices

Before examining specific pots, pans, and materials, understanding how heat moves from the stove to the food is essential to understanding why material selection matters.

**Three mechanisms of heat transfer:**

| Mechanism | How It Works | Where It Occurs in Cooking |
|---|---|---|
| **Conduction** | Heat moves through direct physical contact between objects | Pan bottom → food; food surface → food interior |
| **Convection** | Heat moves through the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas) | Boiling/simmering liquid; oven air circulation |
| **Radiation** | Heat moves through electromagnetic waves without direct contact | Broiler/salamander; charcoal grill; oven walls |

**Why conductivity of the pan material matters:**
- A material with **high thermal conductivity** heats quickly and responds immediately to temperature changes — excellent for precise heat control
- A material with **low thermal conductivity** heats slowly and changes temperature slowly — poor response but good heat retention once hot
- A material with **high heat retention** stores thermal energy and maintains temperature even when cold food is added — critical for searing

**Material thermal conductivity comparison:**

| Material | Thermal Conductivity | Heat-Up Speed | Temperature Response | Heat Retention |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Copper** | ~400 W/m·K — highest | Very fast | Immediate; precise | Low (heats and cools quickly) |
| **Aluminium** | ~205 W/m·K — excellent | Fast | Very good | Moderate |
| **Cast iron** | ~55 W/m·K — moderate | Slow | Poor (changes slowly) | Excellent |
| **Stainless steel** | ~16 W/m·K — poor | Slow | Poor alone | Low |
| **Ceramic/stoneware** | ~1–2 W/m·K — very poor | Very slow | Very poor | Moderate (thick walls retain heat) |

> **Practical implication:** This is why stainless steel pots almost universally have an aluminium or copper core — stainless steel is durable and non-reactive, but it conducts heat so poorly that hot spots form where the flame contacts the thin metal. The core layer spreads heat evenly before it reaches the food-contact surface.

## Pots and Pans — Types, Materials, and Selection

### Pots and Pans: Types in the Range of Variables

---

#### Braising Pan / Brasier / Rondeau

**Physical characteristics:**
- Wide, low-sided, heavy pan with two loop handles on either side
- Diameter typically 28–36 cm; depth 8–12 cm
- Available in stainless steel with aluminium or copper core; or enamelled cast iron

**Why this pan is used for braising:**
- Wide bottom surface provides maximum browning area (Maillard reaction and caramelization of the meat surface)
- Low sides allow moisture to escape and caramelize rather than steam the food
- Heavy construction retains heat evenly during the long, low cooking time of braises
- Tight-fitting lid traps moisture during the covered phase of braising

**Applications:**
- Braises (short ribs, chicken legs, lamb shanks)
- Shallow poaching
- Pan sauces
- Large-volume sautéing in commercial settings

**Selection tip:** For braising on the stovetop and in the oven, the pan must be **oven-safe to the required temperature** (typically 160–180°C). Plastic handles are not oven-safe; stainless or cast iron handles are.

---

#### Roasting Pan

**Physical characteristics:**
- Rectangular; high sides (5–8 cm); with or without a V-rack or flat rack
- Available in stainless steel, carbon steel, or heavy-gauge aluminium

**Why this pan is used for roasting:**
- Rectangular shape accommodates whole birds, roasts, and large cuts efficiently
- High sides prevent splatter in the oven
- A rack within the pan lifts the product off the pan bottom — air circulates underneath, and the bottom does not steam (which would prevent browning)
- The drippings that collect in the pan bottom during roasting are the base for pan sauces and gravies

**Applications:**
- Roasting whole poultry, legs of lamb, beef prime rib
- Making pan drippings for gravy
- Sheet pan baking (without rack)

**Selection:** Choose a gauge (thickness) heavy enough not to warp. Thin, cheap roasting pans warp at high oven temperatures, causing pooled drippings to burn in one area. Warped pans also present tipping hazards on the stovetop when making pan sauce.

---

#### Sauté Pan (Sautoir / Sauteuse)

The word **sauté** comes from the French *sauter* — "to jump." Two related but distinct pan shapes bear the name:

| Type | Shape | Best Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Sautoir** (straight-sided sauté pan) | Straight, vertical sides; wide flat bottom | Reducing sauces; sautéing items that need containment; braising |
| **Sauteuse** (slope-sided sauté pan / skillet / frying pan) | Sloped, flared sides | Tossing vegetables; omelettes; sautéing items that need to be moved or flipped easily |

**Why the slope-sided design matters:**
- Sloped sides allow a spatula or wrist-flip technique to toss food without a utensil — the slope guides food back into the pan
- Straight sides trap liquid and allow for better sauce reduction — the straight walls contain the liquid while the bottom provides the evaporation surface

**Applications:**
- Sautéing vegetables, protein, and fruit
- Making pan sauces (deglaze after sautéing)
- Omelettes and eggs (slope-sided)
- Reducing sauces (straight-sided)

---

#### Stock Pot

**Physical characteristics:**
- Tall, narrow-diameter body; two loop handles; tight-fitting lid
- Available in aluminium (most common for commercial use due to weight and conductivity) or stainless steel
- Sizes: 12 L (standard small), 20 L, 30 L, 60+ L (commercial)

**Why the tall, narrow design:**
- **Minimizes evaporation:** The narrow surface area at the top reduces the rate at which liquid evaporates relative to the volume — critical when making 8-hour stocks that would reduce too quickly in a wide pan
- **Stratifies layers:** Fat rising to the top of stock has less tendency to re-incorporate in a tall pot; it rises and stays at the top where it can be skimmed
- **Stability with large volumes:** A tall pot with a wide base is less likely to tip than a wide, shallow vessel of the same volume

**Applications:**
- Making stocks (chicken, beef, veal, fish, vegetable)
- Large-volume soups
- Blanching vegetables in volume
- Boiling pasta in large quantities

**Food safety — large stock pots and cooling:**
> Large volumes of hot liquid cool very slowly because of the low surface-area-to-volume ratio. A 30 L stock pot of boiling stock may take 12+ hours to cool to ≤4°C in a refrigerator — this is a critical two-stage cooling violation risk.
>
> **Two-stage cooling requirement:**
> - Stage 1: 60°C → 20°C within 2 hours
> - Stage 2: 20°C → 4°C within 4 hours
>
> Large stock volumes must be divided into smaller containers (≤5 cm depth), ice-bathed, or blast-chilled to achieve this. Never place a large, full stock pot in the refrigerator expecting it to cool safely.

---

#### Brazier (Rondeau)

In many Canadian culinary programs and professional kitchens, **brazier** and **rondeau** are used interchangeably with **braising pan** — the same wide, low-sided, two-handled pan described above. The slight distinction in some references:
- **Brazier**: more often refers to a slightly taller vessel (deeper than a brasier) used over direct heat for browning and then transferring to the oven
- **Rondeau**: the classical French name for the wide, low, two-handled pan

**Applications:** Braising, pot roasting, wide-batch sautéing, large-volume sauce making.

---

#### Wok

**Physical characteristics:**
- Round-bottomed (traditional) or flat-bottomed (for Western stoves); thin carbon steel walls; long single handle or two loop handles
- Traditional material: carbon steel; also available in cast iron

**Why the wok shape matters:**
- **Round bottom concentrates heat** at the bottom centre — traditionally used over very high-heat open wood or gas fires (wok burner / wok hei burner) where temperatures exceed 300°C
- **Sloped sides radiate heat downward** while the upper sides are cooler — allows food at the edges to rest while the centre cooks, then food is tossed back to the centre
- **Large surface area to volume ratio** allows rapid evaporation of moisture — essential for stir-frying, where the goal is to cook quickly without steaming the food

**The Maillard reaction and wok hei:**
Traditional stir-frying at very high temperatures in a wok produces **wok hei** ("breath of the wok") — a complex flavour character created by Maillard reactions, slight char, and the rapid evaporation of aromatics. This requires extremely high temperatures (>300°C) that only a professional wok burner can achieve. A standard commercial gas range cannot replicate full wok hei, but a quality wok used on a high-BTU burner produces much better results than a flat-bottomed pan.

**Applications:**
- Stir-frying
- Deep-frying (the shape contains oil efficiently)
- Steaming (with bamboo steamer insert)
- Braising (Chinese red-braised preparations)

**Wok seasoning:**
Like cast iron, carbon steel woks require seasoning before first use and maintenance of the seasoning layer:
1. Wash off factory oil with hot water and soap
2. Heat over high flame until the surface turns slightly blue-grey
3. Apply high-smoke-point oil with a cloth and rub in; heat until smoking
4. Repeat oiling and heating 3–5 times
5. After each use: wipe clean, dry, apply a light oil coat for storage

## Materials Used in Pots and Pans

### Copper

**Properties:** Highest thermal conductivity of any common cookware material; extremely responsive to temperature changes.

**Why copper is the traditional professional choice:**
- Temperature adjustments are immediate — when you reduce the flame, the pan temperature drops instantly
- Even heat distribution eliminates hot spots
- Especially critical for temperature-sensitive preparations (tempering chocolate, custard sauces, candy, egg-based sauces like hollandaise)

**The reactivity problem:**
- Bare copper is a **reactive metal** — it reacts with acids (tomatoes, wine, vinegar, citrus) to form copper compounds that are toxic in sufficient amounts and produce a metallic, astringent flavour
- All culinary copper cookware must be lined with a non-reactive material: traditionally **tin** (soft, food-safe, but wears over time and must be re-tinned); increasingly **stainless steel** (durable, non-reactive, but reduces the responsiveness advantage slightly)

**Care:**
- Tin-lined copper: wash gently; avoid abrasives; never use metal utensils (scratches tin lining); re-tin when lining wears through or scratches expose bare copper
- Stainless-lined copper: more durable; dishwasher generally safe for the interior; exterior copper must be polished
- Copper exterior is cleaned with commercial copper polish (acidic cleaner) or the traditional method: salt + lemon juice + rinse

**Cost:** Copper cookware is the most expensive in the kitchen — handle with corresponding care.

---

### Stainless Steel

**Properties:** Durable; non-reactive; hygienic; poor thermal conductor.

**Why stainless steel is the standard for commercial kitchens:**
- **Non-reactive:** Stainless steel does not react with acids, alkalis, or most food compounds — no metallic flavour transfer, no discoloration of food
- **Durable:** Resists denting, scratching, and corrosion in high-use commercial environments
- **NSF-certifiable and hygienic:** Smooth, non-porous surface; withstands commercial dishwasher cycles; meets food-contact surface standards

**The conductivity problem — resolved with a core:**
- Pure stainless steel (single ply) conducts heat poorly — hot spots form directly over the burner
- Solution: **clad construction** (aluminium or copper core bonded between stainless steel layers) or a **disc bottom** (aluminium or copper disc bonded to the base)
- Look for: "tri-ply" (stainless–aluminium–stainless); "5-ply"; or "disc bottom"

**Rainbow discoloration:**
- High heat can cause bluish or rainbow discoloration on stainless steel — this is **cosmetic only** (chromium oxide at the surface) and does not indicate contamination or affect cooking performance
- Can be removed with stainless steel cleaner or white vinegar

---

### Aluminium

**Properties:** Excellent heat conductor; lightweight; relatively inexpensive; reactive to acids and alkalis.

**Common uses:** Stock pots (commercial), sheet pans, hotel pans, fryer baskets, baking moulds.

**The reactivity concern:**
- Aluminium reacts with highly acidic foods (tomatoes, wine, citrus, vinegar) and highly alkaline foods (baking soda solutions)
- This produces a metallic flavour and can cause discoloration in light-coloured foods (e.g., egg whites turned grey, white sauce turned yellowish)
- The aluminium content transferred to food in typical cooking is low — Health Canada has not issued restrictions on aluminium cookware for general use, and research has not established a causal link between aluminium cookware and Alzheimer's disease

**Solution — anodized aluminium:**
- **Hard-anodized aluminium** has been electrochemically treated to create a harder, denser oxide layer that is non-reactive, scratch-resistant, and more durable than bare aluminium
- Widely used for commercial sauté pans, stock pots, and baking equipment
- More expensive than regular aluminium; much less expensive than copper

**Aluminium and dishwashers:**
- Bare aluminium reacts with the high-alkalinity detergent used in commercial dishwashers — surfaces become dull, dark, and may pit over time
- Hand-wash bare aluminium pots; hard-anodized aluminium is more dishwasher-tolerant but still benefits from hand washing

---

### Non-Stick (PTFE / Polytetrafluoroethylene / Teflon®)

**Properties:** Extremely low friction surface; food releases easily; requires minimal fat; cannot withstand high heat or metal utensils.

**How it works:**
- PTFE (Teflon® is the DuPont brand name) is applied as a coating on a base metal (typically aluminium)
- PTFE has one of the lowest coefficients of friction of any solid material — food simply does not adhere
- Multiple layers of PTFE coating produce more durable non-stick surfaces

**Best applications:**
- Eggs (omelettes, scrambled, fried)
- Delicate fish (skin-on fish fillets)
- Pancakes and crêpes
- Light-duty sautéing with minimal fat

**Critical temperature limitation:**

> **⚠️ Non-Stick Safety — Temperature Limits**
>
> PTFE (Teflon) coating begins to degrade at approximately **260°C (500°F)**. Above this temperature:
> - The coating breaks down and can release toxic fumes (polymer fume fever in humans; can be fatal to pet birds)
> - The coating may flake or chip, creating a **physical contamination hazard**
>
> **Non-negotiable rules for non-stick:**
> - **Never heat an empty non-stick pan** — an empty non-stick pan on a medium-high burner can reach 260°C in as little as 2–3 minutes
> - **Never use on high heat** — use low to medium heat only
> - **Never use metal utensils** — damages the coating (see utensil selection)
> - **Never place in a commercial dishwasher** — high heat, alkaline detergent, and mechanical action strip the coating
>
> **PFOA note:** PFOA (perfluorooctanoic acid), a chemical once used in the manufacture of Teflon, was phased out by major manufacturers by 2013 due to health concerns. Modern PTFE cookware is manufactured without PFOA. If you have very old non-stick cookware, consider replacing it.

**Signs a non-stick pan must be replaced:**
- Visible flaking or peeling of the coating (physical contamination hazard — metal fragments in food)
- Deep scratches exposing the base metal
- Bubbling or blistering of the coating
- Pan overheated to the point where the coating has discoloured significantly

---

### Cast Iron

**Properties:** Excellent heat retention; poor thermal conductivity (slow to heat); naturally non-stick when properly seasoned; reactive (rusts); heavy.

**Why cast iron is valued in professional cooking:**
- **Superior heat retention:** Once hot, cast iron maintains its temperature even when cold food is added — critical for achieving a proper sear on protein (the Maillard reaction requires sustained high temperature at the food surface; a pan that drops temperature when food is added cannot maintain a proper sear)
- **From stovetop to oven:** Cast iron is entirely oven-safe to any temperature — moves seamlessly from searing on the range to finishing in the oven
- **Durability:** A properly maintained cast iron pan lasts generations

**Reactivity:**
- Cast iron reacts with acidic foods (tomatoes, wine, citrus) — prolonged contact can leach iron into food (affecting flavour and in high amounts can be a health concern for people sensitive to excess iron) and will strip the seasoning
- Use enamelled cast iron (cast iron coated in glass-like enamel) for acidic braises and preparations that would be damaged by bare iron

**Enamelled cast iron (Le Creuset, Staub, etc.):**
- Non-reactive; no seasoning required; can be washed normally
- Cannot be used at very high temperatures (enamel can crack)
- Heavy; expensive

**Seasoning cast iron — procedure:**
Seasoning is the process of building a polymerized fat layer on the surface of bare cast iron that creates a natural non-stick finish and prevents rust.

1. Wash with warm water and mild soap; rinse and dry completely (heat briefly on the stove to ensure all moisture evaporates)
2. Apply a very thin, even coating of high-smoke-point oil (flaxseed, refined coconut, or vegetable shortening) to all surfaces, including the exterior
3. Wipe off virtually all the oil — too much oil becomes sticky and gummy, not smooth
4. Place upside down in an oven preheated to 230–250°C; place foil on the rack below to catch any drips
5. Bake for 1 hour; turn off oven; allow to cool in the oven
6. Repeat the process 3–5 times to build a good initial seasoning
7. Over time with regular use, the seasoning improves naturally

**Maintaining seasoning:**
- After each use: wash with warm water and a brush (no soap if possible, or minimal mild soap); dry immediately on the stove over heat; apply a thin oil coat while still warm; store
- Never soak in water — rust develops within hours
- Rust is not permanent: sand with steel wool, re-season from scratch

---

### Ceramic and Stoneware

**Properties:** Non-reactive; very poor heat conductor; good heat retention in the oven; thermal shock risk.

**Ceramic:**
- Typically refers to glazed ceramic cookware (clay fired at high temperature; coated with glass glaze)
- Non-reactive; the glaze surface does not interact with acids or alkalis
- Cannot go from very cold to very hot quickly — **thermal shock** (sudden, extreme temperature change) can crack or shatter ceramic cookware
- Not suitable for stovetop direct flame; primarily oven use

**Stoneware:**
- Denser and thicker than ceramic; fired at higher temperature
- Excellent for slow, even baking — bread, casseroles, soufflés
- Also subject to thermal shock
- The thick walls moderate temperature swings — products bake more evenly

**Thermal shock prevention:**
- Do not place a cold ceramic or stoneware dish into a preheated oven — bring the dish to room temperature first
- Do not place a hot ceramic dish on a cold surface (cold wet towel, refrigerator shelf)
- Do not add cold liquid to a very hot ceramic vessel

**Applications:**
- Soufflé dishes (ramekins)
- Gratin dishes (oven baking)
- Tagines (Moroccan braising vessel — stoneware)
- Baking stones (for bread and pizza — distributes heat from the oven deck)

## Utensils — Types, Characteristics, and Applications

### Wooden Spoons

**Properties:** Insulates heat (wood does not conduct heat — handle stays cool); gentle on pan surfaces; does not melt; porous.

**Applications:**
- Stirring hot preparations — the handle stays cool (unlike metal)
- Sautéing and sauce-making in non-stick or seasoned cast iron (wood does not scratch)
- Stirring bread dough and stiff batters

**Limitation — porosity and hygiene:**
- Wood is porous — it absorbs liquids, fats, and microbial contamination
- A wooden spoon used in raw meat should NOT be used in a sauce without washing and sanitizing — bacterial contamination can survive in the pores of the wood
- Wood is not appropriate for dishwasher cleaning — the heat and moisture warp, crack, and degrade the wood
- Replace wooden spoons when they develop deep cracks or grooves (these cannot be adequately cleaned)
- Do not use with highly acidic preparations (acids stain and degrade wood over time)

**Cross-contamination protocol:**
In professional kitchens, designate separate wooden spoons for different stations or food categories — do not use one wooden spoon throughout the kitchen across raw proteins, vegetables, and finished sauces.

---

### Whisks

**Types and applications:**

| Whisk Type | Shape | Best Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Balloon whisk** | Large, round, many wires | Incorporating air (whipped cream, egg whites, meringue) |
| **French (narrow) whisk** | Narrow, elongated | Sauces; liquids in saucepans; getting into corners |
| **Roux whisk / flat whisk** | Flat bottom, minimal wires | Roux and béchamel in saucepans — reaches flat pan bottom |
| **Ball whisk** | Individual balls on each wire end | Quick emulsification; small-batch work |
| **Piano wire whisk** | Extra-fine wires; balloon shape | Delicate foams; pastry cream; smooth finishing |

**Why wire number and gauge matter:**
- More wires = more air incorporation per stroke (balloon whisk)
- Fewer, thicker wires = more mechanical force with less air (roux whisk)
- Finer wires = smoother texture in finished preparations (piano wire)

**Material:**
- Stainless steel (most common) — durable; dishwasher safe; appropriate for most surfaces
- Silicone-coated (for non-stick surfaces) — the silicone jacket prevents scratching; less efficient at incorporation

---

### Spatulas

**Types and applications:**

| Type | Material | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Offset / palette knife** | Stainless steel blade; wood or plastic handle | Spreading icings, lifting delicate items, plating |
| **Rubber/silicone scraper** | Silicone | Scraping bowls; folding; heat-resistant silicone for hot preparations |
| **Fish spatula / turner (slotted)** | Thin, flexible stainless steel | Lifting and turning delicate fish fillets and fried items |
| **Bench scraper** | Stainless steel or plastic | Cutting dough; scraping cutting boards; portioning; cleaning the bench |
| **High-heat spatula** | Silicone with stainless steel core | Non-stick pans at moderate heat; flipping items in non-stick |

**Why flexible spatulas matter for non-stick:**
- A rigid metal spatula applied to a non-stick surface with force damages the coating
- A flexible fish spatula can be angled under a delicate fish fillet without force — the flexibility allows it to slide under even while lying flat on the pan surface

---

### Scissors / Kitchen Shears

**Applications in professional cooking:**
- Trimming herbs directly into a pan or bowl (faster than knife for chives, scallions, parsley)
- Cutting pizza into portions (faster and cleaner than a rocking knife on pizza)
- Trimming excess fat or silverskin from poultry (especially in areas where a knife is awkward)
- Cutting dried seaweed, noodles, or other long preparations
- Opening vacuum-sealed bags

**Safety:**
- Keep sharp — dull scissors require excessive force and slip more easily
- Dedicated kitchen scissors — do not use the same scissors for food and non-food tasks (packaging, plastic strapping)
- Sanitize between different food uses (especially between raw and cooked/RTE)

---

### Tongs

**Properties:** Spring-loaded mechanical grip; extends the cook's reach into hot areas; replaces bare-hand contact with hot food.

**Types:**
- **Short tongs (15–20 cm):** Fine work; plating; small items
- **Long tongs (30+ cm):** Grill work; fryer; deep pots; keeping hands away from heat

**Applications:**
- Turning grilling or sautéing protein
- Retrieving items from deep fryers or boiling water
- Plating and garnish placement (short tongs)
- Tossing salads and pasta

**Cross-contamination:**
- Tongs that have been used for raw protein must be cleaned and sanitized before contact with cooked or RTE food
- In high-volume service, use dedicated tongs per station/food category and mark them (colour-coded tape on handles or colour-coded tongs)

---

### Tweezers (Plating Tweezers)

**Applications:**
- Precision garnish placement (micro-herbs, edible flowers, fine zest)
- Removing pin bones from fish fillets (bone tweezers have flat, wide tips for grip)
- Fine work on small, delicate items that tongs would crush

**Why tweezers matter for food safety:**
- Tweezers eliminate bare-hand contact with ready-to-eat food on finished plates
- Under most provincial food premises regulations, no bare-hand contact with RTE food is required — tweezers are the professional tool for achieving this at the plating stage

## Utilization Procedures

### Correct Placement on Stovetop

**Centring the pan over the burner:**
- The pan bottom should be centred over the burner — not hanging over the edge
- A pan hanging significantly over a burner edge is unstable; the handle creates a lever that makes the pan tip
- On a gas range: the flame should be contained under the pan; flame licking up the sides wastes energy and can damage the handle

**Handle positioning:**
- Handles must be angled away from the front of the range — a handle pointing directly outward is at waist/hip height and can be caught by a passing body or arm, causing the pan to fall
- Handles should not be positioned over another burner — the heat from the burner below will make the handle dangerously hot
- Standard positioning: angle the handle to the side (10 o'clock or 2 o'clock position when viewed from above)

**Call out when moving hot pans:**
- "Hot behind!" or "Hot coming through!" — a standard kitchen call that warns others before moving hot pans
- Always use dry oven cloths — wet cloths conduct heat and cause steam burns

---

### Correct Placement in the Oven

**Pan placement for even heat:**
- Place pans in the centre of the oven rack — away from the oven walls and elements
- Do not allow pans to touch each other or the oven walls — blocked air circulation creates uneven cooking
- In a convection oven, stagger pans on multiple racks (not directly above each other) to allow air circulation around each pan

**Rack position matters:**
- Upper rack (closer to top element): more top-down radiant heat; better for browning and glazing
- Middle rack: most even temperature; standard position for most baking and roasting
- Lower rack (closer to bottom element): more bottom heat; better for pie shells, bread crusts, and items needing bottom browning

**Using the right pan size for the oven:**
- Pans that are too large for the oven (nearly touching the walls) create poor air circulation
- Oversized pans in convection ovens block the fan's airflow patterns, reducing the efficiency advantage of convection

## B-3.02.03P — Inspect Pots, Pans, and Utensils

**Evidence of Attainment:** Pots, pans, and utensils are inspected according to company policies and jurisdictional regulations.

---

## Inspection — What to Check and Why

**Pre-use inspection is a food safety and worker safety requirement:**

| Component | What to Inspect | Hazard if Defective |
|---|---|---|
| **Handles (riveted or welded)** | Tight, not loose; no wobbling; rivets intact | A loose handle causes the pan to drop when lifted — severe burn and spill hazard |
| **Non-stick coating** | No flaking, peeling, bubbling, or deep scratches | Flaked coating = physical contamination hazard (PTFE particles in food) |
| **Pan bottom** | Flat, not warped | Warped pans rock on burners and tip; uneven heat contact |
| **Ceramic/stoneware** | No cracks, chips, or crazing | Structural failure risk; cracks harbour bacteria; chips create foreign body hazard |
| **Wooden utensils** | No cracks, splits, or excessive grooves | Cracks harbour bacteria that survive cleaning |
| **Whisk wires** | No broken, bent, or loose wires | Broken wire = metal foreign body in food |
| **Rubber/silicone spatulas** | No tears, cuts, or missing pieces | Torn silicone = foreign body hazard |
| **Metal utensils** | No sharp burrs, bends, or broken pieces | Metal burrs cause cuts; broken metal = foreign body |
| **Tong mechanism** | Spring functions; tips align properly | Misaligned tips crush or fail to grip food |
| **Tin-lined copper** | No exposed bare copper (worn tin) | Exposed copper is reactive and toxic with acidic food |
| **Cast iron seasoning** | No rust patches; no loss of seasoning | Rust indicates moisture damage; unseasoned cast iron is reactive |

**When to remove from service:**
- Any pan with a loose or damaged handle
- Any non-stick pan with visible coating damage (flaking, peeling, deep scratches)
- Any utensil with broken or missing pieces
- Any ceramic or stoneware item with cracks or chips
- Any item where food safety or worker safety is at risk

> **Regulatory Requirement:** Under provincial OH&S legislation, damaged equipment that creates a hazard must be removed from service and repaired or replaced before use. Under provincial food premises regulations, equipment with damaged food-contact surfaces that cannot be effectively cleaned must be removed from service.

## B-3.02.04P — Wash, Sanitize, and Dry Pots, Pans, and Utensils

**Evidence of Attainment:** Pots, pans, and utensils are washed, sanitized, and dried according to jurisdictional regulations.

---

## Washing, Sanitizing, and Drying — Material-Specific Procedures

**Universal 6-step sequence (from B-3.03):**
1. Scrape/remove food debris
2. Pre-rinse
3. Wash with hot water and approved detergent
4. Rinse thoroughly to remove all detergent
5. Sanitize with approved sanitizer at correct concentration
6. Air dry

**Material-specific considerations and special treatments:**

| Material | Washing Method | Sanitizing | Drying | Special Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Stainless steel (uncoated)** | Dishwasher or hand wash | Standard chemical or heat sanitize | Air dry | Rainbow discoloration is cosmetic — use SS cleaner if desired |
| **Aluminium (bare)** | Hand wash (dishwasher causes oxidation) | Standard chemical | Air dry | Do not soak in alkaline solutions |
| **Aluminium (anodized)** | Hand wash preferred | Standard chemical | Air dry | Dishwasher will degrade anodization over time |
| **Non-stick (PTFE)** | Hand wash only; warm water; soft cloth | Low-concentration chemical sanitizer | Air dry | No dishwasher; no abrasive scourers; no metal utensils at any stage |
| **Cast iron (bare)** | Minimal water; rinse; rarely use soap | Heat sanitize (stovetop heating) | Heat dry immediately on stove; re-oil while warm | Never soak; never dishwasher; re-season if rust develops |
| **Enamelled cast iron** | Hand wash; mild soap; soft cloth | Standard chemical | Air dry | No abrasive scourers; avoid temperature shock |
| **Copper (tin-lined)** | Hand wash; soft cloth | Standard chemical | Air dry; dry thoroughly to prevent tarnish | Never use abrasives on tin lining; re-tin when worn |
| **Copper (SS-lined)** | Hand wash or dishwasher (interior) | Standard | Air dry | Clean exterior copper with copper polish |
| **Ceramic/stoneware** | Hand wash or gentle dishwasher | Standard chemical | Air dry | Avoid thermal shock during washing (match water temp to dish temp) |
| **Wooden spoons** | Hand wash; hot water; mild soap | Not effective after deep penetration — replace worn items | Air dry; not in dishwasher | Do not soak; dry standing up to avoid wood-end contact with moisture |

---

### Three-Compartment Sink Procedure (Commercial Standard)

Commercial kitchens typically wash pots and pans in a three-compartment sink:

| Compartment | Contents | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Sink 1** | Hot water (minimum 43°C) + detergent | Washing — removes soil and food debris |
| **Sink 2** | Clean hot water | Rinsing — removes all detergent residue |
| **Sink 3** | Sanitizing solution (correct concentration) OR hot water (≥77°C) | Sanitizing — kills remaining microorganisms |

**After the three-compartment sink:**
- Items air dry on a clean drying rack — not towel dried
- Do not stack wet items immediately — the bottoms of stacked items trap moisture and cannot dry

**Commercial dishwasher (pot/rack washer):**
- High-temperature dishwashers use hot water (final rinse ≥82°C) to sanitize — this is the approved heat sanitization method
- Chemical dishwashers use lower temperatures with a chemical sanitizing rinse
- Operators must verify that the final rinse temperature or chemical concentration meets the machine manufacturer's specification AND provincial regulatory standards

---

### Dry Heat Sanitization for Cast Iron

Cast iron cannot undergo standard chemical sanitization without damaging the seasoning. The alternative:
1. Rinse briefly with hot water
2. Return to the stovetop over medium-high heat
3. Heat until all visible moisture evaporates (pan becomes completely dry)
4. The high heat of the stovetop (far exceeding the 77°C threshold for heat sanitization) effectively reduces microbial counts
5. Apply a thin oil coat while still warm; allow to cool; store

## B-3.02.05P — Store Pots, Pans, and Utensils

**Evidence of Attainment:** Pots, pans, and utensils are stored to maximize efficiency, and to prevent damage and unsanitary conditions.

---

## Storage Principles and Methods

**Clean before storing — always:**
Food residue left on pots and pans during storage provides nutrition for bacteria, attracts pests, becomes increasingly difficult to remove (polymerizes and carbonizes), and can contaminate food that is subsequently prepared in the same vessel.

**Non-stick pans — stacking protection:**
- Never stack non-stick pans directly against each other — the bottom of the upper pan scratches the cooking surface of the lower pan
- Use **pan protectors** (soft felt or cloth discs placed between pans) when nesting non-stick cookware
- Alternatively, hang non-stick pans from hooks rather than stacking

**Heavy pots (cast iron, large stock pots):**
- Store on the lowest shelves — heavy items stored high are a falling hazard
- Cast iron: store in a dry area; a slight residual oil coat prevents rust during storage; do not cover tightly (traps moisture)

**Hanging storage:**
- Overhead pot racks (ceiling-mounted) or wall-mounted rails are efficient for frequently used pots and pans
- Items hang by their handles — accessible without reaching into a deep cabinet; allows air circulation so items dry fully
- Ensure rack is rated for the weight of the items hung; check mounting hardware integrity regularly

**Utensil storage:**
- Standing containers (ceramic crocks, utensil holders): keep frequently used utensils accessible; ensure containers are cleaned regularly — debris accumulates at the bottom
- Drawer storage: organizers prevent utensils from piling; flat items (spatulas, whisks) should not be piled under heavy items
- Knife rolls vs. common utensil holders: separate by food category where cross-contamination is a concern

**Storage location — away from contamination sources:**
- Do not store pots, pans, or utensils under leaking pipes or open shelving where they could collect dust, moisture, or debris
- Cover clean items on shelves that are not used frequently — keep dust and grease out
- Do not store near the floor — items stored on or near the floor are subject to pest contamination and water damage during floor cleaning

## B-3.02.07P — Control Heat

**Evidence of Attainment:** Heat is controlled to prevent damage to pots, pans, and utensils.

---

## Heat Control — Science, Safety, and Equipment Longevity

### Thermal Shock — What It Is and How to Avoid It

**Thermal shock** occurs when a material experiences a rapid, extreme change in temperature. Most cookware materials expand when heated and contract when cooled. When this happens too quickly or unevenly, internal stress cracks the material.

**Most vulnerable materials:**

| Material | Thermal Shock Risk | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Ceramic** | Very high | Never place cold ceramic in a hot oven; never place hot ceramic on cold/wet surface |
| **Stoneware** | High | Same precautions as ceramic |
| **Enamelled cast iron** | Moderate | Avoid extreme temperature swings; do not place under cold running water while hot |
| **Glass (Pyrex-type)** | High | Same as ceramic |
| **Bare cast iron** | Low (cast iron itself is tough) | Rapid cooling can still cause cracking in older, brittle cast iron |
| **Stainless steel** | Very low | Excellent thermal shock resistance |
| **Aluminium** | Low | Soft metal absorbs stress through deformation rather than cracking |
| **Copper** | Very low | Excellent malleability |

**Thermal shock in practice:**
- Taking a ceramic gratin dish from the freezer and placing it directly in a hot oven → crack
- Placing a hot Pyrex measuring cup on a cold wet towel → crack
- Pouring cold water into a very hot cast iron pan → possible crack in old/brittle iron; definite warping of thin pans

---

### Dry Heating — Empty Pan Hazards

**Heating an empty pan has different consequences depending on the material:**

| Material | Consequence of Dry Heating | Safe? |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-stick (PTFE)** | Coating reaches critical temperature (260°C) quickly; begins to degrade; releases fumes | **Never heat empty** |
| **Stainless steel** | Discoloration (cosmetic); pan becomes very hot | Technically safe to the pan; creates burn hazard when handling |
| **Cast iron** | Heats slowly; maintains heat well; used deliberately to preheat before searing | Acceptable with care; very hot surface when food is added |
| **Copper** | Heats rapidly; tin lining can melt if pan is dry and on high heat for extended time | Avoid prolonged dry heating |
| **Ceramic/stoneware** | Thermal stress; may crack | Never use on direct flame; oven use only |

**Why preheating a pan correctly matters for food safety:**
- Searing protein requires a very hot surface (>150°C for Maillard reactions to begin; >175°C for good browning speed)
- A properly preheated stainless steel or cast iron pan produces a clean sear — the high surface temperature dries the food surface instantly and initiates browning before the protein can steam
- An under-heated pan causes the food to stick and steam — steam cooking at insufficient temperature may not achieve adequate surface temperatures for safe food handling in all contexts
- For non-stick: preheat on medium, add fat immediately after preheat, then add food

---

### Managing Heat During Use — Practical Techniques

**Heat zones on gas burners:**
- Gas allows immediate heat adjustment — reducing the flame reduces heat input immediately
- When bringing a liquid to a boil, use high heat; reduce to low/simmer for sustained cooking

**Managing heat in cast iron:**
- Cast iron has excellent heat retention but poor heat response — start on medium heat and allow 3–5 minutes to fully preheat before adding food
- Reduce heat earlier than you think you need to — the pan will stay hot long after the burner is reduced
- Adding cold food to a properly preheated cast iron pan will temporarily drop the surface temperature — this is normal; do not increase heat immediately or the outside of the food will burn before the interior cooks

**Flare-ups on gas range:**
- Fat dripping onto an open gas flame creates a flare-up — momentary flash of flame
- Keep a pan lid nearby when working with fat — a lid placed over the pan smothers the flare
- Never use water on a fat flare-up
- If a pan fire occurs: cover with a lid to smother; move to a cool burner; call for help if not immediately controllable

### WHMIS

Cleaning chemicals used for pots, pans, and utensils — detergents, degreasers, sanitizers, specialty cleaners (copper polish, oven cleaner) — are WHMIS-regulated:
- SDS must be accessible for every chemical
- Workers must be trained on the chemicals they use

Never mix bleach-based sanitizers with ammonia-based cleaners → chloramine gas (toxic)
Never mix bleach with acid-based cleaners → chlorine gas (toxic)

### HACCP

**Equipment as a contamination source:**
- Pots, pans, and utensils are food-contact surfaces — their cleanliness and condition directly affect the safety of food produced in them
- A cracked wooden spoon, a pan with flaking non-stick coating, or a whisk with a broken wire can all cause **physical contamination** — a HACCP-identified hazard category
- Cleaning and sanitation of equipment is a **Prerequisite Program (PRP)** in any HACCP plan
- Inspection of equipment condition is a **GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice)** requirement

**Cross-contamination via utensils:**
- Utensil cross-contamination between raw protein and RTE food is a critical biological hazard
- HACCP plans may specify colour-coded utensils (same colour-coding principle as cutting boards) for high-allergen or high-risk proteins

### OH&S

**Physical hazards from pots, pans, and utensils:**
- Loose handles on pots and pans: a worker carrying a pan of hot soup with a failing handle is at immediate risk of a severe burn — inspect before use; report loose handles immediately
- Burns from hot surfaces: dry oven cloths required; call "hot" when moving hot pans
- Cuts from metal utensils, broken whisk wires, or damaged utensil edges
- Musculoskeletal strain from handling heavy cast iron or large stock pots: use proper lifting technique; seek assistance for heavy loads

**The right to refuse unsafe equipment:**
Provincial OH&S legislation gives workers the right to refuse to use equipment they reasonably believe is unsafe (e.g., a pot with a visibly loose handle).

---

### Public Health Regulations

- Pots, pans, and utensils that contact food must have **smooth, non-porous, cleanable surfaces in good repair**
- Damaged surfaces (cracked ceramic, flaking non-stick, warped, rusted) cannot be effectively cleaned and sanitized — must be removed from service
- Cleaning and sanitizing must meet provincial regulatory standards (concentration, temperature, contact time)
- Equipment must be stored in a manner that protects it from contamination

## 12 Q&A Pairs — Red Seal Exam Preparation

**Q1: Why is stainless steel cookware almost always constructed with a copper or aluminium core, and what is the consequence of using single-ply stainless steel?**
A: Stainless steel has very poor thermal conductivity (~16 W/m·K), meaning it does not spread heat evenly across the pan bottom. Single-ply stainless steel forms severe hot spots directly over the burner flame while the edges remain cool — food burns in the centre and remains uncooked at the edges. An aluminium or copper core bonded between stainless steel layers (clad construction) uses the superior conductivity of those metals to spread heat evenly across the entire cooking surface while the stainless steel provides the non-reactive, durable food-contact surface.

**Q2: Why must non-stick (PTFE) pans never be heated empty, and what temperature is the critical threshold?**
A: An empty non-stick pan on a medium-high burner can reach 260°C within 2–3 minutes. Above approximately 260°C, PTFE (Teflon) coating begins to degrade and can release toxic polymer fumes. At higher temperatures, the coating may blister, peel, and flake — creating a physical contamination hazard in the food. The fumes released from severely overheated PTFE can cause polymer fume fever in humans and are fatal to pet birds. The rule is absolute: never heat an empty non-stick pan; always preheat on medium with fat added promptly.

**Q3: What is thermal shock, which materials are most vulnerable, and how is it prevented?**
A: Thermal shock occurs when a material experiences a rapid, extreme temperature change that causes differential expansion or contraction within the material, producing internal stress and cracking. Most vulnerable: ceramic, stoneware, glass, and enamelled cast iron. Prevention: never place a cold ceramic dish directly into a hot oven — bring to room temperature first; never place a hot ceramic item on a cold or wet surface; never pour cold liquid into a hot ceramic vessel; do not rinse cold water over hot glass. Stainless steel, aluminium, and copper are minimally vulnerable due to their ductility and high thermal shock resistance.

**Q4: Why does cast iron have superior heat retention compared to copper, even though copper is the better heat conductor?**
A: These are two different properties. **Thermal conductivity** measures how quickly heat moves through a material — copper is far superior. **Heat retention (thermal mass)** measures how much heat energy a material stores relative to its mass and how slowly it releases it — cast iron's density and specific heat capacity give it excellent retention. A cast iron pan, once thoroughly preheated, holds its temperature even when cold food is added — critical for searing protein (the Maillard reaction requires sustained high surface temperature). Copper heats instantly but cools quickly when food is added, reducing the searing effectiveness.

**Q5: What are the correct utensils for a non-stick pan, and what materials must always be avoided?**
A: Safe utensils for non-stick pans: hardwood, bamboo, high-heat resistant silicone, and non-stick coated (nylon) utensils. These materials are softer than or compatible with the PTFE coating. Never use: any metal utensil (stainless steel, carbon steel, cast iron — any metal scratches the coating), wire whisks, or rough or splintered wooden utensils. Metal utensils scratch through the PTFE coating, exposing the base metal and creating a physical contamination hazard from coating particles entering the food.

**Q6: Why must cast iron never be soaked in water, and what is the correct cleaning and storage procedure?**
A: Cast iron is porous and rusts when water is absorbed. Even a few hours of soaking can produce rust patches that strip the seasoning and contaminate food. Correct procedure: rinse briefly with hot water and a brush (minimal soap if needed); return to the stovetop and heat until completely dry; apply a very thin coat of oil while still warm; store uncovered in a dry location. The dry heat step is the critical difference from all other cookware — it ensures no moisture remains trapped in the porous iron surface.

**Q7: What is the food safety concern with cooking acidic foods in an unlined aluminium pot for extended periods?**
A: Aluminium is a reactive metal that reacts with acids (tomatoes, wine, citrus juice, vinegar). Extended contact with highly acidic food causes aluminium to leach into the food, producing a metallic off-flavour and discolouring light-coloured foods (eggs, white sauce, light soups). While the amounts transferred in typical cooking are small, Health Canada has not issued restrictions — the primary concern in a professional kitchen is the quality impact (metallic flavour), not a documented acute health risk. The solution is to use stainless-lined, enamelled, or anodized cookware for all acidic preparations.

**Q8: What does the 6-step cleaning and sanitizing sequence require, and why must cleaning always precede sanitizing?**
A: The 6 steps: (1) Scrape/remove food debris, (2) Pre-rinse, (3) Wash with hot water and approved detergent, (4) Rinse thoroughly to remove all detergent, (5) Sanitize at correct concentration and contact time, (6) Air dry. Cleaning must precede sanitizing because food debris and organic matter physically shield bacteria from chemical sanitizers. Organic matter also reacts with sanitizer chemicals, depleting their concentration and neutralizing their effectiveness. A surface with food residue that is "sanitized" is not actually sanitized — the pathogen load under the organic matter remains.

**Q9: Describe the three-compartment sink procedure for commercial pot and utensil washing.**
A: Compartment 1: hot water (minimum 43°C) with approved detergent — washing removes soil and food debris. Compartment 2: clean hot water — rinsing removes all detergent residue (detergent residue inhibits sanitizers and can contaminate food). Compartment 3: approved sanitizing solution at correct concentration, or hot water at ≥77°C for heat sanitization. After the three-compartment sink, items are air-dried on a clean rack — never towel dried, as cloths can re-contaminate sanitized surfaces.

**Q10: What inspection criteria determine whether a pot or pan should be removed from service?**
A: Remove from service when: the handle is loose or shows any movement (severe burn and spill hazard when carried with a hot load); non-stick coating is flaking, peeling, bubbling, or has deep scratches (physical contamination from coating particles); the pan bottom is warped (tips on the burner; uneven heat contact); ceramic or stoneware has cracks or chips (structural failure risk; cracks harbour bacteria that cannot be cleaned); tin lining on copper is worn through exposing bare copper (reactive to acidic food); whisk has broken wires (metal foreign body). The governing principle: any damage that creates a worker safety hazard or a food contamination risk requires immediate removal from service.

**Q11: Why does the tall, narrow shape of a stock pot serve an important practical purpose, and what food safety implication does this shape create during cooling?**
A: The tall, narrow shape minimizes the liquid surface area at the top, reducing the rate of evaporation relative to the volume — critical during 6–8 hour stock preparation where excessive evaporation would require constant water addition and alter the concentration. The same shape, however, creates a food safety risk during cooling: the low surface-area-to-volume ratio means heat is released very slowly. A large, full stock pot may take many hours to cool to safe temperature in a refrigerator — far exceeding the regulatory two-stage cooling requirement (60°C → 20°C within 2 hours; 20°C → 4°C within 4 hours). Large stocks must be divided into shallow containers, ice-bathed, or blast-chilled to achieve safe cooling rates.

**Q12: What are the four main considerations when selecting a cooking pan for a specific recipe or preparation?**
A: (1) **Material reactivity:** Does the preparation contain acidic or alkaline ingredients that would react with the pan material? Choose non-reactive stainless, enamelled, or ceramic cookware for acidic preparations. (2) **Heat requirements:** Does the preparation need precise, rapid heat control (copper or aluminium-core), or sustained high heat retention for searing (cast iron)? (3) **Oven compatibility:** Does the preparation move from stovetop to oven? Ensure the pan and all handles are rated for the required oven temperature. (4) **Volume and surface area:** Is the pan the right size? A pan too small crowds the food (steams instead of browns); a pan too large causes drippings to burn at the edges and sauce to evaporate too quickly.
