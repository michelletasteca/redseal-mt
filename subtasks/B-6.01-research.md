# B-6.01 | Orders Products — Enhanced Learning Resource

**Major Work Activity:** B — Common Occupational Skills
**Task:** B-6 | Manages Products

> **What this subtask is about:** Product ordering is the first link in the kitchen's supply chain — and like all first links, a failure here cascades through everything downstream. Ordering the wrong product, in the wrong quantity, from the wrong supplier, at the wrong time produces kitchens that run out mid-service, pay too much for ingredients, receive substandard product that fails quality checks, or take food safety shortcuts when deliveries don't arrive as planned. This document enriches the Red Seal competency with the product knowledge, purchasing science, Canadian grading standards, and operational reasoning an apprentice needs to order products with professional precision.

---

## Why Ordering Is a Food Safety Function — Not Just a Procurement Task

Product ordering directly shapes food safety outcomes in ways that are not always obvious:

- **Specification failures** at ordering produce receiving failures — product accepted that should have been rejected
- **Quantity miscalculation** creates inventory pressure: too little forces emergency substitutions and potentially room-temperature thawing of frozen proteins; too much creates spoilage and FIFO failures
- **Supplier selection** determines whether product arrives with traceability documentation (shellfish tags, lot codes), from approved and inspected facilities, and in temperature-compliant delivery conditions
- **Market form selection** affects how long a product spends in preparation (more time in the temperature danger zone during prep), and how accurately yield and portion cost can be controlled

The purchasing decision made by a cook or chef is a food safety decision made 24–72 hours before the product appears on a plate.

#### Deep Dive: Product Specifications

A **purchase specification** (purchase spec) is a written or formalized standard that precisely defines what a product must be for the operation to accept it. It is the operational translation of a menu decision into a procurement requirement.

**Why written specifications exist:**
- Protect against supplier substitution (being sent a lower-grade or different product without notification)
- Ensure yield and cost calculations remain valid (a 70% yield spec for beef tenderloin is only accurate if the exact spec is consistently delivered)
- Create consistency across deliveries and over time (critical for standardized recipes)
- Provide objective grounds for accepting or rejecting a delivery
- Establish the basis for competitive bidding between multiple suppliers

**The Components of a Product Specification**

| Specification Element | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Product type / name** | Precise product identity | "Atlantic salmon, fresh, farmed" not just "salmon" |
| **Market form** | How the product is processed or packaged | Skin-on fillet; boneless/skinless; whole/round; IQF; etc. |
| **Grade / quality standard** | Government or industry grade designation | Canada AAA beef; Canada Grade A poultry; Canada No. 1 produce |
| **Weight / size range** | Portion or unit weight range | "6–8 oz (170–225g) portions"; "Case of 10–12 oz chicken breasts" |
| **Case size / pack** | Total case quantity and unit configuration | "4 × 5 kg"; "6 × 1L"; "1 × 20 kg" |
| **Colour** | Expected colour at point of use | "Bright red flesh, no browning"; "deep orange yolk" |
| **Fat content / trim** | For proteins and dairy | "Maximum 1 cm fat cap, trimmed"; "35% whipping cream" |
| **Shelf life requirement** | Minimum days of usable shelf life remaining at time of delivery | "Minimum 5 days remaining shelf life at delivery" |
| **Packaging** | Type of packaging and integrity requirements | "Vacuum-packed; no punctures; label intact" |
| **Temperature at delivery** | Maximum acceptable delivery temperature | "Refrigerated ≤4°C; frozen ≤-18°C at time of delivery" |
| **Country of origin** | Where the product was grown, raised, or caught | Required for certain products under Canadian labelling regulations |
| **Certifications** | Third-party quality or sustainability certifications | MSC-certified; organic; Ocean Wise recommended |
| **Allergen / dietary flags** | Any dietary compliance requirements | "Gluten-free certified"; "Halal-certified" |

#### Product Specifications by Category

**Dairy**

| Market Form | Product Description | Key Specifications |
|---|---|---|
| Fluid milk | Whole (3.25% MF), 2%, 1%, skim | Fat content %; pasteurized; best-before date |
| Half-and-half / coffee cream | 10–12% milk fat (MF) | MF%; ultra-pasteurized increases shelf life |
| Table cream | 18% MF | MF%; pasteurized vs. ultra-pasteurized |
| Whipping cream | 33–35% MF (light) | MF% critical for whipping performance |
| Heavy / manufacturing cream | 36–40% MF | Higher fat = better stability in cooked applications |
| Butter | Salted or unsalted; cultured or sweet cream | Salt content matters in baking; fat content ≥80% MF required by Canadian standards |
| Hard cheese | Aged; variety-specific | Age (months); moisture content; rind type |
| Soft cheese | Fresh; bloomy rind; washed rind | Days to expiry; refrigerated throughout supply chain |

> **Canadian Regulatory Context:** Pasteurization of milk and cream for retail sale is required under the *Food and Drug Regulations* administered by Health Canada. Most cheese sold in Canada must be made from pasteurized milk or aged a minimum of 60 days if made from raw milk — verify requirements with your provincial dairy regulator.

**Produce**

| Market Form | Characteristics | When to Specify |
|---|---|---|
| Fresh whole | Highest quality; requires full prep labour | Best flavour; appropriate when time and skill allow |
| Minimally processed (pre-cut, washed) | Reduced prep time; higher cost per kg | High-volume operations with labour constraints |
| IQF frozen | Flash-frozen at peak ripeness; consistent quality | Off-season products; high-volume soups, sauces |
| Canned | Heat-processed; longest shelf life | Tomatoes, legumes, stocks — quality appropriate for cooked applications |
| Dried | Dehydrated; very long shelf life | Mushrooms, herbs, fruits, legumes |
| Purée / paste | Processed and concentrated | Tomato paste, fruit coulis, herb purées |

**Canadian Produce Grades:** The Canadian Horticultural Council and CFIA administer produce grading for many fruits and vegetables:
- **Canada No. 1** — the standard for food service; good quality, consistent sizing, free of defects
- **Canada No. 2** — slight defects permitted; suitable for processed applications (soups, stocks, purees) where appearance is less critical
- **Canada No. 3** — processing grade; rarely specified for food service

Specifying the grade prevents receiving No. 2 product when No. 1 was expected and paid for.

**Meats**

Canada's beef grading system, administered by the CFIA, is one of the most detailed in the world:

| Grade | Marbling | Typical Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Canada Prime** | Most abundant marbling; highest quality | Fine dining; premium steakhouses |
| **Canada AAA** | Abundant to slightly abundant marbling | Most upscale restaurants; dry-aging candidate |
| **Canada AA** | Moderate marbling | Mid-range restaurants; general food service |
| **Canada A** | Slight marbling | Budget food service; long-cook applications (braising) |
| **Canada B1–B4** | Young animals; various quality factors | Institutional; limited use in quality kitchens |
| **Canada D1–D4** | Mature animals (>30 months) | Ground beef, processed meats |
| **Canada E** | Non-graded or defect-related | Processing only |

> **Important:** The beef grade designations (Canada Prime, AAA, etc.) refer to **quality grades** based primarily on marbling and maturity. Yield grades (how much usable meat per carcass) also exist but are less frequently specified in kitchen purchasing.

**Meat inspection:** In Canada, meat sold across provincial borders must come from a **federally inspected establishment** licensed by CFIA under the *Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR)*. Meat sold only within a province may be from a **provincially inspected** facility. Always specify federally inspected meat when regulatory compliance is required.

**Meat Market Forms (Beef Example):**

| Form | Description | Cost per kg | Yield | Labour Required |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Whole primal (e.g., full striploin) | Large section from the carcass | Lowest | ~75–85% | High — full fabrication required |
| Sub-primal (e.g., striploin, untrimmed) | Smaller section from primal | Moderate | ~80–90% | Moderate — trimming and portioning |
| Portion-cut (e.g., 225g striploin) | Pre-cut to spec by butcher | Highest | ~95–98% | Minimal — ready for cooking |
| Ground / minced | Machine-processed; specific fat % | Low | 100% | None |
| Processed / ready-to-eat | Cooked, sliced, cured, etc. | High | 90–100% | None |

> **Operational principle:** The lower the market form cost per kg, the higher the labour and skill required. A kitchen with skilled butchers may benefit from whole-primal purchasing. A kitchen with limited butchery skills or time is better served by portion cuts despite the higher per-kg cost. Total cost (ingredient + labour) is what matters.

**Poultry**

Canadian poultry grades are administered by CFIA:
- **Canada Grade A** — the quality standard for food service; symmetrical shape, well-fleshed, skin intact, no defects
- **Canada Grade Utility** — minor defects; suitable for processing applications

Poultry market forms:

| Form | Description | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| Whole bird (eviscerated, ready-to-cook) | Complete bird, neck and giblets included | Roasting; stock production |
| Split (halved) | Bird cut along the spine and keel | Grilling, roasting |
| Quartered | 2 breast quarters + 2 leg quarters | Efficient portioning; grilling, roasting |
| 8-piece cut | Thighs, drumsticks, breasts (2 pieces each), wings | Fried chicken, institutional production |
| Boneless/skinless breast | Breast only; no skin, no bone | Highest cost/kg; quickest prep; most versatile |
| Bone-in breast | Breast with rib bone; skin-on option | Roasting; higher flavour |
| Tenders | Tenderloin of the breast | Quick-cook; high labour value |
| Thighs and legs | Bone-in or boneless | Braising, confit, grinding |
| Ground poultry | Machine-processed | Burgers, meatballs, stuffings |
| Whole turkey breast | Large boneless or bone-in breast | Institutional; slicing; roasting |

**Seafood**

Seafood market forms are important to understand because they directly affect yield calculation:

| Market Form | Description | Approximate Yield |
|---|---|---|
| **Round / whole** | As caught; not processed | 45–55% (species-dependent) |
| **Drawn** | Eviscerated (guts removed); head and fins intact | 48–58% |
| **Headed and gutted (H&G)** | Head removed, eviscerated | 60–75% |
| **Pan-dressed** | H&G + fins, scales, tail removed | 65–80% |
| **Fillet (skin-on)** | Boneless side of fish; skin attached | 80–90% (pin bones remaining) |
| **Fillet (skinless)** | Boneless, skinless | 75–85% |
| **Steak** | Cross-section cut through round or dressed fish | 75–85% |
| **Portion** | Pre-cut from fillet to exact weight | 95–99% (trimmed to spec) |
| **IQF (Individually Quick Frozen)** | Fillets or portions frozen individually | Consistent weight; 0% thaw loss (if not glazed) |

> **Glaze notation:** IQF frozen seafood is sometimes coated in a water glaze (ice coating) that adds weight. A product labelled "20% glaze" means 20% of the purchased weight is ice, not fish. The true cost per kg of seafood is: (AP price) ÷ (1 − glaze %). Always ask for the glaze percentage when purchasing IQF seafood and adjust yield and cost calculations accordingly.

**Dry Goods and Sundries**

Dry goods are shelf-stable products. Specifications focus on:
- **Case size:** Common formats — 6/1L, 4/4L, 1/10 kg, 1/25 kg, etc.
- **Pack count:** Number of units per case and weight per unit
- **Product type:** Brand, processing method, country of origin where relevant
- **Shelf life remaining:** Minimum best-before date at delivery
- **Packaging integrity:** No swollen cans (botulism risk), no broken seals, no punctured bags

> **Food Safety Note — Swollen Cans:** A swollen or bulging can indicates gas production inside, which can indicate *Clostridium botulinum* growth and toxin production. *C. botulinum* produces the most lethal biological toxin known. **Never use product from a swollen, dented at the seam, or leaking can.** This is a food safety non-negotiable.

#### Deep Dive: Identifying Ingredient Requirements and Building an Order List

**The Order List as a Production Document**

An order list (purchase order) is the formal output of the ordering process. It connects:

```
Menu → Standardized Recipes → Production Schedule → Forecasted Covers
                                          │
                                          ▼
                          Required EP quantities per item
                                          │
                           Apply yield %: EP ÷ yield = AP
                                          │
                                          ▼
                          Current inventory check (on-hand stock)
                                          │
                            AP needed - on-hand = ORDER QUANTITY
```

**Accounting for Dietary Requirements in Ordering**

When ordering to accommodate dietary requirements, the specification changes:
- **Gluten-free:** Must verify product is certified gluten-free (not just "wheat-free"); facility cross-contact matters for celiac guests
- **Halal:** Must source from a halal-certified slaughter facility; pork and pork-derived products excluded; alcohol as ingredient excluded
- **Kosher:** Requires kosher-certified products; complex rules around meat and dairy separation; requires specific certification from a recognized certifying body
- **Allergen-free:** Many allergen-free products require dedicated supplier certifications and facility segregation — not just label claims
- **Vegan / plant-based:** Verify no hidden animal-derived ingredients (e.g., gelatin in some products, rennet in cheese, casein in non-dairy products)

#### Deep Dive: Quantity Determination

**The Par Stock System**

Most commercial kitchens use a **par stock (par level) system** for determining order quantities. Par level is the minimum quantity of a product that must be on hand at all times to meet production needs between deliveries.

```
Par Level Calculation:
Par = (Average daily usage × Days between deliveries) + Safety stock

Safety stock = Buffer for unexpected demand or delivery delay
             = Typically 25–50% of one delivery period's usage

Order Quantity = Par level − Current on-hand inventory
```

*Example:*
```
Boneless chicken breast, average daily usage: 8 kg
Delivery schedule: 3× per week (every 2–3 days)
Par level = (8 kg/day × 3 days) + 25% safety stock
         = 24 kg + 6 kg = 30 kg par

If current on-hand = 12 kg:
Order quantity = 30 − 12 = 18 kg
```

**Ordering Frequency vs. Quantity Trade-off**

| Product Type | Recommended Ordering Frequency | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| **Fresh fish and shellfish** | Daily or every other day | Very short shelf life (1–3 days for highest quality); daily ordering maximizes freshness |
| **Fresh poultry and meat** | 2–3× per week | 3–7 days shelf life; balance freshness with minimum order requirements |
| **Fresh produce** | 2–3× per week | 3–10 days depending on product; frequent ordering reduces shrink and waste |
| **Fresh dairy** | 2–3× per week | 7–14 days shelf life; cream and butter have longer shelf life than fluid milk |
| **Frozen proteins** | Weekly to biweekly | 3–12 months frozen storage; ordering too frequently wastes supplier minimums |
| **Dry goods** | Weekly to monthly | Long shelf life; bulk purchasing reduces per-unit cost and delivery frequency |
| **Specialty items (foraged, artisan)** | As available; pre-order as required | Seasonal and supply-limited; relationships with suppliers often dictate timing |

**Business Volume and Lead Time Adjustments**

Ordering quantity must be adjusted for:
- **Upcoming high-volume events** (large banquets, holidays, special events) — order extra product in advance; communicate projected volume to key suppliers
- **Menu changes** — new menu items may require new products; lead time for first delivery must be planned
- **Seasonal availability shifts** — as local growing seasons begin, adjust orders from imported to local product
- **Extended delivery gaps** — statutory holidays often mean suppliers skip a delivery day; order quantities must increase to bridge the gap

#### Deep Dive: Communicating Orders to Suppliers

**Order Communication Standards**

Regardless of the method used, an order communicated to a supplier should contain:
- Date of order
- Required delivery date
- Product name and precise specification (reference purchase spec number if one exists)
- Quantity and unit of measure (kg, L, cases, portions)
- Price confirmation (especially for market-priced items like fresh seafood, produce)
- Any special instructions (delivery time window, contact at receiving, allergen requirements)

**Confirming Market-Priced Items**

Certain products — primarily fresh seafood, premium meats, and seasonal produce — are priced daily or weekly based on market conditions. Always confirm the current price when placing orders for these items. A verbal quote should be documented (noted in the order record) to avoid billing disputes.

**Order Records and Documentation**

Each order placed should be documented:
- **Purchase order (PO):** Formal document number assigned to the order; used to match the delivery invoice to the original order at receiving
- **Order confirmation:** Email or digital confirmation from the supplier acknowledges the order
- **Price confirmation:** Record the quoted price at time of ordering; compare to invoice at delivery

A purchase order that matches an invoice that matches a receiving log creates a complete procurement audit trail — essential for food cost management and dispute resolution.

#### Deep Dive: Reading Vendor Product Listings

Vendor catalogs, price lists, and electronic product listings use standardized formats. Learning to interpret them quickly is a practical skill.

**Key Elements of a Vendor Product Listing**

| Field | What It Means | How to Use It |
|---|---|---|
| **SKU / product code** | Unique identifier for the specific product | Use this code when ordering — eliminates ambiguity |
| **Product description** | Name, market form, processing | Verify it matches your specification exactly |
| **Pack size** | Format: units per case × size per unit | Calculate cost per kg: case price ÷ (units × unit weight) |
| **Unit of measure** | How it's sold (kg, L, each, case) | Confirm what "unit price" refers to |
| **Price** | Per case, per unit, or per kg | Normalize to cost/kg to compare across products and suppliers |
| **Availability** | In stock, on allocation, or temporarily unavailable | Plan substitutes for unavailable items before finalizing the order |
| **Minimum order** | Minimum quantity required for delivery | Factor into order frequency and batch sizing |
| **Origin** | Country or region of origin | Relevant for label compliance, quality expectations, and sourcing preferences |
| **Pack date / best before** | Production date and shelf life information | Verify adequate shelf life remaining at delivery |
| **Certifications** | Quality, sustainability, allergen-free marks | Confirm required certifications are present |

**Calculating Cost per Kg from Pack Size**

Vendor listings often show a case price. To compare fairly:

```
Cost per kg = Case price ÷ Total weight in case

Example:
  Listing shows: "Chicken breast, boneless/skinless — $68.50/case, 4 × 2.5 kg"
  Total case weight: 4 × 2.5 = 10 kg
  Cost per kg: $68.50 ÷ 10 = $6.85/kg

  Compare to competitor listing: "Chicken breast B/S — $6.50/kg" (same spec)
  → Competitor is $0.35/kg less; over 20 kg/week usage: $7/week, $364/year savings
```

**Reading Substitution Notices**

Suppliers sometimes issue substitution notices — informing operators that a regular product is temporarily unavailable and proposing a substitute. When reading these notices:
1. Verify the substitute meets your specification (grade, market form, size)
2. Re-cost the substitute to determine price impact
3. If the substitute differs significantly (different yield, different market form), recalculate your recipe costs
4. Communicate any necessary menu pricing adjustments to management

#### Deep Dive: Vendor Types and Their Characteristics

**Types of Vendors in Canadian Foodservice**

**Broadline / Major Suppliers**

Large distribution companies that carry thousands of products across all categories — proteins, produce, dairy, dry goods, smallwares, disposables. They typically:
- Serve large geographic areas with scheduled delivery routes
- Offer competitive volume pricing
- Provide online ordering platforms with full catalog access
- Provide credit terms and invoicing systems
- May offer value-added services (menu consulting, product demonstrations)

*Trade-off:* Deep product breadth but products are often commodity-grade. Building relationships with sales representatives is important for access to specialty items and advance notice of supply issues.

**Specialty and Category Suppliers**

Focus on a single product category (e.g., meat, seafood, artisan cheese, specialty produce, organic produce). Characteristics:
- Higher product quality and specificity within their category
- Deeper expertise and customization capability (custom butchery, specific catch sourcing)
- Often higher cost per unit than broadline
- May have higher minimum orders or limited delivery areas
- Better suited for operations that prioritize quality over price in specific categories

**Local Community Suppliers**

Small and medium-sized farms, processors, and artisan producers operating in the local region. Characteristics:
- Short supply chains — product may arrive within 24–48 hours of harvest
- Fresher product with better flavour and nutritional retention
- More opportunity to verify production methods (visit the farm)
- Supports local economy; strong marketing story for restaurant
- May have limited product availability and shorter growing seasons
- Cash payment or shorter credit terms often required
- Less formal documentation (though food safety requirements still apply)

**Foragers**

Individuals who harvest wild ingredients — mushrooms (chanterelles, morels, porcini), ramps, fiddleheads, wild berries, pine nuts, sea vegetables. Characteristics:
- Highly seasonal, limited-window availability
- Unique flavours and textures not available from cultivated sources
- Requires trust and relationship with specific foragers
- **Food safety consideration:** Wild-harvested products have no production chain documentation. Proper washing, inspection, and sourcing from knowledgeable foragers is critical. Some wild plants are toxic if misidentified — only purchase from experienced, reputable foragers.
- Regulatory note: commercial wild harvesting is governed by provincial regulations in Canada; verify forager is operating legally in their jurisdiction

**Farmers' Markets and Direct Farm Sales**

- Direct purchasing from producers at markets or farm gates
- Cash or e-transfer transactions; limited invoicing
- Excellent for seasonal, fresh, unique products
- Building relationship with specific farms enables advance reservations for specialty items
- Documentation (receipt at minimum; written specification agreed verbally) is important for food cost management

#### Sustainability, Seasonal, and Local Availability

**Sustainable Sourcing in Canadian Foodservice**

*Seafood sustainability:*
- **Marine Stewardship Council (MSC):** Third-party certification for wild-caught fisheries that are managed sustainably. The MSC blue fish label on a product or menu indicates verified sustainable wild-catch sourcing.
- **Aquaculture Stewardship Council (ASC):** Third-party certification for farmed seafood operations that meet environmental and social standards.
- **Ocean Wise:** Program managed by the Vancouver Aquarium; provides recommendations on sustainable seafood choices specific to Canadian and North American markets. An Ocean Wise–recommended product meets their science-based sustainability criteria.
- **SeaChoice:** Another Canadian seafood sustainability guide; provides green/yellow/red ratings based on fishery health.

*Organic certification in Canada:*
- Governed by the **Canada Organic Regime (COR)** administered by CFIA
- Products labeled "organic" in Canada and crossing provincial or international borders must be certified by a CFIA-accredited certification body
- Certification is voluntary for products sold only within a province, but use of the "Canada Organic" logo requires certification regardless

*Other sustainability certifications:*
- **Fair Trade:** Ensures producers in developing countries receive fair compensation
- **Rainforest Alliance:** Environmental and social standards for agriculture and forestry

**Why Sustainability Matters for Red Seal Cooks**

Sustainability knowledge is specifically called out in the Red Seal standard because it reflects evolving professional expectations for Canadian cooks. Demonstrating awareness of where food comes from, how it was produced, and the environmental implications of sourcing decisions is part of contemporary professional competency.

**Seasonal Availability in Canada**

Canada's growing seasons create dramatic variation in local ingredient availability. A cook who understands seasonality can build menus that take advantage of peak quality and lowest cost:

| Season | Representative Local Canadian Produce |
|---|---|
| **Spring (April–May)** | Fiddleheads, ramps/wild leeks, greenhouse lettuce, early radishes, rhubarb, asparagus |
| **Early summer (June–July)** | Strawberries, peas, early beans, zucchini, herbs, new potatoes, early corn |
| **Peak summer (August)** | Tomatoes, corn, peppers, cucumbers, blueberries, raspberries, peaches, stone fruits, eggplant |
| **Fall (September–October)** | Winter squash, apples, pears, late corn, brassicas (broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts), root vegetables, wild mushrooms |
| **Winter (November–March)** | Storage crops (carrots, beets, turnips, celeriac, potatoes, onions, garlic), hothouse tomatoes, some greenhouse greens, preserved product |

> **Operational Recommendation:** A seasonal menu notation strategy — identifying which dishes are available only when specific ingredients are in season — manages guest expectations, gives the menu narrative authenticity, and provides operational justification for rotating menu items.

#### Deep Dive: Ordering Procedures and Methods

**The Complete Ordering Cycle**

```
1. FORECAST
   Estimate covers for upcoming service period
   Review upcoming events, bookings, seasonal menu changes

2. CALCULATE REQUIREMENTS
   Review menu × forecasted covers = EP quantities needed
   Apply yield %: EP ÷ yield % = AP quantities required

3. TAKE INVENTORY
   Count all on-hand stock
   Check shelf life and FIFO compliance of existing product
   Identify items below par level

4. BUILD ORDER LIST
   AP quantity needed − on-hand = order quantity
   Add safety stock buffer for high-demand items
   Account for delivery frequency and lead time

5. VERIFY SPECIFICATIONS
   Confirm each product on the order matches current specifications
   Check for substitution notices from supplier
   Confirm availability of seasonal items

6. PLACE ORDER
   Transmit order by chosen method (phone, electronic, face-to-face)
   Confirm delivery date and time window
   Confirm price for market-priced items
   Obtain order confirmation number or acknowledgement

7. DOCUMENT
   Record order details (what, quantity, spec, price, supplier, date)
   File purchase order number for matching at receiving

8. PREPARE FOR RECEIVING
   Ensure refrigerated and frozen storage space is cleared and at temperature
   Review delivery schedule; ensure staff available at receiving
   Have purchase orders available for delivery verification
```

**Ordering Methods**

| Method | Advantages | Disadvantages | Best Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Electronic / online portal** | 24/7 availability; automatic order record; catalog browsing; recurring orders; reduces verbal errors; price and availability visible in real time | Requires internet access and device; system outages possible; less personal relationship | Standard orders from major broadline suppliers; high-frequency, high-SKU orders |
| **Phone / verbal** | Real-time conversation; allows negotiation and questions; useful for market-priced items | No automatic written record; relies on both parties to transcribe correctly; must follow up with written confirmation | Market-priced items; special requests; emergency orders; relationship-building with key suppliers |
| **Face-to-face** | Strongest relationship-building; opportunity to sample new products; detailed product discussion | Time-intensive; limited to scheduled sales rep visits or market days | Specialty suppliers; local farmers; farmers' markets; product discovery |
| **EDI (Electronic Data Interchange)** | Automated order transmission between systems; eliminates manual re-entry; used by large chains | Requires technical integration; not available with all suppliers | Large chains and corporate foodservice; automated replenishment systems |

**Standing Orders**

Many operations set up **standing orders** — recurring automatic deliveries of consistently used products at standard quantities. Examples:
- Milk and cream: standard quantity delivered 3× per week
- Eggs: 5 cases per delivery
- Bread: 10 loaves daily

Standing orders are efficient but require regular review — they can create over-purchasing if business volume changes or if a menu item is discontinued.

## Food Safety at the Ordering Stage

Purchasing decisions made at the ordering stage have downstream food safety implications:

| Purchasing Decision | Food Safety Impact |
|---|---|
| Ordering from CFIA-inspected/registered suppliers | Ensures product comes from a regulated, inspected facility with food safety controls |
| Specifying delivery temperature in purchase spec | Creates a binding standard for receiving inspection; grounds for rejection if temperature is exceeded |
| Requiring lot traceability documentation | Supports rapid response to recalls; required for shellfish (CSSP tags) and increasingly expected for all proteins |
| Ordering fresh seafood from CSSP-approved areas | Ensures shellfish comes from a monitored growing area not closed for biotoxins or contamination |
| Specifying allergen certifications | Reduces risk of allergen cross-contact in supply chain before product reaches the kitchen |
| Ordering too much perishable product | Creates FIFO pressure; old product remains in inventory; increases spoilage and food safety risk |
| Ordering too little | Creates shortage pressure; emergency substitutions; potential room-temperature thawing; food safety shortcuts |

## Common Mistakes in Product Ordering

| Mistake | Consequence | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Ordering without checking current inventory | Ordering what's already in stock; over-purchasing; waste; inflated food cost | Always take a physical inventory count before building an order |
| Vague or incomplete specifications | Receiving wrong product; grade, size, or market form mismatch; yield calculations invalidated | Write and use a formal purchase specification for every high-cost item |
| Not confirming market price before ordering | Invoice price differs from budget assumption; unexpected food cost increase | Confirm quote at time of ordering for market-priced items; document the quoted price |
| Ordering the same quantities regardless of forecasted volume | Over-purchasing on slow periods (waste/spoilage); under-purchasing on busy periods (86'd items, rushed purchasing) | Calculate order quantities from forecasted covers; adjust weekly for known events |
| Ignoring minimum order requirements | Item not delivered because minimum not met; last-minute gap in production | Know supplier minimums; plan orders to meet them or consolidate suppliers |
| Ordering fresh seafood too far in advance | Product is beyond acceptable shelf life before service date; quality and safety failure | Order fresh fish no more than 1–2 days before intended use; use IQF for longer planning horizon |
| Not reading substitution notices before delivery | Unanticipated product arrives; recipes, portions, and costs recalculated under time pressure | Review all pending substitution notices before delivery day |
| Ignoring the IQF glaze percentage | Paying for 20% water weight; undercosting true seafood cost per kg | Ask suppliers for glaze % on all IQF seafood; adjust yield and cost calculations accordingly |
| Ordering from a supplier not on the approved vendor list | Product may not come from an inspected facility; food safety and legal compliance risk | Purchase only from approved, qualified vendors; document the approval process |
| Standing orders not reviewed regularly | Accumulation of slow-moving or discontinued items; over-purchasing as business volume changes | Review all standing orders monthly; adjust quantities to current usage |

## Memory Aids for Apprentices

**"SPECS" — The Five Elements of a Purchase Specification**
> **S**ize (weight/case) | **P**roduct type | **E**xact grade | **C**ondition at delivery | **S**helf life remaining

**The Order Quantity Formula**
> *Par level − On-hand inventory = Order quantity*
> Par level = (Daily usage × Days between deliveries) + Safety stock

**Market Forms from Whole to Portioned (Fish)**
> *Round → Drawn → H&G → Pan-dressed → Fillet → Steak → Portion*
> Each step removes more material; yield goes down; cost per kg goes up; labour requirement goes down.

**Canadian Beef Grade Order (Highest to Lowest Quality)**
> *Prime → AAA → AA → A → B → D → E*
> "**P**lease **A**lways **A**sk **A**bout **B**eef **D**iversity **E**very time"

**IQF Glaze Math**
> *True seafood weight = Case weight × (1 − glaze %)*
> *True cost per kg = Case price ÷ True seafood weight*

**"Buy Fresh, Buy Often"**
> Fresh seafood: daily or every-other-day. Fresh meat/poultry: 2–3× per week. Dry goods: weekly to monthly. The more perishable the product, the more frequently it should be ordered in smaller quantities.

## Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge

This section summarizes the most commonly tested concepts from this subtask for Red Seal examination preparation.

| Product | Food Service Standard Grade | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Beef | **Canada AAA** (most common); Canada Prime (premium) | Quality based on marbling and maturity |
| Poultry | **Canada Grade A** | Standard for food service; symmetrical, well-fleshed, no defects |

| Produce | **Canada No. 1** | Standard for food service presentation use |

### Likely Exam Questions and Concepts

1. **What is a purchase specification and why is it important?**
   → A written document defining the exact quality, grade, market form, size, packaging, and delivery conditions required for a product. It protects against receiving substandard or incorrect product, ensures yield calculations remain valid, and provides grounds for rejecting non-conforming deliveries.

2. **What is the difference between AP and EP weight, and why does it matter when ordering?**
   → AP (As Purchased) is the weight received, before any trimming or processing. EP (Edible Portion) is the usable weight after fabrication. Order quantity must be calculated in AP units: AP needed = EP quantity required ÷ yield %. Ordering in EP quantities without applying the yield factor results in receiving insufficient product.

3. **Name the Canadian beef quality grades in order from highest to lowest.**
   → Canada Prime → Canada AAA → Canada AA → Canada A → Canada B → Canada D → Canada E

4. **What are the market forms of fish from whole to portioned? What happens to yield and cost per kg at each step?**
   → Round → Drawn → H&G → Pan-dressed → Fillet → Steak → Portion. As processing increases, yield decreases (less of the AP weight is usable) and cost per kg increases. Labour requirement decreases.

5. **What is an IQF glaze and how does it affect cost calculation?**
   → IQF (Individually Quick Frozen) seafood may be coated with a water-ice glaze. Glaze adds to the purchased weight but not to the usable seafood weight. True cost per kg = Case price ÷ (Case weight × (1 − glaze %)). Ignoring the glaze causes significant underestimation of true ingredient cost.

6. **What is the par stock system and how is order quantity calculated?**
   → Par is the minimum quantity that must be on hand between deliveries. Par level = (daily usage × days between deliveries) + safety stock. Order quantity = Par level − current on-hand inventory.

7. **Name four types of vendors used in Canadian foodservice and describe one advantage of each.**
   → Broadline/major suppliers (wide product range, competitive volume pricing); specialty suppliers (higher quality in specific categories); local community suppliers (fresher product, shorter supply chain, local sourcing story); foragers (unique wild-harvested ingredients with seasonal availability)

8. **What are the three ordering methods listed in the Range of Variables? What is the main advantage of electronic ordering?**
   → Phone, electronic, face-to-face. Electronic ordering creates automatic records, allows 24/7 ordering, enables catalog browsing, and reduces verbal miscommunication errors.

9. **Why is it important to confirm the price for market-priced items when placing an order?**
   → Market-priced items (fresh seafood, premium meats, seasonal produce) fluctuate daily or weekly based on supply and demand. Confirming the price at time of ordering prevents unexpected invoice amounts and allows accurate food cost calculations.

10. **What food safety risk is associated with swollen or bulging cans?**
    → Swollen cans indicate internal gas production, which may indicate *Clostridium botulinum* contamination and toxin production. *C. botulinum* toxin is the most lethal biological toxin known. Product from swollen, seam-dented, or leaking cans must never be used and must be discarded safely.

11. **Name three sustainability certifications relevant to seafood purchasing in Canada and what each certifies.**
    → MSC (Marine Stewardship Council) — wild-caught fisheries managed sustainably; ASC (Aquaculture Stewardship Council) — responsibly farmed seafood; Ocean Wise (Vancouver Aquarium program) — Canadian-specific sustainability recommendations for both wild and farmed seafood.

12. **What is the difference between a quality grade and a yield grade in beef?**
    → A quality grade (Prime, AAA, AA, A) reflects eating quality, primarily based on marbling and animal maturity. A yield grade reflects the proportion of usable meat per carcass (how much trim vs. bone vs. usable meat). Food service purchasing specifications typically focus on quality grades; yield grades are more relevant for wholesale and packer use.
