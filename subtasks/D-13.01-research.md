## Why "Selection" Is the Make-or-Break Step

A stock is mostly water, time, and what you put in it. Once the pot is on the fire, you cannot improve the bones, the freshness of the vegetables, or the cleanness of the water. Every defect you select will be **concentrated** by reduction:

- Old, ammonia-smelling fish bones → fumet that tastes like a tide pool.
- Oily, fatty veal bones → cloudy, greasy stock that won't clarify.
- Bruised celery and watery carrots → flat, vegetal off-notes.
- Tap water with chlorine and sulfur → metallic, off-flavour stocks.
- Aromatic herbs past their prime → bitter, oxidized notes.

**Selection is the first quality-control gate.** Once a stock is built on poor ingredients, no amount of reduction, seasoning, or technique fixes it.

## Foundational Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Stock (Fr. *fond*)** | A flavourful liquid made by simmering bones (and often vegetables, aromatics) in water. Stocks are the **foundation** (fond = "foundation") of sauces, soups, braises. Typically unseasoned. |
| **Broth (Fr. *bouillon*)** | A flavourful liquid made by simmering **meat** (with or without bones) in water; seasoned and often consumed as-is. |
| **Fond** | French for stock; also the browned protein/sucs left in a roasting pan, the basis of a pan sauce. |
| **Fumet** | A concentrated fish (or sometimes shellfish/mushroom) stock; from Fr. *parfum*. |
| **Court-bouillon** | "Short broth" — an acidulated, aromatic poaching liquid; usually NOT a stock, made fresh for a single use. |
| **Glace** | A stock reduced by 75–90% to a syrupy, gelatinous concentrate (e.g., *glace de viande*). |
| **Remouillage** | "Re-wetting" — a second extraction from already-simmered bones, weaker than the first stock. |
| **Mirepoix** | A vegetable foundation, classically **50% onion, 25% carrot, 25% celery** by weight. **[Classical]** |
| **White mirepoix** | Onion, celery, leek, parsnip (no carrot) — used for **white** stocks to keep colour pale. |
| **Matignon** | Edible mirepoix — diced finely and cooked with butter and wine; meant to be served with the dish. |
| **Bouquet garni** | A small bundle of fresh herbs (parsley stems, thyme, bay) tied in leek leaf or cheesecloth. |
| **Sachet d'épices** | "Spice sachet" — peppercorns, bay, thyme, parsley stems, optionally clove and garlic tied in cheesecloth. |
| **Oignon piqué** | "Pricked onion" — onion studded with a bay leaf pinned by a whole clove; used in béchamel and some stocks. |
| **Oignon brûlé** | "Burnt onion" — onion charred cut-side down on a dry pan; adds colour and depth to brown stocks/consommés. |
| **Sucs** | The caramelized fond left after browning protein; flavour gold for brown stocks. |
| **Collagen / Gelatin** | Connective-tissue protein in bones and cartilage that hydrolyzes to gelatin during long, gentle simmering — gives body. |

## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### D-13.01.01P — Identify and Choose Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** identify and choose ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** ingredients are identified and chosen according to stock or broth desired.

#### The five ingredient categories (Range of Variables)

##### 1. Bones — the structural foundation

Bones contribute **body** (via gelatin from collagen) and **flavour** (via marrow, residual meat, and bone minerals).

| Bone type | Source / use | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Knuckle / joint** | Veal, beef, pork | **Highest cartilage content → highest gelatin yield** — body champion |
| **Neck, back, shank** | Veal, beef, chicken | High collagen, meaty flavour |
| **Marrow bones (femur, thigh)** | Veal, beef | Rich flavour from marrow fat; lower collagen than knuckles |
| **Feet / trotters** | Pork, veal, chicken | Very high gelatin — added to other bones for body (often half-and-half) |
| **Chicken carcasses, backs, necks, wings** | Chicken | Standard for chicken stock; backs and wings have high collagen-to-meat ratio |
| **Fish frames (heads, bones, fins)** | Lean white fish: sole, halibut, turbot, snapper | For **fumet** — fatty fish (salmon, mackerel) give cloudy, fishy stock; avoid |
| **Shellfish shells** | Lobster, shrimp, crab | For shellfish stock/bisque base |
| **Lamb bones** | Lamb | Strong flavour; mostly for lamb-specific applications |
| **Pork bones** | Pork | Strong flavour; common in Asian stocks (tonkotsu, Cantonese, etc.) |
| **Veal bones (Young animal)** | Veal | **Classic neutral all-purpose foundation** — high collagen, mild flavour; backbone of French haute cuisine |

##### Why veal bones are classical king
- **Young animal** = high collagen, low residual fat, mild flavour.
- **Neutral palette** that takes the character of whatever sauce builds on top.
- Used as the universal foundation for *espagnole*, *demi-glace*, and *glace de viande* in classical French cuisine. **[Classical]**

##### Cartilage content = gelatin potential
- **Collagen** (a structural protein) hydrolyzes into **gelatin** at ~ 70–80 °C with prolonged gentle simmering (typically 6–12 hours for veal stock; 2–4 hours for chicken; 30–45 min for fish).
- A properly extracted stock sets to a **wobbly, gelatinous mass when chilled** — that's the visible sign of dissolved gelatin and is a primary quality marker.
- **Cut bones (knuckles split, joints cracked)** expose more cartilage surface and dramatically increase yield.

##### 2. Vegetables — usually mirepoix

| Component | Classical ratio | Role | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Onion** | 50% | Sweetness, depth, sulphur compounds for savoury notes | Skins on for colour in brown stocks; remove for white |
| **Carrot** | 25% | Natural sweetness; orange hue | Omit for white stocks (use white mirepoix) |
| **Celery** | 25% | Aromatic backbone; salty mineral notes | Stalks; tops can be bitter |

**Variants:**
- **White mirepoix:** onion, celery, leek (whites and light greens), parsnip — preserves pale colour in white stocks, fumet, and chicken velouté.
- **Cajun "holy trinity":** equal parts onion, celery, **green bell pepper** — used in Cajun/Creole cuisine, not classical French.
- **Soffritto (Italian):** onion, carrot, celery — same idea, often started in olive oil.
- **Suppengrün (German):** carrot, celery root, leek, parsley root.

##### Why mirepoix is added at a specific time
- **White stock:** vegetables added with cold water at the start (sweat-and-extract).
- **Brown stock:** vegetables **roasted alongside bones** to caramelize sugars and develop colour and depth via the **Maillard reaction**.
- **Fumet:** vegetables **sweated in fat first** to extract aromatics without colouring; then bones added.
- **Last hour rule [Best Practice]:** in some classical kitchens, mirepoix is added in the last 60–90 minutes of a long beef/veal simmer because prolonged cooking can mute vegetable freshness; modern Canadian practice varies.

##### 3. Aromatics — herbs and spices

| Aromatic | Use |
|---|---|
| **Parsley stems** | Cleaner flavour than leaves; mild, herbaceous; foundational |
| **Thyme (fresh)** | Earthy, mildly minty; classic; dried thyme acceptable in some ops |
| **Bay leaf (laurel)** | Subtle eucalyptus and tea-like notes; 1–2 per medium pot |
| **Peppercorns (whole)** | Black for most stocks; white for white stocks (no dark specks) |
| **Clove (whole)** | Sparingly — overpowering; common in *oignon piqué* |
| **Garlic** | Common in modern stocks; classical French stocks often skip it for neutrality |
| **Ginger** | Asian stocks (chicken, pork, dashi-adjacent broths) |
| **Star anise, fennel seed** | Asian stocks (pho, bone broths) |
| **Cinnamon stick** | Pho and Asian broths |
| **Dried mushroom (porcini, shiitake)** | Adds umami; used in vegetable stocks and modern bone broths |
| **Dried kombu** | Foundation of dashi |
| **Bonito flakes (katsuobushi)** | Foundation of dashi |

##### Bouquet garni vs sachet d'épices

| Form | Contents | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Bouquet garni** | Parsley stems + thyme sprigs + bay leaf, tied with twine or wrapped in leek leaf | Fresh herbs; easy retrieval; classical white stocks, soups |
| **Sachet d'épices** | Parsley stems + thyme + bay + peppercorns + optional clove + garlic, tied in cheesecloth | When dry/whole spices are used; cleaner stocks; easier removal |

##### 4. Water — the often-overlooked variable

- **Cold water start.** Always. Adding bones to hot water seals proteins to the bone surface and prevents extraction.
- **Cold water also allows blood and impurities to come to the surface as a scum that can be skimmed** — leaving a cleaner final stock.
- **Potable cold water** at minimum. Some kitchens use filtered water (especially with hard or chlorinated municipal supplies) for cleaner flavour.
- **Bone-to-water ratio (rule of thumb):**
  - **White / brown stocks:** roughly **1 part bones to 1.5–2 parts water by weight** (bones should be covered by ~5 cm / 2 in water).
  - **Fumet:** higher bone density; shorter cook.
  - **Court-bouillon:** water-based, no bones.
- **Cover bones by ~5 cm / 2 in** — too much water = weak stock; too little = exposed bones, scorching, hot spots.
- **Don't top up with cold water mid-simmer** unless necessary — drops temperature out of the extraction range and can disturb the simmer.

##### 5. Proteins

For broths specifically (vs stocks), **meat (with or without bones)** is the primary source. Examples:

- **Boiling fowl / stewing hen** for chicken broth — older, tougher birds have more connective tissue and richer flavour.
- **Beef shin, brisket, neck** for beef broth.
- **Whole fish carcass with flesh attached** for fumet (some flesh adds richness).
- **Konbu + bonito** for dashi.
- **Soy beans / miso paste** for miso-based broths.

Stocks traditionally use **bones with very little meat attached**; broths intentionally include more meat for body and flavour.

### D-13.01.02P — Identify Indicators of Freshness and Quality

**Performance Criteria:** identify indicators of freshness and quality
**Evidence of Attainment:** indicators of freshness and quality of ingredients are identified according to sensory assessment.

From Range of Variables: **firmness, colour, texture.**

#### Sensory assessment by ingredient category

##### Bones (general)
| Sense | Fresh / good | Stale / reject |
|---|---|---|
| **Sight** | Marrow pink or creamy; bone white-to-ivory; minimal blood pooling | Brown/grey discolouration; dried-out marrow; slime on surface |
| **Smell** | Neutral; faintly mineral or "meaty" | Sour, ammonia, sulphur, or any putrid odour |
| **Touch** | Firm; slight tackiness from gelatin | Slimy, sticky, dry-and-leathery |
| **Temperature on receipt** | **≤ 4 °C** for fresh / ≤ −18 °C for frozen | Above 4 °C → cold-chain break → reject |
| **Packaging** | Intact, sealed, dated | Torn, leaking, past best-before |

##### Veal / beef bones specifically
- **Knuckles** should show **firm, glossy cartilage** at the joints — this is your gelatin source.
- Avoid bones that look dehydrated or have visible bruising.

##### Chicken bones / carcasses
- **No greenish discolouration** (early spoilage near bone).
- **No off-smell** — chicken is one of the fastest-spoiling proteins; sensory cues are crucial.
- Frozen-thawed acceptable but verify temperature.

##### Fish bones / frames (for fumet)
- **Eyes clear and bright** (not sunken or cloudy) if heads attached.
- **Gills bright red or pink** — brown/grey gills = old.
- **Smell:** clean ocean / pond; **never ammonia** (ammonia = decomposition; reject).
- **Flesh:** firm, springs back when pressed; no slime.
- **Skin:** shiny, intact, scales firmly attached.
- **Avoid:** oily fish (salmon, mackerel, herring) — too strong, too fatty for clear fumet.

##### Shellfish shells
- **Smell:** clean sea; **never sulphurous or rotten.**
- **No black discolouration** on lobster/shrimp shells.

##### Vegetables (mirepoix)
| Vegetable | Fresh | Stale |
|---|---|---|
| **Onion** | Firm, papery skin tight, no soft spots, no green sprouts | Soft, sprouting, mouldy, slimy, sour smell |
| **Carrot** | Firm, vivid orange, no rubbery flex | Bendable, dull, sprouting white roots, fungal |
| **Celery** | Firm stalks, vivid green tops, snaps cleanly | Limp, yellowing, brown spots at base, slimy |
| **Leek (for white mirepoix)** | Firm, white-and-light-green portion intact, leaves perky | Yellowing, slimy between layers, dirty bottom rotted |
| **Parsnip** | Firm, ivory-pale, no soft spots | Rubbery, brown, sprouting |

##### Aromatics
- **Fresh herbs**: vibrant colour, no wilting, no black spots, fragrant when crushed.
- **Dried spices**: aromatic when crushed; old spices smell dusty or flat — replace.
- **Peppercorns**: hard, no dust at the bottom of the jar (dust = age).
- **Bay leaves**: still slightly aromatic; very old leaves are flavourless.

##### Water
- **Tap water acceptable** in most Canadian municipalities (potable).
- **Filtered preferred** in areas with strong chlorination, hard water, or unusual taste.
- **Never** use hot tap water (older plumbing can leach metals; lower oxygen).

#### "Use sensory assessment" — what the evidence statement actually means
A cook should evaluate ingredients with **sight, smell, touch, and temperature** at three points:
1. **At receiving** — reject anything outside spec before it enters the kitchen.
2. **At storage retrieval** — re-evaluate before use; cold-chain may have failed.
3. **At pot-side** — final check before commitment.

> ⚠️ **Pathogens are usually invisible and odourless.** Sensory cues catch spoilage (which affects quality and contains higher pathogen loads), but **always combine sensory assessment with temperature checks and use-by-date verification.** Carry-over from [A-2.01-research.md](A-2.01-research.md).

> **Memory aid — "FREQ":** *Firmness, Resilience, Even colour, Quiet smell.* A good ingredient holds its shape, springs back, looks consistent, and has a mild, expected smell.

## 2. Knowledge Objectives — Deeper Dive

### D-13.01.01L — Stocks, Broths, and Their Applications

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of stocks and broths, and their applications.

#### Terminology (recap and extension)

| Term | Definition / use |
|---|---|
| **Stock (fond)** | Bone-and-aromatic-based unseasoned base for sauces, soups, braises |
| **Broth (bouillon)** | Meat-based, seasoned, often consumed directly |
| **Consommé** | Clarified broth — crystal-clear, intensely flavoured, traditionally clarified with a meat-and-egg-white "raft" |
| **Velouté** | A "mother sauce" — white stock thickened with a white roux |
| **Espagnole** | Brown veal/beef stock thickened with brown roux, often with tomato; foundation of demi-glace |
| **Demi-glace** | Espagnole + brown stock, reduced by half |
| **Glace** | A finished stock reduced by 75–90% to a syrupy concentrate; intensely flavoured |
| **Jus** | Pan drippings deglazed and reduced; the "natural sauce" of a roast |
| **Aspic** | Gelled stock; set as a clear coating or layer |
| **Nage** | "Swimming" — a light court-bouillon-style broth used for poaching, often finished with butter or cream |

#### Types of stocks (Range of Variables)

##### White stock (fond blanc)
- **Bones:** veal, chicken, or fish.
- **Blanched first** — bones placed in cold water, brought to a boil, drained, rinsed; removes blood and impurities → clearer final stock.
- **Vegetables (white mirepoix or standard, depending)** added cold.
- **Cold water cover.**
- **Slow simmer (never boil)** — boiling emulsifies fat and proteins → cloudy stock.
- **Result:** pale, neutral, versatile foundation for velouté, white sauces, light soups, risottos, braises of white meats.

##### Brown stock (fond brun)
- **Bones:** veal, beef, lamb, game, or chicken.
- **Bones roasted at ~ 200–230 °C / 400–450 °F** until deep mahogany brown — develops Maillard compounds, the source of brown stock's depth.
- **Tomato product** (paste, purée, or fresh) often added; provides acidity, pigment, and umami.
- **Mirepoix roasted alongside or after bones** until caramelized.
- **Deglaze the roasting pan** with water or wine; scrape *sucs* into the pot. **Never discard the fond/sucs** — they are the entire reason brown stock is brown.
- **Simmer** for 6–12+ hours.
- **Result:** rich, golden-brown to mahogany, gelatinous when cold. Foundation for *espagnole*, *demi-glace*, brown sauces, beef and lamb braises.

##### Court-bouillon
- **"Short broth"** — quick, acidulated water for **poaching** fish, shellfish, vegetables, or offal.
- **Typical composition:** water + white wine OR vinegar OR lemon, mirepoix or onion-and-carrot, herbs, peppercorns, salt.
- Simmered only 30–45 minutes — extracts vegetable/aromatic flavour, not bone collagen.
- **Not a true stock.** Discarded after use (or sometimes strained and reduced for a sauce).
- **Acidic medium tightens fish proteins**, holds delicate items together during poaching, and adds bright flavour.

##### Fumet (fish fumet)
- **Bones:** lean white fish frames (sole, turbot, halibut, snapper, cod) — never oily fish.
- **Sweat the bones and mirepoix in butter first** (no colour).
- **Add white wine** — acidity helps protein extraction and adds aromatic complexity.
- **Add cold water** to cover.
- **Simmer gently 30–45 minutes** — fish bones over-extract and become bitter after ~ 1 hour.
- **Result:** pale, delicate, intensely fish-flavoured. Foundation for fish sauces (e.g., beurre blanc-style), fish soups, bisques.

##### Vegetable stock (additional common type)
- Not in the Range of Variables list but ubiquitous in modern Canadian kitchens.
- Mirepoix + leek + garlic + mushroom + tomato + aromatics + cold water.
- **Sweat the vegetables in oil first** for depth.
- **Simmer 45–60 min only** — longer extraction makes the stock bitter and muddy.

##### Glace de viande, jus, remouillage (extensions of stock)
- **Glace** — finished stock reduced by 75–90% until syrupy. Gels firmly at fridge temp; spoonable concentrate; used to fortify sauces.
- **Remouillage** — already-simmered bones re-covered with water and simmered again; weaker stock used for soups or as the starting liquid for a fresh batch of stock.
- **Jus** — roasting pan drippings deglazed and reduced; the natural sauce of a roast.

#### Types of broths (Range of Variables)

##### Dashi (Japanese)
- **Foundation of Japanese cuisine.**
- **Ichiban dashi (first dashi):** cold water + **kombu** (dried kelp) steeped, brought just below the boil, kombu removed, then **katsuobushi** (smoked, fermented, dried bonito flakes) added off heat, steeped briefly (3–5 min), strained.
- **Niban dashi (second dashi):** the used kombu and katsuobushi simmered with fresh water for a weaker secondary stock; used for general cooking.
- **Pure umami foundation** — the kombu provides glutamate; katsuobushi provides inosinate; together they multiply each other's perception of umami (the "synergy effect").

##### Emulsified broth
- A broth in which fat is forcibly suspended in the liquid (e.g., with a high-speed blender or by emulsifying with butter/cream).
- **Examples:** classic French butter-mounted broths; modern foam/cappuccino-style restaurant garnishes.
- **Tonkotsu ramen broth** is the famous example: pork bones boiled vigorously for 12+ hours, emulsifying collagen and fat into the liquid — opaque white, rich body.

##### Clear broth
- **Bouillon** in the classical sense — meat simmered gently, skimmed obsessively, strained.
- May be further **clarified to a consommé**.
- **Clear** means visible-through (light passes through); requires gentle simmering (no agitation) and skimming.

##### Bone broth
- **Modern term** for long-simmered stock (often 12–24+ hours) intended as a beverage or "wellness" product.
- Distinct from classical stock mainly in **seasoning** (bone broth is salted and consumed) and **marketing**.
- Nutritionally provides collagen-derived gelatin (and the amino acids glycine and proline) plus minerals — but the popular health claims often exceed the evidence. **[Operational caveat]**

##### Miso broth
- **Miso paste** (fermented soybean, sometimes with rice or barley koji) **dissolved into dashi** or water.
- **Critical:** miso should **never be boiled** after addition — high heat denatures aromatic and probiotic compounds; the broth becomes dull.
- Add miso off heat or just below simmer.

#### Factors to consider when selecting ingredients (Range of Variables)

| Factor | Considerations |
|---|---|
| **Freshness** | Sensory + temperature + dating; older bones/vegetables give muddier flavours; old fish can be unsafe |
| **Cost** | Veal bones are expensive; "managing trim" (using vegetable scraps appropriately) controls cost; chicken backs are very cheap |
| **Fat content** | Excess fat creates greasy stock; lean lean fish for fumet; trim heavy fat caps from beef bones |
| **Intended application** | Match stock to dish: white for cream sauces, brown for braises, fumet for fish, dashi for Japanese |
| **Type of bone** | Marrow (flavour) vs knuckle (body) vs neck (both); use complementary mix |
| **Cartilage content** | Drives gelatin yield; the more cartilage, the more body |
| **Managing trim** | Onion ends, carrot peels (clean), celery tops, mushroom stems — yes; potato peels, beet, brassicas (cabbage/cauliflower/broccoli) → avoid (muddy / off-flavour) |
| **Availability** | Seasonal vegetables; supplier consistency; substitutions when classical not available |

##### Trim management — what to use, what to skip

**Use in stocks:**
- Onion ends and skins (skin adds golden colour to brown stock)
- Carrot peels and ends (clean)
- Celery tops and butts
- Leek tops (greens, well washed)
- Parsley stems
- Mushroom stems and trim
- Thyme stalks
- Tomato cores

**Avoid in stocks:**
- **Brassicas** — cabbage, broccoli, cauliflower, kale (sulphur compounds get cabbage-y)
- **Potato peels** — starch turns stock cloudy and gummy
- **Beet** — colours stock pink/red; very strong flavour
- **Garlic skins in large quantity** — bitter
- **Bell pepper trim** — strong flavour for general-purpose stocks
- **Rotting trim** — even slightly off-smelling vegetables carry into the final stock at concentration

#### Ingredients and ratios used in stocks and broths

Approximate ratios (vary by source and operation):

| Stock | Bones : Water (by weight) | Mirepoix (% of bone weight) | Simmer time |
|---|---|---|---|
| **White veal/beef** | 1 : 1.5–2 | 8–10% | 8–12 h |
| **Brown veal/beef** | 1 : 1.5–2 | 8–10% | 8–12 h |
| **White chicken** | 1 : 1.5–2 | 8–10% | 3–4 h |
| **Brown chicken** | 1 : 1.5–2 | 8–10% | 3–4 h |
| **Fish fumet** | 1 : 1–1.5 | 10–15% | 30–45 min |
| **Court-bouillon** | (no bones) | (mirepoix is the structure) | 30–45 min |
| **Vegetable stock** | (no bones) | (vegetables are the body) | 45–60 min |
| **Dashi (ichiban)** | (kombu + katsuobushi) | n/a | 30 min total steep |

> **[Best Practice]** Exact ratios vary by reference (CIA *Professional Chef*, Pauli *Classical Cooking*, Larousse) and by individual operation's house recipe. Apprentices should learn their workplace's house standard but understand the underlying principles.

#### Dietary requirements specific to stocks and broths

From Range of Variables: **cultural, allergies, ethical, religious, health-related, environmental, preferences.**

| Dietary need | Implication for stocks/broths |
|---|---|
| **Vegetarian / vegan** | No animal bones; build flavour from mushroom (umami), kombu, tomato, roasted aromatics, fermented soy (miso, soy sauce) |
| **Halal** | Bones from animals slaughtered per Islamic rite (*zabihah*); no pork bones, no alcohol/wine; certified supplier chain |
| **Kosher** | Bones from kosher animals only; no pork; meat and dairy separation (meat stock not finished with cream/butter on kosher meat lines); certified supplier |
| **Hindu** | Many Hindus avoid beef; vegetarian broths or chicken/lamb alternatives |
| **Jain** | No animal products; no onion, garlic, root vegetables in strict observance — challenging for traditional stocks |
| **Fish/shellfish allergy** | Fumet, dashi, bisque base are off-limits; cross-contact with shared equipment matters |
| **Soy allergy** | Miso, soy sauce, tofu off-limits; many "vegetable" broths contain soy |
| **Gluten / celiac** | Most pure stocks are gluten-free, but commercial "broths" / bouillon cubes may contain wheat or barley — read labels |
| **Sulphite sensitivity** | Some wines used for deglazing contain sulphites at declared levels; substitute non-sulphited wine or non-alcoholic |
| **MSG / glutamate sensitivity** | Anecdotal in most cases; clear labelling matters; modern Canadian science does not strongly support most claimed sensitivities but the operator should disclose |
| **Sodium restriction** | Stocks unseasoned by definition; commercial bases often very high sodium; check labels |
| **Sustainability** | Whole-animal use (nose-to-tail), local sourcing, ocean-friendly fish (Ocean Wise, MSC) for fumet |

### D-13.01.02L — Procedures for Selecting Ingredients

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to select ingredients for stocks and broths.

#### Procedure for selecting stock ingredients

1. **Determine the target stock or broth** — white, brown, fumet, dashi, court-bouillon, etc.
2. **Identify required bones / protein** — type, ratio to water, cartilage requirement.
3. **Check supplier and inventory** — what's fresh, what's frozen, what's in trim from yesterday's prep.
4. **Inspect at receiving / retrieval:**
   - Verify temperature (≤ 4 °C cold; ≤ −18 °C frozen).
   - Sensory check — smell, sight, touch.
   - Check date and packaging integrity.
   - Reject if non-compliant.
5. **Plan vegetable / aromatic complement** — match mirepoix to stock type (white vs standard); choose herbs/spices.
6. **Verify water source** — potable cold tap or filtered.
7. **Check seasoning/dietary requirements** — any service-specific needs (vegetarian, halal, etc.).
8. **Stage everything before commitment** — *mise en place* before the pot goes on.

#### Procurement and supplier considerations
- **Bones** can be sourced from in-house butchery (best — full traceability and freshness) or from suppliers; specify cuts and freshness in the order.
- **Fish bones** must come from very fresh fish — many Canadian seafood suppliers will reserve frames if requested.
- **Trim management** lowers cost and improves sustainability; a "stock bag" (clean trim collected daily in the freezer) is a common practice. **[Operational / Best Practice]**

## 3. Food Safety Considerations Specific to Stocks

Even at the "ingredient selection" stage, food safety is paramount because stocks have several high-risk characteristics:

### Why stocks are high-risk for *Clostridium perfringens*

- **Large volumes** that cool slowly.
- **High protein content** (perfect bacterial growth medium).
- **Spore-forming bacteria** survive the long simmer.
- **Cooling through the danger zone** is where *C. perfringens* spores germinate and multiply.

> **Cooling rule (Canada, Health Canada / CFIA aligned):** **60 °C → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 °C → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.** See [A-2.02-research.md](A-2.02-research.md) for the full cooling protocol.

This is the underlying reason stocks are typically:
- **Cooled in shallow pans** rather than the original stockpot.
- **Cooled with ice wands** stirred through the volume.
- **Cooled in an ice bath** with the pot in the sink.
- **Blast-chilled** in the gold-standard operation.

### Receiving and storage requirements (carry-over)

- **Receive bones at ≤ 4 °C** (or frozen ≤ −18 °C).
- **Use raw bones within 1–2 days of receipt** or freeze immediately.
- **Store on the bottom shelf** of refrigeration (raw protein hierarchy).
- **Cover and label** with date.
- **FIFO** rotation.

### Cross-contamination during selection

- **Dedicated bone-handling tools** (knife, board) — sanitize before/after.
- **Don't handle aromatics with bone-juice hands** — wash and change gloves.
- **Wash all vegetables thoroughly** under potable running water; cut and trim on a vegetable board (green).
- **Wash hands and sanitize boards** between protein and vegetable selection.

## 4. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Selecting bones for a brown veal stock for tonight's demi-glace
1. **Target:** ~ 6 L of finished brown veal stock from ~ 4 kg of bones.
2. **Bone mix:** 60% knuckles (gelatin), 40% neck/back (flavour). Cracked or split where possible.
3. **Inspect:** marrow pink/cream; cartilage glossy; no off-smell; ≤ 4 °C at storage retrieval.
4. **Roast bones** at 220 °C until deep mahogany.
5. **Mirepoix:** standard 50/25/25 onion/carrot/celery, ~ 400 g total (≈ 10% of bone weight); roast separately to caramelize.
6. **Tomato paste:** 50–80 g, painted onto bones in the last 20 minutes of roasting to caramelize without burning.
7. **Aromatics:** sachet with parsley stems, thyme, bay, black peppercorns, ½ tsp clove.
8. **Cold water cover** by 5 cm.
9. Skim, simmer (never boil), strain, **rapid cool per the 2/4/6 rule.**

### Scenario 2 — Receiving fish frames for fumet
1. Inspect at delivery: ≤ 4 °C, eyes clear (if heads on), gills pink/red, no ammonia smell, intact packaging.
2. Reject if any sensory red flag — ammonia is non-negotiable.
3. Use within 24 hours if possible; freeze if delayed.
4. **Avoid oily fish** (salmon, mackerel) for clear fumet.
5. Plan a 30–45 minute simmer only — no longer.

### Scenario 3 — A guest declares a peanut allergy and wants soup
1. The stock itself should not contain peanut, but verify:
   - No peanut oil in mirepoix sweating.
   - No peanut-contaminated tools used during stock construction.
   - No cross-contact in storage (peanut sauces stored elsewhere).
2. Cross-reference modern broths (e.g., Asian fusion broths sometimes use peanut paste).
3. Confirm with chef; use allergen-isolated tools (purple board, fresh equipment).
4. (See [A-2.04-research.md](A-2.04-research.md) for full allergen protocol.)

### Scenario 4 — Vegetable stock for a vegan event
1. **Build umami without animal products:**
   - Dried shiitake or porcini for deep umami.
   - Kombu for glutamate.
   - Roasted tomato for complexity.
   - Roasted onion ends and garlic (with skins on) for colour and depth.
   - Splash of soy sauce or miso (off heat) for finishing umami.
2. **Sweat aromatics first** to develop flavour.
3. **Simmer 45–60 minutes only** — longer extraction makes vegetable stock muddy and bitter.
4. **Strain through fine mesh and cheesecloth** for clarity.
5. Cool rapidly.

### Scenario 5 — Using trim from the day's prep
1. **Collect cleanly:** onion butts, carrot peels (washed), celery butts and tops, mushroom stems, parsley stems, leek tops.
2. **Avoid:** brassicas, beet, potato peels, anything off-smelling.
3. **Wash all trim** under cold running water before use.
4. **Freeze if not used immediately** — keep "stock bag" in the freezer; once full, defrost and build a vegetable or chicken stock.
5. **Date the bag** — don't accumulate beyond practical shelf life (a month or two frozen is typical).

## 5. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Hot water start** — seals proteins, prevents extraction, clouds the stock.
- **Boiling instead of simmering** — emulsifies fat and proteins → cloudy, greasy, sometimes bitter stock.
- **Skipping the blanching step** for veal/beef bones — leaves blood and impurities that scum the surface continuously.
- **Salting the stock** — stocks are unseasoned by definition; salt belongs in the final dish where reductions concentrate it.
- **Overloading with vegetables** — vegetables can dominate flavour and dilute body; stick to ~10% of bone weight.
- **Using oily fish for fumet** — too strong, too fatty, never clarifies.
- **Including brassicas, potato peels, beet trim** — muddy or off-coloured stocks.
- **Long-simmering vegetable stocks** — turns muddy and bitter after ~ 60 min.
- **Boiling miso** — denatures aroma and probiotics.
- **Topping up with cold water mid-simmer** — drops temperature out of the extraction zone.
- **Not skimming** — fat and impurities reincorporate during simmering; results in cloudy, greasy stock.
- **Using rotten or near-rotten trim** — flavours concentrate; you can't fix it later.
- **Storing stock in a sealed lid before cooled** — cooling failure, *C. perfringens* risk.
- **Re-using stockpot bone-water (remouillage) as the primary stock** — weaker product; should be labelled as remouillage, not stock.
- **Not labelling stocks in storage** — easy to confuse white chicken with white veal; date and identify.

## 7. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with D-13.01.

1. **Stock = bone-based, unseasoned, foundation.** Broth = meat-based, seasoned, often eaten as-is.
2. **Five ingredient categories (Range of Variables):** bones, vegetables, aromatics, water, proteins.
3. **Mirepoix classical ratio:** **50% onion, 25% carrot, 25% celery by weight.**
4. **White mirepoix:** onion, celery, leek, parsnip (no carrot) — for **white** stocks.
5. **Types of stocks (Range of Variables):** **white, brown, court-bouillon, fumet** (plus vegetable, glace, remouillage, jus).
6. **Types of broths (Range of Variables):** **dashi, emulsified, clear, bone, miso.**
7. **Bones — cartilage content drives gelatin yield.** Knuckles, joints, feet, necks are top body sources.
8. **Collagen → gelatin** hydrolysis at ~ 70–80 °C over hours.
9. **Veal stock is the classical universal foundation** — neutral, high gelatin.
10. **Brown stock**: bones and mirepoix roasted; tomato product; Maillard reaction provides colour and depth.
11. **Fumet**: lean white-fish frames only; sweat in butter, add white wine, simmer 30–45 minutes max.
12. **Court-bouillon**: acidulated poaching liquid with mirepoix and aromatics; not a true stock.
13. **Dashi (ichiban)**: kombu + katsuobushi → glutamate + inosinate umami synergy; never boil katsuobushi.
14. **Cold water start** — always.
15. **Bones-to-water ratio:** roughly **1 : 1.5–2** by weight; cover bones by ~ 5 cm / 2 in.
16. **Mirepoix ratio:** **8–10% of bone weight** as a starting reference.
17. **Simmer times** (rough): veal/beef stocks 8–12 h; chicken 3–4 h; fish fumet 30–45 min; vegetable 45–60 min.
18. **Bouquet garni** vs **sachet d'épices** — fresh herb bundle vs cheesecloth spice pouch.
19. **Sensory assessment** — firmness, resilience, even colour, quiet smell; reject ammonia-smelling fish bones, slimy bones, bruised/sprouted vegetables.
20. **Receive bones at ≤ 4 °C / frozen ≤ −18 °C**; raw bone storage on bottom shelf; FIFO; use within 1–2 days fresh.
21. **Avoid for stock pots:** brassicas, potato peels, beet trim, off-smelling trim, oily fish.
22. **Trim management** controls cost; "stock bag" in the freezer is common practice.
23. **Dietary requirements** — vegan, halal, kosher, allergens (fish/shellfish/soy), gluten, sulphites, sodium, sustainability.
24. **Vegan stocks** rely on mushroom + kombu + tomato + roasted aromatics for umami.
25. **Halal/kosher** stocks require certified supplier chain and no pork.
26. **Food-safety after the pot:** Canadian cooling rule **60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h** — stocks are *C. perfringens* high-risk if cooled improperly.
27. **Never** salt stock during simmering — reductions concentrate; salt belongs in the final dish.
28. **Never boil** stock — gentle simmer for clarity.
29. **Never** top up with cold water mid-simmer — drops extraction temperature.
30. **Skim early, skim often** — fat and protein scum reincorporate if left.
