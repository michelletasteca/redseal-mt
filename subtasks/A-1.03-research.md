## Why Personal Hygiene Is the #1 Defence in Food Safety

The **food handler is the most common vector** for foodborne illness in commercial kitchens. According to Public Health Agency of Canada (PHAC) surveillance, **norovirus** and **bacterial pathogens carried by hands** (e.g., *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Salmonella*, *Shigella*, *E. coli*) cause the majority of restaurant-linked outbreaks. The single most cost-effective intervention against all of these is **proper handwashing combined with personal hygiene**.

Key microbiology to anchor everything in this subtask:

- **Resident flora** — microbes that live deep in skin pores and follicles. Cannot be fully removed; can be reduced.
- **Transient flora** — microbes picked up from surfaces (raw chicken, doorknobs, phones, money). These are the dangerous ones in a kitchen, and they wash off readily with **friction + soap + warm water**.
- **Fecal-oral route** — the dominant transmission path for *E. coli*, norovirus, Hepatitis A, *Salmonella*. One unwashed hand after the toilet can contaminate an entire service.
- **Asymptomatic carriers** — workers can shed *Staph aureus* from skin/nose, or norovirus for up to 2 weeks after symptoms end. This is why hygiene rules don't depend on "feeling sick".

## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### A-1.03.01P — Follow Handwashing Procedures

**Performance Criteria:** follow handwashing procedures
**Evidence of Attainment:** handwashing procedures are followed according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### The standard Canadian handwashing procedure
Provincial public health units and Health Canada/PHAC guidance describe substantially the same protocol. **[Regulatory + Best Practice]**

1. **Wet** hands with warm running potable water (≈ **38–43 °C / 100–110 °F**). Comfortable, not hot — too hot damages skin and breaks soap's surfactant action; too cold doesn't dissolve fats.
2. **Apply soap** (liquid, dispenser-fed; bar soap is not used in commercial kitchens).
3. **Lather and scrub for at least 20 seconds** — the "Happy Birthday twice" rule. Cover:
   - Palms and backs of hands
   - **Between fingers** (a frequently missed area)
   - **Under and around fingernails** (use a nail brush; this is where most transient flora hide)
   - **Wrists and forearms** up to the elbow when handling raw protein
4. **Rinse** thoroughly under warm running water, fingertips pointing **down** so contaminated water flows away from clean skin.
5. **Dry** with a **single-use paper towel** or a hot-air dryer where permitted. Cloth towels are prohibited at handwash sinks. **[Regulatory]**
6. **Use the paper towel to turn off the tap** and open the washroom door — the tap and door handle are re-contamination points.

**Total time:** target **40–60 seconds end-to-end**.

#### WHEN to wash — every food handler must know this list
**[Regulatory under all provincial public-health food regulations]**

- Before starting work and after every break.
- After using the washroom (this is non-negotiable).
- After handling raw meat, poultry, seafood, or eggs.
- Before and after handling ready-to-eat foods.
- After handling garbage, dirty dishes, or cleaning chemicals.
- After touching face, hair, mouth, nose, ears.
- After coughing, sneezing, blowing nose, or using a tissue.
- After eating, drinking, smoking, or chewing gum.
- After handling money or a phone.
- After handling animals or pets (where applicable, e.g., service animals on premises).
- After any task change between allergen-isolated and general production.
- Whenever gloves are changed, **before** putting on new ones.

#### The handwash sink is sacred
- Hand sinks are **for hands only**. **[Regulatory]**
- They must be:
  - Conveniently located in food prep, dish, and washroom areas.
  - Supplied with **hot and cold running water**, **liquid soap**, **single-use towels** (or hot-air dryer), and a **waste receptacle**.
  - **Never used** for rinsing tools, dumping mop water, thawing food, or filling pots.
- A handwash sink blocked, dirty, or used for other purposes is one of the **most-cited violations** during Canadian public-health inspections.

#### Gloves do not replace handwashing
- Hands **must be washed before donning gloves** — the sweaty inside of a glove is a bacterial culture environment.
- Gloves do **not** make hands sanitary; they make food **physically separated** from hands.
- **Change gloves and wash hands** between tasks (see A-1.02 for triggers).

#### Hand sanitizer — the supporting role, not the substitute
- **Alcohol-based hand rubs (60–80% ethanol or isopropanol)** kill many bacteria and most enveloped viruses, but are **less effective against norovirus and *Clostridium difficile* spores**.
- Sanitizer is acceptable **only on visibly clean hands** as a supplement.
- Sanitizer is **not a substitute** for washing after washroom use or after handling raw meat. **[Regulatory + PHAC guidance]**

> **Memory aid — "Wet, Lather 20, Rinse, Dry, Don't re-touch":** the five-step handwash skeleton.

#### Common mistakes
- Skipping the **nail-bed scrub** — this is where the most transient bacteria hide.
- Drying on the apron (re-contamination from kitchen surfaces).
- Touching the tap with bare hands after washing.
- Using one paper towel for both drying and turning off the tap (use a fresh one to dry first).
- Sanitizer-only "wash" after the washroom.
- Putting gloves on wet hands (encourages microbial growth, plus the gloves tear).

### A-1.03.02P — Maintain Good Personal Hygienic Conditions

**Performance Criteria:** maintain good personal hygienic conditions
**Evidence of Attainment:** good personal hygienic conditions are maintained according to company policy and jurisdictional regulations.

#### Daily hygiene baseline
From the Range of Variables: *frequent handwashing, wearing clean uniforms, wearing hair and beard nets, using single-use towels, using deodorant, using hair restraints, oral health, frequent bathing.*

- **Daily shower** before shift — removes overnight skin flora and odour.
- **Deodorant**, preferably **unscented or lightly scented** — strong perfumes/colognes are inappropriate around aroma-sensitive food and guests. **[Best Practice / Operational]**
- **Oral health** — brush teeth before shift; chewing gum and eating on the line are prohibited.
- **Clean uniform daily.** A uniform that has been through a service shift is contaminated — soiled with food protein, sweat, and microbes.
- **Trimmed, clean fingernails.** (See A-1.03.05P.)
- **No bare-hand contact** with ready-to-eat food where required by jurisdiction; otherwise pristine hand hygiene + utensils. **[Regulatory varies by province; many require utensils/gloves/tongs for ready-to-eat]**

#### Illness exclusion — the rule that protects the public
**[Regulatory under provincial public-health food premises regulations.]** A food handler must not handle food (and in some cases must not even be on premises) when experiencing:

- **Vomiting**
- **Diarrhea**
- **Jaundice** (yellowing of skin/eyes — can indicate Hepatitis A)
- **Fever with sore throat**
- **Open, infected, or weeping skin lesions** on hands or exposed skin
- **Confirmed or suspected foodborne illness** (e.g., norovirus, *Salmonella*, *Shigella*, Hep A, *E. coli* O157:H7, STEC)

**Return-to-work rules** (common Canadian convention, varies by province):
- **Vomiting/diarrhea** — typically symptom-free for **48 hours** before returning to food handling. **[Best Practice; some provinces specify exact intervals or require medical clearance]**
- **Confirmed reportable pathogens** — public-health clearance may be required.

This is one of the most heavily tested concepts on Red Seal: **"can this employee work today?"** is a fair-game scenario question.

#### Coughs, sneezes, and the production line
- Cough/sneeze into the **inside of the elbow**, not hands.
- Step away from food, exhale before turning back.
- Wash hands afterward.
- If symptoms are persistent, leave food production and inform the supervisor.

#### Skin conditions
- **Boils, infected cuts, fungal infections, weeping rashes** disqualify a worker from food contact until healed. **[Regulatory]**
- **Acne, dry skin** — not exclusionary, but workers should avoid touching the face.

#### Smoking, vaping, eating, drinking
- Only in **designated areas**, never in the kitchen or storage areas. **[Regulatory]**
- **Drinking water in the kitchen** — many ops allow a **lidded container with a straw**, kept below food-prep surfaces. **[Best Practice / Operational — provincial codes differ]**
- **Wash hands** after smoking, vaping, eating, drinking, or chewing gum.

### A-1.03.03P — Wear Clean Work Attire

**Performance Criteria:** wear clean work attire
**Evidence of Attainment:** clean work attire is worn.

#### The clean-uniform principle
A uniform's job is to be a **barrier** between the food handler's body/personal clothes and the food. Once a uniform is soiled with raw protein, blood, sweat, or food debris, it is a **fomite** — a contaminated surface that can transfer microbes back to food.

#### Uniform components (carry-over from A-1.02)
- Chef jacket
- Pants (loose, often houndstooth)
- Apron(s) — bib or waist
- Side towels (dry)
- Hat / skull cap / hairnet (see A-1.03.04P)
- Approved footwear

#### Best-practice handling
- **Change uniform** between shifts; never wear yesterday's jacket.
- **Change uniform** mid-shift if contaminated (oil splash, raw meat juice, vomit).
- **Apron off** when leaving the kitchen for the washroom, garbage, smoking break, or guest interaction.
- **Laundered separately from personal laundry**, hot wash; many operations contract a commercial linen service.
- **Aprons stored on hooks** when not in use, not on prep surfaces.
- **Street clothes stored in lockers**, not in the kitchen. **[Regulatory in most public-health codes]**
- **No personal sweater or hoodie over the chef jacket** — fibres, sleeve cuffs, and trim pose contamination risks.

#### Common mistakes
- Wiping dirty hands on the apron, then handling clean plates.
- Wearing the apron into the washroom.
- Using a side towel as a sweat rag, then as a pan-handle.
- Storing rolled-up dirty uniforms in lockers without laundering.
- Personal scarves, beanies, or fashion hats replacing approved headwear.

### A-1.03.04P — Wear Hair and Beard Nets and Hats

**Performance Criteria:** wear hair and beard nets and hats
**Evidence of Attainment:** hair and beard nets and hats are worn to reduce contamination risks according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### Why hair restraint is mandatory
- **Physical contamination** — guest finds a hair in food → enforceable public health complaint, brand damage, possible foodborne illness if hair carries pathogens.
- **Microbial reservoir** — hair, scalp, and beards carry *Staphylococcus aureus* and other skin flora.
- **Behavioural reinforcement** — wearing a hat reminds the worker not to touch their head during service.

#### What counts as effective restraint **[Regulatory across all Canadian provinces for food premises]**
- **Hat / skull cap / toque / hairnet** that **fully contains** the hair, including the hairline at the back of the neck.
- **Beard net or beard cover** for facial hair **longer than approximately 1 cm**, or any beard styled outward.
- **Bandana or wrap** in some operations, provided it fully covers hair.
- **Long hair tied back AND covered** — a ponytail alone is not sufficient.

#### Practical tips
- Put on hair restraint **before** putting on the uniform, so any loose hairs fall onto the body and away from food.
- Replace hairnet daily; replace immediately if damaged.
- Don't adjust hair restraint during service — if you do, wash hands.
- Eyebrow and eyelash hairs are short and pose minor risk, but the rule still applies broadly to facial hair growing outward.

### A-1.03.05P — Ensure Nails Are Short

**Performance Criteria:** ensure nails are short
**Evidence of Attainment:** nails are kept short according to company policy and jurisdictional regulations.

#### Why nails matter scientifically
Under-nail spaces (the **subungual area**) are the **most-contaminated region of the hand** in microbiology studies. Bacteria, soil, and food residue accumulate there and resist routine handwashing. Long nails:
- Trap pathogens — including fecal bacteria, *Pseudomonas*, *Staphylococcus*.
- Tear gloves from inside (creating a hidden contamination route).
- Increase risk of nail breaking and contaminating food (physical hazard).

#### The Canadian standard **[Regulatory or Best Practice depending on province]**
- **Nails trimmed** so the tip does not extend past the fingertip.
- **Nails clean** — scrubbed with a nail brush during handwashing.
- **No nail polish** while handling food. Polish can chip into food and harbours bacteria in cracks.
- **No artificial nails / acrylics / gel extensions / nail jewellery** while handling food. The artificial-nail-to-natural-nail interface is a known *Pseudomonas* reservoir and is a top citation in food-handler audits.
- Some operations permit polish/artificial nails **only if completely covered by gloves** that are changed frequently, but this is a tighter operational policy and **not the regulatory baseline**.

#### Operational tips
- Keep a **personal nail kit** (clippers, file) in the locker, not in the kitchen.
- Trim and file at home before shift.
- Avoid biting nails on shift (introduces oral flora, increases hangnail-and-bandage cycles).

### A-1.03.06P — Remove Jewelry and Accessories

**Performance Criteria:** remove jewelry and accessories
**Evidence of Attainment:** jewelry and accessories are removed to reduce contamination risks according to company policy and jurisdictional regulations.

#### Why jewellery is restricted
- **Physical contamination** — gemstones, earrings, charms, watch components can fall into food. These are also **foreign-body hazards** (recall driver in larger operations).
- **Microbial reservoir** — the underside of rings, watch bands, and bracelets is essentially unwashable. Skin bacteria multiply in the warm, moist micro-environment.
- **Cross-contact / injury risk** — rings can catch on equipment, causing degloving injuries; piercings near food zones can dislodge.

#### Standard rule across Canadian food premises **[Regulatory]**
Jewellery and accessories **are not permitted** while handling food, with one common exception:
- **A plain wedding band** (no stones, no engraving cavities) is typically allowed. Some operations also require it to be removed.

Items that must be removed:
- Watches and fitness trackers (smartwatches included).
- Rings with stones, engravings, or settings.
- Bracelets, friendship bands, charm bracelets.
- Necklaces hanging outside the uniform (a necklace tucked inside may be tolerated in some ops, but pendants on long chains are foreign-object risks).
- Visible piercings on the face — many ops allow stud earrings or small piercings only if covered; nose rings, eyebrow rings, lip rings often must be removed during shift.
- Hanging earrings and large hoops.
- **Hearing aids and medical devices** — generally permitted but should be checked for security.

#### Practical tips
- Store jewellery in a locker, not in pockets where it can fall out.
- A laminated photo or sticker of removed jewellery on the locker can be a memory cue.
- Some couples switch to silicone wedding bands for kitchen work — apprentice-friendly and degloving-resistant.

### A-1.03.07P — Cover All Exposed Cuts and Wounds

**Performance Criteria:** cover all exposed cuts and wounds
**Evidence of Attainment:** all exposed cuts and wounds are covered according to jurisdictional regulations.

#### Why this is critical
- **Open wounds carry *Staphylococcus aureus***, which produces a heat-stable enterotoxin. Once produced in food, cooking will not destroy it. *S. aureus* food poisoning is a textbook 1–6-hour-onset illness traced to mishandled food prepared by a cook with an infected cut.
- **Blood and body fluids** in food are an obvious physical and microbiological contamination.
- **Cuts collect food debris**, slow healing, and make the cook miserable.

#### The Canadian protocol **[Best Practice — substantially mandated by public-health regulations]**

1. **Stop work.** Move away from food and exposed surfaces.
2. **Wash and disinfect** the wound. Direct pressure to stop bleeding.
3. **Apply a waterproof, brightly coloured bandage** — **blue** is the kitchen standard because:
   - Blue rarely occurs in natural food → visible if it falls off.
   - **Metal-detectable blue bandages** allow detection in operations with metal detectors (industrial bakeries, large processors).
4. **Cover the bandage with a single-use glove** (disposable nitrile) if the wound is on a hand or finger. The glove provides a secondary barrier and keeps the bandage in place.
5. **Change glove and re-check bandage** frequently — every ~2 hours, after any wetting, or if dislodged.
6. **Document significant injuries** in the workplace incident log (OH&S requirement in most provinces).
7. **Severe cuts** — apply pressure with a clean dressing, elevate, call for help. Tendon, nerve, or arterial injury → emergency care.

#### Wounds that disqualify a worker from food contact **[Regulatory]**
- **Infected wounds** (red, swollen, hot, draining pus).
- **Boils, abscesses.**
- **Weeping rashes or dermatitis** on hands/forearms.

A cook with an infected wound should be reassigned to a non-food-contact role (dish pit, storeroom) or sent home, until medically cleared.

#### Common mistakes
- Beige/skin-colour bandage — invisible if it falls into food.
- No glove over the bandage on the hand.
- Continuing to work with a bleeding cut.
- Re-using a wet, peeling bandage rather than replacing.

## 2. Knowledge Objectives — Deeper Dive

### A-1.03.01L — Hygienic Practices & Terminology

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of personal hygiene principles and practices.

#### Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Pathogen** | A disease-causing microorganism (bacteria, virus, parasite, prion, fungus). |
| **Foodborne illness** | Illness resulting from consumption of contaminated food. |
| **Infection** | Illness caused by ingestion of live pathogens that multiply in the body (e.g., *Salmonella*, norovirus). |
| **Intoxication** | Illness caused by toxins pre-formed in food (e.g., *Staph aureus* enterotoxin, *Bacillus cereus* emetic toxin, botulinum toxin). |
| **Toxin-mediated infection** | Pathogen ingested, then produces toxins in the gut (e.g., *Clostridium perfringens*, STEC). |
| **Carrier (asymptomatic)** | A person harbouring a pathogen with no symptoms — still infectious. |
| **Fecal-oral route** | Pathogen transmission from feces of one host to mouth of another, typically via unwashed hands. |
| **Cross-contamination** | Transfer of pathogens or allergens from one food/surface/utensil to another. |
| **Cross-contact** | Allergen-specific cross-contamination (e.g., peanut residue on knife → peanut-free dish). |
| **Resident vs. transient flora** | Permanent vs. picked-up skin microbes. |
| **TCS / PHF** | Time/Temperature Control for Safety (food) — foods that support pathogen growth. |
| **Subungual area** | The space under the fingernails — highest microbial load on the hand. |
| **Fomite** | A surface or object that carries pathogens (apron, doorknob, phone). |
| **Sanitize** | Reduce pathogens on a clean surface to a safe level (typically 99.999% / 5-log). |
| **Hand antisepsis** | Reducing microbes on hands using antimicrobial soap or sanitizer. |

#### Hygienic practices summary
From the Range of Variables:

| Practice | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Frequent handwashing** | Removes transient flora — the #1 control on the fecal-oral route. |
| **Clean uniforms** | Prevents the uniform from becoming a fomite. |
| **Hair and beard nets** | Prevents physical and microbial contamination. |
| **Single-use towels** | Cloth towels at handwash sinks re-contaminate hands. |
| **Deodorant** | Sweat odour management; unscented preferred. |
| **Hair restraints** | Doubled-up with hairnet/hat. |
| **Oral health** | Brush, don't touch mouth, no gum in production. |
| **Frequent bathing** | Baseline skin flora control. |

### A-1.03.02L — Procedures for Personal Hygiene and Clean Apparel

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures to maintain personal hygiene.

#### Apparel handling procedure (typical Canadian operation)
1. Arrive in street clothes; store in locker.
2. **Wash hands** before donning uniform.
3. Put on **hair restraint first**, then chef jacket, then apron.
4. Inspect apparel: no stains, no holes, fastenings intact.
5. Tie apron strings securely (front knot for quick release).
6. Mid-shift: change apron when contaminated; remove apron when leaving kitchen.
7. End of shift: place soiled uniform in laundry hamper; don't take home in personal bag if commercial laundry is provided.

#### Handwashing procedure recap
See A-1.03.01P. The 6-step **Wet → Soap → Lather 20s → Rinse → Dry single-use → Paper-towel tap-off** sequence is the most testable element.

#### Procedure for a glove change between tasks
1. Remove old gloves (peel from wrist, ball inside-out).
2. Dispose in waste — never reuse.
3. **Wash hands** — even though gloves were worn.
4. Dry thoroughly.
5. Don new gloves.
6. Resume work.

### A-1.03.03L — Regulatory Knowledge

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of jurisdictional regulations pertaining to health and safety.

#### Regulatory landscape (food-handler personal hygiene)

| Authority | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Health Canada** | Sets national food-safety policy and guidance documents. |
| **CFIA — *Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR)*** | Federally-registered operators must have a **Preventive Control Plan (PCP)** addressing personal hygiene of food handlers. |
| **Provincial Public Health (food premises regulations)** | Restaurants and food-service establishments. Examples: Ontario *Food Premises Regulation (O. Reg. 493/17)*; BC *Food Premises Regulation*; Alberta *Food Regulation (AR 31/2006)*; Québec *Règlement sur les aliments (MAPAQ)*. **[Regulatory]** |
| **Provincial Public Health Acts** | Outbreak reporting, communicable disease control, exclusion of ill workers. |
| **Provincial OH&S** | Worker health and safety, including PPE, JHSC, first aid, illness-related work refusals. |
| **Municipal health authority** | On-site inspection of food premises. |

#### Common requirements across provincial food regulations
- Operators must ensure food handlers practice personal hygiene to prevent contamination.
- Food handlers must wash hands before handling food and at appropriate times.
- Workers with communicable disease symptoms (vomiting/diarrhea/jaundice/etc.) must not handle food.
- Hair restraint required.
- Smoking prohibited in food-handling areas.
- Clean clothing and washable footwear required.
- A handwash sink with hot/cold water, liquid soap, and single-use towels must be provided and accessible.

#### Mandatory food handler certification (varies)
Many provinces require at least one certified food handler on duty:
- **Ontario:** locally approved certification programs.
- **British Columbia:** FoodSafe Level 1 widely recognized.
- **Alberta:** approved equivalents.
- **Québec:** MAPAQ-recognized course; managers and/or one designate.
- **Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Atlantic provinces:** varying approved programs.

> The **exact program name and per-shift requirement varies by province** — confirm with the local public health unit. **[Regulatory — jurisdiction-specific]**

#### Allergen labelling and cross-contact (related but worth knowing)
Federal labelling under the *Food and Drug Regulations* requires declaration of Canada's **priority allergens, gluten sources, and added sulphites** ≥ 10 ppm. **[Regulatory]** In foodservice, the equivalent obligation manifests as:
- Honest disclosure to guests asking about allergens.
- Operational controls to prevent cross-contact (dedicated gloves/utensils/cutting boards).

## 3. Microbiology in 90 Seconds — Why Hygiene Works

| Pathogen | Source | Onset | Why hygiene matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Norovirus** | Vomit, feces, contaminated hands/surfaces | 12–48 h | Extremely contagious, low infectious dose (<100 particles), survives sanitizers; **handwashing with soap is the primary control** (alcohol sanitizer less effective). |
| **Salmonella** | Raw poultry, eggs, fecal contamination | 6–72 h | Hands after handling raw poultry are the #1 transmission. |
| ***E. coli* O157:H7 / STEC** | Raw ground beef, contaminated produce, fecal route | 1–10 d | Very low infectious dose; bandage + handwash discipline crucial. |
| ***Staphylococcus aureus*** | Nose, skin, cuts, boils of food handlers | 1–6 h (toxin) | Heat-stable toxin produced in temperature-abused food; **cover wounds**. |
| ***Shigella*** | Fecal-oral, infected handler | 1–7 d | Highly contagious; **exclude ill workers**. |
| **Hepatitis A** | Fecal-oral, infected handler | 15–50 d | **Vaccination** common for food handlers; jaundice excludes work. |
| ***Listeria monocytogenes*** | RTE foods, deli, soft cheese, smoked fish | days–weeks | Grows at refrigeration temps; bandage and apron discipline matter on RTE lines. |
| ***Bacillus cereus*** | Cooked rice, starches held warm | 1–16 h | Spores survive cooking; **temperature** is the primary control, but hygiene supports it. |
| ***Clostridium perfringens*** | Cooked meats held in danger zone | 6–24 h | Spores germinate during slow cooling; hygiene + cooling control. |

> Anchor numbers (carry-over): **Danger Zone 4–60 °C; cool 60→20 °C in 2 h, 20→4 °C in 4 h; reheat to 74 °C within 2 h; hot hold ≥ 60 °C; cold hold ≤ 4 °C.**

## 4. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — You return from the washroom
1. Remove apron in the kitchen *before* leaving for the washroom (if not already done).
2. Wash hands in the washroom **and again at the kitchen handwash sink** on return — two-stage wash is the Canadian best-practice norm.
3. Don a fresh apron if needed.
4. Sanitize knife handles/cutting board edges if you'd been on a station with raw protein.

### Scenario 2 — You cut your finger while breaking down chicken
1. Step away from the food zone.
2. Discard contaminated chicken in immediate splash radius (judgement call; when in doubt, discard).
3. Wash and dress the wound: blue bandage + glove.
4. Sanitize knife handle, board, and station.
5. Change uniform if blood-stained.
6. Wash hands thoroughly (with brush under nails).
7. Report to supervisor; log injury.

### Scenario 3 — A colleague says they have diarrhea but want to finish the shift
- Politely but firmly: **they must go home**, and not handle food until **48 hours symptom-free** (or per local public-health rule).
- Inform the chef / manager.
- If symptoms persist > 48 hours or include vomiting + fever, recommend medical attention.
- Sanitize their station thoroughly (norovirus survives many sanitizers — chlorine at higher concentrations is more effective).
- Document.

### Scenario 4 — Apprentice arrives with bright nail polish and a stud ring on the index finger
- Polish must be removed before handling food (or covered fully by gloves, per operation policy — but baseline is no polish).
- Ring must be removed.
- Coach the apprentice on **why** (subungual *Pseudomonas*, foreign-object risk).
- Provide nail polish remover wipes from first-aid kit if available; otherwise reassign to a dish-pit task until polish is removed.

## 5. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **"My gloves keep food clean, so I don't need to wash my hands."** False — washing must occur *before* donning, and gloves must be changed with handwashing in between.
- **"Hand sanitizer is just as good as washing."** Not for norovirus, not after the washroom, not for visibly soiled hands.
- **"I always wear a ring with my watch — they're never an issue."** Both harbour bacteria and pose foreign-object risk.
- **"The bandage is fine without a glove on top."** Wet, peeling bandages dislodge into food.
- **"It's just a small cough, I can stay."** A small cough may be the early sign of a contagious illness.
- **"My nails are long but clean."** The subungual area is the most-contaminated region of the hand regardless of scrubbing.
- **"This apron is just a bit dirty, it's fine for one more service."** A protein-soiled apron is a fomite.
- **"My hair is tied back, I don't need a hat."** Tied-back hair still sheds — full coverage is the rule.
- **"I always wash my hands at the prep sink because the hand sink is far."** Prep sinks for handwashing = cross-contamination of food + violation.
- **"My fingernail polish is chip-proof."** Polish always degrades under hot water, knife work, and dishwashing.

| **HANDS-ONLY SINK** | Handwash sink for hands only | Sink discipline |
| **TWO-STAGE WASH** | Wash in washroom + wash at kitchen hand sink | Washroom return |

## 7. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with A-1.03.

1. **Handwashing duration:** at least **20 seconds** scrub, total **40–60 seconds** end-to-end, warm water (**38–43 °C / 100–110 °F**), liquid soap, single-use towel, paper-towel-off the tap.
2. **When to wash:** before starting work, after washroom, between raw and ready-to-eat, after garbage, after handling face/hair/phone/money, before/after gloves, after coughing/sneezing/eating.
3. **Gloves do not replace handwashing.** Wash → dry → glove.
4. **Hand sanitizer** (60–80% alcohol) supplements but does not replace washing — and is **less effective against norovirus**.
5. **Hand sinks are for hands only**, must be stocked with hot/cold water, soap, and single-use towels.
6. **Illness exclusion:** vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice, fever + sore throat, infected wounds, confirmed foodborne pathogens → no food handling. Common return-to-work: **48 hours symptom-free**.
7. **Cuts and wounds:** **blue waterproof bandage + glove over**, change frequently, infected wounds disqualify from food contact.
8. **Hair restraint mandatory** — hat, hairnet, or full-cover wrap; beard net for facial hair >~1 cm. **[Regulatory]**
9. **Nails:** trimmed to fingertip, clean, **no polish, no artificial nails** when handling food.
10. **Jewellery:** **none except plain wedding band** when handling food. No watches/fitness trackers, no stones, no hanging items.
11. **No perfume / strong cologne** — affects food aroma and guest allergies.
12. **Clean uniform daily**; apron off when leaving the kitchen; uniforms stored separately from street clothes.
13. **No eating, drinking (uncontrolled), smoking, vaping, chewing gum** in food production areas. Lidded water with straw often permitted.
14. **Cough/sneeze into elbow**, step away from food, wash hands.
15. **Fecal-oral route** is the primary transmission for norovirus, *E. coli* O157:H7, *Salmonella*, *Shigella*, Hep A — hygiene is the primary defence.
16. ***Staphylococcus aureus*** produces a **heat-stable toxin** — cooking does NOT inactivate it. Cover wounds and exclude infected workers.
17. **Subungual area** under the nails holds the most transient flora — scrub with nail brush.
18. **Asymptomatic carriers** can shed norovirus for ~2 weeks post-recovery — hygiene rules don't depend on feeling sick.
19. **Provincial public-health food premises regulations** govern personal hygiene at restaurants; **CFIA / SFCR** govern federal processors via Preventive Control Plans.
20. **Mandatory food handler certification** varies by province (e.g., FoodSafe in BC, MAPAQ-recognized in QC).
21. **Cross-contact (allergens)** requires the same hand-hygiene and utensil-change discipline as raw-to-cooked transitions.
22. **Two-stage handwash after the washroom** (washroom + kitchen sink) is a Canadian best-practice norm.
23. **Apron** is a fomite — change when soiled, never wear into the washroom.
24. **Anchor temperatures (carry-over):** danger zone **4–60 °C**; reheat **74 °C** within 2 h; cool 60→20 °C in 2 h, 20→4 °C in 4 h.
25. **Blue, metal-detectable bandages** are the food-industry standard.
