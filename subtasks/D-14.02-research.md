## Why Processing Discipline Matters in Soup Production

Soups are unforgiving in the bowl. Every cut, weight, and prep decision shows up:

- **Uneven brunoise** in a consommé reveals careless knife work — the soup is the showcase.
- **Wrong-size dice** for cream of broccoli changes cook time; some pieces dissolve, others stay raw.
- **Off-portion ingredient ratios** (too much featured solid, not enough liquid) produces a stew, not a soup.
- **Cold-chain or cross-contamination lapses during processing** carry directly into a finished product that often won't be re-cooked before service (cold soups especially).

The processing stage is where the cook turns selected raw materials into the **measured, cut, staged, and ready-for-pot components** the soup actually needs.

### D-14.02.01P — Select and Use Tools and Equipment

**Performance Criteria:** select and use tools and equipment
**Evidence of Attainment:** tools and equipment are selected and used according to recipe specifications.

#### The soup-processing toolkit (Range of Variables, expanded)

##### Scales

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **Digital benchtop scale (≤ 5 kg)** | Mirepoix, aromatics, dairy, dry goods |
| **Digital platform scale (15–30 kg)** | Featured vegetables/legumes in bulk; large batches |
| **Tare function** | Essential — zero the container weight |
| **Dual unit g/oz/lb** | Convenient for recipes in mixed unit systems |

**Why weight over volume** (carry-over from [D-13.02-research.md](D-13.02-research.md)): consistency, scalability, translatability across kitchens. A "cup of chopped onion" varies; 400 g is 400 g.

##### Cutting boards

| Colour | Use |
|---|---|
| **Green** | Produce — most soup vegetable prep happens here |
| **Yellow** | Raw poultry |
| **Red** | Raw red meat |
| **Blue** | Raw seafood |
| **Brown** | Cooked meats |
| **White** | Dairy, bread, neutral |
| **Purple** | Allergen-isolated production |

> **[Best Practice]** Canadian industry-standard colour scheme. Always sanitize between proteins and between raw and ready-to-eat. (See [A-2.04-research.md](A-2.04-research.md).)

##### Knives

| Knife | Use in soup processing |
|---|---|
| **Chef's knife (20–25 cm)** | The everyday workhorse — mirepoix, dicing, slicing, chopping |
| **Paring knife** | Trimming, peeling, fine work, removing eyes from potatoes |
| **Boning knife** | Trimming proteins for clarifying raft or specialty broths |
| **Slicer / carving knife** | Slicing meats for clear soups, chowders |
| **Mandoline** | Uniform thin slices, julienne, brunoise (with the cut-resistant glove + guard — see [A-1.02-research.md](A-1.02-research.md)) |
| **Bench scraper** | Transferring cuts from board to bowl quickly and cleanly |

**Knife condition:** sharp knives cut accurately and reduce slip-related injuries. Dull knives are more dangerous — more force, more slippage, more cuts. **[Best Practice / OH&S]**

##### Whisks

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **Balloon whisk** | Roux + liquid incorporation; foaming cream; smooth mixing |
| **French whisk (narrower head)** | Stirring while reducing; reaching into corners; sauce work |
| **Flat / "saucier" whisk** | Sauce making in shallow pans; deglazing |
| **Coiled / "spring" whisk** | Small-batch mixing of vinaigrettes, slurries |

##### Food processors and blending equipment

| Tool | Use | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Stand blender (counter-top)** | Puréeing cooked vegetables, cold soups (gazpacho), emulsifying | Hot-soup safety: never fill more than ⅔ full, vent the lid, drape a towel — steam can blow the lid off; hot liquid burns |
| **Immersion blender ("stick" / "burr mixer")** | In-pot purée of cream soups, purée soups; quick clean-up | Lockout when cleaning; submerge before turning on; lift the cup over the pot to break vacuum, then off |
| **Food processor (bowl + S-blade)** | Chopping, puréeing thick mixtures, making bread crumbs, brunoise in some operations | Use the pulse function for control; lockout for blade removal (two-hand technique) |
| **Vertical Cutter Mixer (VCM, Robot-Coupe)** | High-volume puréeing, soup base production | Industrial-grade; cleaned per manufacturer; allergen-isolated cleaning between productions |
| **Standing mixer with grinding attachment** | Grinding meat for consommé clarification raft | Pre-chill meat and grinder parts ≤ 4 °C to keep fat intact |

> ⚠️ **Hot-liquid blending safety:** Never fill a stand blender past ⅔. Pulse rather than running continuously. Vent the lid (most have a removable centre piece) with a folded towel over the opening. Hot liquid expands and steam pressure can blow the lid off — serious burn risk. **[Operational / OH&S]**

##### Cheesecloth

| Use in soup processing | How |
|---|---|
| **Sachet d'épices** | Wrap herbs and spices, tie with twine for easy retrieval |
| **Final straining of consommé** | Cheesecloth-lined fine chinois removes the finest particulates |
| **Straining puréed soups** | For ultra-smooth velouté or bisque — pass through cheesecloth-lined sieve |
| **Squeezing pulp** | For vegetable juice extractions in some modern soup work |

**Use food-grade cheesecloth.** Rinse before first use to remove starch/sizing. **[Best Practice]**

##### Additional commonly used items

| Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| **Stockpot / soup pot** | Tall-and-narrow for stocks; **wider and lower** pots often preferred for soup cooking (better evaporation, easier stirring) |
| **Sauce pots, saucepans** | Smaller-batch soups; sauce reductions |
| **China cap and fine chinois** | Staged straining (carry-over from D-13) |
| **Spider / mesh skimmer** | Lifting solids out of broth |
| **Ladle (graduated)** | Portioning; mixing |
| **Heat-resistant rubber spatula** | Scraping pots clean; stirring |
| **Wooden spoon / silicone spatula** | Stirring without scratching |
| **Hotel pans (shallow, ≤ 5 cm deep)** | Cooling staged ingredients and the finished soup |
| **Cambros / food storage containers** | Holding processed ingredients pre-service |
| **Probe thermometer** | Verifying ingredient temperature; later, verifying soup temperatures |
| **Strainers and sieves** | Various mesh sizes for purées and finishings |
| **Tasting spoons (sanitized, single-use)** | Quality control during processing |

#### Tool-to-task matching by soup type

| Soup type | Critical tools |
|---|---|
| **Clear** | Sharp knives for precise garnish cuts, china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth, ladle |
| **Consommé** | Grinder (meat for raft), bowl scale, whisk, fine chinois + cheesecloth |
| **Cream** | Roux pan, whisk, immersion blender or stand blender, fine sieve, dairy thermometer |
| **Purée** | Stockpot, immersion blender or stand blender, fine sieve or chinois, ladle |
| **Specialty** | Cuisine-specific (wok, donabe, tagine); whisk, mortar and pestle for spice pastes |
| **Cold** | Stand blender for cold purées, sieve, ice bath bowl, refrigerated storage |

### D-14.02.02P — Calculate Quantity of Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** calculate quantity of ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** quantity of ingredients for yield are calculated according to recipe specifications.

#### Standard Canadian soup portion sizes

| Course | Typical portion |
|---|---|
| **Appetizer / first-course soup** | **180–250 mL (6–8 fl oz)** |
| **Main-course soup** (lunch entrée) | **350–500 mL (12–16 fl oz)** |
| **Cup of soup (side / starter)** | **180–240 mL (6–8 fl oz)** |
| **Bowl of soup** | **300–400 mL (10–14 fl oz)** |
| **Tasting / amuse** | **30–60 mL (1–2 fl oz)** |

> **[Operational]** Exact portion sizes are operation-specific and vary by menu pricing, service style, and bowl design.

#### Yield math for soup

##### Step 1 — Define the working yield

Working yield = (number of portions) × (portion size) × (1 + buffer).

Example: 40 portions of cream of mushroom × 200 mL × 1.15 buffer = **9.2 L** target finished yield.

##### Step 2 — Account for processing losses

| Loss source | Typical % |
|---|---|
| **Vegetable trim** (peels, ends, woody parts) | 10–25% depending on vegetable |
| **Pre-cook reduction** (sweating, sautéing) | 5–10% |
| **Final reduction** (some soups reduced for flavour concentration) | 10–25% depending on intent |
| **Straining loss** (pulp left in chinois) | 5–10% |

A common rule of thumb for cream and purée soups: **start with ~ 1.2–1.3 × final yield in liquid + featured solids** to cover total processing losses.

##### Step 3 — Reverse-calculate ingredient weights

For a cream-soup recipe scaled from a base of 10 portions to 40 portions:
- **Multiplier = 40 ÷ 10 = 4**.
- Apply to every ingredient line.
- Round to practical quantities (e.g., 1.27 kg → 1.25 kg or 1.3 kg).

#### Standard cream-soup ratio reference (apprentice baseline)

For a classical cream soup (per litre of finished soup, approximate):

| Component | Weight or Volume |
|---|---|
| **Stock base** | ~ 700 mL |
| **Featured vegetable** | ~ 300–400 g |
| **Aromatics (onion, leek, garlic)** | ~ 80–120 g |
| **Roux (50:50 butter:flour by weight)** | ~ 30–60 g each (5–10% of stock for medium body) |
| **Heavy cream (≥ 33% MF)** | ~ 150–250 mL (10–25% of total) |
| **Seasoning (salt, white pepper, optional nutmeg)** | To taste at the end |

> **[Best Practice]** Ratios vary by recipe and house standard. Apprentices should learn their workplace's reference and understand the underlying balance.

#### Unit conversions (recap from D-13.02)
- **1 L water ≈ 1 kg**
- **1 fl oz ≈ 30 mL**
- **1 oz dry ≈ 28.35 g**
- **1 lb ≈ 453.6 g**
- **°C = (°F − 32) × 5/9**

### D-14.02.03P — Gather Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** gather ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** individual ingredients are gathered and accessible in a sequential manner according to recipe specifications.

#### Mise en place for soup production

**Mise en place** ("everything in place") for soup means:

1. **Read the recipe end-to-end** before gathering anything.
2. **List every component by category** (base, featured solid, aromatics, thickeners, finishing).
3. **Lay out tools** in the order they'll be used.
4. **Pre-portion** by weight into clearly labelled containers.
5. **Verify dietary flags** for the service (allergens, vegetarian, halal, etc.).
6. **Sequence the ingredients** in the order they enter the pot.

#### Sequential gathering — example for cream of broccoli soup

| Order | Item | Why this order |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Broccoli (washed, cut, weighed) | Featured solid; cooks first |
| 2 | Onion, leek, garlic (cut and weighed) | Aromatics; sweated before the main cook |
| 3 | Butter and flour (weighed separately) | Roux components; built early |
| 4 | Chicken or vegetable stock (measured) | Liquid; added after roux |
| 5 | Heavy cream (measured) | Finishing; added near end of cook |
| 6 | Salt, white pepper, nutmeg | Seasoning; final adjustment |
| 7 | Fresh garnish (chive, parsley, croutons) | Plated at service |
| 8 | Immersion blender / stand blender | Ready for purée step |
| 9 | Fine chinois + cheesecloth | Ready for final straining |
| 10 | Cooling pans + ice bath | Pre-staged for end-of-cook rapid cooling |

#### Why sequential gathering matters

- **No mid-process scrambling** → reduces food-safety errors (forgetting sanitation, leaving items in danger zone).
- **Better quality** — every step happens at its optimal moment.
- **Faster overall** — parallel readiness beats serial chaos.
- **Less fatigue** — a major source of kitchen injury.

> **Memory aid — "MEP" (Mise En Place):** *Mental prep, Equipment ready, Pre-measured.*

### D-14.02.04P — Measure Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** measure ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** ingredients are measured according to recipe specifications.

#### Weight vs volume — the professional default

| Ingredient | Best measure |
|---|---|
| **Featured vegetables and meats** | Weight (g, kg) |
| **Aromatics (mirepoix, garlic, herbs)** | Weight (g) |
| **Flour, sugar, dry seasonings in bulk** | Weight (g) |
| **Stock and liquids** | Volume (L, mL) is standard; weight equivalent fine |
| **Cream and dairy** | Volume (mL) is fine; weight is more precise |
| **Butter** | Weight (g) — pat sizes are inconsistent |
| **Salt and spices in small quantities** | Tsp/tbsp acceptable; weight for precision |
| **Wine and acidic finishing** | Volume (mL) |

#### Measuring discipline

- **Tare every container** before weighing.
- **Use clean containers** — moisture, residual product, or other ingredients throw off the reading.
- **Pre-portion liquids** in graduated measuring cups for cooking flow.
- **Measure salt last** — easier to add than remove.

#### Salt and seasoning measurement
Stocks are unseasoned (carry-over from D-13). **Soups are seasoned** — finish-of-cook salt is part of the recipe but should be:
- **Added in stages**, tasted between additions.
- **Held back for the final adjust** at service temperature (cream soups taste different hot vs warm).
- **Cross-checked against reductions** — if the soup will reduce further, undersalt initially.

## 2. Knowledge Objectives — Deeper Dive

### D-14.02.01L — Soups and Their Applications (Processing Lens)

#### Factors to consider when processing ingredients

| Factor | Processing implication |
|---|---|
| **Freshness** | Sensory check at retrieval; process within 24 h of receipt for delicate items |
| **Cost** | Manage trim; scale batch size to avoid surplus; use scraps cleverly (mushroom stems for cream of mushroom) |
| **Fat content** | Trim excess fat from proteins for clarification rafts; reserve fat for sautéing aromatics |
| **Intended application** | Cut sizes match cook time and presentation: brunoise for consommé, rough chop for purée |
| **Type of soup** | Brunoise vegetables for clear / consommé garnish; rough chop for purée and cream; raw and very fine for cold |
| **Managing trim** | Clean trim feeds future stocks; allergen-isolated trim handled separately |
| **Availability** | Substitute thoughtfully; specialty Asian ingredients may have allergen statements to verify |

#### Serving temperatures by type of soup (recap from D-14.01)

| Soup type | Serving target |
|---|---|
| **Clear** | > 70 °C |
| **Consommé** | > 75 °C |
| **Cream / velouté** | 70–75 °C |
| **Puréed** | 70–75 °C |
| **Specialty hot** | > 75 °C; bowl preheated |
| **Cold** | ≤ 4 °C |

**Hot hold ≥ 60 °C; cold hold ≤ 4 °C; reheat to 74 °C within 2 h.** **[Regulatory under provincial public-health food premises regulations]**

#### Dietary requirements (carry-over from D-14.01)

- **Cultural / religious:** halal, kosher, Hindu, Jain
- **Allergies (Health Canada priority allergens):** eggs, milk, mustard, peanuts, crustaceans/molluscs, fish, sesame, soy, sulphites, tree nuts, wheat/triticale
- **Ethical:** vegetarian, vegan
- **Health:** celiac/gluten-free, diabetic, low-sodium, renal
- **Environmental:** sustainable seafood, local, seasonal
- **Preferences:** umami-forward, spicy, mild

### D-14.02.02L — Procedures Used to Process Ingredients for Soups

**Learning Outcomes:** demonstrate knowledge of procedures used to process ingredients for soups.

#### Knife cuts and standard sizes — the soup garnish reference

| Cut | Approximate dimensions | When used |
|---|---|---|
| **Fine brunoise** | 1.5 mm × 1.5 mm × 1.5 mm cube | Elegant consommé garnish |
| **Brunoise** | **3 mm × 3 mm × 3 mm cube** | Standard consommé garnish, clear soup garnish |
| **Small dice** | 6 mm cube | Cream soups, chowders |
| **Medium dice** | 1.2 cm cube | Hearty soups, stews |
| **Large dice (parmentier)** | 2 cm cube | Rustic soups, garbure |
| **Julienne** | 3 mm × 3 mm × 5 cm matchstick | Clear soup, consommé, decorative garnish |
| **Fine julienne (allumette)** | 1.5 mm × 1.5 mm × 5 cm | Refined consommé garnish |
| **Paysanne** | Thin flat squares ~ 1.2 cm | Country/peasant soups, minestrone |
| **Chiffonade** | Ribbon shreds of leafy greens / herbs | Finishing garnish for clear soups |
| **Tourné** | 7-sided football-shape carved vegetable | Garbure, presentation work |
| **Mince** | Very fine, irregular | Aromatics like garlic, ginger |

**Uniformity matters:** all pieces of a given cut should cook in the same time; uneven cuts mean some pieces overcook while others undercook.

#### Vegetable preparation procedures

##### Washing
- **All produce washed under cold running potable water.** **[Regulatory under public-health codes]**
- Leeks: split lengthwise, fan under running water to flush soil from layers.
- Mushrooms: brush dry; rinse quickly only if needed (they absorb water).
- Leafy greens: cold-water bath; lift out (don't drain through — soil settles in the basin).

##### Trimming
- Onion: ends, root, papery skin.
- Carrot: top, tip, peel (or wash skin-on if recipe permits).
- Celery: butt, leaf-tops (use leaf-tops for stock or garnish).
- Leek: root, dark green tough tops (keep light green; use dark tops for stock).
- Squash: stem, blossom end, hard skin.
- Tomato: core; **for cream-of-tomato soup**, peel by scoring an X on the bottom, blanching 30 sec, ice bath, peeling.

##### Cutting strategy by soup type

| Soup type | Cut size |
|---|---|
| **Clear / consommé garnish** | Fine brunoise or julienne — uniform, visible |
| **Cream soup aromatics** | Medium dice — cooks tender during sweating |
| **Cream soup featured vegetable** | Medium chop — will be puréed, so visual uniformity matters less than cooking-time uniformity |
| **Purée soup main ingredient** | Rough chop (large dice) — will blend; the goal is even cook time, not visual |
| **Specialty regional cuts** | Per cuisine (e.g., Japanese kasugiri for julienne) |
| **Cold soup** | Very fine, often through a food processor or blender |

#### Aromatic preparation procedures

- **Garlic:** smash to release oils; mince for cream soups; whole-clove for stocks/braises; sliced for slower release.
- **Ginger:** peel with a spoon edge; slice into coins, julienne, or grate depending on application.
- **Fresh herbs:** wash gently, dry thoroughly, chiffonade or fine chop just before service (oxidation darkens cut herbs quickly).
- **Citrus zest:** wash fruit; zest only the coloured layer (white pith is bitter); use Microplane for soups.
- **Spice pastes (specialty):** for tom yum, curry, harissa — pound in mortar and pestle or process in food processor.

#### Roux preparation procedure (cream soups, gumbo)

Roux is the cornerstone of velouté and cream soups. The thickening power and flavour depend on cooking colour:

| Roux stage | Cook time at low heat | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **White roux** | ~ 3–5 min | Béchamel, cream soups, white velouté |
| **Blond roux** | ~ 5–10 min | Velouté, light gravies, light cream soups |
| **Brown roux** | ~ 15–30 min | Espagnole, beef gravies, French onion |
| **Dark brown / chocolate roux** | 45+ min (Cajun standard) | Gumbo |

**Standard ratio:** **equal parts by weight of fat (butter, oil, lard) and flour.**

##### Procedure
1. Melt fat over medium-low heat.
2. Add flour; whisk to combine into a smooth paste.
3. Cook to desired colour, whisking constantly — burning is a fast disaster (bitter, scorched, will not thicken properly).
4. **Cool slightly** before adding hot liquid, or add cold liquid to hot roux — the **temperature differential** matters:
   - **Hot roux + cold liquid** → smooth incorporation (preferred for cream soups).
   - **Cold roux + hot liquid** → also works; classic for some sauces.
   - **Hot roux + hot liquid** → tends to lump.
5. Whisk constantly while adding liquid in stages.
6. Bring to gentle simmer; thickening fully develops over 15–20 minutes of cooking.

> **Memory aid:** "**HOT+COLD = SMOOTH** / **HOT+HOT = LUMPS**." **[Best Practice]**

#### Consommé raft preparation

For consommé, the **clarification raft** is built during processing and cooks into the broth:

1. **Lean ground meat** (beef shin/neck for beef consommé; lean chicken for chicken; etc.) — **400–500 g per 4 L of broth** typical.
2. **Egg whites** — 4–6 per 4 L; coagulation filter.
3. **Mirepoix in fine brunoise** — adds flavour and a physical filter network.
4. **Crushed ice** — keeps the meat cold during initial incorporation, slowing protein denaturation so the raft forms after gentle heating.
5. **Optional acid** — tomato or wine for clarity assistance.

**Combine cold, add cold stock, then heat slowly.** The actual clarification cook is a D-14.03 topic.

#### Protein preparation for soups

| Protein | Preparation |
|---|---|
| **Diced chicken (clear soup)** | Trim, dice ~ 1 cm; sauté or poach before adding to clear broth |
| **Sliced beef (Vietnamese pho)** | Slice paper-thin against the grain (freeze briefly to firm); add raw to hot broth at service |
| **Ground meat (consommé raft)** | Pre-chilled meat ≤ 4 °C; grind through medium plate; keep cold |
| **Shellfish (chowder, bisque)** | Clean, devein, trim; pre-cook in court-bouillon or sauté; add at service to prevent overcooking |
| **Cured meats (caldo verde, minestrone)** | Slice or dice; sauté at start to render fat and flavour |
| **Tofu (miso soup)** | Cube; add to hot broth at service; never boil (rubbery) |

#### Cold-soup processing

| Soup | Critical processing |
|---|---|
| **Gazpacho** | Tomatoes, cucumber, peppers, garlic — all raw, processed in stand blender; bread (slightly stale) soaked in liquid; sherry vinegar and olive oil whisked in; strain for refinement; chill ≥ 4 hours; serve ≤ 4 °C |
| **Vichyssoise** | Cooked potato + leek puréed, finished with cream, chilled; the *cooked* part of the prep follows D-14.03 rules; the chilled hold follows A-2.02 cooling |
| **Ajo blanco** | Blanched almonds, soaked stale bread, garlic, sherry vinegar, water, olive oil — all blended, strained, chilled |
| **Cucumber-yogurt** | Cucumber grated/processed, yogurt, herbs, garlic; mixed cold; held cold |
| **Cold borscht** | Cooked beets puréed or grated; chilled stock; sour cream finish |

#### Sanitation during processing (carry-over from A-2.04, A-2.05)

- **Wash hands** between protein and produce tasks.
- **Change gloves** between raw and ready-to-eat.
- **Sanitize boards and knives** between proteins and after raw produce on a soiled board.
- **Use colour-coded boards** — green for produce, yellow for poultry, red for raw red meat, blue for seafood.
- **Sanitize the blender/food processor** between productions, especially between allergen-isolated runs.
- **Disassemble equipment for cleaning** — immersion blender wand off motor; food processor S-blade out per [A-2.05-research.md](A-2.05-research.md); never bare-hand the blade.
- **Air-dry food-contact surfaces** before reassembly.

## 3. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Production prep for 40 portions of cream of broccoli (200 mL/portion)
1. **Working yield target:** 40 × 200 × 1.15 = ~ 9.2 L → round to **10 L** with buffer.
2. **Ingredient calculations:**
   - Stock: 7 L chicken stock (verify dated ≤ 5 d cold).
   - Broccoli: 3 kg (yields ~ 2.2 kg trimmed florets).
   - Onion: 800 g.
   - Leek: 600 g (whites and light green).
   - Garlic: 50 g.
   - Roux: 250 g butter + 250 g flour.
   - Cream (33% MF): 1.5 L.
   - Salt, white pepper, nutmeg.
3. **Mise en place:**
   - Stockpot, sauté pan for sweating, roux pan, whisk, immersion blender, fine chinois, cheesecloth.
   - Hotel pans + ice bath for cooling staged.
4. **Processing order:**
   - Wash broccoli, separate stems and florets; chop stems (cook longer), reserve florets.
   - Cut onion, leek, garlic — small dice/medium dice for aromatics.
   - Weigh butter and flour for roux.
   - Measure stock and cream.
   - Stage everything before starting the cook.

### Scenario 2 — Setting up to clarify a consommé
1. **Pre-chill** ground beef and ice ≤ 4 °C.
2. **Mise en place:**
   - 500 g lean ground beef (shin/neck).
   - 5 egg whites.
   - Fine brunoise mirepoix: 200 g (onion, carrot, celery; consider an *oignon brûlé* for colour).
   - 200 g crushed ice.
   - 50 g tomato (optional, for colour and clarity).
   - 4 L cold (or barely warm) starting broth.
3. **Combine** cold; do not pre-warm.
4. **Whisk together** in a tall pot.
5. **Gradually heat** while stirring — the actual cook is a D-14.03 topic.

### Scenario 3 — Cold gazpacho processing
1. Wash all vegetables under cold running water.
2. Core tomatoes; peel cucumber; remove pepper seeds.
3. Soak stale bread (~ 50 g per L) in some of the liquid (water or tomato juice).
4. Combine all vegetables + soaked bread + sherry vinegar + olive oil + salt in stand blender.
5. **Blender safety:** fill ≤ ⅔ full; pulse first.
6. **Strain** through fine chinois for refinement (optional — many rustic gazpachos are not strained).
7. **Chill ≥ 4 hours** to meld flavours; hold ≤ 4 °C until service.
8. **Serve in pre-chilled bowls**; garnish at table.

### Scenario 4 — Processing for celiac guest's order of cream of tomato
1. **Substitute the roux** with a **cornstarch slurry** (cornstarch + cold water mixed to thin paste) added at end of cook OR **rice flour roux** (gluten-free, builds similar body).
2. **Use a sanitized, gluten-free-isolated pot and utensils.**
3. **Verify stock base** is gluten-free (no commercial bouillon with wheat).
4. **Cream and aromatics** OK as standard.
5. **Document** the allergen-isolated production.

### Scenario 5 — Mid-shift blender accident: hot soup blown out of stand blender
1. **Stop immediately**, turn off blender.
2. **Make safe:** check if anyone splashed; cool with running water 10–20 min if burned (carry-over from [A-1.02-research.md](A-1.02-research.md)).
3. **Investigate cause:** likely overfilled (> ⅔), lid not vented, or run continuously instead of pulsed.
4. **Discard contaminated soup** — cross-contamination from the kitchen environment likely.
5. **Sanitize area.**
6. **Coach team** on hot-blender safety: ≤ ⅔ full, vent lid, towel over, pulse.
7. **Document** in OH&S log.

## 4. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Filling stand blender past ⅔ with hot liquid** → steam pressure blows lid; serious burn risk.
- **Running blender continuously with hot soup** → same. Pulse to release pressure between bursts.
- **Cold roux into cold liquid** → lumps; thickening incomplete.
- **Hot roux into hot liquid** → lumps.
- **Adding salt early in a soup that will reduce** → over-salted final product.
- **Inconsistent knife cuts** → some pieces overcook, others undercook.
- **Skipping the cold-pre-chill of consommé raft ingredients** → raft denatures too fast and doesn't form properly.
- **Cross-contaminating mise en place** — onion next to raw chicken trim on the same board.
- **Forgetting allergen substitutions** — celiac guest's order routed to standard cream-soup line by mistake.
- **Eyeballing instead of weighing** — inconsistent yields and quality shift-to-shift.
- **Mandoline without the guard or cut-resistant glove** — preventable severe injury.
- **Bare-hand removal of food-processor or immersion-blender blade** — preventable severe injury.
- **Hot tap water as stock substitute** — older plumbing can leach metals; lower oxygen; flat-tasting.
- **Storing processed mise en place in danger zone** — keep ≤ 4 °C until use.
- **Reassembling food processor or immersion blender wet** — water trapped in seams promotes microbial growth.

## 6. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with D-14.02.

1. **Soup-processing tools (Range of Variables):** scales, cutting boards, knives, whisks, food processors, cheesecloth — plus supporting items (china cap, chinois, ladle, immersion blender, hotel pans).
2. **Weight over volume** is the professional measurement default; tare every container.
3. **Standard portion sizes:** appetizer soup ~ 200 mL; entrée soup ~ 350–500 mL; tasting ~ 30–60 mL.
4. **Yield buffer 15–20%** for service production losses.
5. **Standard knife cuts:** brunoise 3 × 3 × 3 mm; julienne 3 × 3 × 50 mm; small dice 6 mm; medium dice 12 mm; paysanne for rustic soups; chiffonade for leafy garnish.
6. **Uniformity matters** — even cuts cook at the same rate.
7. **Colour-coded cutting boards:** green produce / yellow poultry / red raw red meat / blue seafood / brown cooked / white dairy / purple allergen-isolated.
8. **Sharp knives are safer than dull knives** — less force, less slip.
9. **Sanitize between proteins** and between raw and RTE tasks.
10. **Roux:** equal parts fat and flour by weight; cook colour (white, blond, brown, dark) determines flavour and thickening power.
11. **Roux temperature rule:** **hot roux + cold liquid** (or cold roux + hot liquid) for smooth incorporation; hot + hot = lumps.
12. **Cream soups require ≥ 33% MF heavy cream** for stability under heat (table cream curdles).
13. **Hot-liquid blending safety:** ≤ ⅔ full, vent lid, towel over, pulse — never run continuously with hot soup.
14. **Immersion blender:** submerge before turning on; lift the cup over the pot to break vacuum, then off; lockout for cleaning.
15. **Food processor / VCM blades:** two-hand removal with blade handler; never bare-handed.
16. **Mandoline:** cut-resistant glove + food guard always.
17. **Consommé raft ingredients** (combined cold): lean ground meat, egg whites, brunoise mirepoix, crushed ice, optional acid; raft cooks during clarification (D-14.03 topic).
18. **Cold soup processing** demands raw-ingredient discipline and cold-chain integrity throughout.
19. **Mise en place ("MEP"):** read recipe → list components → stage tools → pre-portion by weight → sequence ingredients into the pot.
20. **Standard Canadian cream soup ratio reference (per L):** ~ 700 mL stock + ~ 300–400 g featured vegetable + ~ 80–120 g aromatics + roux 5–10% of stock + ~ 150–250 mL cream.
21. **Salt held back** for final adjustment at service temperature; reductions concentrate salt.
22. **Health Canada priority allergens** scanned for every soup: eggs, milk, mustard, peanuts, crustaceans/molluscs, fish, sesame, soy, sulphites, tree nuts, wheat/triticale.
23. **Celiac substitution:** cornstarch slurry or rice flour instead of wheat-flour roux; allergen-isolated tools.
24. **Vegan substitution:** vegetable stock; cashew/oat/coconut cream; mushroom + kombu + miso for umami.
25. **Halal / kosher:** certified supplier chain; no pork; meat-dairy separation for kosher cream soups.
26. **Cold-chain discipline during processing:** keep dairy ≤ 4 °C; processed mise en place ≤ 4 °C until use.
27. **Cooling rule for finished soup (carry-over from A-2.02):** 60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.
28. **Serving temperatures:** hot hold ≥ 60 °C; cold hold ≤ 4 °C; reheat to 74 °C within 2 h.
29. **Sanitation discipline** — boards, knives, blender bowls, food processor blades cleaned and sanitized between productions; air-dry before reassembly.
30. **Document mise en place** for HACCP-aligned operations — batch ID, item list, dates, allergen flags, weight checks.
