## Where Stocks Are Actually Made

You can have perfect bones, perfectly-sized mirepoix, a beautifully-built sachet — and still produce a cloudy, greasy, weak, or unsafe stock. **The cooking stage is where stocks earn their final identity.** Three forces are operating simultaneously:

1. **Extraction** — collagen, flavour compounds, minerals, and aromatics are leaching from solids into water.
2. **Reactions** — Maillard browning (brown stocks during roasting), gelatinization of starches if vegetable trim included, denaturation and coagulation of blood proteins (forming the scum that must be removed).
3. **Risk accumulation** — every minute the stock spends at warm temperatures is a window for spore-formers like *Clostridium perfringens* and *Bacillus cereus* during cooling.

Mastery means controlling all three at once: extracting maximally while keeping the stock clear, building flavour without burning, and managing the danger zone on the way down.

## The Heat-Transfer Foundation Under Every Step

| Mechanism | In a stockpot |
|---|---|
| **Conduction** | Burner → pot bottom → water at the base; bone surface ↔ surrounding water |
| **Convection** | Hot water rising along the sides, cooler water descending in the centre — the "stockpot column" |
| **Radiation** | Mostly during roasting in the oven; minimal in a covered pot |
| **Phase change** | Water → steam during boiling carries off flavour and aroma compounds; this is why **simmering preserves flavour better than boiling** |

A **gentle simmer** maintains a controlled convection pattern with minimal agitation — bubbles rise lazily from the bottom, fat and scum separate cleanly to the surface. A **rolling boil** creates violent agitation that re-emulsifies fat back into the liquid and forces protein particles to break up into a permanent cloud.

> **Anchor rule:** *A stock simmers; it does not boil.* (The one exception is tonkotsu-style emulsified ramen broth, which intentionally exploits boiling-induced emulsification.) **[Classical / Best Practice]**

### Target simmer temperature

A "barely-trembling simmer" — wispy bubbles, surface that visibly shifts but doesn't roll — corresponds to roughly **85–95 °C / 185–205 °F** in the pot. The water is not quite at full boil (100 °C at sea level), and the gentler thermal load reduces both fat emulsification and the bitter / muddying effects of overcooking aromatics.

## 1. Performance Skills (with Reasoning)

### D-13.03.01P — Cook Ingredients

**Performance Criteria:** cook ingredients
**Evidence of Attainment:** ingredients are cooked according to recipe specifications.

#### The cooking arc of a classical stock

| Stage | What happens | Key control |
|---|---|---|
| **Cold-water start** | Bones submerged; blood proteins denature and rise as scum | Don't seal proteins to bone with hot start |
| **Slow heat-up to ≈ 95 °C** | Convection establishes; scum forms heavily | Skim aggressively; don't stir |
| **Simmer phase** | Collagen hydrolyzes to gelatin (~70–80 °C is required, sustained for hours); flavour compounds extract | Maintain temperature; don't disturb |
| **Aromatic phase** | Sachet/bouquet garni releases volatile compounds; vegetables soften | Time aromatic addition for optimal extraction |
| **Strain / finish** | Solids removed; liquid concentrated as needed | Use staged strainers for clarity |
| **Cool** | Through danger zone per 2/4/6 rule | Active cooling; the most dangerous stage microbiologically |

#### Standard simmer durations (carry-over from D-13.01)

| Stock | Simmer time | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **White / brown veal / beef** | 8–12 hours | Dense bones, much collagen, slow release |
| **Chicken** | 3–4 hours | Smaller bones, faster extraction; longer = bitter |
| **Fish fumet** | 30–45 minutes | Delicate bones over-extract and turn bitter after ~1 hour |
| **Court-bouillon** | 30–45 minutes | Extracts aromatics, not bone |
| **Vegetable stock** | 45–60 minutes | Longer ≈ muddy and bitter |
| **Dashi** | 30 min (kombu steep) + 3–5 min (katsuobushi) | Tea-like infusion; longer extracts bitterness |
| **Emulsified (tonkotsu)** | 8–14 hours **at rolling boil** | Intentionally violent to emulsify collagen and fat |

> **[Best Practice]** Exact times vary by reference (CIA *Professional Chef*, Pauli *Classical Cooking*, Larousse) and individual operation house recipes. Learn your workplace's standard.

### D-13.03.02P — Prepare Bones and Vegetables

**Performance Criteria:** prepare bones and vegetables
**Evidence of Attainment:** bones and vegetables are prepared according to recipe specifications.

> Detailed bone and vegetable preparation is covered in [D-13.02-research.md](D-13.02-research.md). Here, the focus is the heat-stage of bone and vegetable preparation that bridges into cooking.

#### Bone preparation methods at the cooking stage

| Method | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Blanching** | White veal / beef stocks | Cold water → boil 5–10 min → drain → rinse; removes blood and impurities for cleaner stock |
| **Roasting** | Brown stocks | 200–230 °C / 400–450 °F until mahogany; develops Maillard compounds — colour, depth, umami |
| **Sweating** | Fish fumet | Butter + low heat with white mirepoix; flavour without browning |
| **Direct simmer** | Chicken white stock (sometimes); short-cook stocks | Simpler workflow when stock will be used in lightly flavoured applications |

#### Vegetable cooking-stage considerations

| Method | When | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Add cold with bones** | White stocks | Long extraction; minimal pre-cook needed |
| **Roast separately or with bones** | Brown stocks | Caramelizes sugars for colour/flavour |
| **Sweat in butter** | Fumet, vegetable stock | Develops aromatic complexity without colour |
| **Add late in simmer** (last 60–90 min) | Long beef/veal stocks | Some classical chefs prefer this to keep mirepoix fresh; modern practice varies |

#### The Maillard reaction — why brown stock is brown

- A non-enzymatic browning between **amino acids** (from bone protein) and **reducing sugars** (from vegetables) at temperatures **≥ ~ 140–150 °C / ~ 280–300 °F**.
- Produces hundreds of new aromatic and flavour compounds — the signature roasted/savoury/umami profile.
- **Requires dry surfaces** — moisture keeps temperature at ≤ 100 °C, preventing Maillard.
- Single-layer roasting in a hot oven is the prerequisite.

> ⚠️ **Burnt ≠ browned.** Once bones go past mahogany into charred black, **bitter pyrazines and acrolein** dominate; the stock cannot recover.

### D-13.03.03P — Deglaze Cooking Vessel

**Performance Criteria:** deglaze cooking vessel
**Evidence of Attainment:** cooking vessel is deglazed by adding liquid to base to capture flavour profile according to recipe specifications.

#### What deglazing is

When proteins and sugars caramelize on a hot surface (roasting pan, sauté pan), they leave behind a brown crust called **sucs** or **fond** (literally "foundation"). These sucs are concentrated flavour compounds, including:
- Maillard reaction products
- Caramelized sugars
- Roasted protein flavours
- Concentrated mineral notes

**Deglazing** is the act of dissolving these sucs back into a liquid by adding water, wine, stock, or vinegar to the still-hot pan and scraping with a wooden or silicone spatula.

#### Deglazing for brown stock

1. Once bones and mirepoix are transferred from the roasting pan to the stockpot, the **fond on the roasting pan is the entire reason brown stock is brown.**
2. Place the roasting pan over a burner (or on the warm oven shelf).
3. Add ≈ 250–500 mL of **water or wine**.
4. Scrape every brown bit off the pan with a wooden spatula — the liquid will go dark golden to brown.
5. Pour the deglazing liquid into the stockpot.
6. Repeat if needed until the pan is clean.

#### Liquids used for deglazing

| Liquid | When | Flavour effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Water** | Standard, neutral | Lifts fond without adding character |
| **White wine** | Fumet, white sauces | Acidity, fruit, complexity |
| **Red wine** | Brown stocks, demi-glace, beef braises | Body, tannins, depth — but reduce alone first to drive off raw alcohol |
| **Madeira / port / sherry** | Refined demi-glace, sauces | Sweet wine concentration |
| **Brandy / cognac** | Game and rich brown stocks | Spirit complexity |
| **Vinegar** | Court-bouillon, certain Asian broths | Bright acidity |
| **Beer / cider** | Regional / casual brown stocks | Malt and yeast notes |

> **[Best Practice]** For sauce-grade applications, **reduce wine alone first** (the so-called *cuisson*) to ~ ⅓ volume to drive off raw alcohol and concentrate flavour before adding to the stockpot.

#### Common deglazing mistakes
- **Letting the pan cool** before deglazing — sucs harden and stick irreversibly.
- **Using metal utensils on coated pans** — scratches.
- **Discarding the roasting pan unwashed** — losing the most flavour-dense element of the stock.
- **Over-aggressive deglazing of burnt fond** — bitter compounds extract too.

### D-13.03.04P — Bring Stock to Simmer

**Performance Criteria:** bring stock to simmer
**Evidence of Attainment:** stock is simmered according to recipe specifications.

#### The slow heat-up

- **Cold water start, slow heat-up over 30–60 minutes** to simmer.
- A slow heat-up allows blood proteins to **denature and rise gradually** rather than dispersing into the liquid as a permanent cloud.
- Aggressive heat-up emulsifies fat into the water before skimming has a chance to remove it.

#### Detecting the right simmer

| Visible cue | Temperature range | What's happening |
|---|---|---|
| **No bubbles, just heat shimmer** | 70–80 °C | Below simmer; collagen extraction begins but slow |
| **Tiny bubbles on the bottom; surface trembles** | 82–88 °C | Ideal "**poach**" / very gentle simmer; great for fumet end-stage |
| **Bubbles break the surface lazily; minimal agitation** | 88–95 °C | **Standard simmer**; the classical stock target |
| **Vigorous, continuous bubbling; rolling movement** | 95–100 °C | Boil; **too aggressive** for clear stocks (acceptable for tonkotsu) |
| **Violent rolling boil with foam** | 100 °C | Not used for clear stocks |

> **Memory aid — "TREMBLING NOT ROLLING":** the surface should look like a gentle shiver, not a wave pool. **[Classical]**

#### Lid usage

- **Uncovered** is the classical preference for clear stocks — allows scum to rise visibly and be skimmed; allows controlled reduction during the cook.
- **Partially covered** (lid ajar) is a compromise — reduces evaporation slightly while still allowing skimming and aroma escape.
- **Fully covered** is rarely used for clear stocks — traps fat and prevents controlled evaporation.
- **Cartouche** (parchment circle resting on the surface, with a vent hole) is sometimes used in classical practice — minimizes evaporation while still allowing aroma escape and gentle skimming. **[Classical]**

### D-13.03.05P — Monitor Broth Temperature

**Performance Criteria:** monitor broth temperature
**Evidence of Attainment:** broth temperature is monitored according to recipe specifications.

#### Why monitor

- **Quality:** maintaining the simmer band (≈ 85–95 °C) prevents both under-extraction and the boil-induced cloud problem.
- **Food safety:** the broth must stay **above 60 °C** to be considered hot-held; the cook must verify against this threshold during the entire production window.
- **Equipment behaviour:** burners drift, ovens cycle, induction units vary — periodic verification keeps the simmer on track.

#### How to monitor

- **Probe thermometer** at multiple times during the cook (every 1–2 hours minimum for long simmers).
- **Insert into the geometric centre** of the broth, not touching the pot bottom (radiates burner heat).
- **Sanitize the probe** between checks and between different stocks. (Carry-over from [A-2.01-research.md](A-2.01-research.md).)
- **Visual cue cross-check** — even when a thermometer is on hand, the cook should learn the visual signs of a proper simmer.

#### Recording the data

Under a HACCP-aligned operation, stock cooking is often logged:
- Start time and temperature.
- Periodic checks during the simmer.
- End-of-cook temperature.
- Cooling milestones (2-hour check ≤ 20 °C; 6-hour check ≤ 4 °C).

**[Best Practice / Regulatory under SFCR PCP and many provincial public-health expectations]**

### D-13.03.06P — Skim Stock or Broth

**Performance Criteria:** skim stock or broth
**Evidence of Attainment:** stock or broth is skimmed to remove impurities to ensure desired consistency and flavour according to recipe specifications.

#### What gets skimmed

- **Scum** — coagulated blood proteins, denatured albumin, micro-particles of bone and flesh. Grey-brown foam that rises in the first 30–60 minutes.
- **Fat** — yellow-to-clear liquid layer that accumulates on the surface throughout the simmer.
- **Foam from aromatic compounds** — minor, late-stage; often skimmed at strain time.

#### Why skimming matters

| Defect prevented | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Cloudy stock** | Scum re-emulsifies into the liquid if left and agitated; floating particles disperse |
| **Greasy stock** | Surface fat re-emulsifies under any agitation, especially boiling |
| **Off-flavours** | Scum proteins denature further and can develop "barnyard" notes; oxidized fat goes rancid |
| **Reduced sauce yield** | Greasy stocks "break" when reduced for sauces |

#### How to skim

1. **Heaviest scum forms in the first 15–60 minutes.** Skim continuously during this window.
2. Use a **wide flat skimmer** or a **shallow ladle** — gentle, surface-only motion.
3. **Don't stir** before or during skimming — stirring drags scum back into solution.
4. Tip the pot gently if needed to gather scum to one side.
5. **Continue periodic skimming** throughout the simmer (every 30–60 minutes during long cooks).
6. **Final degreasing** is most efficient when the stock is cold and the fat has solidified — lift off as a solid layer.

> **Memory aid — "SKIM EARLY, SKIM OFTEN":** the first hour does 80% of the work; periodic light skimming does the rest.

### D-13.03.07P — Monitor Cooking Time and Aromatics

**Performance Criteria:** monitor cooking time and aromatics
**Evidence of Attainment:** cooking time and aromatics are monitored according to recipe specifications and desired results.

#### Time discipline

- **Recipe times are guidelines**, not commandments — the cook verifies extraction by tasting, by the gelatin set, and by visual clarity.
- **Under-extraction** leaves a weak, watery stock with poor body — common when veal/beef stocks are pulled at 4–5 hours instead of 8–12.
- **Over-extraction** introduces bitter compounds (especially from bone marrow, certain herbs, and vegetable matter).

#### Aromatic timing

| Aromatic | When to add | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Sachet d'épices / bouquet garni** | At the start of the simmer | Full extraction over the cook |
| **Mirepoix** | Start (white stocks) or roasted-then-added (brown) or last 60–90 min (some classical schools) | Balance extraction with freshness |
| **Tomato paste** | Roasted onto bones in last 15–20 min of roast | Caramelization without burning |
| **Wine for deglazing** | At deglaze step, then added to pot | Reduce alcohol before final use |
| **Delicate herbs (basil, tarragon)** | Last 5–10 min or as finishing | Volatile aromatics; long simmer destroys them |
| **Bay leaf, thyme, parsley stems** | Start (in sachet) | Robust; benefit from extraction |
| **Black pepper (whole)** | Start (in sachet) | Slow flavour release; cracked pepper can over-extract and become harsh |
| **Garlic (if used)** | Start or middle | Bitterness if over-cooked at boil |
| **Konbu (dashi)** | Cold-water steep, remove before boil | Heat extracts bitter compounds |
| **Katsuobushi (dashi)** | Off heat, brief 3–5 min steep | Like tea infusion |

#### Pulling the aromatics at the right moment

- **Sachet retrieval at end of simmer** — pull the long tail of twine; squeeze gently (or not at all for highest clarity).
- **Don't leave aromatics in during cooling** — continued extraction at warm temperatures muddies flavour.

### D-13.03.08P — Reduce Stock or Broth

**Performance Criteria:** reduce stock or broth
**Evidence of Attainment:** stock or broth is reduced to a concentrate according to desired results.

#### Why reduce

- **Concentrate flavour** — driving off water intensifies every flavour compound (and salt, if present — another reason stocks are unseasoned).
- **Build body** — gelatin per litre increases as water decreases; stock becomes silky, then syrupy.
- **Storage efficiency** — a reduced stock takes less freezer space.
- **Sauce-making leverage** — a glace fortifies pan sauces dramatically with a small spoonful.

#### Reduction stages

| Stage | Approximate volume remaining | Common name |
|---|---|---|
| **Full stock** | 100% | Stock (white or brown) |
| **Half reduction** | 50% | Stronger stock; sauce base |
| **Demi-glace style reduction** | ~ 25–30% (or 50% if reducing a 50/50 mix of stock and espagnole) | Demi-glace |
| **Heavy reduction** | ~ 10–15% | Glace / glacé / glaze |
| **Final glace** | 5–10% | Glace de viande — gels firmly at fridge temp, syrupy hot |

#### Reduction technique

1. After straining, transfer stock to a **wide, shallow pan** — greater surface area = faster evaporation.
2. **Bring to a gentle simmer** — never a hard boil for sauce-grade reductions (sauce can scorch and develop bitter notes).
3. **Skim** any final fat or foam that surfaces.
4. **Track volume** — visible level marks on the pot or periodic measurement.
5. **Don't season** until the very end of the dish (reduction concentrates salt aggressively).
6. **Watch for scorching** — caramelized residue at the pan edges can taint flavour.

#### Acid timing in reductions
- Acidic ingredients (wine, vinegar, citrus) should usually be **reduced separately first** to drive off raw alcohol and balance, then combined with the stock reduction.

#### Quality losses from over-reduction
- **Bitter notes** from over-concentrated tannins or burnt sugars.
- **Texture changes** — gelatin becomes too dense; sauce becomes sticky rather than silky.
- **Flavour imbalance** — strong notes drown subtle ones.

### D-13.03.09P — Strain and Filter Stock or Broth

**Performance Criteria:** strain and filter stock or broth
**Evidence of Attainment:** stock or broth is strained and filtered to remove undesired elements.

#### Staged straining for maximum clarity

1. **First pass — china cap (coarse):** removes bones, mirepoix, sachet, large solids. Use a skimmer/spider to lift bones first; then ladle (don't pour) the stock through the china cap.
2. **Second pass — fine chinois (fine mesh):** removes small bone shards, vegetable particles, scum residue.
3. **Third pass — cheesecloth-lined fine chinois:** removes the finest particulates. For consommé-grade clarity.
4. **Optional — through a coffee filter or paper filter:** rarely done in commercial stocks but used for ultra-clear consommé or competition work.

#### Technique tips

- **Ladle, don't pour.** Pouring stirs up the settled bed of solids and increases cloud.
- **Don't press the solids.** Squeezing the sachet or pressing the bones extracts bitter / cloudy compounds.
- **Let the stock settle** for a few minutes before straining — heaviest particles sink; clearer liquid pours off the top.
- **Strain into a clean, sanitized vessel** sized appropriately. Often the cooling pans (shallow hotel pans) are pre-staged so straining + cooling start happen simultaneously.

#### Special case: clarifying a consommé

Beyond simple straining, **consommé clarification** uses an **egg-white-and-meat raft**:
1. Combine **lean ground meat, mirepoix brunoise, egg whites, and crushed ice** in a clean pot.
2. Add the (cold) stock.
3. Bring to a simmer slowly, **stirring gently at first**.
4. As proteins coagulate, they form a "raft" that floats and **mechanically traps impurities** like a coagulation filter.
5. Once the raft forms, **stop stirring**. Make a small vent hole.
6. Maintain a very low simmer for 30–60 min.
7. Carefully ladle the now-crystal-clear stock from beneath the raft through cheesecloth-lined chinois.

This is classical haute-cuisine territory and a Red Seal exam favourite for advanced demonstrations. **[Classical]**

### D-13.03.10P — Evaluate Quality and Clarity of Stock or Broth

**Performance Criteria:** evaluate quality and clarity of stock or broth
**Evidence of Attainment:** quality and clarity of stock or broth is evaluated according to sensory assessments.

#### The sensory checklist

| Sense | Good quality | Poor quality |
|---|---|---|
| **Sight (clarity)** | Translucent (you can read newsprint through it for white; deep colour for brown); minimal sediment | Cloudy, milky, opaque (except intentional tonkotsu); visible particles |
| **Sight (colour)** | White: pale ivory or straw; Brown: rich amber to mahogany; Fumet: pale gold; Dashi: pale tan/gold | White: yellow-green, cloudy; Brown: dull tan or burnt brown; Fumet: grey or cloudy |
| **Smell** | Clean, savoury, characteristic of the protein; balanced aromatic notes | Acrid, burnt, sour, ammonia, off-protein notes |
| **Taste** | Deep, savoury, complex; balanced; no off-flavours | Flat, bitter, sour, metallic, harsh |
| **Body / mouthfeel** | Slight viscosity when hot; **wobbly gel when cold** (the gelatin test) | Watery hot and cold; "thin" texture |
| **Surface** | Clean, minimal fat once cold-degreased | Greasy, with floating particles |

#### The "wobbly gel" test
A properly-extracted veal or chicken stock, **chilled overnight in the fridge**, should set to a **gelatinous mass that wobbles when the container is tapped**. This is the visible sign of dissolved gelatin and is **the primary indicator of body**.

#### Tasting protocol
- **Sanitize a tasting spoon** before each taste.
- **Use one spoon per taste** — never re-dip into the pot.
- **Taste at the temperature of intended use** (hot for sauce work; cold for aspic).
- **Taste in stages** during reduction — flavour and salinity concentrate continuously.

#### What to do with poor quality
- **Cloudy white stock:** can sometimes be partially clarified with the consommé raft technique; otherwise reserved for less-visible applications (braises, dark soups).
- **Bitter stock:** generally not salvageable; flag for the chef.
- **Greasy stock:** chill, lift off solidified fat, and re-evaluate — often acceptable after degreasing.
- **Burnt notes:** not salvageable; discard.
- **Off-smell / off-taste:** discard. Document and investigate.

### D-13.03.11P — Cool Finished Stock or Broth

**Performance Criteria:** cool finished stock or broth
**Evidence of Attainment:** finished stocks or broths are cooled using methods according to jurisdictional regulations and manufacturers' specifications.

> **This is the most food-safety-critical step in stock production.** See [A-2.02-research.md](A-2.02-research.md) for the full rapid-cooling protocol.

#### The Canadian cooling rule (recap)

> **60 °C → 20 °C within 2 hours**
> **20 °C → 4 °C within an additional 4 hours**
> **Total ≤ 6 hours from hot to cold.**

**[Regulatory / Best Practice — adopted across most Canadian jurisdictions via Health Canada / CFIA guidance]**

#### Why stocks are top *C. perfringens* / *B. cereus* risk

- **Large volumes** that cool slowly.
- **High protein content** — perfect bacterial growth medium.
- **Spores survive the simmer** — even an 8-hour simmer does not destroy *C. perfringens* and *B. cereus* spores.
- **Slow cooling through 45–55 °C** is exactly where these spores germinate and multiply rapidly.

#### Methods (Range of Variables) — ranked by speed

##### Blast chiller (fastest, gold standard)
- Drops 90 °C product to 3 °C in ~ 90 minutes for shallow pans.
- Product probe ends the cycle at target temperature.
- Best when available; especially for high-volume operations.

##### Ice wand / ice paddle
- Frozen, sealed plastic wand inserted into the stock.
- Stirred gently to cool from the core outward.
- Especially useful for deep stockpots where dividing is impractical mid-cooling.
- **Two or more wands rotated** for continuous heat removal.

##### Water bath (ice bath in the sink)
- Pot placed in a basin of **ice + water** (water is essential — ice alone is mostly air gaps).
- **Stir periodically** to break up the hot boundary layer.
- **Salt the ice water** (~ 1 cup kosher salt) to drive temperature below 0 °C — significantly faster.

##### Venting and dividing
- **Divide the strained stock into shallow stainless containers (≤ 5 cm / 2 in deep).**
- **Leave uncovered** or vented during active cooling — covering traps heat.
- Place on a wire rack in the walk-in for maximum air circulation underneath.
- **Surface area-to-volume** is the secret — same volume, more shallow pans, dramatically faster cooling.

> ⚠️ **Common failure:** placing a deep covered cambro of hot stock in the walk-in. The centre stays > 20 °C for 8+ hours, well outside the 2-hour first milestone. **One of the leading causes of *C. perfringens* outbreaks in commercial kitchens.**

#### The cooling log

| Field | Why |
|---|---|
| **Item / batch** | Traceability |
| **Start time & temp (≥ 60 °C)** | Begins the cooling clock |
| **Method used** | Process verification |
| **2-h check (target ≤ 20 °C)** | First HACCP milestone |
| **6-h check (target ≤ 4 °C)** | Second HACCP milestone |
| **Final action** | Cover, label, store / corrective action |
| **Initials** | Accountability |

**[Regulatory / Best Practice]** Required documentation under SFCR PCPs and many provincial public-health programs.

#### After cooling

- **Cover, label, and date** once at ≤ 4 °C.
- **Lift off the solidified fat cap** from the top before use (degreasing is much easier on a chilled stock than a hot one).
- **Store** in the cold-storage hierarchy (RTE shelf — top, separate from raw protein).
- **Use within typical shelf life** — commonly 3–5 days refrigerated for stock; longer if frozen. **[Best Practice — varies by operation]**

### D-13.03.01L — Stocks, Broths, and Their Applications (Cooking Lens)

#### Factors to consider when cooking (Range of Variables)

| Factor | Cooking-stage implication |
|---|---|
| **Fat content** | Skim aggressively; chilled-fat-cap removal at storage |
| **Intended application** | Adjust reduction level and clarity to match downstream use (light soup vs sauce vs glace) |
| **Type of bone** | Longer simmer for veal/beef knuckles; shorter for chicken; very short for fish |
| **Cartilage content** | Drives gelatin — taste and gel test confirm extraction |
| **Managing trim** | Use up vegetable trim before it spoils; rotate stock production around inventory |
| **Availability of ingredients** | Substitute thoughtfully — e.g., chicken backs for veal knuckles changes flavour and body profile |

#### Jurisdictional regulations for cooking, holding, and storing

| Layer | Scope |
|---|---|
| **Health Canada** | Recommended cooking and cooling guidance (e.g., 60→20→4 °C) |
| **CFIA / SFCR** | Federally-registered: PCP with stock-production CCPs |
| **Provincial Public Health Food Premises Regulations** | Restaurants: hot hold ≥ 60 °C, cold hold ≤ 4 °C, cooling and reheating rules |
| **WHMIS 2015** | Chemical hazards in cleaning during/after production |
| **Provincial OH&S** | Burn prevention, lifting injuries from heavy stockpots, PPE for hot work |

#### Methods for cooling (Range of Variables, expanded above)
- Ice wands
- Venting (uncovered shallow pans during active cooling)
- Blast chiller
- Water bath (ice bath)

#### Dietary requirements (carry-over from D-13.01)
- **Cultural / religious:** halal, kosher, Hindu (vegetarian), Jain (strict)
- **Allergies:** fish, shellfish, soy (miso), gluten (some commercial bases)
- **Ethical:** vegetarian, vegan
- **Health-related:** celiac, low-sodium, renal
- **Environmental:** sustainable seafood, local sourcing
- **Preferences:** umami-forward, lower fat, etc.

### D-13.03.02L — Procedures to Prepare and Cool Stocks and Broths

**Procedures (Range of Variables): selecting cooking time and temperature, straining, reduction, de-glace, skimming.**

#### Selecting cooking time and temperature
- Match to the stock type (see standard simmer times table above).
- Verify with thermometer; correct burner adjustment if drift detected.
- **Don't shortcut simmer time** to save labour — under-extracted stocks have poor body and weak flavour.

#### Straining
- Staged: china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth-lined chinois.
- Ladle, don't pour.
- Don't press.
- Strain directly into cooling vessels.

#### Reduction
- Wide shallow pan; gentle simmer.
- Track volume to target reduction level.
- Skim residual fat.
- Never season at the reduction stage if downstream use is unknown.

#### Deglazing (de-glace)
- Capture *sucs* from roasting pan with water or wine.
- Scrape with wooden/silicone spatula.
- Add deglazed liquid to stockpot.

#### Skimming
- Early and often.
- Wide flat skimmer or shallow ladle.
- No stirring before/during.
- Final degreasing easiest when chilled.

#### Methods for cooling
- See D-13.03.11P above.

## 3. Practical Kitchen Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — A full-day brown veal stock production
**07:00**: Inspect bones, preheat oven to 220 °C, set up roasting pans.
**07:15**: Bones in single layer in roasting pans → oven.
**07:45**: Turn bones; add mirepoix to a second roasting pan; both roast.
**08:15**: Brush tomato paste on bones; check colour (target mahogany).
**08:30**: Transfer bones and mirepoix to stockpot; cover with cold water (1:1.5–2 by weight).
**08:35**: Deglaze roasting pans with water/wine; add to stockpot.
**08:40**: Add sachet d'épices.
**09:00**: Slow heat-up begins; skim heavily as scum rises.
**09:30**: Reduce to a barely-trembling simmer (~ 90 °C confirmed by probe).
**10:00–18:00**: Maintain simmer, skim periodically, top up water only if necessary (and minimally).
**18:00**: Lift bones with spider; ladle through china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth-lined chinois into shallow stainless pans.
**18:15**: Cooling begins — pans in ice bath at first; **probe at 2 h target ≤ 20 °C**.
**20:15**: Confirm ≤ 20 °C; transfer to walk-in cooling shelf, uncovered.
**00:15** (overnight): Confirm ≤ 4 °C; cover, label "Brown veal stock — [date] — yield X L"; store on RTE shelf.

### Scenario 2 — Mid-cook clarity failure: white veal stock looks cloudy at hour 4
1. **Confirm visible cloudiness** — cup of stock against white tile shows opaque vs translucent.
2. **Check temperature** — probably running at hard simmer or boil. Reduce to gentle simmer.
3. **Skim aggressively** — collect any new scum.
4. **Don't stir** — let solids settle.
5. **Continue simmer** for full duration; final clarity may still be acceptable.
6. **If still cloudy at strain**: stock can be used in less-visible applications (braises, dark soups); or, for serious clarity needs, build a consommé raft to clarify.
7. **Lesson:** verify simmer level with probe earlier next time.

### Scenario 3 — Fumet over-extracted: tastes bitter and slightly fishy-sharp at 50 min
1. **Cut the cook short** — strain immediately.
2. **Taste again post-strain** — sometimes the bones contribute the worst notes; once removed and chilled, the stock improves.
3. **If usable:** reserve for strong-flavoured applications (cioppino-style soups, robust bouillabaisse).
4. **If unsalvageable:** discard; document; investigate (overcooked? Wrong fish species? Stale bones?).
5. **Lesson:** fumet timer is strict — 30–45 minutes max.

### Scenario 4 — Reducing a stock for demi-glace, sauce starts to scorch on the pan edges
1. **Immediately remove from heat.**
2. **Pour into a clean pan** — leave any scorched residue in the original pan.
3. **Don't scrape the burnt fond** — that's the bitter residue you want to leave behind.
4. **Reduce to gentle simmer** in the new pan.
5. **Lesson:** wide shallow pan + gentle heat for reductions; never high heat with concentrated liquids.

### Scenario 5 — Cooling failure at the 2-hour check
1. **Probe shows 28 °C at 2 hours.**
2. **Intensify cooling immediately:**
   - Add fresh ice to ice bath; refresh water.
   - Stir more aggressively.
   - Add another ice wand.
   - Or move to blast chiller.
3. **Re-probe in 30 min.**
4. **If still failing at 2.5 hours:** **reheat to ≥ 74 °C within 2 hours, then restart the cooling cycle** with corrected methodology. Document the deviation.
5. **If salvage isn't realistic:** discard; document. Inform supervisor.
6. **Investigate root cause:** vessel too deep? Ice bath too small? Walk-in too crowded?
7. **Lesson:** plan cooling vessels before the simmer ends; check 2-hour milestone.

### Scenario 6 — Sanitation lapse: probe used on raw bones, then directly on finished stock
1. **Confirmed:** the probe was not sanitized between uses.
2. **Action:** treat the finished stock as cross-contaminated; depending on the contamination point in the production timeline, may require:
   - Re-boiling the stock to ≥ 74 °C if just probed at the very end of cook.
   - Discarding if probed during cooling (no further kill step).
3. **Lesson:** sanitize the probe between every use. (See [A-2.01-research.md](A-2.01-research.md).)

## 4. Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

- **Boiling instead of simmering** — emulsifies fat and proteins; cloudy and greasy.
- **Stirring the pot** — drags scum and fat back into solution.
- **Covering tightly during the simmer** — traps fat aerosol; concentrates the stock unevenly.
- **Skipping early skimming** — first hour does most of the heavy work; missed = permanent cloudiness.
- **Pressing the strainer or sachet** — extracts bitter compounds and small particles.
- **Pouring instead of ladling** through strainers — disturbs sediment.
- **Topping up with cold water during the simmer** — drops temperature out of extraction zone.
- **Salting the stock** — concentrates aggressively during reduction; downstream uses lose flexibility.
- **Using oily fish in fumet** — strong, greasy, won't clarify.
- **Overcooking fumet (> 1 hour)** — bitter, fishy-sharp.
- **Overcooking vegetable stock (> 60 min)** — muddy, bitter.
- **Burning the bones during roast** — bitter, blackened, unsalvageable.
- **Discarding the roasting pan unwashed** — losing the *sucs* (the whole point of brown stock).
- **Letting the roasting pan cool before deglazing** — *sucs* harden and stick.
- **Reducing too aggressively in a deep pot** — scorching, slow evaporation, scorched-flavour transfer.
- **Cooling deep cambros in the walk-in** — *C. perfringens* paradise.
- **Covering during active cooling** — traps heat, slows cooling.
- **Not labelling the finished stock** — confused with other stocks; cross-use, allergen risk.
- **Adding bay leaves whole and forgetting them** — leaves stay in storage and over-extract.
- **Using a hot probe on raw bones immediately after on a finished stock** — cross-contamination.
- **Not logging cooling milestones** — invisible failures; HACCP non-compliance.

## 6. Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge — Summary

> The highest-yield content for written and practical assessments aligned with D-13.03.

1. **Simmer, don't boil.** Target ~ 85–95 °C; visible cue is a "trembling not rolling" surface.
2. **Cold water start, slow heat-up** — preserves clarity by allowing scum to denature and rise gradually.
3. **Skim early, skim often** — most scum forms in the first 30–60 minutes.
4. **Don't stir, don't press** — agitation re-emulsifies fat and scum.
5. **Heat transfer:** boiling drives flavour off as steam; simmering preserves it.
6. **Maillard reaction** ≥ 140–150 °C — the chemistry that browns bones for brown stock. Single-layer roasting required.
7. **Burnt ≠ browned** — past mahogany, bitter pyrazines dominate and the stock is unsalvageable.
8. **Deglaze the roasting pan** — *sucs* are the entire reason brown stock is brown.
9. **Standard simmer times:** veal/beef 8–12 h; chicken 3–4 h; fumet 30–45 min max; court-bouillon 30–45 min; vegetable 45–60 min; dashi 30 min + 3–5 min.
10. **Sachet d'épices and bouquet garni** added at the start; delicate herbs added late.
11. **Kombu removed before boil** in dashi; **katsuobushi steeped off heat 3–5 min** without pressing.
12. **Lid usage:** uncovered or cartouche-vented for clear stocks; never tightly covered.
13. **Stocks are unseasoned** — salt belongs in the final dish.
14. **Reduction stages:** stock → demi → glace; wider shallow pan, gentle heat.
15. **Staged straining:** china cap → fine chinois → cheesecloth-lined chinois. Ladle, don't pour. Don't press.
16. **Consommé clarification** uses a meat-and-egg-white raft as a coagulation filter.
17. **Quality test (gelatin):** a properly extracted veal/chicken stock sets to a wobbly gel when chilled.
18. **Cooling rule (Canada):** **60 → 20 °C in 2 h; 20 → 4 °C in 4 h; total ≤ 6 h.**
19. **Cooling methods (Range of Variables):** ice wands, venting, blast chiller, water bath.
20. **Blast chiller** drops 90 °C → 3 °C in ~ 90 min for shallow pans (gold standard).
21. **Ice bath needs water** (not ice alone); salt the ice water for sub-zero baths.
22. **Shallow pans ≤ 5 cm / 2 in** — surface-area-to-volume drives speed.
23. **Stocks are top *C. perfringens* / *B. cereus* risk** — spores survive simmer, germinate in slow cooling.
24. **Cover only after ≤ 4 °C** — covering during active cooling traps heat.
25. **Cooling logs** with 2 h and 6 h milestones — HACCP / PCP requirement under SFCR and many provincial programs.
26. **Hot hold ≥ 60 °C; cold hold ≤ 4 °C; reheat to 74 °C within 2 h.**
27. **Probe sanitation** between raw bones and finished stock — prevent cross-contamination.
28. **Tonkotsu exception:** intentional rolling boil to emulsify collagen and fat for opaque-white ramen broth.
29. **Acid (wine, vinegar, citrus)** reduced separately first to drive off raw alcohol before adding to stock reductions.
30. **Don't leave aromatics during cooling** — continued warm-temperature extraction muddies flavour.
