# C-12.05 | Finishes Fruit — Enhanced Learning Resource

**Major Work Activity:** C — Produce
**Task:** C-12 | Prepares Fruit
**Enrichment Level:** Red Seal Exam Preparation + Apprentice Field Reference

---

## Overview and Why This Skill Matters

Finishing is the bridge between a technically correct fruit preparation and a plate that is genuinely appealing and restaurant-ready. Every finishing decision — the choice of glaze, the sauce style, the garnish, the timing of service — affects how the guest experiences the dish. A beautifully poached pear served with a curdled sabayon fails. A well-caramelized pineapple left sitting in a steam table for 45 minutes fails. A strawberry fan garnish placed before the sauce is applied fails.

Finishing also carries significant **food safety responsibility**: it is the last stage before the food reaches the guest, and many finishing sauces (sabayon, crème anglaise, fruit coulis with cream) are TCS foods that require strict temperature discipline. The Red Seal exam tests whether cooks understand not just how to finish fruit, but why each technique is used and what the food safety obligations are at the finishing and holding stage.

## Why Finishing Technique Selection Matters

Each finishing technique serves a specific purpose:

| Technique | Primary Purpose | Secondary Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Glazing** | Protect surface; add shine; prevent oxidation/drying | Flavour addition; professional appearance |
| **Saucing** | Add flavour, moisture, richness; enhance appearance | Create a flavour bridge between fruit and other dish components |
| **Sautéing** | Re-thermalize; final caramelization for service | Activate last-minute Maillard/caramelization for a finished appearance |
| **Garnishing** | Add visual interest; textural contrast; colour | Reinforce flavour theme; signal to the guest what the dish contains |
| **Seasoning** | Balance and elevate existing flavours | Surprise the palate; bridge sweet and savoury elements |
| **Caramelizing** | Create a crisp sugar crust; add colour; deepen flavour | Textural contrast between crisp surface and soft interior |
| **Grilling** | Add grill marks; final caramelization; smoky note | Re-thermalize with visual finish for service |

Selection depends on:
- **The fruit:** Delicate berries need gentle finishing (sauce, garnish); robust stone fruit can handle grilling, caramelizing, or glazing
- **The dish context:** A plated dessert needs precise, refined finishing; a buffet needs practical, hold-stable finishing
- **Desired result:** Shine → glaze; flavour bridge → sauce; drama → flambé or brûlée; freshness → garnish
- **Service context:** À la minute service (individual orders) vs. batch production for banquets

## The Seven Finishing Techniques

### 1. Glazing

**Definition:** Applying a thin, shiny, cohesive coating to the surface of fruit to protect it from oxidation and drying, extend visual appeal, and add a controlled flavour note.

**Why glazing matters:**
- Cut fruit surfaces are exposed and vulnerable to enzymatic browning (PPO enzyme) and drying
- A glaze acts as a physical barrier, reducing oxygen contact and moisture loss
- A glaze on fresh fruit tarts and platters can extend attractive appearance by 30–60 minutes (common industry practice estimate)
- Glaze also adds a professional sheen that signals attention to craft

---

**Types of glaze and their applications:**

#### Apricot Nappage (Apricot Glaze) — Classical French Technique

**What it is:** Strained apricot jam, melted with a small amount of water, applied hot.

**Preparation:**
1. Combine apricot jam with a small amount of water (approximately 1 part water to 4 parts jam)
2. Bring to a simmer; cook until fully melted and smooth
3. Pass through a fine-mesh sieve to remove any remaining fruit pieces
4. Keep warm — the glaze must be hot and liquid to flow and set correctly (common working temperature: 60–70°C)
5. Apply immediately with a pastry brush in light, even strokes — do not re-brush areas that have begun to set

**Best fruit applications:** Apple slices, pear fans, pineapple, fresh fruit tarts, stone fruit on tarts and galettes

**Science:** Apricot jam sets to a light gel on cooling because of its natural pectin content. Applied hot, it is liquid enough to brush on smoothly. As it cools to room temperature, the pectin reforms a light gel that adheres to the surface and creates the characteristic sheen.

**Why apricot?** Apricot jam has a neutral, slightly tangy flavour that does not compete with the fruit it is applied to. Neutral glaze (nappage neutre — see below) is even more flavour-neutral, but apricot is the classical standard for pastry applications.

---

#### Nappage Neutre (Neutral Mirror Glaze)

**What it is:** A clear, flavour-neutral glaze made from a base of sugar, water, and a setting agent (glucose, pectin, or gelatin). Used in contemporary pastry to achieve a flawless mirror-like finish.

**Properties:**
- Transparent — shows the true colour of the fruit beneath
- Applied at approximately 30–35°C (must be fluid but not hot enough to wilt or melt delicate items)
- Sets to a firm, glossy gel at room temperature or refrigerator temperature

**Application:** Pour or brush over assembled fruit desserts; tarts; mirror-glazed entremets. Not typically used for large-volume service — primarily a refined pastry technique.

---

#### Reduced Poaching Liquid Glaze

**What it is:** The poaching liquid from C-12.04 (poached fruit), reduced by simmering until nappé consistency (coats the back of a spoon and holds a clean line when drawn through).

**Process:**
1. Strain poaching liquid after removing fruit
2. Simmer in a wide, heavy-bottomed saucepan — wide surface area promotes rapid evaporation
3. Reduce by 50–75% depending on starting sugar concentration
4. Test for nappe: coat the back of a spoon; draw a finger across — the line holds cleanly
5. Adjust sweetness or flavour if needed; strain again
6. Apply warm (50–60°C) as a glaze over the poached fruit using a pastry brush or spoon

**Double use:** The same liquid that cooked the fruit is transformed into its finishing sauce/glaze. This maximizes flavour concentration, minimizes waste, and creates a coherent dish where every component supports the same flavour profile.

---

#### Fruit-Based Glazes

- **Simple syrup reduction:** 2 parts sugar to 1 part water, reduced until thick; neutral; brushed over plated fresh fruit
- **Honey glaze:** Warm honey diluted slightly with warm water or fruit juice; applied with a brush; excellent with figs, pineapple, stone fruit
- **Chocolate glaze:** Melted dark or white chocolate, sometimes thinned with cream; for dipped strawberries, chocolate-dipped citrus, chocolate-glazed fruit tarts

---

**Glazing tools:**

| Tool | Use | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Pastry brush** | Applying warm glazes | Natural bristle or silicone; keep one dedicated for sweet applications |
| **Spoon** | Spooning glaze over fruit | For informal application; controlled drape effect |
| **Squeeze bottle** | Precision application of liquid glazes | For refined plating; small diameter tip for detail work |
| **Ladle** | Mirror glazes poured over entremets | From height; gravity ensures even coverage |

### 2. Saucing

**Definition:** Applying a complementary liquid preparation to accompany or coat the fruit, contributing flavour, richness, texture contrast, and visual appeal.

**Sauce selection logic:**

| Sauce Type | Flavour Profile | Best Fruit Pairing | Texture |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fruit coulis** | Bright, acidic, concentrated fruit | Same or complementary fruit; contrasting colour | Smooth, flowing; nappé |
| **Crème anglaise** | Rich, creamy, vanilla | All fruit; especially strong with poached pear, tarte Tatin | Light, flowing custard |
| **Sabayon** | Eggy, wine-forward, light and frothy | Stone fruit, figs, berries, pear | Airy, foam-like |
| **Caramel sauce** | Rich, sweet, slightly bitter | Apple, banana, pear, stone fruit | Thick, glossy |
| **Chocolate sauce** | Rich, intense, cocoa | Strawberry, banana, pear, orange | Glossy; pourable when warm |
| **Crème fraîche** | Tangy, rich, cool | Berries, stone fruit, citrus | Dollop; swirl; quenelle |
| **Honey** | Floral, sweet | Figs, peach, pear, cheese and fruit boards | Drizzle; thin thread |
| **Balsamic reduction** | Acidic, sweet, complex | Strawberry, fig, pear | Thick; concentrated; drizzle |
| **Whipped cream** | Neutral rich dairy | Most fruit | Piped or spooned |

---

#### Crème Anglaise — Classical Custard Sauce

**Composition:** Egg yolks, sugar, cream or milk, vanilla — cooked to a nappé consistency.

**The critical temperature — 74°C and the "ribbon stage":**

> **Food Safety — Egg-Based Sauces:**
> Crème anglaise contains raw egg yolks. *Salmonella* can be present in raw eggs. The sauce must reach **74°C** internally to pasteurize the egg yolks. This is a food safety requirement, not a stylistic choice.
>
> At 74°C, the egg yolks also partially coagulate — this is what creates the nappé (coating) consistency. At this temperature, the sauce is done.
>
> **Overcooking danger:** Above 82°C, egg proteins fully coagulate and the sauce curdles (scrambles). The finished product cannot be saved once curdled. This is why crème anglaise is cooked over a double boiler with constant stirring — the water bath's maximum temperature of 100°C slows heat transfer and gives the cook control.

**Crème anglaise doneness test:**
- Coat the back of a clean spoon; draw a finger across — the line holds cleanly (nappe)
- Use an instant-read thermometer: target 74–82°C; remove from heat immediately

**Crème anglaise as a TCS food:**
- Contains egg and dairy — two TCS components
- Must be cooled rapidly after cooking (ice bath) and stored at ≤4°C
- Maximum hot-holding time at ≥60°C for service: use within the 2-hour TDZ limit
- Cold crème anglaise served cold: must stay at ≤4°C throughout service
- Discard any crème anglaise held at room temperature for more than 2 hours

---

#### Sabayon — Egg-Based Foam Sauce

**Composition:** Egg yolks + sugar + liquid (white wine, Champagne, Marsala, fruit juice, water) — whipped over a hot water bath into a light foam.

**Process:**
1. Combine egg yolks, sugar, and liquid in a round-bottomed bowl (cul de poule)
2. Place over barely simmering water (bain-marie) — bowl must not touch the water
3. Whisk constantly and vigorously — the mixture expands 3–4 times in volume as air is incorporated and eggs warm
4. Monitor temperature: target **74°C** (food safety and optimal coagulation for stability)
5. Remove from heat; continue whisking for 1–2 minutes (stabilizes the foam)
6. Serve immediately — sabayon deflates within 15–20 minutes

**Why sabayon must be served immediately:**
The foam structure relies on protein networks formed by partially coagulated egg proteins. As it cools and the proteins relax, the structure collapses and liquid separates (syneresis). There is no recovery — serve at the moment of completion.

> **Food Safety — Sabayon:**
> Same egg pasteurization requirement as crème anglaise: 74°C internal temperature. Confirm with a thermometer — vigorous whisking and foam can make visual temperature assessment unreliable. Hot sabayon served immediately is safe. If a cold sabayon is required (for assembled desserts), cool rapidly in an ice bath immediately after cooking and refrigerate.

---

#### Fruit Coulis — Revisited at the Finishing Stage

Fruit coulis (covered in C-12.03 as a processing method) is used as a finishing sauce in plated presentations. At the finishing stage, the focus is on **application technique and plate presentation**:

**Sauce application methods:**

| Technique | Method | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Flood (lake)** | Pour sauce into the centre of a shallow bowl; tilt to spread | Even coverage; elegant for composed plates |
| **Swoosh** | Spoon sauce onto plate; drag back of spoon through it in one smooth motion | Directional; modern; casual-elegant |
| **Dots and smear** | Place dots in a line; drag a spoon tip through each to create a teardrop | Contemporary; refined; individual |
| **Drizzle** | Squeeze bottle or spoon; thin stream over the plated fruit | Adds visual texture without covering |
| **Frame** | Apply sauce around the perimeter of the plate; fruit in the centre | Classical; formal presentations |
| **Quenelle** | Form thick sauce into an oval quenelle shape; place beside fruit | Dimensional; elegant |

**Nappe consistency — the finishing standard:**
A sauce applied to a plated dish must hold its position on the plate. Too thin: it runs immediately and puddles at the plate edge. Too thick: it looks stiff and globs. **Nappe** — where the sauce coats the back of a spoon and holds a clean line when a finger is drawn through — is the target consistency for a plate-ready sauce.

---

#### Balsamic Reduction — Modern Classic

**What it is:** Balsamic vinegar reduced by approximately 50–75% over medium heat until syrupy, sweet-acidic, and concentrated.

**Why it works with fruit:**
- The acidity of balsamic vinegar contrasts with the sweetness of fruit, creating a balanced flavour dynamic
- The viscous, syrupy texture creates an elegant drizzle
- Dark brown colour provides visual contrast against bright fruit

**Best applications:** Strawberry, fig, pear, grilled stone fruit, melon. Also used as a finishing element in savoury applications with cheese plates featuring fruit.

---

### 3. Sautéing (as a Finishing Technique)

In the finishing context, sautéing is a **brief, high-heat final step** applied to already-prepared or pre-cooked fruit immediately before plating. Its purpose is not primary cooking but final activation of caramelization, re-thermalization, or sauce integration.

**Finishing sauté applications:**

| Application | Method | Service Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Re-thermalization** | Return refrigerated cooked fruit to a hot pan; sauté briefly until 74°C internal | Pre-prepared fruit compotes, poached fruit warmed to order |
| **Final caramelization** | Fresh-cut banana, pineapple, or stone fruit sautéed à la minute in butter and sugar | À la minute plated desserts; ensures maximum caramelization freshness |
| **Sauce integration** | Add sauce (cream, wine reduction) to pan after fruit; toss briefly to coat | Creates a unified glaze on the fruit surface |
| **Flambé finish** | Warm liqueur added to pan; ignite; allow to burn off; service immediately | Tableside drama; Bananas Foster; crêpes Suzette |

**Food safety — re-thermalization via sautéing:**
- Any previously cooked TCS fruit preparation re-thermalized via sautéing must reach **74°C internally** within **2 hours**
- Sautéing achieves this rapidly due to high surface temperature (160–190°C pan) — almost always achieves the target within the first few minutes
- Confirm with a thermometer for accountability in HACCP-managed kitchens

### 4. Garnishing

**Definition:** Adding elements to the finished plate that provide visual interest, textural contrast, colour contrast, or aromatic cues — without overwhelming or competing with the primary fruit component.

**The role of garnish in professional presentation:**
- A garnish should be **edible and intentional** — every element on the plate should be consumed and contribute to the eating experience
- Inedible or purely decorative garnishes are no longer standard in contemporary professional kitchens
- Garnish **signals what is in the dish**: fresh mint on a melon preparation; a citrus twist on a citrus dessert; a toasted almond on an amaretto-glazed peach

**Principles of effective garnishing:**

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Complementary colour** | Bright green mint against red strawberry; yellow lemon zest against purple berry coulis |
| **Proportional size** | Garnish should not compete with the primary component — a single small sprig, a pinch of zest |
| **Textural contrast** | Soft cooked fruit → crunchy garnish (toasted nuts, tuile, crumble); smooth sauce → something with texture |
| **Flavour alignment** | The garnish flavour should belong in the dish — mint on berries; vanilla on pear; chocolate on orange |
| **Placement** | Garnish placed last; after sauce; at a height that adds dimension without tipping |
| **The odd number rule** | Groups of 3 or 5 identical garnish elements appear more natural than 2 or 4 (common industry practice) |

**Common fruit garnishes and their applications:**

| Garnish | Application | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Fresh mint sprig** | Most fruit presentations | Universal; add at the last moment — wilts in heat; do not place in warm sauce |
| **Icing sugar (powdered sugar) dusting** | Berries, tarts, baked fruit, crêpes | Apply through a fine sieve immediately before service; dissolves quickly in moisture |
| **Citrus zest curls** | Citrus desserts; panna cotta; cheesecake | Channel knife or peeler; curl around skewer; remove skewer |
| **Candied citrus peel** | Chocolate and citrus combinations; refined tarts | Labour-intensive; produced in advance; holds well |
| **Toasted nuts (almond, hazelnut, pistachio)** | Stone fruit, pear, banana | Toast in dry pan; cool completely before using; allergen — declare |
| **Edible flowers** | Refined plated desserts; high-end banquet | Viola, nasturtium, borage; must be food-grade, pesticide-free |
| **Chocolate shavings or curls** | Chocolate and fruit pairings | Chocolate at room temperature produces curls; cold chocolate shatters |
| **Crème fraîche or whipped cream quenelle** | Most plated fruit desserts | Forms with two spoons; place beside, not on top of, warm fruit |
| **Tuile or brandy snap** | Refined plated desserts | Adds height; architectural element; bake fresh daily |
| **Fresh basil leaves (small)** | Strawberry, stone fruit, tropical fruit | Unexpected flavour bridge — basil's anise note complements fruit |

---

#### Mint Garnish — Practical Notes

Mint is the most common herb garnish for fruit in commercial kitchens. Key practical points:
- Pick mint **immediately before service** — it wilts rapidly after picking
- Refresh in cold water (4°C) for 5 minutes to firm the leaves and restore crispness before plating
- Pat dry before placing — excess water on the plate is an appearance issue
- **Never place mint in hot sauce or on hot surfaces** — heat wilts it within seconds
- For banquet production: refresh in cold water; spin gently; keep in a cold, damp container; use within the service period

### 5. Seasoning

**Definition:** Adjusting or amplifying the flavour of fruit through the targeted addition of salt, acid, sugar, spice, or alcohol.

**Why seasoning fruit is necessary:**

Most fruit contains a balance of sugar, acid, and aromatic compounds. Seasoning does not override this — it **reveals and enhances** what is already there. A pinch of salt on melon tastes more like melon, not like salt. A squeeze of lemon on strawberries tastes more like strawberry.

The science: salt suppresses bitterness receptors and amplifies sweetness and aroma perception. Acid (lemon juice) brightens volatile aromatics and increases the perception of freshness. Sugar increases sweetness and promotes browning when heated.

---

**The SASF Seasoning Sequence — Applied to Fruit:**

A useful decision framework for building balanced flavour in finished fruit preparations:

| Step | Element | Example in Fruit Context |
|---|---|---|
| **S — Salt** | Small amount of finishing salt to amplify sweetness | Fleur de sel on sliced melon; pinch on strawberry salad |
| **A — Acid** | Lemon juice, lime juice, balsamic | Lime juice and zest on mango; lemon juice on berry coulis |
| **S — Spice/heat** | Cinnamon, vanilla, cardamom, black pepper | Cinnamon on baked apple; pepper on strawberry; cardamom on peach |
| **F — Fat** | Butter (monter au beurre), cream, crème fraîche | Butter-finish on pan sauce; crème fraîche quenelle alongside |

---

**Seasoning elements and their effects on fruit:**

| Seasoning | Mechanism | Application | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Salt (finishing)** | Suppresses bitterness; amplifies sweetness and aroma | Fleur de sel or Maldon on melon, strawberry, watermelon | Small amount only — flavour, not a salting |
| **Lemon / lime juice** | Acid brightens volatile aromatics; inhibits PPO browning; increases Vitamin C | Strawberry, mango, melon, berry preparations | Add just before service; too early and aromatics escape |
| **Balsamic vinegar** | Complex acid-sweet; polymerized tannins add depth | Strawberry, fig, pear | Use good quality; aged balsamic has the most complexity |
| **Simple syrup** | Sweetens without grain texture; coats evenly | Macerating fruit; adjusting coulis sweetness | Room temperature; let dissolve fully |
| **Icing sugar** | Immediate sweetness and visual effect when dusted | Plate finish; crêpes; tarts | Apply last second — dissolves in moisture |
| **Black pepper** | Piperine amplifies aromatic compounds; warming counterpoint | Strawberry (classical French technique); fig; melon | Very small amount; freshly ground |
| **Cinnamon** | Warm, sweet-spicy aromatic | Apple, pear, banana, stone fruit | Complements caramelization flavours |
| **Vanilla** | Sweet, floral, amplifying | Nearly all fruit | Use with cream or custard elements; or as infusion |
| **Cardamom** | Floral, citrusy, exotic | Mango, peach, citrus | Pairs exceptionally well with stone fruit |
| **Chili / cayenne** | Capsaicin heat; counterpoint to sweetness | Mango, pineapple, watermelon | Used in Latin and Southeast Asian cuisine; increasingly on Canadian menus |
| **Liqueurs / spirits** | Alcohol carries and amplifies aromatic compounds; adds sweetness and complexity | Grand Marnier on citrus; Chambord on berries; rum on banana; Cointreau on peach | Alcohol is a solvent for aromatics; flambéing burns off most alcohol |

---

**Maceration — a key seasoning technique:**

**What it is:** Combining cut fruit with sugar, acid, and/or alcohol and allowing it to rest so that the flavour compounds marry and the fruit releases its natural juice.

**Science of maceration:**
- Sugar draws moisture out of the fruit by osmosis, creating a natural syrup from the fruit's own juice
- The released juice and the flavouring agents (lemon zest, vanilla, liqueur) create a self-made sauce that surrounds the fruit
- The fruit softens slightly and flavour compounds integrate over the maceration time

**Maceration time guidelines:**

| Fruit | Maceration Time | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Strawberries | 15–30 minutes | Ideal; longer = mushy |
| Mixed berries | 10–20 minutes | Delicate; shorter time |
| Stone fruit (peach, plum) | 30–60 minutes | Firmer; longer needed |
| Citrus supremes | 10–20 minutes | Juice integrates rapidly |
| Tropical fruit | 15–30 minutes | High juice content |

> **Maceration and food safety:** Macerating fruit at room temperature should not exceed 2 hours total (TDZ rule for TCS foods). For batch preparation, macerate in the refrigerator — allow 1–2 hours at ≤4°C for flavour development.

### 6. Caramelizing

**Definition:** Applying intense heat to a sugar-containing surface to produce caramelization — the thermal degradation of sugar into complex, sweet-bitter flavour compounds with deep amber colour and a crisp texture.

**Why caramelizing is used as a finishing technique:**
- Creates **textural contrast**: crisp, glass-like sugar surface against soft fruit interior
- Deepens **flavour complexity**: caramelized sugar has a bittersweet, nutty depth that plain sugar does not
- Provides **visual drama**: the shining, amber surface signals skill and refinement to the guest

---

**Brûlée — Sugar Crust with a Torch**

**What it is:** A thin layer of sugar (usually white granulated or superfine) applied to the surface of a fruit or cream preparation, then caramelized with a culinary torch until amber and glassy.

**Technique:**
1. Surface of the fruit or cream must be **level** — angled surfaces allow caramelizing sugar to run before it sets
2. Blot any excess moisture from the surface — moisture prevents even caramelization and creates steam spots
3. Apply a thin, even layer of superfine or granulated sugar (about 1–2 mm deep)
4. Sweep the torch flame across the surface in a continuous circular motion, keeping the flame 3–5 cm from the surface
5. The sugar melts, bubbles, and caramelizes within seconds — work quickly and evenly
6. Allow to set for 30–60 seconds before service — the surface is extremely hot immediately after torching
7. Serve within 3–5 minutes — the sugar crust softens as it absorbs moisture from the fruit beneath

**Torch safety — OH&S requirements:**

> **⚠️ Culinary Torch Safety**
> A culinary (butane) torch reaches flame temperatures exceeding 1,000°C and the tip becomes extremely hot in use.
>
> - **Keep torch away from flammable materials** — paper towels, cloth, cardboard, plastic
> - **Never leave a lit torch unattended**
> - **Check that the flame is fully extinguished** after use — gas can continue to flow briefly after release of the trigger
> - **Cool the torch tip** before storing — do not place in storage while still hot
> - **Use on a stable, heat-resistant surface** — ceramic plate, marble surface, stainless steel
> - **Point flame away from persons** — caramelizing sugar is liquid before it sets and can run
>
> Torch use is covered under provincial OH&S legislation as use of a tool with a hazard. Workers must be trained on safe use before operating a culinary torch.

**Brûlée fruit applications:**
- Crème brûlée (custard — the classic)
- Grapefruit brûlée: halved grapefruit, sugar applied to cut surface, torched; an elegant brunch preparation
- Fig brûlée: halved figs, lightly sugared, torched; pairs with cheese or yogurt
- Banana brûlée: banana half, brown sugar, torched; no pan required

---

**Pan Caramelization (without torch):**

- High-heat pan technique; fruit is sautéed in sugar and butter until caramel forms
- Covered in C-12.04 as part of sautéing, but used at the finishing stage when a pan-caramelized element is incorporated into the final plate
- **Tarte Tatin technique:** Apples caramelized directly in caramel in a heavy pan, then covered with pastry and baked; inverted at service — the caramelized surface is revealed at the finish

---

**Praline and Nut Brittle as Caramelized Garnish:**
- Praline: caramelized sugar poured over toasted nuts; cooled; crushed
- Used as a textural garnish on plated fruit desserts
- Provides sweetness, crunch, and nut flavour in one element
- **Allergen:** Contains nuts — must be declared

---

### 7. Grilling (as a Finishing Technique)

In the finishing context, grilling serves as a **final application** for:
- Last-minute caramelization for à la minute service
- Adding grill marks to a previously prepared component for visual enhancement
- Re-thermalizing pre-prepared fruit while adding a finishing charred note

**When to use grilling as a finish vs. as a cooking method:**
- As a **cooking method** (C-12.04): transforms raw or pre-prepared fruit into the primary cooked component
- As a **finishing technique** (C-12.05): brief, targeted grill exposure to achieve a specific surface result on otherwise-completed food

**Salamander (broiler) as a finishing grill:**
- A salamander (overhead broiler at 315–540°C) provides intense top-down heat
- Used for: glazing fruit tarts (final browning), brûlée-style caramelization, warming pre-assembled fruit preparations for service
- Faster than oven broiling; more controllable; essential finishing equipment in a professional kitchen

**Evidence of Attainment:** Cooked fruits are held according to jurisdictional regulations, recipe specifications, and desired results.

## Holding Cooked Fruit — Temperature, Time, and Quality

### The Holding Challenge for Cooked Fruit

Holding cooked fruit is more difficult than holding cooked protein because:
- Fruit textures degrade faster under prolonged heat — pectin continues to dissolve; grill marks fade; caramelization becomes sticky
- Fruit sauces (coulis, crème anglaise, sabayon) have strict time and temperature windows
- Fruit garnishes (fresh herbs, whipped cream, icing sugar) cannot be applied until the moment of service — they must be held separately
- Glazes on fruit dry and crack over time; a re-glaze may be needed

---

## Hot-Holding Requirements

> **Regulatory Requirement:** Under Canadian provincial food premises regulations, hot-held TCS food must be maintained at **≥60°C** at all times. This includes cooked fruit preparations served warm (compotes, sautéed fruit, poached fruit, glazed baked fruit).

**Steam table setup for fruit:**
- The water in the steam table must reach **≥82°C** before food is placed in it (common industry practice / HACCP prerequisite) — this ensures the food is actively maintained at ≥60°C, not slowly cooling from insertion temperature
- Pre-heat the steam table well before service
- Use shallow hotel pans (½ size or ⅙ size) for fruit — smaller volumes maintain temperature more evenly and allow faster turnover
- Check fruit temperature regularly with a calibrated probe thermometer; log every 2 hours in HACCP-managed operations

**Quality degradation during hot-holding — fruit-specific:**

| Time in Hot-Hold | Typical Quality Loss | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 0–15 minutes | Minimal; serving temperature | Ideal service window |
| 15–30 minutes | Texture begins to soften further; colour may shift | Acceptable if garnished à la minute |
| 30–60 minutes | Noticeable texture loss; colour fading in berry-based | Quality concern; batch replenishment advisable |
| Beyond 60 minutes | Mushy texture; grey/olive colour possible in green fruit; unappetizing | Discard and replace with fresh batch |

> **Industry Best Practice:** For fruit preparations with visible colour (berry compote, glazed tropical fruit), maximum hot-hold quality is 30–45 minutes. Beyond this, the product appearance degrades to an unacceptable service standard even if food safety temperature is maintained. Discard is a quality AND food safety decision.

---

## Cold-Holding Requirements

> **Regulatory Requirement:** Cold TCS food must be held at **≤4°C**. This applies to all chilled fruit preparations: fresh-cut fruit, fruit salads, fruit coulis, chilled poached fruit, crème anglaise, fruit tarts, assembled dessert plates waiting for service.

**Cold-holding specifics:**

| Preparation | Temperature | Maximum Hold Time | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fresh-cut fruit (no sauce) | ≤4°C | Per shelf life (2–5 days depending on fruit) | Covered; labelled; dated |
| Assembled fruit platter | ≤4°C or on ice | 2 hours at ambient; indefinite at ≤4°C | Uncovered platters at ambient temperature: strict 2-hour rule |
| Fruit coulis | ≤4°C | 3–5 days | Covered; in squeeze bottle or container |
| Crème anglaise | ≤4°C | 2–3 days | TCS; egg and dairy |
| Sabayon | ≤4°C (if chilled) | Same day; serve within hours | Deflates and weeps over time |
| Poached fruit in syrup | ≤4°C | 3–5 days | Stored in syrup maintains quality |
| Baked fruit (whole) | ≤4°C | 2–3 days | Cool before refrigerating |
| Glazed fruit tart | ≤4°C | 24 hours | Pastry absorbs moisture over time |
| Macerated fruit | ≤4°C | 1–2 days | Quality declines as fruit softens |
| Fruit salad (mixed) | ≤4°C | 1–2 days | Fastest-decaying component sets the limit |

## The Temperature Danger Zone and Fruit Finishing

**TDZ:** 4°C–60°C
**Maximum cumulative time for TCS food in TDZ:** 2 hours

At the finishing stage, time in the TDZ accumulates from:
- Time for the cook to plate the dish
- Time waiting on the pass
- Time in service before the guest consumes it
- Time on a buffet or platter at ambient temperature

In a busy service, this accumulation can be rapid. Tracking is essential.

**Buffet / platter service — fruit display:**

| Holding Method | Temperature Control | Maximum Display Time |
|---|---|---|
| **On ice** (contact or surround) | ≤4°C maintained | Indefinite if ice level maintained; replenish ice |
| **Ambient (room temperature)** | No control | **2 hours maximum** — then discard |
| **Hot-hold in chafing dish** | ≥60°C | Operational service period; quality monitor every 30–45 min |
| **Refrigerated display case** | ≤4°C | Full service period |

> **Regulatory Note:** Once cut fruit has been displayed at ambient temperature for 2 hours, it must be discarded. This is not optional — it is required under Canadian provincial food premises regulations governing TCS foods.

## Discard Decision Framework

When assessing whether held cooked fruit should be served or discarded:

**Discard immediately if:**
- Temperature has been in TDZ for a cumulative total exceeding 2 hours
- Visible mold or spoilage
- Off-odour (sour, fermented, or otherwise abnormal)
- Texture is completely unacceptable (mushed, collapsed, leaking excessively)
- Not sure how long it has been held — when in doubt, throw it out

**Discard as a quality decision:**
- Even if food safety criteria are met (temperature within limits), fruit held beyond quality thresholds (60 minutes hot-hold for most cooked fruit) should be replaced with a fresh batch
- A professional kitchen discard decision protects the guest experience AND food safety

**Logging discards:**
In HACCP-managed operations, discards — including the reason — are logged. Temperature logs that show a product exceeded holding time or temperature limits are regulatory documents that must be retained. This protects the operation in the event of a foodborne illness investigation.

## Jurisdictional Regulations at the Finishing and Holding Stage

### WHMIS

**Relevance to finishing:**
- Culinary torches use butane (compressed flammable gas) — WHMIS regulated; a Safety Data Sheet (SDS) must be accessible; workers must be trained on storage, use, and what to do in case of a release or fire
- Cleaning chemicals used on finishing stations require SDS compliance
- Torch canisters stored correctly — upright, away from heat sources, in a ventilated area per SDS

> **Regulatory Requirement:** WHMIS is federal legislation (Hazardous Products Act). All workers who use or may be exposed to hazardous products — including butane torch fuel — must be trained on safe use.

### HACCP

**Finishing and holding as HACCP control points:**

| HACCP Element | Finishing Stage Application |
|---|---|
| **CCP: Hot-holding temperature** | Monitor and log hot-held cooked fruit at ≥60°C; log every 2 hours |
| **CCP: Re-thermalization** | Log time, start temperature, end temperature (74°C target) |
| **PRP/GMP: Personal hygiene** | No bare-hand contact with ready-to-eat finished fruit; gloves or utensils |
| **PRP/GMP: Glaze/sauce time-temperature** | Glazes and sauces applied at correct temperature; record |
| **CCP: TDZ time tracking** | Log start time for ambient-temperature fruit display |
| **Documentation** | Temperature logs are legal documents — retained per health authority requirements |

**Critical point — no bare-hand contact:**
Ready-to-eat (RTE) food at the finishing stage must not be touched with bare hands. This includes:
- Placing garnishes
- Applying glaze
- Arranging fruit on the plate
- Adjusting plating

Use clean utensils (tongs, spoons, tweezers, gloved hands). This is a food safety requirement in most provincial food premises regulations.

### OH&S

**Finishing-specific OH&S hazards:**

| Hazard | Risk | Control |
|---|---|---|
| Culinary torch flame | Burns; fire; ignition of nearby materials | Training; clear workspace; extinguisher nearby |
| Caramelized sugar (liquid at high temp) | Severe burns — sugar sticks to skin and maintains heat | Long-handled tools; heatproof gloves; don't lean over |
| Steam from steam table | Burns on face, arms | Tilt lid away when lifting; approach from the side |
| Hot saucepans | Burns | Dry cloths; clear calling ("hot behind"); signal with damp cloth on handle |
| Slip hazards from sauce spills | Falls | Immediate cleanup; anti-slip mats; non-slip footwear |
| Repetitive motion during plating (high-volume service) | Strain injury | Ergonomic tool design; rotation; breaks |

### Public Health Regulations

**Relevant requirements at the finishing stage:**
- No bare-hand contact with RTE food
- TCS food must be maintained at ≤4°C (cold) or ≥60°C (hot)
- Time in TDZ ≤2 hours cumulative
- Sauces and glazes containing egg or dairy must be prepared with pasteurized eggs or heated to 74°C
- Ready-to-eat food must be protected from contamination at all points, including during plating and garnishing
- Personal hygiene: clean hands, gloves for RTE food contact, no ill workers handling food

## Presentation Principles for Finished Fruit

**CCHH — Four visual principles:**
- **C**olour: choose garnish and sauce for colour contrast with the primary fruit
- **C**lean plate: wipe any sauce drips, fingerprints, or smudges from the plate rim before service
- **H**eight: create dimension — a quenelle, a tuile, a stacked component adds vertical interest
- **H**armony: every element on the plate belongs there and contributes flavour, texture, or colour coherence

**The plate should answer three questions:**
1. What is the primary component? (the fruit — clearly dominant)
2. What accompanies it? (sauce, cream, complementary element — supporting, not competing)
3. What signals freshness and skill? (garnish, glaze, clean plate edges)

## Common Mistakes — Finishing Fruit

| Mistake | Consequence | Correct Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Applying glaze to wet surface | Glaze slides off; uneven coat; poor adhesion | Blot surface dry before glazing |
| Glazing with cooled apricot nappage | Thick, sticky, uneven coat; lumps | Keep glaze hot (60–70°C) and fluid during application |
| Placing mint on hot sauce or hot fruit | Mint wilts within seconds; visual failure | Apply mint last, to a cool surface or beside the hot element |
| Saucing the plate before adding fruit | Sauce disturbed by fruit placement; messy plate | Place fruit first; sauce after; garnish last |
| Holding sabayon for extended service | Collapses and weeps within 15–20 minutes | Make sabayon to order; or chill immediately and re-warm |
| Re-thermalizing in a bain-marie | May not reach 74°C; food safety violation | Reheat on stovetop or in oven; bain-marie for hot-holding only |
| Bare-hand contact with finished plate elements | Cross-contamination of RTE food | Use tongs, tweezers, gloved hands |
| Displaying fruit at ambient for more than 2 hours | TCS violation; pathogen growth risk | Use ice; time-stamp; discard at 2 hours |
| Applying icing sugar too early | Sugar dissolves in moisture; visual effect lost | Dust icing sugar in the final 30 seconds before service |
| Torching caramel over a wet surface | Steam spots; uneven caramel | Blot dry; level surface |
| Overcrowding the plate with garnishes | Loses visual clarity; each element competes | One or two well-chosen garnishes; less is more |
| Not logging temperature of held preparations | HACCP non-compliance; traceability failure | Log at minimum every 2 hours for hot-held TCS food |

## 12 Q&A Pairs — Red Seal Exam Preparation

**Q1: What is the purpose of apricot nappage, and what is the correct working temperature for application?**
A: Apricot nappage is a strained, melted apricot jam glaze used to protect cut fruit from oxidation and drying, extend visual appeal, and add a professional shine. It must be applied hot — approximately 60–70°C — to remain fluid enough to flow smoothly across the surface. As it cools, the natural pectin in the jam reforms a light gel that adheres to the fruit and creates the characteristic gloss. Applying cold or cooled apricot nappage produces a thick, lumpy, unattractive coating.

**Q2: Why must crème anglaise reach 74°C, and what happens if it goes above 82°C?**
A: Crème anglaise contains raw egg yolks, which can harbour *Salmonella*. Reaching 74°C is the food safety requirement for pasteurizing the eggs — this is a regulatory requirement, not a stylistic choice. The sauce also requires partial egg coagulation for its nappé consistency, which occurs in the 74–82°C range. Above 82°C, egg proteins fully coagulate and the sauce curdles (scrambles) — the product is ruined and cannot be recovered.

**Q3: Why must sabayon be served within 15–20 minutes of production?**
A: Sabayon's foam structure depends on partially coagulated egg proteins holding an air network. As the foam cools, the protein networks relax and collapse, liquid separates (syneresis), and the light, frothy texture is lost. There is no recovery — it cannot be re-whipped. For service, sabayon must be made to order or in very small batches timed precisely to service.

**Q4: What is the SASF seasoning sequence, and how does it apply to finishing fruit?**
A: SASF stands for Salt → Acid → Spice/heat → Fat. Applied to fruit: (S) a pinch of finishing salt on melon or strawberry suppresses bitterness and amplifies sweetness; (A) a squeeze of lemon juice brightens aromatics and inhibits browning; (S) spice such as black pepper on strawberry or cinnamon on apple adds a warming counterpoint; (F) a final touch of butter or crème fraîche adds richness and rounds the finish.

**Q5: What is the 2-hour rule for cut fruit displayed at ambient temperature, and what must happen at that point?**
A: Under Canadian provincial food premises regulations, TCS food — including cut fresh fruit — cannot remain in the Temperature Danger Zone (4°C–60°C) for more than 2 hours cumulatively. At the 2-hour mark for ambient-temperature display, the fruit must be discarded. It cannot be returned to refrigeration and re-displayed. This is a regulatory requirement. The alternative is to keep the display at ≤4°C (on ice) or ≥60°C (in a heated unit), which removes the time restriction.

**Q6: What is the safe procedure for using a culinary torch, and what OH&S requirement covers it?**
A: Safe procedure: ensure the workspace is clear of flammable materials; keep flame 3–5 cm from the surface; sweep continuously; confirm the flame is fully extinguished after use; cool the tip before storing. OH&S regulations require that workers be trained on the safe use of tools and equipment. Butane torch fuel is also a WHMIS-regulated product, requiring SDS access and user training. Culinary torches involve both flame and contact burn risks — caramelized sugar is liquid before setting and extremely hot.

**Q7: What is the purpose of maceration, and what is the food safety limit for macerating at room temperature?**
A: Maceration combines cut fruit with sugar, acid, and/or alcohol and allows it to rest so that sugar draws natural juice out of the fruit by osmosis, creating a self-made syrup. The acid, alcohol, and flavour compounds then marry into the fruit and its released juice. At room temperature, maceration is limited to 2 hours — the TDZ rule for TCS foods. For longer maceration, the process must be carried out in the refrigerator at ≤4°C.

**Q8: Why must cooked fruit preparations be re-thermalized on the stovetop or in an oven rather than in a steam table?**
A: The re-thermalization requirement is 74°C internal temperature within 2 hours. A steam table is a hot-holding unit that maintains food at ≥60°C — it heats slowly from the outside and may not bring the interior of a chilled preparation to 74°C within the required 2-hour window. Using a steam table for re-thermalization is a food safety violation. The correct method: reheat on stovetop or in an oven until 74°C is confirmed, then transfer to the steam table for continued hot-holding at ≥60°C.

**Q9: What are three principles of effective garnishing for finished fruit plates?**
A: (1) **Complementary colour:** the garnish should contrast or complement the fruit's colour (mint green against red strawberry; white crème fraîche quenelle against dark berry coulis). (2) **Proportional size:** the garnish should enhance, not compete — one small sprig, a pinch of zest, not a large pile. (3) **Flavour alignment:** the garnish should belong in the dish — it should reinforce the flavour theme (basil on stone fruit; cinnamon on apple; vanilla on pear).

**Q10: When does no-bare-hand contact with finished fruit apply, and what tools are used instead?**
A: No bare-hand contact with ready-to-eat (RTE) food is required under most provincial food premises regulations. At the finishing stage, all fruit has been cleaned, processed, cooked, and is now RTE — no further kill step follows. This rule applies to: placing garnishes, applying glaze, arranging fruit on the plate, adjusting plating. Tools used: tongs, tweezers, clean spoons, offset spatulas, gloved hands. One exception in some jurisdictions: gloved hands are acceptable where bare-hand contact is otherwise permitted — but the gloves must be clean and changed between tasks.

**Q11: What is the nappe consistency standard for a finishing sauce, and how is it tested?**
A: Nappe means the sauce coats the back of a spoon and holds a clean line when a finger is drawn through it. To test: dip the back of a clean metal spoon into the sauce; hold it horizontally; draw a finger across the coated surface. If the line holds cleanly without the sauce flowing back into it, the sauce is at nappe consistency. Too thin — the line fills in immediately. Too thick — the sauce hangs in a glob. Nappe is the standard for crème anglaise, reduced fruit sauces, and coulis used for plate saucing.

**Q12: What are the four quality characteristics of held cooked fruit that must be maintained, and what specific actions protect each?**
A: (1) **Colour** — maintain hot-hold temperature at ≥60°C but minimize hold time (anthocyanins degrade with extended heat; carotenoids are stable); apply acid to berry preparations. (2) **Texture** — avoid prolonged hot-holding (pectin continues to dissolve; maximum 30–60 minutes for most cooked fruit); do not re-cook when re-thermalizing. (3) **Flavour** — minimize hold time; apply volatile garnishes (herbs, fresh acid) à la minute rather than in advance. (4) **Temperature** — monitor with calibrated probe thermometer; hot-hold ≥60°C; cold-hold ≤4°C; document in temperature logs.
