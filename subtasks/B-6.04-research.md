# B-6.04 | Maintains Inventory — Enhanced Learning Resource

**Major Work Activity:** B — Common Occupational Skills
**Task:** B-6 | Manages Products

> **What this subtask is about:** Inventory maintenance is the active, ongoing management of everything in the kitchen's storage system — not just the act of ordering and putting product away, but continuously verifying what's there, how much is there, whether it's still good, whether it's being used in the right order, and what to do when it isn't. A kitchen that maintains its inventory well wastes less, costs less, feeds guests safer food, and passes health inspections. A kitchen that ignores inventory accumulates spoilage, generates mystery waste, and runs out of product unexpectedly. This document enriches the Red Seal competency with the science, methodology, regulations, and financial reasoning behind professional inventory management.

---

## Inventory Maintenance as an Integrated System

Inventory maintenance is not a single task performed once a week — it is a continuous process with daily, weekly, and periodic components:

| Frequency | Activity | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Daily** | Visual quality check of high-risk perishables (fresh protein, seafood, open dairy, fresh produce) | Detect deteriorating product before it causes a food safety issue or service failure |
| **Daily** | FIFO verification at each station's mise en place prep | Ensure oldest product is drawn first; catch rotation violations immediately |
| **Per service** | Par level check during mise en place setup | Ensure adequate quantities for service; trigger ordering for next delivery |
| **Weekly** | Full physical inventory count | Calculate food cost; update ordering quantities; detect waste and shrink patterns |
| **Weekly / biweekly** | Deep inspection of all storage areas | Remove expired product; verify storage organization; check temperature logs |
| **Monthly** | Inventory variance analysis | Compare actual food cost to theoretical food cost; identify over-portioning, spoilage, or theft |

### B-6.04.01P
**Performance Criteria:** Verify quantities of products

**Evidence of Attainment:** Quantities of products are verified to ensure par levels according to business volume

---

#### Deep Dive: Verifying Quantities and Par Levels

**What "Verifying to Par" Means**

Verifying quantities means physically counting current on-hand stock and comparing it to the par level for each item. This comparison answers the ordering question: *how much do I need to order to restore inventory to the level required to meet production needs until the next delivery?*

```
Order quantity = Par level − Current on-hand + Anticipated production draw-down before next delivery

Where:
  Par level = (Average daily usage × Days between deliveries) + Safety stock buffer
  Current on-hand = Physical count of product in storage right now
  Anticipated draw-down = How much will be used between now and the next delivery
```

**Conducting a Physical Inventory Count**

A physical inventory count is the foundation of accurate inventory management. To be meaningful, it must be conducted systematically:

**The Count Procedure:**

1. **Use a standardized count sheet** — organized by storage area (walk-in cooler, freezer, dry storage); listed in the same physical order you walk through the area so nothing is missed
2. **Count one area at a time** — complete the walk-in before moving to the freezer; do not jump between areas
3. **Two-person count for high-value items** — one person counts; one person records; counters verify each other
4. **Count in consistent units** — use the same unit of measure every time (kg, L, each, case); never mix units within the same item's history
5. **Do not estimate** — physical count means physically counting or weighing; "approximately 3 kg of chicken" is not an inventory entry; weigh it
6. **Date and sign the count sheet** — required for HACCP documentation and food cost management records
7. **Count before the delivery, not after** — so the count reflects what is actually on hand independent of the new delivery

**Conducting the Count in Each Storage Area**

| Storage Area | Count Method | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Walk-in cooler** | Count from front to back, shelf by shelf, top to bottom | Check dates while counting; flag any items that need to be moved forward (FIFO violation) |
| **Freezer** | Check all items; check dates; note any frost-obscured labels | Temperature of the freezer; note any items showing thaw evidence |
| **Dry storage** | Count by shelf; note any damaged packaging; check pest evidence | Look behind items; pests and expired product hide at the back |
| **Prep containers in service** | Count dated containers; check rotation labels against today's date | Identify any prep containers approaching use-by date |

**Adjusting Par Levels for Business Volume**

Par levels are not static — they must be adjusted for:
- **Seasonal fluctuations:** Summer volume vs. winter slow season; holiday peaks
- **Upcoming events:** A large banquet this Friday requires a temporarily elevated par for specific proteins and produce
- **Menu changes:** A new dish added to the menu requires a new par level for its key ingredients
- **Historical waste analysis:** If a product consistently reaches par level and part of it spoils before use, the par level is set too high for actual consumption patterns

> **Operational Recommendation:** Review par levels at minimum quarterly. Compare them against actual usage over the preceding period. A par level that made sense in summer may be causing chronic over-purchasing in November.

### B-6.04.02P
**Performance Criteria:** Verify quality of perishable products

**Evidence of Attainment:** Quality of perishable products are verified according to sensory assessment and date received

#### Deep Dive: Quality Verification in Storage

Quality verification during ongoing inventory maintenance is different from receiving inspection (B-6.02). At receiving, you inspect product arriving from outside. During inventory maintenance, you inspect product that has been in storage — verifying it remains acceptable and safe to use before it reaches a cook's hands.

**The Daily Quality Walk**

High-risk perishables should be visually and olfactorily inspected every day:

**What to look for and what it means:**

**Fresh Proteins (Meat, Poultry)**

| Sign | What It Indicates | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Bright red beef → gradually browning at surface | Surface oxidation; protein aging; may still be safe within shelf life | Check date; use promptly if within shelf life |
| Grey or brown throughout | Advanced age; possible spoilage | Check date and smell; if sour or off-odour, discard |
| Slimy film on surface | Bacterial colonization of surface; *Pseudomonas* and *Lactobacillus* species produce biofilm | Discard |
| Sour, ammonia, or putrid odour | Active bacterial proteolysis; protein breakdown underway | Discard |
| Excessive drip in container | Protein weeping from age or previous temperature abuse | Check date; smell; if within shelf life and no off-odour, inspect closely before use |
| Vacuum-packed with gas inflation | Bacterial gas production inside pack; packaging compromised or product spoiling | Discard |

**Fresh Seafood**

| Sign | What It Indicates | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Fresh ocean smell → increasingly "fishy" | TMA (trimethylamine) accumulation from bacterial TMAO decomposition | Use immediately if still mild; discard if strong |
| Flesh losing firmness | Proteolytic enzyme and bacterial activity breaking down muscle structure | Check date; if soft and yielding to moderate pressure, use for cooked applications only |
| Edges of fillet turning opaque white | Surface drying and protein denaturation; quality loss | Trim and use in cooked applications; do not use raw |
| Any ammonia smell | Advanced decomposition | Discard immediately |

**Fresh Produce**

| Sign | What It Indicates | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Slight wilting of leaf greens | Moisture loss; refrigerator humidity too low; approaching end of quality life | Use today; soaking in cold water may revive wilted greens |
| Yellowing at leaf margins | Chlorophyll breakdown from age or ethylene exposure | Use today; trim affected portions |
| Visible mold (fuzzy growth) | Fungal colonization | Discard entire item; inspect all adjacent items |
| Soft spots or browning on solid vegetables | Bacterial soft rot; cellular breakdown | Trim generously if superficial; discard if extensive |
| Off odour from produce cooler | Fermentation or rot underway somewhere | Systematic search; remove the source immediately |

**Dairy**

| Sign | What It Indicates | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Sour smell in cream or milk | Lactic acid bacterial growth; acidification | Discard |
| Cream curdles when heated | Acidity too high; cream is too old or has been temperature-abused | Discard |
| Butter with rancid smell (soapy, cheesy, or paint-like) | Short-chain fatty acid oxidation (butyric acid, valeric acid) | Discard |
| Surface mold on hard cheese | Normal on some aged cheeses; abnormal on young/soft cheeses | Hard cheese: cut away 2.5 cm around and below the mold, use if remainder smells and tastes fine; soft cheese: discard entire piece |

**The Critical Rule: Never Taste-Test Suspect Product**

> **Food Safety Warning:** The smell of a product can give you a significant amount of information about its safety. Taste should only be used for quality evaluation of product that has already passed visual and olfactory inspection. **Never taste-test a product that smells off or has visible spoilage.** Certain foodborne illness toxins (Staphylococcus aureus enterotoxins, Bacillus cereus emetic toxin, botulinum toxin) are odourless and tasteless — and re-thermalization will not destroy preformed toxins. If a product has visible spoilage or off-odours, discard it without tasting.

**Date-Based Quality Assessment**

Every quality check is tied to the date received or preparation date on the rotation label:

```
Today's date: May 22
Product: Ground beef
Received date (from label): May 19
Refrigerated shelf life for ground meat: 1–2 days
Days in storage: 3 days
Status: EXCEEDED MAXIMUM SHELF LIFE → Discard regardless of appearance
```

> **Critical principle:** If a product has exceeded its shelf life reference, it should be discarded even if it looks and smells acceptable. Bacteria that have multiplied to dangerous levels in the product do not necessarily produce visible or olfactory signs. The date is an independent safety criterion, not a secondary one.

### B-6.04.03P
**Performance Criteria:** Perform stock rotation

**Evidence of Attainment:** Stock rotation is performed according to company policies

---

#### Deep Dive: Stock Rotation in Practice

**FIFO: The Rule and the Execution**

First In, First Out means the oldest product is always the next product used. The **system** of FIFO is built into how products are stored (covered in B-6.03); the **practice** of FIFO is verified through ongoing inventory maintenance.

**Active FIFO Rotation — What It Looks Like in a Working Kitchen**

*When new product arrives:*
1. Before putting anything away, identify all existing stock of the same item
2. Move existing stock forward/out; place new product behind/below it
3. Verify that the rotation labels on existing stock are visible and legible after the new product is placed

*During daily inventory walk:*
1. For each product in refrigerated storage, check that the item nearest the front (easiest to access) has the oldest date
2. If a newer item has been placed in front of an older one, correct it immediately
3. Flag any item that is approaching its use-by date for immediate use or disposal decision

*During mise en place prep:*
1. Pull product from the oldest batch first
2. If two containers of the same item are open, use the older one before opening the newer one
3. Never reach past a dated item to use a newer one because it's "easier to reach"

**What to Do When FIFO Has Been Violated**

Discovering that newer product has been used before older product doesn't just require organizational correction — it requires a quality decision about the older product:

```
Discovery: Chicken breast from May 19 is behind chicken from May 21
Today is: May 22

Analysis:
  May 19 chicken: 3 days old (shelf life: 1–3 days for poultry)
  → At or beyond shelf life limit
  → Must be quality-inspected immediately
  → If within appearance/odour standards: prioritize for immediate use
  → If any doubt: discard

  May 21 chicken: 1 day old
  → Move to front for next use after May 19 batch
```

**Rotation in Freezers: Special Challenges**

Freezer rotation is harder to maintain than refrigerator rotation because:
- Labels can become covered with frost or ice
- Visibility inside a loaded freezer is limited
- Items can freeze together and become difficult to rearrange

*Best practices for freezer FIFO:*
- Label every item with a **permanent marker** before freezing; labels should be on the **outside face** of the package, not the top
- De-ice labels that have become obscured (a warm, damp cloth over the frost briefly)
- Dedicate specific freezer zones to specific products; always load new product in the zone in a consistent direction
- Perform a freezer organization audit monthly: pull everything out; check dates; reload with proper FIFO

#### Deep Dive: Expired Product Disposal

**The Decision to Discard**

Disposal decisions involve two independent criteria, both of which can independently trigger a discard:

1. **Date-based:** The product has exceeded its maximum safe shelf life (use-by or calculated use-by date)
2. **Condition-based:** The product shows visible or olfactory signs of spoilage or quality failure, regardless of date

A product that fails *either* criterion must be discarded. A product does not need to be both overdate AND visually spoiled to be discarded — either alone is sufficient.

**Documenting Disposal**

All disposal of expired or spoiled product should be recorded in a **waste log**:

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| Date of disposal | Today's date |
| Product description | What was discarded (name, spec, market form) |
| Quantity | Weight or unit count discarded |
| Approximate value | Cost of the discarded product (for food cost tracking) |
| Reason for disposal | Expired (date exceeded); Spoilage (condition failure); Recalled; Temperature abuse detected; etc. |
| Employee responsible | Who made the disposal decision |
| Supervisor notification | Was the supervisor informed? (required for high-value disposals) |

*Why documenting disposal matters:*
- Food cost tracking: waste that isn't documented creates an unexplained gap between theoretical and actual food cost
- HACCP: removal of unsafe product is a corrective action that should be recorded
- Pattern analysis: repeated disposal of the same product may indicate over-ordering, storage temperature issues, or ordering lead times that don't match the product's shelf life

**Disposal Methods by Product Type**

| Product Type | Disposal Method | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Vegetable and fruit trim; produce waste** | Composting / organic waste bin | Clean organic material; separate from packaging; compostable by most municipal programs |
| **Meat, poultry, seafood (expired/spoiled)** | Sealed bag → general waste or commercial organic waste container | Never dispose in open compost bins accessible to pests; double-bag and seal; dispose in covered containers |
| **Dairy (expired)** | Organic waste / drain in some municipalities | Check local organic waste guidelines; avoid pouring large quantities of dairy down drains (creates BOD issues) |
| **Cooked food (expired prep)** | Organic waste / compost where accepted | Dated containers make disposal decisions clear |
| **Bread and baked goods** | Organic waste / compost | Do not repurpose overdate baked goods for service |
| **Dry goods (expired, opened, contaminated by pests)** | Sealed in plastic bag → general waste | If pest-contaminated: seal immediately; notify pest control |
| **Swollen or damaged cans** | Do not open; double-bag; label "HAZARDOUS – DO NOT OPEN"; dispose according to local hazardous waste guidelines | *C. botulinum* risk; do not puncture or empty; treat as potentially hazardous material |
| **Chemical products (WHMIS-classified)** | Dispose according to SDS Section 13 and provincial hazardous waste regulations | Do not pour concentrated chemicals down drains; do not mix chemicals for disposal |

**Recalled Products**

When a food product is subject to a recall by the Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA) or a supplier:
1. Stop using the product immediately
2. Isolate the product from all other food in storage — a physical barrier (locked area, separate container) with a "DO NOT USE — RECALLED PRODUCT" label
3. Verify the recalled product against your receiving records and shellfish tags (for shellfish recalls)
4. Document all recalled product on-hand: product name, lot code, date received, quantity
5. Follow the recall notification instructions — the CFIA or supplier will specify whether to dispose of the product or hold it for pickup
6. Do not serve recalled product even if you believe it is safe — a recall is a public health order

#### Deep Dive: Inventory Terminology

| Term | Definition | Practical Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Par level** | Minimum on-hand quantity required to meet production needs between deliveries | Set by: daily usage × delivery frequency + safety stock |
| **Safety stock** | Buffer above par level to account for unexpected demand or delivery delays | Typically 25–50% of one delivery period's usage |
| **Physical inventory** | Actual count of all product on hand at a specific moment | The definitive measure of what is actually in storage |
| **Perpetual inventory** | Running tally updated with every receipt and every issue | Theoretical on-hand between physical counts |
| **Inventory variance** | Difference between perpetual inventory and physical count | Indicates waste, theft, counting errors, or unrecorded usage |
| **FIFO** | First In, First Out; oldest stock used before newer stock | Prevents spoilage; food safety function |
| **LIFO** | Last In, First Out; used in some accounting contexts | NOT appropriate for perishable food storage (causes older stock to remain until spoilage) |
| **Shrinkage** | Unaccounted loss of inventory (through waste, theft, evaporation, overportioning) | Tracked by comparing theoretical to actual inventory |
| **Turnover rate** | How quickly inventory is used relative to the amount held | Higher turnover = fresher product; lower turnover = potential over-purchasing or slow sales |
| **Shelf life** | The period during which a product remains safe, wholesome, and of acceptable quality under specified storage conditions | Determines use-by dates and acceptable rotation timing |
| **Use-by date** | Date after which a product should not be used; safety-critical | Applied to high-risk perishables; calculated from preparation date + shelf life |
| **Best-before date** | Date after which quality may decline but product may still be safe | Consumer-label term; beyond best-before ≠ automatically unsafe, but evaluate carefully |
| **Lot code** | Alphanumeric identifier on commercial packaging indicating the production batch | Essential for product recalls; enables tracing a specific batch |
| **Opening inventory** | Value of product on hand at the start of an accounting period | Used to calculate cost of goods used |
| **Closing inventory** | Value of product on hand at the end of an accounting period | Used to calculate cost of goods used |
| **Cost of goods used (COGS)** | Opening inventory + Purchases − Closing inventory | The dollar value of all food used during the period |
| **Food cost percentage** | (COGS ÷ Food revenue) × 100 | Key financial metric; benchmark: 28–35% for most operations |
| **Waste log** | Record of all discarded product with quantity, reason, and value | Connects spoilage to food cost variance; HACCP documentation |

#### Deep Dive: Inventory Control Systems

**Four Inventory Control Systems in Commercial Kitchens**

**System 1: Manual Periodic Inventory**

The most common approach in independent restaurants and small foodservice operations.
- Physical count taken on a defined schedule (typically weekly for perishables; monthly for dry goods)
- Counted on standardized inventory sheets organized by storage area
- Values calculated: quantity × unit cost = item value; sum of all items = total inventory value
- Cost of food used = Opening inventory + Purchases − Closing inventory

*Advantages:* Simple; no technology required; provides accurate snapshots
*Disadvantages:* Only accurate on count days; variance detected after the fact; time-intensive

**System 2: Perpetual Inventory System**

A running count of inventory updated in real time with every receipt and every issue.
- Requires disciplined recording of every product received (added to inventory) and every product issued from storage to production (subtracted from inventory)
- Provides theoretical on-hand quantity at any moment between physical counts
- Physical counts verify perpetual records; any difference = inventory variance requiring investigation

*Advantages:* Provides current inventory picture without counting; enables real-time ordering decisions
*Disadvantages:* Requires consistent recording discipline; errors accumulate between physical counts

**System 3: Point-of-Sale (POS) Recipe Depletion**

Modern POS systems can be configured with recipe ingredient lists so that each sale of a menu item automatically reduces the theoretical inventory of its components.
- Ingredient usage is tracked in real time based on dishes sold
- System generates "theoretical usage" reports
- Comparing theoretical usage to actual receipts and physical counts reveals variance (over-portioning, waste, theft)

*Advantages:* Highly detailed; identifies variance by ingredient and by dish
*Disadvantages:* Requires accurate recipe data entry; complex to configure; breakage and prep waste not automatically captured

**System 4: Inventory Management Software**

Dedicated software platforms (common in larger operations and chains) that combine ordering, receiving, production, and inventory management into a single system.
- Can set par levels and automatically generate purchase orders when levels fall below par
- Tracks supplier pricing history
- Integrates with accounting software
- Provides food cost reporting, variance analysis, and trend data

*Advantages:* Integrated; reduces administrative time; powerful analytics
*Disadvantages:* Cost of implementation; requires training; data quality only as good as the inputs

**Connecting Inventory to Food Cost**

```
The Food Cost Equation:

  Opening Inventory:    $12,500 (value of product at start of week)
+ Purchases (received): $8,200  (value of all deliveries received this week)
- Closing Inventory:    $10,800 (value of product remaining at end of week)
= Cost of Goods Used:   $9,900

  Food Revenue this week: $32,000

  Food Cost %: ($9,900 ÷ $32,000) × 100 = 30.9%  ← Within benchmark (28–35%)
```

**Inventory Variance Analysis**

When actual food cost % differs from theoretical (recipe-based calculation), investigation is required:

| Variance Cause | How to Detect | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Over-portioning** | Actual usage higher than theoretical based on covers; check with portion weights | Retrain; add portion accountability tools (scales at station) |
| **Spoilage/waste** | Waste log shows high disposal rates for specific items | Investigate: over-ordering? Storage failure? FIFO violation? |
| **Theft** | Variance consistent but waste log doesn't explain it | Investigate; audit access controls |
| **Counting error** | Single-period variance that corrects in following period | Recount; verify counting methodology |
| **Receiving error** | Product invoiced but not received; or quantity discrepancy | Cross-check receiving logs to invoices |
| **Recipe/costing error** | Theoretical cost was calculated incorrectly | Retest recipe; verify yield factors |

#### Deep Dive: Waste Management Procedures

**The Waste Hierarchy: Reduce, Re-Use, Recycle, Recover, Discard**

Waste management in a commercial kitchen is both an environmental responsibility and a direct financial lever. Every kilogram of food thrown away represents a purchased ingredient that generated zero revenue.

**1. REDUCE — Stop the Waste Before It Happens**

Reduction is the most effective waste management strategy because it prevents waste at the source:
- **Accurate forecasting:** Order to actual cover counts, not habit; avoid chronic over-purchasing of perishables
- **Menu design for cross-utilization:** Every ingredient has multiple applications so nothing expires unused
- **Proper storage:** FIFO rotation, correct temperatures, appropriate packaging — extends shelf life and prevents waste
- **Right-sizing portions:** Enforcing standardized portions reduces both plate waste (guest returns) and production waste

**2. RE-USE / RE-PURPOSE — Extract Value Before Disposal**

Before discarding a product that is still safe but past its peak quality for original use, evaluate whether it can serve another purpose:

| Product Situation | Re-purposing Option |
|---|---|
| Vegetable trim (peels, tops, cores) | Stock production; vegetable stock costs nothing in ingredient terms |
| Stale bread (1–2 days) | Croutons; breadcrumbs; bread pudding; panzanella; French onion soup base |
| Overripe fruit (soft but not spoiled) | Coulis; compote; smoothie; sorbet; sauce for desserts |
| Beef trim from butchery | Grinding for burgers; braised filling for pasties; staff meal |
| Outer cabbage/lettuce leaves | Stock; soup; staff meal |
| Fish bones and heads | Fish stock; fumet |
| Citrus halves after juicing | Flavouring for water; zest for desserts before juicing |
| Slightly wilted herbs | Herb oil; compound butter; dried and stored |

> **Industry Best Practice:** A systematic approach to re-purposing trim and surplus is called **whole-animal** or **nose-to-tail** (for meat and poultry) and **root-to-stem** (for produce) cooking. This philosophy is both financially sound and increasingly valued by guests as a sign of culinary integrity.

**3. RECYCLE — Divert Clean Materials from Landfill**

Recyclable materials from commercial kitchens include:
- Cardboard and paper (from packaging)
- Glass bottles and jars (clean and empty)
- Metal cans (clean and empty)
- Plastic containers (food-grade; clean)
- Cooking oil (collected by licensed grease recyclers for biodiesel or animal feed production)

> **Operational Note:** Used cooking oil must be collected by a licensed waste oil contractor — it should not be poured down drains (causes sewer blockages and environmental contamination). Many municipalities require licensed collection.

**4. RECOVER / COMPOST — Return Organic Matter to the Cycle**

Food scraps, inedible trim, plate waste, and expired organic product can be diverted from landfill through composting or organic waste programs:

- **On-site composting:** Practical for operations with outdoor space; produce and grain waste composted on-site
- **Municipal organic waste pickup:** Many Canadian municipalities now offer commercial organic waste collection; some have made it mandatory for large food service operators (requirements vary by municipality — verify locally)
- **Commercial composting facility** pickup: Organic waste collected and processed at an industrial composting facility

> **Regulatory Note:** Requirements for commercial organic waste diversion vary significantly by municipality across Canada. Some cities have mandatory diversion programs for commercial food operations above certain sizes; others are voluntary. Toronto, Vancouver, and many other major Canadian cities have commercial organic waste programs. Verify your municipality's specific requirements with your local waste management authority.

**5. DISCARD — Last Resort, Properly Handled**

When waste cannot be reduced, re-purposed, recycled, or composted, proper disposal into general waste is the final option. Disposal requirements:
- Organic waste must be sealed in bags before general waste disposal to prevent pest attraction
- Recalled products follow CFIA or supplier recall disposal instructions
- Chemical waste (cleaning products) follows SDS Section 13 and provincial hazardous waste regulations — do not pour concentrated chemicals down drains

#### Deep Dive: Units and Weight in Inventory Management

**Why Units and Weight Matter for Inventory Accuracy**

An inventory that mixes units of measure creates errors in cost calculations. Consistency is essential.

**Common Units of Measure in Canadian Commercial Kitchens**

| Category | Units Used | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Solid products (proteins, produce)** | Kilograms (kg), grams (g) | Weight is always preferred over count for pricing and costing accuracy |
| **Liquid products (dairy, oils, stocks)** | Litres (L), millilitres (mL) | Volume measurement; note that density affects weight (1L water ≠ 1L cream in weight) |
| **Countable individual items (eggs, lemons, heads of lettuce)** | Each (ea), dozen, case | Count-based; unit cost = case price ÷ count per case |
| **Packaged dry goods** | Kilogram, gram, or case quantity | Use weight; note case size (e.g., 4 × 4L means 4 units of 4L each = 16L total) |

**Reading Case Size Notation**

Case size notation describes the packaging structure of a product:

```
Format: [Units per case] / [Size per unit]

Examples:
  6/1L    = 6 bottles of 1L each = 6L total per case
  4/4L    = 4 jugs of 4L each = 16L total per case
  1/10 kg = 1 bag of 10 kg per case
  12/400g = 12 cans of 400g each = 4,800g (4.8 kg) total per case
  1/20 kg = 1 bulk bag of 20 kg per case

Unit cost calculation:
  Case of "6/1L" olive oil priced at $48.00/case
  Cost per litre: $48.00 ÷ 6 = $8.00/L
  Cost per 250 mL (recipe portion): $8.00 × 0.25 = $2.00
```

**AP Weight vs. EP Weight in Inventory**

Inventory is always counted and valued in **AP (as purchased) weight** — the actual weight of the product in storage, before any trimming or processing. EP (edible portion) weight is used for production planning and recipe costing but not for inventory records.

*Why this distinction matters:*

If you have 5 kg of whole chicken thighs (bone-in, skin-on) in your walk-in, your inventory record shows 5 kg at the AP price per kg. When the cook uses those thighs and debones and skins them, the EP yield is perhaps 65% = 3.25 kg of usable meat. The difference between 5 kg AP and 3.25 kg EP is not waste — it's expected yield loss accounted for in the recipe cost.

**Unit Conversion Reference**

| Convert From | To | Multiply By |
|---|---|---|
| Kilograms (kg) | Pounds (lb) | × 2.205 |
| Pounds (lb) | Kilograms (kg) | × 0.454 |
| Litres (L) | Fluid ounces (fl oz) | × 33.8 |
| Millilitres (mL) | Fluid ounces (fl oz) | × 0.0338 |
| Grams (g) | Ounces (oz) | × 0.0353 |
| Ounces (oz) | Grams (g) | × 28.35 |

> **Canadian Context:** Canada uses the metric system in commercial food operations. Recipe quantities, purchase orders, and inventory records should consistently use metric (kg, g, L, mL). However, many food service products (especially from US-origin supply chains) may be labelled in imperial; always convert to metric for consistency in Canadian operations.

#### Deep Dive: Verifying Product Specifications During Inventory

Inventory maintenance includes verifying that what is in storage still matches the specifications it was ordered and received to:

- **Market form:** Has the product been moved from its original container in a way that changed its identification? (e.g., a whole fillet cut into portions and placed in an unlabelled container)
- **Shelf life:** Does the remaining product have enough shelf life to be useful, or is it dangerously close to its use-by date?
- **Colour:** Is the colour consistent with the expected appearance for the product's age and storage conditions?
- **Case size / quantity:** Is the quantity in storage consistent with what the inventory record shows? Discrepancies indicate unrecorded usage, waste, or theft.
- **Packaging integrity:** Has any vacuum-sealed product developed a leak since receiving? Has any dry-goods packaging been compromised by moisture or pests?
- **Allergen information:** Are allergen-containing products still clearly labelled and segregated?

#### Deep Dive: Regulatory Framework for Inventory Maintenance

**HACCP Prerequisite Programs and Inventory**

Under HACCP, the practices of inventory maintenance — temperature monitoring, FIFO rotation, product labeling, waste management, and storage organization — are governed by **prerequisite programs (PRPs)**. These are foundational controls that support the HACCP system:

| Prerequisite Program | Inventory Maintenance Connection |
|---|---|
| **Temperature control** | Refrigerator and freezer logs confirm product is held at safe temperatures throughout its storage life |
| **FIFO / Stock rotation** | Dating and labeling procedures; physical rotation practice; audit of rotation compliance during inventory walks |
| **Pest management** | Inventory checks detect early signs of pest activity (insect damage, rodent evidence in dry goods); enables early intervention |
| **Cleaning and sanitation** | Clean storage areas prevent contamination of stored product; inspections during inventory walks assess cleanliness |
| **Product traceability** | Lot codes, receiving dates, and shellfish tags documented at receiving and maintained through FIFO rotation |

**CFIA Product Recalls**

The Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA) issues public food safety recalls for products that pose a health risk. Categories of recalls:
- **Class I:** A situation where there is a reasonable probability that using or being exposed to a recalled product will cause serious adverse health consequences or death
- **Class II:** A situation where using or being exposed to a recalled product may cause temporary adverse health consequences or where the probability of serious adverse health consequences is remote
- **Class III:** A situation where using or being exposed to a recalled product will not cause any adverse health consequences

*When a recall affects your inventory:*
1. Check all product in storage against the recalled lot codes
2. Immediately remove and isolate any recalled product (do not serve)
3. Follow the CFIA recall instructions for the specific product
4. Document removal from service in HACCP corrective action records

**Provincial Food Premises Regulations — Storage-Specific Requirements**

Common regulatory requirements for storage and inventory management across Canadian provinces:
- Temperature maintenance with documentation (temperature logs)
- Food stored at minimum 15 cm off the floor
- No room-temperature thawing of frozen products
- Proper labelling and dating of stored food
- No use of expired or adulterated food
- Chemical storage separate from food
- Active pest management program

## Common Mistakes in Inventory Maintenance

| Mistake | Risk / Consequence | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Taking inventory after delivery (not before) | Delivery inflates the count; opening inventory is inaccurate; food cost calculation is distorted | Always count before the delivery arrives |
| Estimating quantities rather than weighing or counting | Inaccurate inventory value; food cost errors; par-level ordering based on wrong numbers | Physically count or weigh everything; no estimates on inventory forms |
| Discarding product based on appearance alone without checking the date — and vice versa | Missing a product that has exceeded shelf life but "looks fine"; OR discarding product that is still within shelf life and perfectly good | Both date and condition are independent criteria; check both every time |
| Tasting product to check if it's still good | Risk of ingesting preformed toxins that are odourless and tasteless | Visual and olfactory assessment only for safety evaluation; taste only confirmed-safe product |
| Re-purposing product that has exceeded its shelf life | Serving unsafe or substandard product to guests; food safety risk | Re-purposing is appropriate for product still within shelf life; overdate product goes to waste |
| Not documenting disposal in the waste log | Unaccounted food cost variance; no pattern data to improve ordering; gaps in HACCP records | Every disposal is documented; date, item, quantity, reason, value |
| Using LIFO (Last In, First Out) in practice | Oldest product never moves; spoilage accumulates; hidden safety risks in storage | FIFO always; every new delivery goes behind existing stock |
| Not adjusting par levels for seasonal or volume changes | Chronic over-purchasing in slow periods; under-purchasing during busy periods; waste or 86'd items | Review par levels at least quarterly; adjust for known upcoming changes |
| Mixing units of measure on inventory sheets (some items in kg, others in lb, others in each) | Conversion errors; cost calculations become unreliable | Standardize on metric (kg, L, g, mL, ea) for all inventory records |
| Pouring cooking oil or concentrated chemicals down drains | Environmental damage; sewer infrastructure damage; regulatory violation | Cooking oil: licensed grease recycler; concentrated chemicals: provincial hazardous waste disposal per SDS Section 13 |

## Memory Aids for Apprentices

**"REDUCE, RE-USE, RECYCLE, RECOVER, DISCARD" — The Waste Hierarchy**
> In that order. Reduction prevents waste at source. Discarding is always the last option.
> The earlier in the hierarchy, the more value recovered and the less financial loss.

**The Food Cost Equation**
> **"Open + Buy − Close = Cost"**
> Opening Inventory + Purchases − Closing Inventory = Cost of Goods Used
> Cost of Goods ÷ Revenue × 100 = Food Cost %

**"Date AND Condition — Both, Not Either"**
> A product must pass BOTH date check AND condition check.
> Old but looks fine → still discard if shelf life exceeded.
> Fresh but smells off → still discard.

**Case Size Math**
> "Units times size equals total."
> 6/1L = 6 × 1 = 6L. 4/4L = 4 × 4 = 16L. 12/400g = 12 × 400g = 4,800g = 4.8 kg.

**Why "Looks Good" Is Never Enough for Safety**
> Botulinum toxin: colourless, odourless, tasteless. Staphylococcal enterotoxin: colourless, odourless, tasteless. E. coli O157:H7 in meat: no change in appearance or smell. The date is the safety check that sensory assessment cannot replace.

**Inventory Count Timing**
> "Count before, not after."
> Inventory count happens BEFORE the delivery arrives, so the count reflects actual on-hand stock uninfluenced by the new delivery.

## Key Red Seal Exam Knowledge

This section summarizes the most commonly tested concepts from this subtask for Red Seal examination preparation.

### The Food Cost Equation

```
Opening Inventory + Purchases − Closing Inventory = Cost of Goods Used (COGS)
COGS ÷ Food Revenue × 100 = Food Cost %
Target range: 28–35% (common industry benchmark)
```

### Waste Hierarchy (Highest Priority to Last Resort)

1. **Reduce** — prevent waste before it happens
2. **Re-use / Re-purpose** — cross-utilize product
3. **Recycle** — cardboard, glass, metal, plastics
4. **Recover / Compost** — organic waste diversion
5. **Discard** — last resort; proper disposal method

### Likely Exam Questions and Concepts

1. **What is the formula for calculating cost of goods used (food cost)?**
   → Opening Inventory + Purchases − Closing Inventory = Cost of Goods Used. Dividing by food revenue and multiplying by 100 gives food cost percentage.

2. **Define FIFO and explain why it is a food safety principle, not just a convenience.**
   → First In, First Out — oldest product used before newer product. It is a food safety principle because pathogens (*Listeria* in particular) continue to accumulate in properly refrigerated product over its shelf life. FIFO ensures product is used before it reaches a safety-relevant age, regardless of appearance.

3. **What are two independent criteria for discarding a product, and how do they interact?**
   → Date-based (product has exceeded maximum shelf life) and condition-based (product shows signs of spoilage). Either criterion alone is sufficient to discard. A product should be discarded if its shelf life is exceeded *even if* it appears and smells normal — some toxins are undetectable by sensory assessment.

4. **Name four inventory control systems used in commercial kitchens.**
   → Manual periodic inventory (physical count on schedule); perpetual inventory (running tally updated with every receipt and issue); POS recipe depletion (sales drive automatic ingredient draw-down); inventory management software (integrated platform with ordering and analytics)

5. **What is inventory variance and what are four possible causes?**
   → Inventory variance is the difference between the theoretical perpetual inventory and the actual physical count. Causes: over-portioning; spoilage/waste not documented; theft; counting errors; receiving errors; recipe costing errors.

6. **Explain the case size notation "12/400g" and calculate the total weight and cost per gram if the case price is $14.40.**
   → 12 units × 400g per unit = 4,800g (4.8 kg) total per case. Cost per gram: $14.40 ÷ 4,800g = $0.003/g ($3.00/kg).

7. **What is the difference between "best-before" and "use-by" dates?**
   → Best-before: quality may decline after this date but the product may still be safe; used on most commercially packaged goods. Use-by: the product should not be consumed after this date; a safety-based date applied to high-risk perishables. In a commercial kitchen, internally prepared products are given a calculated use-by date (prep date + maximum shelf life).

8. **Name five re-purposing options for common kitchen surplus or trim.**
   → Vegetable trim → stock; stale bread → croutons/breadcrumbs/bread pudding; overripe fruit → coulis/compote/sauce; beef trim → ground meat/braised filling; fish bones and heads → fish stock (any five from the list)

9. **What are the five categories of the waste management hierarchy?**
   → Reduce, Re-use/Re-purpose, Recycle, Recover/Compost, Discard — in priority order from most preferred to least preferred.

10. **What is the appropriate disposal method for cooking oil and concentrated cleaning chemicals?**
    → Cooking oil: collected by a licensed waste grease contractor for recycling into biodiesel or animal feed; not poured down drains. Concentrated cleaning chemicals: disposed of according to SDS Section 13 and provincial hazardous waste regulations; not poured down drains.

11. **When must inventory be counted — before or after a delivery? Why?**
    → Before a delivery. Counting after a delivery mixes the new stock with the existing stock, making it impossible to determine opening inventory accurately. The opening inventory count must reflect what was on hand before the new delivery inflated the stock.

12. **What is the purpose of a waste log in the context of inventory management?**
    → A waste log records all discarded product with quantity, reason, and approximate value. It connects disposal events to food cost variance (when actual food cost exceeds theoretical, the waste log explains why), supports HACCP corrective action documentation, and generates pattern data to improve ordering accuracy and storage practices.
